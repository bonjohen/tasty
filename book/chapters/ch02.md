# Chapter 2: What the Captain Keeps

The demon had been dead since before dawn and the cold had done half of Vark's work for her. She hung it from the gambrel by its ankles, waited for the last of the blood to drain into the trough, and watched the body sway once and settle. Then she wiped her knife on her thigh and began.

The skin came first. She scored along the inside of both legs, then down the midline from pelvis to sternum, working the blade shallow enough to preserve the fascia underneath. Demon hide was worth more intact. One careless nick through the underlayer would start the enzymes that turned good leather into paste within a day, and she had seen that mistake cost a hunter a season's wages. She peeled the skin in sections, pulling with her left hand and cutting with her right, and the hide separated with the sound of something consenting to come apart.

She had done this forty, maybe fifty times. The first had been difficult — not emotionally, she had seen worse by then, but technically. Demon anatomy ran close enough to human that the instinct was to cut where you would cut for a human carcass, and that instinct was wrong in several important places. The organs sat differently. The muscle attachments anchored at slightly different points along the bone. The rib structure was wider and more dense than it should have been. Learning the map had cost her three kills and the loss of a liver that would have covered two months of supplies.

She knew the map now. She could have drawn the cuts with her eyes closed.

The workspace was a rented stone room behind a tanner's yard in Cassavon — cold floor, drain in the center, iron hooks in the beams above. She had used it for the last four jobs and the tanner had stopped asking questions after the second payment. Vark paid in advance, cleaned thoroughly, and kept her work to the hours between midnight and dawn when the streets were empty and curiosity was sleeping. The tanner understood what she did in his back room the way the rest of the trade town understood: with the specific knowledge that not knowing was worth more than knowing, and that the woman who rented the room paid enough to make the distinction comfortable.

She separated the liver and laid it in the lined box she kept packed with snow and salt. The liver was the most valuable single organ per weight. A fresh one from a mature demon — properly drained, cleanly removed, no puncture to the bile sac — could cover her costs for a season. She had seen what the final buyers paid for the same liver after it passed through two more hands, and the number was enough to make a person angry if anger were useful. It was not. Vark understood the architecture of the trade the way she understood anatomy: as a system with fixed relationships between parts, where her position was defined by what she could reach and what she could not. She was the hands. The profit belonged to the people who owned the table.

After the liver came the kidneys, then the heart. The heart carried less value in isolation, but certain physicians in the southern cities prescribed it paired with the liver — something about the interaction of properties when consumed together. Vark did not know the theory and did not need to. She knew the price, and the price was better when the pair arrived in the same box from the same body.

The hands she removed at the wrists, wrapped separately, and placed in the second box. Demon hands carried a value she had always found faintly absurd — she suspected superstition more than pharmacology — but the market set the price and the price was the price. Bone she stacked for later. Marrow was worth extracting only when a buyer specified it in advance. Her current buyer had not.

She cut the major muscle groups from the thighs and packed them in salted cloth. She worked the shoulders next, then stripped the long muscles running parallel to the spine. These carried a modest value for their density and preserved well if salted within the first few hours. The work was physical and repetitive, and she moved through it with the rhythm of long practice — attention without ceremony, care without sentiment. A job. Her job. She was good at it.

She stepped back and looked at what remained on the gambrel. A carcass, opened and reduced to its useful components. It still had a face. The face had been unremarkable alive and was less so now. The demon had been young, male, and traveling alone through the passes above Cassavon — moving like someone who did not expect to be watched. He had been wrong about that. She had tracked him for a day and a half, taken him at dawn while he slept beside a dead fire, and the kill had been clean enough that the product was undamaged. That was the standard. Clean kill, clean product. Everything else was waste.

By the time she heard the knock she was nearly finished. She wiped her hands, checked the floor for anything visible from the doorway, and unbarred the door.

Koss stood outside with his hands deep in his coat and his breath coming fast. He always breathed hard after climbing the hill to the tanner's yard. He was a factor for one of the southern trading houses — a middleman who connected hunters like Vark to the buyers who consumed what hunters provided. He was not fat, exactly, but he carried the softness of a man whose labor was conducted at desks and in counting rooms. His eyes found the carcass on the gambrel. His eyes left the carcass.

"You could have brought it to the depot," he said.

"The depot is three streets from the garrison. I don't carry product past soldiers."

"It's a short walk."

"It's a short walk to prison, too." She stood aside to let him in. "Everything is separated. Liver, kidneys, heart, hide, hands, long muscle, shoulder cuts. Bone if you want it."

Koss moved through the room with the careful steps of a man who did not want to touch anything and did not want to be seen not touching anything. He inspected the lined boxes, lifting each lid and leaning in to assess what was inside without quite bringing his face close enough to smell. The ritual of inspection was part of the transaction. Vark doubted Koss could tell a good liver from a bad one without help, but the performance served its purpose and she allowed it because Koss paid reliably, did not renegotiate after agreeing to a price, and did not ask where the bodies came from. That last quality was worth more than the other two combined.

"The liver is clean?" he asked.

"As clean as they come."

"Last time the client raised a concern about the taste."

"Last time the client was blending product from three suppliers and calling it a single source. That is a quality problem I cannot solve from my end."

Koss conceded the point with a movement of his head that fell short of a nod. He looked at the boxes again. "I can take the organs and the hide today. The muscle I'll send someone for tomorrow."

"Tomorrow the price goes up."

"Why?"

"Because storage is a service. I don't keep this room for free and I don't keep product overnight for charity." She said it the way she said most things — as a statement of fact that did not require agreement in order to be true.

Koss produced a purse and counted out the agreed sum. Vark watched him count because she always watched. Not because she expected him to short her. Because watching was part of the discipline of being someone who was not shorted. He finished counting, then added three coins above the sum and placed them on the table beside the boxes.

"For the trouble of tomorrow," he said. "And because I have a question."

She took the coins, counted them herself, and put them away. "Ask."

"There's been talk from the eastern ranges. The houses up there are putting word out for specific product. Mountain stock. Deep-range. Not the usual crossroads kills." He looked at her as though the next part required something from her — permission or patience, she was not sure which. "Someone is paying a premium for fresh material from the interior. Not aged, not dried, not preserved. Fresh. They want it still warm when it arrives."

"Who?"

"I don't have a name yet. I have the price they're offering and I know it doesn't make sense."

"Tell me why."

"It's too high. By a factor of three, maybe four. Nobody pays that much for mountain stock unless they want something specific inside the meat. Something that doesn't survive the usual preservation."

Vark lifted one of the hide sections from the drying rack and folded it. The hide was heavy — denser than human skin, tougher at the edges where the demon's shoulders had thickened from whatever forces shaped them in the deep ranges. She folded it with one hand, gripping and turning the stiff leather with an ease that had nothing to do with technique. It was strength. More strength than a woman her size should carry, and Koss looked away from the display with the practiced discomfort of a man who knew exactly what that strength meant and preferred not to think about it.

That was fair. Most people preferred not to think about it. Vark herself thought about it only in practical terms. You ate what you killed, in measured amounts, over time. The eating changed you. The changes made you better at the killing, which improved the quality of what you ate next, which deepened the changes further. It was a closed system. Not moral, not immoral. Efficient. She was stronger than she had been five years ago. Faster. Her night vision was better. She healed from cuts and bruises in days rather than weeks. She could work in cold that would have put her on her back a decade ago. These were professional advantages. She maintained them the way a carpenter maintained tools.

She set the folded hide on the table. "I'll think about it."

"Think quickly. The word is traveling east and you're not the only capable hunter working this range."

"I'm the best hunter working this range."

Koss did not argue the point. He gathered the boxes and the folded hides and let himself out into the cold. Vark barred the door behind him and stood alone in the stone room with what was left of what had been a living creature and was now waste and inventory. She cleaned her tools with the methodical attention she gave everything that might rust, dull, or leave a trace. She scrubbed the stone floor until the water ran clean into the drain. She took the carcass down from the gambrel and wrapped it in limed canvas, tying the bundle with the knots a woman in the eastern camps had taught her years ago — the same woman who had also taught her that squeamishness was the first thing this work stripped from you and the last thing anyone missed.

The room was empty when she left it. The tanner would find nothing in the morning except the smell of lye and salt, and those were his own smells, and he would not look further. The coins sat heavy and warm in Vark's coat as she walked the dark streets toward her room. The cold was the ordinary cold of a trade town in late winter — nothing like the cold in the mountains, which had teeth and patience and did not care whether you understood it or not.

She thought about the eastern passes as she walked. Mountain stock. Fresh. A price that was wrong by a factor of three or four. Someone with money and appetite building something or buying something that required material the usual supply chain could not provide. These things happened periodically. A physician with a new theory. A noble with a new craving. A collector who had read too many old texts and believed that the right combination of parts, consumed in the right order, could grant something more than ordinary strength.

Usually it came to nothing. Usually the money evaporated when the results failed to match the promise, and the hunters who had chased the contract found themselves overextended in dangerous country with nothing to show for it but risk.

But the number Koss had named was not a collector's number. It was an investor's number. And investors, in Vark's experience, did not pay four times the market rate unless they had already seen returns that justified the expense.

She filed it away. She would ask around. She would do it carefully, because careful was the only way she had ever done anything that mattered.

The streets were quiet. The work was done. The money was good.

That was enough for tonight.
