# Writing-Blunders Review — Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

**Review type:** Genre control, emotional timing, suspense management, reveal discipline
**Rules applied:** `.claude/rules/writing-blunders.md`
**Chapter intent (from section_03_outline.md):** Show the last full expression of Hakiia as the brother Hurkuzak has always known — fierce, tactically brilliant, terrifying even when broken. Then show the cost. The wolf attack turns disaster into doom.

---

## Individual Issues

---

### Issue 1

**Quoted passage:**
> "They moved in the same direction he moved. They maintained the same pace. They were not pursuing. They were accompanying."

**Problem:** Narration interprets the scene before the reader can experience it.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The phrase "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying" is an editorial gloss — a neat conceptual frame that the author applies before the reader has had time to feel the difference. The visual information (same direction, same pace) already does the work. The interpretive sentence converts unease into a small intellectual satisfaction, releasing tension at the exact moment it should be tightening. Suspense lives in ambiguity; labeling the wolves' behavior as "accompanying" resolves an ambiguity that should stay open through the next paragraph.

**Revision direction:** Cut "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying." Let the behavior — same direction, same pace — speak without annotation. The reader will feel the wrongness of it more sharply without the label.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 2

**Quoted passage:**
> "It came from the left, angling in from behind a stand of dead timber, and it stopped twenty paces away and stood there. It was large — larger than the dogs had been, with a thick winter coat and a wide head and eyes that caught the failing light and gave none of it back."

**Problem:** Detail sequence releases tension prematurely.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The wolf is described fully — size, coat, head shape, eyes — before Hurkuzak reacts. This is the observational rhythm of a safe narrator, not a man on the edge of his survival. The moment a threat stops twenty paces away, a frightened man's attention is not comprehensive: it lands on the most dangerous thing (the eyes, the posture, the size) and then snaps to his own weapon. The thorough inventory creates a brief tonal drift toward nature writing that costs the scene its edge.

**Revision direction:** Compress the description. Let Hurkuzak register size and eyes and stop — the coat and wide head arrive in a second look if they arrive at all. His hand going to the hatchet should follow immediately on the wolf stopping, not after a full physical catalog.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 3

**Quoted passage:**
> "After a time the wolf turned and walked back toward the trees. Not running. Walking. With the unhurried gait of something that had seen what it needed to see and was going to report."

**Problem:** Narration interprets animal psychology in a way that explains rather than unsettles.

**Why it weakens the effect:** "Going to report" is a metaphor that converts the wolf's behavior into comprehensible social communication. It makes the wolf feel more like a military scout than an animal, which risks deflating the threat into something almost legible. More damaging: it tells the reader exactly what to think before the next beat (Hakiia's "Two more. Behind us, right side") lands. That beat should feel like a cold shock of confirmation — wolves have been reading the situation, are coordinated, are already ahead of Hurkuzak's understanding. The editorial metaphor anticipates that beat and softens it.

**Revision direction:** Cut "going to report." Let the wolf's retreat be only a retreat — deliberate, unhurried, unexplained. The reader's unease about what the retreat means is more useful than the answer.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Quoted passage:**
> "They spread themselves around the clearing in a loose formation that covered every approach, and they watched with the shared attention of animals that had hunted together long enough that their silence was its own language — spread wide, none of them looking at each other, and yet the spread was exact."

**Problem:** Overwritten sentence that strains for effect and explains subtext directly.

**Why it weakens the effect:** "Their silence was its own language" is the kind of phrase that calls attention to the author rather than disappearing into the scene. Worse, "and yet the spread was exact" tells the reader the conclusion — coordinated — when the image of five wolves spread precisely without looking at each other already delivers that conclusion without commentary. The dash-and-gloss construction ("long enough that their silence was its own language") is a writer reaching for resonance in a sentence that should be lean and cold. The section outline asks for the wolves to feel like animals, not symbols; this sentence is working against that note.

**Revision direction:** Cut the clause from the dash onward. "They spread themselves around the clearing in a loose formation that covered every approach, and they watched — spread wide, none of them looking at each other, and yet the spacing was exact." Stop there. The exactness of the spacing does the symbolic work without the author pointing at it.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 5

**Quoted passage:**
> "a compressed bark of direction that meant move, turn, look."

**Problem:** Narration translates nonverbal signal into explicit meaning.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The parenthetical gloss — "that meant move, turn, look" — removes the reader from Hurkuzak's experience and positions them above it, watching a subtitled scene. The power of the moment is that Hakiia communicates through sound alone, that Hurkuzak knows what it means through years of living beside this man. If the narrative renders that translation explicitly, the reader gets information but loses the felt relationship. The chapter should make the reader feel how completely these two brothers can communicate without words; labeling the bark's meaning explains that feeling instead of producing it.

**Revision direction:** Cut the translation. "Behind him Hakiia was making a sound that was not a word but a command." Period. Let Hurkuzak's response (he turns, looks) demonstrate what it meant.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Quoted passage:**
> "What happened next was fast enough that he only understood it afterward, in pieces — what he saw, what the blood in the snow showed him, what the sounds matched to."

**Problem:** Pacing frame signals a slow-motion moment before the moment arrives, deflating its shock.

**Why it weakens the effect:** This is a structuring device — "I'll now describe something fast by describing it slowly" — and the reader feels the scaffolding. The sentence announces that something extraordinary is coming, gives the reader a moment to brace, and then delivers it. That preparation softens impact. Hakiia's attack should arrive with the same shock it has for Hurkuzak: fast, wrong, impossible. The frame converts shock into narrated recollection before the event has even happened on the page.

**Revision direction:** Delete the framing sentence entirely. Start with the action: "Hakiia's upper body rose from the travois in a motion that should not have been possible—" Let the prose itself carry the fragmentation and speed through short, punching sentences. The reader will feel the disorientation without being warned about it first.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 7

**Quoted passage:**
> "His left hand reached for the wolf — the arm grinding in the shoulder, the fingers slow to close, the joint protesting with a sound Hurkuzak could hear from five paces away — and caught the animal by the scruff of the neck. The grip was imperfect, the damaged shoulder shuddering under the weight, but it held because Hakiia forced it to hold."

**Problem:** "but it held because Hakiia forced it to hold" — explaining the mechanism of will breaks the register.

**Why it weakens the effect:** This is the climactic moment of Hakiia's final act of terrifying competence. The phrase "because Hakiia forced it to hold" is a narrative explanation of what the scene should make the reader feel without being told. The image — a failing grip holding anyway, a grinding shoulder still pulling the wolf — already communicates will and violence. Naming "forced it to hold" as a causal explanation converts visceral dread into character psychology summary. The section outline specifically asks for this moment to "unsettle the reader" — that effect requires the reader to witness the impossible without the narration mediating it into an explanation.

**Revision direction:** Cut the explanatory clause. "The grip was imperfect, the damaged shoulder shuddering under the weight, but it held." Full stop. Trust the previous physical detail to carry the force.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 8

**Quoted passage:**
> "The remaining wolves watched from a distance. Two dead. One limping from the blow to its shoulder. The rest — three, maybe four — stood in a loose arc at the edge of the clearing and looked at what had just happened and recalculated."

**Problem:** "recalculated" anthropomorphizes the wolves at the wrong moment.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The chapter has been careful — mostly — to keep the wolves as animals. "Recalculated" is the language of decision-making under information, of a mind revising a plan. Coming immediately after the most visceral moment in the chapter, it introduces a small tonal misfire: the wolves become briefly rational agents rather than opportunistic predators. The section outline notes that wolves should feel like animals and that "the symbolic weight should emerge from the situation, not from the prose pointing at it." "Recalculated" is a gentle version of the prose pointing.

**Revision direction:** Replace with animal behavior: "stood in a loose arc at the edge of the clearing and looked at what was left of the attack." Let the reader infer the arithmetic without the wolves doing it consciously.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 9

**Quoted passage:**
> "The living ones had looked like something else."

**Problem:** Vague resonance statement that tries to elevate the scene without committing to what the something else was.

**Why it weakens the effect:** This sentence reaches for the effect of a closing thought without earning it. "Something else" is an intentional withhold, but the withhold feels writerly rather than felt — an author applying a foreboding quality by deferring meaning. What did the living wolves look like? The prose has already shown it: coordinated, reading weakness, probing methodically, waiting with a patience that suggested intelligence. The reader has seen the "something else" already. Naming it as withheld implies the reader needs a nudge toward significance that the scene itself has already delivered.

**Revision direction:** Cut the sentence entirely, or replace it with a specific physical image from the chapter — something Hurkuzak actually saw — that carries the same weight without the vague gesture. "The living ones had looked patient" works harder than "something else."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 10

**Quoted passage:**
> "Hurkuzak looked at his brother. Hakiia lay in the wolf's blood with his eyes closed and his chest rising and falling in a pattern that was wrong — too shallow, too fast. Something had changed in him — a slackening that did not look like rest, a settling that did not look like ease."

**Problem:** The narration over-interprets the physical observation, converting sensory detail into emotional conclusion.

**Why it weakens the effect:** "Something had changed in him" is the narrator stepping in to confirm what the physical detail — the wrong breathing pattern, the slackening — has already communicated. The reader does not need to be told something has changed after being shown the wrong breathing and the slackening. What follows — "a slackening that did not look like rest, a settling that did not look like ease" — is good specific observation. The sentence that precedes it ("Something had changed in him") is an unnecessary introduction to those specifics that pre-empts the reader's experience of making the inference themselves. This is a direct violation of the book's principle: do not summarize emotion; show the behavioral evidence and trust the reader.

**Revision direction:** Cut "Something had changed in him —" entirely. Begin with: "a slackening that did not look like rest, a settling that did not look like ease." Or restructure: "His chest rose and fell in a pattern that was wrong — too shallow, too fast. There was a slackening in him that did not look like rest." Let the reader do the work of naming the change.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 11

**Quoted passage:**
> "The look was not correction. Not assessment. It was brief and unguarded and Hurkuzak could not hold it any more than Hakiia could."

**Problem:** The emotional payoff of the unguarded look is named and labeled rather than rendered through behavior.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The section outline identifies this as one of the chapter's key emotional beats — the moment Hakiia's face does something other than assess, command, or correct. The outline specifically warns that this must not be oversold, and that the warmth should be ambiguous enough that Hurkuzak can doubt it later. This version over-delivers the significance. "Unguarded" is an interpretive word that tells the reader what quality the look has; "could not hold it any more than Hakiia could" symmetrizes the vulnerability in a way that makes the moment feel mutual and confirmed. That symmetry closes something that should remain open. The reader should not be certain whether Hakiia's look was tenderness or pain or recognition or nothing at all.

**Revision direction:** Render only what Hurkuzak physically sees. "Hakiia looked at Hurkuzak. The look lasted a moment and then it was gone." Strip the interpretation. Let Hurkuzak — and the reader — sit with not knowing what it was. The chapter outline notes that the almost-tender moment belongs to Chapter 7, with the thumb stump pressing against the arm. This look in Chapter 6 should be the opening of ambiguity, not its partial resolution.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 12

**Quoted passage:**
> "his body vibrating with something that was not shaking and not stillness and not anything he recognized."

**Problem:** Emotion described through negation rather than sensation.

**Why it weakens the effect:** The triple negative construction — "not shaking and not stillness and not anything he recognized" — is an elegant dodge. It signals that the experience is hard to name (which is correct) but does so by stacking what it is not rather than finding a specific sensation that carries the unnamed quality. The technique is more visible than effective: the reader recognizes the rhetorical pattern of "ineffable experience rendered through negation" and processes it at the level of craft rather than feeling it as Hurkuzak's body. A single physical specific — the way his hands felt, the sound in his ears, the texture of the moment — would do more work with less author-visibility.

**Revision direction:** Replace with a sensory specific. What does Hurkuzak's body actually do when it is in that state? His ears ring. His vision is too sharp. He does not feel cold. One physical detail that is wrong in a recognizable way — not a catalog of what the state is not.

**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 6 is substantially well-executed. The physical construction of the wolf attack, Hakiia's impossible competence from the ground, the mechanical destruction of his hand, and the cauterization scene all demonstrate strong control of this genre's specific register — literary dark fantasy that earns its horror through implication and precise physical detail rather than gore or drama. The chapter succeeds at its hardest task: making Hakiia terrifying and real in the same moment.

The recurring weakness is narration that cannot resist confirming what it has already shown. Multiple times the prose delivers a strong sensory or behavioral image and then appends a sentence explaining what the reader should take from it: "going to report," "their silence was its own language," "something had changed in him," "it held because Hakiia forced it to hold." Each of these represents a failure of trust in the reader — a small but consistent habit of reaching for a landing that the image has already provided. In a chapter this strong, these gestures stand out as interruptions to an otherwise disciplined performance.

There is also a pattern of suspense-releasing interpretation: the wolves' behavior labeled as "accompanying," their departure labeled as tactical recalculation, their threat confirmed as "something else." In each case the prose is trying to generate resonance but achieves the opposite — it closes ambiguity at the moment when ambiguity is most valuable.

3. **Issue 10 — "Something had changed in him"**: Pre-empts the reader's inference just before the specific physical observations that would have produced that inference. Violates the book's core rule on showing vs. summarizing. *High*

4. **Issue 1 — "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying"**: Releases suspense at the exact moment the wolf-approach sequence should be tightening. Converts unease into a labeled concept. *High*

5. **Issue 3 — "going to report"**: Softens the wolf-coordination reveal that Hakiia delivers two lines later. The scene's best horror beat is partially telegraphed by the narration. *High*

6. **Issue 4 — "their silence was its own language"**: Explains the wolves' coordination and reaches for symbolic resonance in violation of the section outline's explicit instruction that symbolic weight must emerge from situation, not from prose pointing at it. *High*

7. **Issue 7 — "because Hakiia forced it to hold"**: Converts the chapter's most unsettling image — impossible competence — into a psychology summary. Explains the effect instead of producing it. *High*

8. **Issue 12 — "not shaking and not stillness and not anything he recognized"**: Negation construction that signals ineffability rather than delivering it. The technique is visible enough to pull the reader out of the scene. *Medium*

## Pattern Summary

The writer has strong instincts for physical specificity and restraint in the chapter's hardest moments — the hand destruction, the cauterization, the wolf's mechanical jaw. The recurring failure is a habit of double-delivering: showing the right image and then appending the interpretation the image already contains. This pattern appears in approximately eight of the twelve issues identified. It suggests a writer who trusts the image when working in full prose, but becomes uncertain at the moment of landing — and reaches for a confirming sentence as a hedge against the reader missing the point.

A secondary pattern is suspense-release by labeling: converting threatening ambiguity into legible frames ("accompanying," "going to report," "recalculated," "something had changed"). Each label is modestly done and could be defended individually, but together they constitute a consistent habit of interpreting the scene for the reader at the moments when the reader's own unease would produce more effect than any interpretation.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Remove the pacing frame before the wolf kill** (Issue 6). Cut "What happened next was fast enough that he only understood it afterward, in pieces — what he saw, what the blood in the snow showed him, what the sounds matched to." Begin the action cold.

2. **Strip the emotional interpretation from the closing look** (Issue 11). Render only the physical fact of the look and its brevity. Leave its meaning ambiguous.

3. **Cut "Something had changed in him"** (Issue 10). Start with the physical specifics ("a slackening that did not look like rest") directly.

4. **Remove "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying."** (Issue 1). The behavioral evidence already produces this understanding.

5. **Cut "going to report"** (Issue 3). End the wolf's retreat at "deliberate, unhurried" — no explanation.

6. **Cut the resonance gloss from the wolf formation sentence** (Issue 4). End at "the spacing was exact."

7. **Cut "because Hakiia forced it to hold"** (Issue 7). "The grip was imperfect, the damaged shoulder shuddering under the weight, but it held."

8. **Replace the negation-stack** (Issue 12) with a single physical specific.

9. **Cut "The living ones had looked like something else"** (Issue 9) or replace with a specific image already present in the chapter.

10. **Replace "recalculated"** (Issue 8) with animal behavior language.

---

## Primary Problem Note

The main problem is **premature interpretation** — a consistent habit of the narration reaching in to confirm or label what the imagery has already communicated. This is not a tone mismatch or structural tension failure; the chapter's architecture is sound and its emotional escalation is correctly sequenced. It is a line-level discipline problem: the prose delivers the right image and then does not trust it. In a book whose core rule is "show the behavioral evidence, trust the reader," that habit is the principal obstacle to the chapter reaching its full force.
