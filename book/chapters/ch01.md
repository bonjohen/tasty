# Chapter 1: The Older Brother's Country

The snow was three days old and the wind had packed it into something harder than ground. Hurkuzak's boots broke through the crust with every third step, and each time the shock climbed into his calves. Ahead of him, Hakiia walked without breaking through at all.

Hakiia moved over the world as though it had been laid for his particular weight. Hurkuzak moved through it like something the landscape had not agreed to carry.

The dogs pulled steady. Four of them, thick-shouldered and patient, their breath smoking in the still air. The sled rode heavy behind them — furs, smoked meat, bundles of traps, the skinning tools Hakiia had sharpened the night before while Hurkuzak watched his hands work because watching was better than attempting conversation. The runners hissed over the packed snow. The trees on either side were black pillars crusted white, the sky the color of spent ash. Nothing in any direction suggested that anyone had ever been here before or would come here after.

Hakiia stopped. He did not announce the stop or signal it. He simply was no longer moving, and Hurkuzak caught up and halted beside him, pretending the sudden change of pace had not made him stumble.

"Your grip." Hakiia was looking at Hurkuzak's hand on the hatchet hanging from his belt. Not the bow, not the knife. The hatchet. "You carry it like something you're afraid of."

Hurkuzak adjusted his hand.

"Higher."

He moved it.

"Now you'll drop it when you swing. Where were you before?"

Hurkuzak tried to remember where his hand had been. It had been wrong, apparently. Where it was now was also wrong. Hakiia watched the attempt to recover the original error and then turned away with a sound that carried no word in it — half cough, half dismissal — and walked on. The correction had taken less than a minute. The feeling of having failed a test he had not known he was taking would last the rest of the day.

They walked for another hour. The terrain climbed. The dogs worked harder and Hurkuzak worked harder and Hakiia simply walked, his pace unchanged, his breathing even. The cold deepened as the trees thinned and the wind found room to move. Hurkuzak's face went numb until his cheeks felt like something he was wearing rather than something he was. He pulled his wrapping tighter and said nothing about it because Hakiia's face was bare. It was always bare. Mentioning the cold was the same as mentioning weakness.

Hakiia raised his hand.

Hurkuzak stopped. The dogs stopped. Everything stopped except the wind and the cold. Hakiia spit into the air. The wind took it, and it froze with a crack before skittering away across the frozen snow.

Hakiia was watching the tree line a hundred paces uphill. His head was tilted in the way that meant he was listening with more than his ears — a stillness that tightened the air around him.

"Elk," he said quietly. "Upwind. Hundred and forty, maybe hundred and fifty paces. Bedded in the scrub." He pointed without moving his arm much. "Come around to the left. Stay below the ridgeline. I'll push it toward you."

"The snow is too loud on that side. It'll hear me before I'm in range."

"Then be quieter."

Hurkuzak unslung the bow. It was a good weapon — Hakiia had strung it himself, had tested the draw against his own arm and found it acceptable. He nocked an arrow and started left, keeping low, stepping where the crust had already been broken by wind or animal weight. The trees here were sparse and the ground between them was open and white and merciless. Every step announced itself. He could feel Hakiia watching him from behind, cataloguing the noise, and he tried to move the way Hakiia moved — placing weight slowly, rolling from heel to toe — but his body did not have the knowledge his brother's body had, and the snow cracked under him like dry bone.

The elk was bigger than he had expected. It was still bedded when he first caught sight of it through a gap in the scrub, a mound of gray-brown against the white, and its head was up and its ears were working. It had already heard something. Maybe the dogs. Maybe Hurkuzak's breathing. Maybe just the weight of a body in a space that had been empty.

He drew. The string pressed into his fingers and the bow bent and the arrow waited and the elk turned its head. He could see the eye now — dark, wet, alive — and the eye saw him and did not understand what it saw.

He held.

The elk stood. It was fast, bigger standing than it had been lying down, and it was going to run and he was going to miss and he released. Not a killing shot. The arrow struck behind the shoulder and too high, and the animal staggered and cried out and ran badly, its legs driving it forward in a lopsided sprint that sprayed snow and blood in equal measure.

Hakiia came from the right side of the tree line like something that had always been there and had simply decided to move. He covered the ground without visible effort, without noise, without the gracelessness that physical exertion cost ordinary bodies. The elk saw him too late. Hakiia's knife opened its throat in a single motion, and the animal dropped into the snow with a sound that was wet and final and surprisingly quiet for the amount of blood it produced.

Hakiia crouched beside it. He was already working — pulling the head back, opening the belly with two long cuts, reaching in with both hands to find the organs that mattered before the heat left. His forearms were red to the elbow. Steam rose from the cavity.

"You hesitated," he said. He did not look up.

"I was adjusting."

"You were not adjusting. You were deciding whether you wanted to kill it. That is not a decision that belongs at full draw." His hands kept working while he spoke, separating and sorting with the efficiency of long practice. "The decision is made when you nock. After that, there is only the act."

Hurkuzak stood with the bow at his side, watching his brother dress the kill. There was nothing useful he could say.

Hakiia separated the liver quickly, setting it on clean snow. He cut the heart free and wrapped it in a piece of hide. He found the kidneys and placed them beside the liver. Then he began on the muscle, cutting with a precision that made the butchery look like the animal came apart along lines only Hakiia could see.

"Take the liver," Hakiia said. "Wrap it properly this time."

Hurkuzak knelt and picked up the liver. It was warm on one side, and frozen on the other. It was heavier than it should have been, as though organs carried a different weight than the bodies that housed them. He wrapped it in the oiled cloth Hakiia kept for this purpose, tying the ends the way he had been shown, and Hakiia watched him tie it and said nothing, which meant it was adequate.

They loaded the sled in silence. Hakiia placed each wrapped piece in its correct position and handed Hurkuzak the lighter pieces without comment. Not because he was being kind. Because it was faster. He carried the haunches himself, not because Hurkuzak could not lift them, but because he would not trust the placement to someone who held a hatchet like it frightened him. Somewhere inside that refusal to delegate was a kind of attention that in a different family might have been called care.

They made camp in a stand of trees where the wind broke against the trunks and the snow was shallow enough to clear. Hakiia built the fire and began cutting and stacking blocks of hacked snow. He did not ask for help and Hurkuzak did not offer because the offer would be refused in a way that made offering feel foolish. The fire and the shelter came fast under Hakiia's hands. They always did.

"Set the perimeter stakes," Hakiia said. "And check the dogs. The left rear is favoring a paw."

Hurkuzak drove the stakes into the frozen ground, pounding them in with a heavy rock where the frozen soil resisted. He checked the dogs afterward. The left rear had a split in one pad that the cold had cracked further. He cleaned it and wrapped it and the dog watched him steadily while he worked. He ran his hand over its ear once. The dog leaned into his palm. He did not name them aloud — Hakiia kept no names for the dogs — but he named them privately and the left rear was the one he thought of as the quiet one, because it pulled without complaint and ate last.

When he returned to the fire, Hakiia had the liver sliced and cooking on a flat stone at the fire's edge. The smell was rich and dark and ancient. Hurkuzak's stomach pulled tight. He sat. Hakiia handed him a piece on the point of his knife and Hurkuzak took it and ate it and it was good. Hakiia's cooking was always good. That never helped.

They ate in something close to comfort. The fire settled into its work. The dogs curled into one another. The cold pressed in from every direction but the flames held it to a distance that felt temporary and precious. In the silence Hakiia's face softened, or the firelight made it seem so, and for a few minutes there was nothing between them but warmth and meat and the sound of the wood breaking down into heat.

"Mother's people held land near here," Hakiia said. He said it the way he said most things — as information that carried an expectation, though the expectation was not yet visible.

"I know."

"You know the name. You've never walked it." He tore a strip of meat with his teeth and chewed slowly. "She could move through snow the way wind moves through open country. Without asking permission." Something in his voice was almost generous when he said it, as though the memory of their mother was one of the few things he could hold without squeezing.

"You didn't get that from her," Hakiia said. "You walk through winter like someone who thinks the cold owes him an apology." He looked at Hurkuzak across the fire. "I brought you here because you have never been cold enough. You eat well, you sleep warm, you dress against the weather, and you think that means you understand it. You don't understand it. You survive it. That is not the same thing, and the difference will kill you when I am not here to cover it."

Hurkuzak said nothing. There was nothing to say that would not become another lesson, and he was tired of lessons, tired of learning from them mainly that his brother thought suffering could fix him. He looked at the fire and felt the familiar collapse — the moment where the warmth between them turned into something instructional, and the instruction carried inside it the unshakable belief that Hurkuzak was not enough. Not strong enough. Not hard enough. Not willing enough to do what the world required.

And the worst of it — the part he could never say and could never stop knowing — was that Hakiia was not wrong. Hurkuzak was softer. Slower. Less willing to do what cold and hunger and violence demanded. He knew this about himself the way he knew his own hands. Beneath the resentment and the envy and the fear, there was something worse: the knowledge that without Hakiia the world would be a place he did not know how to survive. Not because he was incapable. Because Hakiia had made sure, through years of correction and dominance and casually brilliant care, that Hurkuzak had never had to learn.

They slept with an oil lamp on, all wrapped in furs, and close enough that Hurkuzak could hear his brother's breathing. Hakiia fell asleep the way he did everything else — quickly, completely, and without apparent difficulty. One moment he was awake. The next he was gone into whatever country men like him went when they closed their eyes.

Hurkuzak lay on his back and watched the dark. The fire had burned to embers and the cold was winning the ground the flames had held. Above him the sky was enormous and black and the stars were scattered across it like spilled salt on a dark hide. The trees creaked in the wind. A dog shifted and sighed. Somewhere far off, something howled — a long, clean sound that carried no anger, only hunger and distance and the patience of a creature that understood the night belonged to it.

He thought about the elk. The dark eye. The held breath. He thought about Hakiia's knife, the speed and the ease and the absolute absence of hesitation. He thought about the liver cooking on the stone and the warmth of it in his mouth.

He did not sleep for a long time.

Beside him, Hakiia breathed evenly, like a man who had never once found the world difficult enough to keep him awake.
