# Chapter 8: What Hakiia Asks

The march was failing. Hurkuzak knew it. The knowing changed nothing. His body moved the way a wheel turns after the hand that spun it is gone, and the travois dragged behind him through the snow. Step and drag. Step and drag. The poles had worn grooves into his palms deeper than skin. The blood from them had frozen into the wood, and the wood had become a part of his hands or his hands had become a part of the wood, and the distinction no longer seemed important.

The valley had narrowed into something close to a gorge. The trees pressed in on both sides, dark and heavy with snow, and the sky above was a strip of gray. The cabin was ahead. He had to believe it because the alternative was not something he could carry in addition to everything else.

Behind him, Hakiia had been silent for hours. Not the silence of a man who chooses when to speak. The silence of withdrawal — a body turning inward toward something that did not include Hurkuzak. When Hurkuzak had checked the travois at the last stop, Hakiia's eyes had been open but unfocused, watching the sky.

Then Hakiia spoke.

"Stop."

Hurkuzak stopped. He set down the drag poles and his shoulders screamed with the release of tension and his knees tried to fold and he locked them and stood. He turned to look at his brother.

Hakiia was looking at him. Not the drifting, dulled eyes of the last several hours but the sharp, certain eyes that Hurkuzak had known his entire life — the eyes that assessed the world and found it legible. He was fully himself.

"Sit down," Hakiia said. His voice was thin, but the authority in it was intact. "We need to talk."

Hurkuzak sat in the snow beside the travois. The cold was no longer an event but a condition, like the weight of stone, or grief.

Hakiia looked at the sky for a moment. Then at his legs. Then at Hurkuzak. He looked at him the way he had looked at the elk's body before cutting it open.

"My right leg is dead," he said. "The flesh is dying from the ankle upward. By tonight or tomorrow it will be the knee. After that, the rot will move into the blood, if it hasn't already. I can feel it. It is warm in ways that warm should not be."

"We can still — "

"We cannot. Listen to me." He let the words drop like weight. "The left leg is broken in two places. Even if the right leg were whole, I would not walk for months. With both legs broken and one of them rotting, I will not walk again." He paused. "And the hands. You have seen the hands."

Hurkuzak had seen the hands. He had cauterized the right one himself, pressing heated metal against torn flesh while his brother made a sound that no distance or years would separate from the smell of burning skin. He had watched the left arm stop responding, watched it become dead weight, watched Hakiia try to lift it and fail.

"I cannot hold a knife," Hakiia said. "I cannot hold food. I cannot hold a waterskin. I cannot, if the need arose, do the thing myself." He said this last part with a flatness that carried more weight than any emphasis could have. He had already looked at the knife lying in the snow beside the travois and could not close a hand around it. "I know this. You know this. We have been pretending otherwise because pretending was easier than what comes after we stop, and now we are going to stop."

"The cabin may be a day away," Hakiia said. "It may be two. You are injured, exhausted, and moving at a pace that is slower every hour. If you reach it with me, you arrive with a dying man who needs constant care, no food worth counting, no medicine, no help coming, and winter still at the door. You know what happens then. You spend your strength keeping me alive for days, maybe a week, and then I die anyway, and then you are alone in a cabin in the mountains in winter with nothing, and you die too." He paused. "That is the reckoning. And we are done pretending it works out any other way."

"What are you asking?"

His stomach dropped. His throat tightened. His hands, ruined as they were, curled into themselves as though trying to hold something that was already gone.

"Kill me," Hakiia said. "Here. Now. While I am still myself and the asking is mine."

"No."

"Listen — "

"No."

He stood too fast. His bad knee buckled. He caught himself and did not sit back down. He could not sit beside this demand.

"Sit down," Hakiia said.

"No."

"You are standing in the snow refusing to listen because you are afraid of what you will hear. Sit down. I am not going to stop saying this because you are standing."

Hurkuzak sat. Not because Hakiia told him to. Because his legs gave out.

Hakiia watched him settle into the snow and then he spoke again with the same voice he had used for every correction, every hard lesson he had ever delivered.

"I have thought about this," Hakiia said. "Not for minutes. For hours. Since the wolves, since the fire, since I felt the leg stop hurting in the way that means the nerves are dying. I have read my body the way I read any ground, and the ground is against us. I am dying. The question is not whether. The question is how long it takes and how much it costs. If I die slowly — in the cabin, over days, while you watch — the cost is both of us. If I die here, now, by your hand, you walk to the cabin alone and lighter and you survive the winter." He held Hurkuzak's gaze. "That is not cruelty. That is the clearest love I can offer."

"Don't call it love."

"What would you call it?"

"I don't know. I don't care. You are asking me to kill my brother and I am not going to do it."

Hakiia closed his eyes for a moment. When he opened them, the sharpness had shifted — not softer, but older, drawing on something deeper than argument.

"You know what is asked," he said. "You know the right. You know what our people do when the body will not carry the person any further." He did not explain. He did not need to. The words called up the same thing they had talked about at the fire — the draught, the family's duty, the proverb Hurkuzak had heard since childhood. Never live so long that you become a burden. "There is no draught here. There is no mother to give it and no warm room to receive it. There is you and the knife. The cruelty is not what I am asking. The cruelty is that there is nothing gentler to ask for."

The words settled over Hurkuzak like snowfall. He thought of the draught. The gentle version of this act. A dying person in a familiar room, surrounded by family, the cup brought with care and received with dignity. That was what the practice looked like when the world was being kind. This — a knife in the snow, a brother with no hands, the cold pressing in from every direction — this was what the practice looked like when it was not.

Hakiia's mouth moved in the shape that was not a smile. "You killed the dog."

The words landed in a place that had no defense. The comparison was precise and monstrous and it used the truth the way Hakiia had always used the truth — as a blade.

"The dog was different."

"The dog was in pain. The dog could not survive. You looked at him clearly and you acted. That was not weakness. That was the first useful thing you did after the cliff." Hakiia's voice softened, and the softening was worse than the blade because it carried inside it the thing Hurkuzak had felt at the fire — the almost-tenderness, the brief warmth that lived behind the correction. "Look at me the same way. Do the same."

"You are not a dog."

"No. I am your brother, who is asking you. The dog could not ask. I am asking. The dog could not hold a knife to do it itself. I cannot hold a knife either. The difference between me and the dog is that I can still speak, and I am using speech to ask you for what our people have always given their own. That should make it easier, not harder."

"It makes it impossible."

The silence after was long. The wind moved through the trees and the snow shifted on the branches above them and a small cascade of powder fell through the air and landed on Hakiia's chest and he did not brush it away. The cold did not care that they were arguing about something that would change everything. The cold had its own work.

"You want me to die slowly," Hakiia said. "You want to drag me to the cabin and watch me rot because watching is easier than acting. Because as long as I am breathing, you can pretend that what is happening is not what is happening, and the pretending is more important to you than my pain."

"That is not what I want."

"Then what do you want? Tell me. I am lying here with both legs broken and one of them dying and I am asking you to help me and you are refusing because it makes you feel something you do not want to feel. That is not mercy. That is not love. That is a man choosing his own comfort over his brother's suffering."

There was truth in it. Not all truth — it was not comfort he was protecting. It was something about who he would be on the other side of this act. But there was enough truth in Hakiia's words that they could not be dismissed. Hakiia knew it. He used that knowledge the way he used everything: precisely.

"Tell me the plan," Hakiia said. His voice had dropped. The anger had changed into something colder — anger at the situation, at the legs, at the cliff that had broken them and the wolves that had worsened them and the cold that was finishing the work. "Tell me the version of this where you haul me to a cabin with no food and no help and both of us survive. Tell me the plan that makes refusing me something other than selfishness."

Hurkuzak opened his mouth and closed it. The plan did not exist. The cabin was shelter, not rescue. There was no one coming. There was no help within reach. There was only winter and cold and two brothers, one dying, the other being asked to make the dying faster and cleaner. He could not. The word was in his body like a bone, weight-bearing and immovable. He could not kill his brother.

"I don't have a plan," he said. "I don't have an answer. I have that I don't want to do this, and I know that isn't enough, and I know you're right about the leg and the cold and what happens next, and I still cannot do what you are asking me to do."

Hakiia looked at him. The look lasted a long time. In it was everything the brothers had ever been to each other — the corrections and the care, the contempt and the protection.

"You can," Hakiia said. "You killed the wolf. You killed the dog. You have done hard things in the last three days that I did not believe you could do. Do not tell me you cannot do this. Tell me you will not. Those are different words and you know the difference."

"I will not."

"Yet."

The word hung in the cold air between them.

Hakiia closed his eyes. His breathing was wrong — shallow, irregular. He did not withdraw the demand. He simply stopped pressing it.

The snow fell. The trees creaked. The cold continued.

Hurkuzak sat beside his brother and felt the refusal inside him and felt the erosion underneath it. Hakiia was right. About the leg. About the cold. About what happened next if Hurkuzak dragged him to the cabin and watched. He was right. And the rightness was wearing through the refusal from below, finding the cracks that stubbornness could not seal.

He did not say yes. He did not say anything. He picked up the drag poles and he pulled.

Behind him, Hakiia breathed the wrong breathing and said nothing, and the silence between them was no longer the silence of a man who had stopped commanding.
