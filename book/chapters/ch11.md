# Chapter 11: The Cabin at the End

The march became a country he lived in. It had its own geography — the same white ground, the same gray sky, the same trees repeating themselves like a thought the landscape could not stop having. It had its own time, measured not in hours but in the distance between one collapse and the next, the interval narrowing as his body consumed whatever it had been drawing on and found less and less each time it reached.

He fell. The first time was a stumble that turned into a knee-drop that turned into a slow sideways descent into the snow, and the snow was soft against his face and the softness was dangerous because it felt like comfort and comfort was what killed you when the cold decided to finish the work. He got up. He pulled the travois straight. He pulled.

He fell again. This time he went all the way down, flat on his stomach, and the drag poles slid from his hands and the travois behind him stopped and the silence that followed was the silence of a system that had broken down and was waiting to see if anyone would restart it. He lay in the snow and breathed and the breathing was the hardest thing his body had done today, which was saying something, because his body had done many hard things today and would need to do more, and the breathing was still the hardest because breathing required the decision to continue and the decision to continue required believing that continuation had a purpose.

He got up. He pulled.

The terrain changed without improving. The valley floor had been rising for what felt like hours, a gradual climb that added resistance to every step and made the travois heavier with each degree of incline. The trees grew denser, which blocked the wind but also blocked what little light the sky offered, and Hurkuzak moved through a dim corridor of trunks and snow that seemed to narrow as he advanced, as though the forest were closing around him the way a throat closes around something it is trying to swallow.

His hands had stopped hurting. This was not recovery. This was the nerve endings giving up, surrendering their reports because the reports had been ignored so many times that sending them had become pointless. The grooves in his palms were deep enough now that the poles sat in them like tracks, and his fingers had curled around the wood into a position that might have been grip or might have been the beginning of rigor and the distinction between the two was smaller than it should have been.

He fell a third time. A fourth. Each fall was longer on the ground and shorter on his feet, and each time he went down he had to retrieve the travois and get the body back into position because the body had slid during the fall, and the body was stiff now, the cold and the hours having locked the joints and the muscles into something that was no longer flexible enough to be repositioned with care, and so Hurkuzak repositioned it with force, pulling limbs and turning the torso the way a man moves furniture, and the force was necessary and the necessity felt like the loss of something he had been holding onto without knowing it.

The fifth time he fell, he did not immediately get up. He lay beside the travois and looked at Hakiia's face — sideways, from the snow, at an angle that made the face look different, younger, less severe — and he thought, with the cold clarity that exhaustion sometimes produces in the moments before collapse: I could leave him here.

The thought was not a decision. It was a fact that arrived in his mind the way facts arrive — without permission, without warning, without the moral framework that would have accompanied it if he had been capable of moral thought. He would move faster alone. The travois was wreckage. The body was weight. The cabin might be close enough to reach if he walked without pulling and the reaching would be the difference between survival and a slow freeze that ended with two bodies in the snow instead of one.

He could leave Hakiia's body in the forest and walk to the cabin and survive.

The thought stayed for several breaths. It did not elaborate itself. It did not argue its case. It simply presented the mathematics and waited, and the mathematics were correct, and Hurkuzak lay in the snow beside his brother's body and felt the mathematics press against the part of him that was not mathematics and the two forces met and held and neither gave way.

He could not leave Hakiia here.

Not because leaving him was wrong, though it probably was. Not because Hakiia would have wanted to be carried, though he probably would not have — Hakiia's position on the matter had been that Hurkuzak should stop carrying him and let him die in the open, and the fact that Hurkuzak had not done that and had instead killed him and was now dragging the body toward a cabin that might not exist was not a series of decisions Hakiia would have endorsed.

He could not leave Hakiia here because leaving him here would make the body into something left. Something discarded. Something that had been loved and carried and killed and then, when the carrying became too much, set down beside a tree in the snow like a piece of equipment that was no longer worth the effort of transport. And Hurkuzak was not yet capable of making that true. Not yet. Perhaps later. Perhaps tomorrow, if tomorrow came, he would set the body down and walk away and the walking would be the rational thing and the rational thing would be the right thing and the right thing would be one more destruction added to the list of destructions that this trip had produced.

But not yet. He was not yet the person who left his brother in the snow. He was still the person who pulled.

He got up. He pulled.

He lost time. Not consciousness — he was aware of pulling, of the sound of the travois, of the cold — but the connective tissue between moments dissolved and the moments themselves became isolated, unlinked, beads on a string with no string. He was pulling. Then he was resting. Then he was pulling again and the resting was gone from his memory as though it had happened to someone else. The trees moved past him in the same repeating pattern and he began to suspect that the valley was a loop, that he had been through this stretch before, that the same rock and the same bent pine and the same drift of snow against the same fallen trunk had already been passed and were coming around again, and the suspicion was paranoia or it was truth and he did not have the capacity to determine which.

The light changed. He noticed it the way a man underwater notices the surface — as a brightening that comes from above and means something important that the body knows before the mind can name. The trees were thinning. The valley wall on his right was rising, steepening, turning from slope into cliff face. The terrain was changing in ways that suggested boundary, edge, arrival.

The smell of smoke came first.

He did not trust it. He had hallucinated the cabin once already and the hallucination had been vivid enough to cost him ten minutes of walking toward a rock face that had nothing behind it but more rock. But the smell persisted. Not fire — not current fire — but the old, embedded smell of smoke that lives in wood and stone after years of burning, the smell of a place where fires have been built many times, the smell of habitation.

Then the trees opened.

The cabin stood at the end of a small clearing where the valley wall rose into a granite face. It was small — smaller than he had expected, though he had not been carrying a specific expectation, only the vague shape of shelter. Stone walls, low and thick, built by someone who understood what winter did to anything that was not as heavy as the landscape it sat in. A timber roof pitched steep enough to shed snow, though the snow had accumulated anyway and the roof sagged under its weight. A door, closed, the wood dark with age and weather. One window, shuttered, the shutters hanging slightly askew. The whole structure looked like something the mountain had grown rather than something a person had built — low, dense, patient, and entirely indifferent to whether anyone came to it or not.

He did not feel relief. He had expected to feel relief and the absence of it was its own kind of sensation — a hollowness where the relief should have been, as though the part of him that processed salvation had been used up on more immediate tasks and had nothing left for the arrival at destination. What he felt instead was a recognition. The cabin was real. The cabin was here. The march was ending. And the ending did not mean safety. It meant a different kind of enclosure.

He pulled the travois across the clearing. The snow was shallower here, in the lee of the rock face, and the travois moved more easily, and the easier movement felt wrong because nothing about this should have been easy. He reached the door. He tried the latch. It moved, stiff with cold and disuse, and the door swung inward into a dark space that smelled of old smoke and cold stone and the particular emptiness of a place that had not been occupied in a long time.

The cabin was a single room. A stone hearth against the far wall, black with soot, the firebox empty. A raised sleeping platform along one side, bare wood, no bedding. A shelf built into the wall above the platform, holding nothing. A table, small and rough, with one leg shorter than the others. The floor was stone, uneven, with gaps between the slabs where dirt and debris had collected. The walls were thick enough to block the wind but not the cold, and the cold inside the cabin was a different cold from outside — still, enclosed, the cold of a room that had given up on warmth.

No food. No fuel beyond what he might scavenge from the forest. No supplies. No sign of recent human presence — no ashes in the hearth, no marks on the door frame, no smell except the old ghost of smoke that lived in the stone itself. A second door at the back, smaller, lower, latched from inside, leading to what might have been a storage room or a second exit. The walls were a foot thick at the base, tapering slightly, the stones fitted without mortar and held by their own weight and the weight of the years that had pressed them together.

The cabin was shelter. That was all it was. Four walls and a roof and a floor and the absence of wind. It was not rescue. It was not safety. It was a room where the problem changed shape — from exposure to enclosure, from the open hostility of landscape to the closed, patient hostility of a space with nothing in it. He had traded the killing cold for a different kind of cold, one that did not move but did not relent, and the trade was the only option the world had offered and he had taken it.

He stood in the doorway and looked at the room that would be everything now — kitchen and bedroom and prison and the place where whatever came next would happen. The room looked back at him with the indifference of a space that had sheltered people before and would shelter people again and did not care what happened between.

He dragged Hakiia's body inside because leaving it in the snow was still unthinkable. The travois would not fit through the door, so he lifted the body — heavy, stiff, resisting the handling with the passive resistance of dead weight — and carried it the way a man carries something too large for his arms, pressed against his chest, staggering under the load, moving through the doorway sideways and lowering the body onto the stone floor with a care that his muscles could barely execute and his mind demanded.

Hakiia lay on the floor of the cabin. Hurkuzak stood over him and looked down at the face he had closed the eyes of and the hands he had positioned on the chest and the body that had been his brother and was now the thing he had carried across a wilderness because he could not yet let it be anything else.

He should close the door. He should build a fire. He should assess the space. He should look for anything useful in the cabin or its surroundings — firewood, food cached by previous occupants, tools, cloth, anything that converted this stone box into something a person could survive in for more than a night. He should think about what came next — food, warmth, time, the weeks of winter that still remained, the question of how a man survives alone in a cabin in the mountains with nothing but grief and cold and the body of his brother on the floor.

He could not. His body had given everything it had to the march and the march was over and the giving was over and the body was collecting its debt. His legs folded. His knees hit the stone floor and the impact sent a flare through his damaged knee and he did not feel it because feeling required a kind of attention his mind no longer possessed. He slumped forward, catching himself on his hands, and his hands were the ruined hands that had gripped the drag poles and the knife and his brother's body, and they held him for a moment and then they did not hold him, and he went down.

He lay on the stone floor of the cabin beside his brother's body and the cold was on both of them equally and the dark was filling the room because the door was open and the light outside was failing and neither the cold nor the dark distinguished between the man who was dead and the man who was not yet dead but was no longer, in any meaningful sense, awake.

The last thing he heard was the wind in the trees outside and the silence inside and the space between those two sounds, which was the space in which everything that had happened and everything that would happen existed together, unresolved and absolute.

Then nothing.
