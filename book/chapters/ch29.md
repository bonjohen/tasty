# Chapter 29: What Hakiia Carried

The wreck was smaller than catastrophe should be. That was the first thing he noticed when he reached it — the scale was wrong, too modest for the weight of what had happened here. A few lengths of splintered wood. Metal fittings rusted the color of old blood. A scatter of debris compressed into the mud by months of snow and melt, the wreckage of a sled that had been, for a few days in deep winter, the only vehicle carrying his life and his brother's life and had failed them both in the space of a heartbeat.

He stood at the edge of it and the mud was at his ankles and the smell was earth and mineral rot and the cold groundwater that pooled in the ravine's lowest places. The spring had filled the ravine with a shallow, sluggish current that moved through the debris field and around the rocks and over the bones of the dogs that lay scattered in the mud downstream, disarticulated by scavengers, bleached where the sun had reached them, dark where the earth had held them. He did not count the bones. He did not look for the skull of the one he had killed himself — the quiet one, the left rear, the one whose back had broken in the fall and whose eyes had been clear and calm when Hurkuzak put the knife in. He looked at the wreck and began.

The work was slow. The mud resisted. It had spent months absorbing the sled's remains, drawing them down, filling the spaces between broken boards with silt and organic matter and the particular dense clay that lined the ravine floor. Pulling pieces free required force and patience and the willingness to kneel in cold water and dig with hands that had killed a brother and consumed a brother and were now excavating the last physical evidence of the life those brothers had shared.

He found the runner first — the broken one, not the intact one he had used for the travois. It was wedged between two rocks, the wood swollen and soft, the metal banding underneath still holding its shape. He pulled it free and laid it on the bank and looked at it and felt nothing that he could articulate and everything that articulation cannot reach, the way a dead man's shoe produces grief more efficiently than a dead man's grave because the shoe is specific and the specificity is what breaks you.

He found rope. The rope had rotted but the knots had held, and the knots were Hakiia's — the particular hitches and bends that Hakiia tied with the automatic precision of a man who considered every knot a small test of character and judged the tier accordingly. Hurkuzak held the rotted rope and felt the knots and the knots were his brother's hands preserved in fiber and the preservation was grotesque in its fidelity, the way the hands of the dead are sometimes more recognizable than their faces.

He found a tool roll. Leather, Hakiia's stitching, the contents rusted but identifiable — a small saw, an awl, a set of needles for heavy repair, a whetstone in a fitted pocket. The leather had protected them from the worst of the water. The rust was surface. The tools were still essentially whole. They were the tools of a man who planned to work in the wilderness for an extended period and who packed with the foresight that Hakiia brought to everything: meticulous, comprehensive, prepared for contingencies that other men would not imagine until they were already dying of them.

He spread the tools on the bank and looked at them and the looking was a form of conversation with a man who was dead and present and whose hands had touched these things with purpose and whose purpose was the question that Hurkuzak had been circling since Merrath, since the dream, since the voice first supplied information that had no innocent source.

He went back into the mud.

The sled's storage compartments had been built into the frame — Hakiia's design, because Hakiia designed everything, because the act of making a thing exactly right was Hakiia's way of imposing order on a world he considered fundamentally hostile and the imposing was never optional. The main compartment had broken open in the fall and its contents — their food, their gear, the ordinary supplies of a winter hunting trip — had scattered across the ravine and been destroyed or dispersed by weather and animals over the intervening months. But the sled had secondary compartments. Smaller. Built into the frame's cross-members, accessible only if you knew where to look, sealed against moisture and cold with wax and treated leather.

Hurkuzak had not packed those compartments. Hakiia had packed them himself, the morning they left, while Hurkuzak loaded the dogs and checked the harnesses and did the work that Hakiia assigned him while Hakiia did the work that Hakiia reserved for himself, and the reservation had seemed ordinary at the time because Hakiia always reserved the important work for himself, because Hakiia trusted his own hands and trusted nothing else, and the trust was so consistent that it was invisible.

He found the first compartment by feel. His hands moved along the cross-member — swollen, mud-caked, the wood soft enough that his fingers left impressions — and found the seam where the compartment lid met the frame. The wax seal had held. The treated leather gasket underneath had held. He pried the lid open with his knife and the wood split along the grain and inside was a space the size of two fists and inside the space was a bundle wrapped in oiled cloth.

He pulled it out. The cloth was dark and the oiling had preserved it and the preservation meant that when he unwrapped it his hands were suddenly holding something that belonged to the before — to the life on the other side of the cliff, to the trip as it had been planned rather than the trip as it had happened. The bundle contained a small wooden box, sealed with wax, and inside the box were three containers. Ceramic. Small — each the size of his thumb. Stopped with wax seals that bore no mark he recognized. The containers were heavy for their size. He held one up to the light and the light did not penetrate the ceramic and the contents did not shift when he tilted it, which meant whatever was inside was either solid or packed so tightly that it had no room to move.

The voice stirred. Not as a word. As a pressure — a sudden dense attention behind his eyes, the way a sound that has been barely audible suddenly becomes loud when the listener recognizes what it is. Something inside him was reacting to the containers with an intensity that had nothing to do with curiosity and everything to do with recognition. The presence knew these objects. Not as ideas. As things it had touched.

He set the containers aside and went back to the wreck.

The second compartment was harder to find. It was built into a different cross-member, lower in the frame, and the frame had partially collapsed onto it. He had to lever a section of broken wood away with the hatchet, working carefully because the compartment underneath was intact and he did not want to damage whatever it contained, and the not-wanting was already evidence that he had crossed from salvage into investigation, from accident into intent.

This compartment was longer, narrower, built to hold something flat. He opened it and inside was an oiled leather tube, the kind used for carrying documents in wet conditions. The tube was sealed at both ends with wax. He broke one seal and slid the contents out and the contents were papers.

Not ordinary papers. Not the casual notes of a hunting trip — the weather observations, the game counts, the rough maps that any experienced hunter might carry. These were written in Hakiia's hand but not Hakiia's ordinary hand. The writing was smaller, more careful, more deliberate. The hand of a man recording information he considered important enough to warrant precision. The hand of a man who was documenting rather than noting, preserving rather than scribbling, and the distinction between those activities was the distinction between a man who writes for himself and a man who writes for a purpose.

Hurkuzak held the papers and did not read them. He held them and looked at the handwriting and the handwriting was Hakiia's and the care in the handwriting was Hakiia's and the care was love, in its way — the love that Hakiia expressed through competence, through precision, through the absolute refusal to do anything carelessly. Whatever these papers contained, Hakiia had cared about them. The caring was visible in every letter.

He began to read.

The first page was a diagram. Lines and symbols arranged in a pattern he did not recognize — concentric circles bisected by straight lines, notations at the intersections in a shorthand that was not language but something between language and mathematics, the kind of notation that compresses complex information into minimal space because the information is sensitive or because the author assumed the reader would already know the system. Hurkuzak knew neither the system nor the sensitivity. He turned the page.

The second page was a list. Items, quantities, preparation methods. Dried materials — some named, some described by effect rather than identity. Powders derived from biological sources, described by their origin tissues with the clinical specificity of someone who understood anatomy. Preserved substances in containers sealed against degradation, their shelf lives noted, their active properties catalogued. The list was methodical. It was the list of a person assembling a kit for a specific procedure, checking each component against a requirement, noting substitutions where the primary material was unavailable.

The physician's jars in Merrath. The notation on the parcels. The grading language, the preparation methods, the specificity of which tissues produced which effects. The list in his hands used the same vocabulary. Not identical — Hakiia's notes predated the market by months — but adjacent. The same conceptual framework. The same understanding of demon flesh not as food but as materia, as an inventory of transferable properties organized by source tissue, preparation method, and intended effect.

The voice was no longer stirring. It was pressing. A weight behind his eyes and in his chest and at the base of his skull, a physical sensation like a hand pushing against the inside of his forehead. Not words. Something larger than words and more frightened. The presence inside him was reacting to these papers with the intensity of a man watching his own diary read aloud in a room full of strangers, and the reaction was not commentary. It was panic.

He turned another page. More diagrams. These were anatomical — demon anatomy, rendered with a precision that required either direct observation or access to detailed source materials. The diagrams showed tissues, organs, connections between systems. Arrows indicated transfer pathways. Notations described what moved and what remained, what was absorbed and what was lost, what strengthened and what eroded. The diagrams were studying the mechanism by which one body's properties could be absorbed by another body through consumption.

He turned another page and the page was about siblings.

The notation changed. The clinical distance compressed into something more urgent, more focused. The diagrams became specific to a pairing — two related bodies, shared lineage, overlapping physiological signatures. The notes described what happened when the consumption occurred between siblings: the transfer was deeper, more complete, more durable. Not merely physical properties but reflexive memory, instinctual patterns, fragments of identity. The word used was not transfer. The word was inheritance.

Hurkuzak's hands were shaking. He noticed them shaking because the papers were trembling and the trembling was not wind and there was no wind in the ravine. His body was responding to the information before his mind had finished processing it, the way his body had responded to the cliff before his mind recognized the cliff, the same mechanism of knowledge arriving through the flesh before the intellect could catch up and organize it into something bearable.

He found the correspondence at the bottom of the tube. Three fragments — not complete letters but portions, torn or cut from larger documents, preserved as a man preserves evidence or instructions. The handwriting was not Hakiia's. It was a different hand — precise, educated, the writing of someone accustomed to giving instructions and expecting them followed. The tone was transactional. The language was the language of arrangement, of schedule, of logistics. References to materials supplied. References to a window of opportunity tied to season and location. References to a subject that were clearly references to a person, though the word used was vessel, and the word vessel had a meaning in this context that Hurkuzak's mind was approaching the way a man approaches the edge of a cliff — slowly, unwillingly, with the full knowledge that the drop is there and the looking will change everything.

He put the papers down. He picked them up. He read the passage about the vessel again because the first reading had been a wound and the second reading was the confirmation that the wound was real and not imagined and the confirmation was worse than the wound because the wound could have been a mistake but the confirmation could not.

He gathered the materials — the ceramic containers, the document tube, the loose pages — and held them against his chest the way he had held Hakiia against the travois, with the same grim obligation, the same understanding that what he was carrying would change the shape of everything it touched. He climbed out of the ravine. The climb was harder than the descent, the mud giving way under his feet, his hands finding holds in the eroded earth and pulling himself up with a strength that belonged to the brother who had packed these materials into hidden compartments and sealed them against weather and carried them into the mountains with a purpose that the papers were beginning to describe and the description was a sound like the ground giving way, like the road collapsing, like the sled going over the edge and taking everything with it.

He reached the top. The late afternoon light was golden and indifferent — the light of a season that did not care what he had found, that would continue warming the mud and growing the grass and turning the winter's wreckage into spring's foundation regardless of what the wreckage contained or what the containing meant for the man who had dug it out.

He sat on the ridge above the ravine. He spread the materials before him on the ground — the containers in a row, the document tube beside them, the pages weighted with stones against the breeze. The light gave him enough clarity to read. He was not ready to read. He was going to read anyway, because the not-reading was no longer a shelter and the reading was the only direction left and the direction led somewhere terrible and the terrible place was where the truth lived and the truth had been living there since the morning Hakiia packed the sled and told Hurkuzak to load the dogs and kept the important work for himself.

The voice inside him was a weight without words. A pressure that occupied the space behind his thoughts and pressed against every surface it touched. Whether the pressure was grief or guilt or the desperate energy of something caught and unable to flee — he could not tell. He could not tell because the voice was Hakiia and Hakiia had never been readable even when he was alive and the death had not simplified him and the discovery would not simplify him either.

The light faded. Hurkuzak sat with the evidence of his brother's care spread before him and the evidence of his brother's intent folded inside it and the two kinds of evidence were made of the same materials and written in the same hand and the hand was the hand of the man who had loved him and the man who had brought him to this mountain to do something that the papers were about to explain and the explanation was a door and the door was opening and behind the door was not darkness but clarity, and the clarity was worse.
