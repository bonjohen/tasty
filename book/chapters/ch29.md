# Chapter 29: What Hakiia Carried

The wreck was smaller than catastrophe should be. A few lengths of splintered wood. Metal fittings rusted the color of old blood. Debris compressed into the mud by months of snow and melt.

He stood at the edge of it. The mud was at his ankles. The smell was earth and mineral rot and the cold groundwater that pooled in the ravine's lowest places. The spring had filled the ravine with a shallow, sluggish current that moved through the debris field and around the rocks and over the bones of the dogs scattered in the mud downstream, disarticulated by scavengers, bleached where the sun had reached them, dark where the earth had held them. He did not count the bones. He did not look for the skull of the one he had killed himself — the quiet one, the left rear, the one whose back had broken in the fall and whose eyes had been clear and calm when Hurkuzak put the knife in. He looked at the wreck and began.

The work was slow. The mud resisted. It had spent months absorbing the sled's remains, drawing them down, filling the spaces between broken boards with silt and dense clay. Pulling pieces free required kneeling in cold water and digging with both hands until the wreckage surrendered each piece separately, like a mouth releasing a held thing.

He found the runner first — the broken one, not the intact one he had used for the travois. It was wedged between two rocks, the wood swollen and soft, the metal banding underneath still holding its shape. He pulled it free and laid it on the bank and looked at it. His hands stayed at his sides.

He found rope. The rope had rotted but the knots had held, and the knots were Hakiia's — the particular hitches and bends that Hakiia tied with the automatic precision of a man who considered every knot a small test of character and judged the tier accordingly. Hurkuzak held the rotted rope and traced the knots with his thumb. His brother's hands preserved in fiber.

He found a tool roll. Leather, Hakiia's stitching, the contents rusted but identifiable — a small saw, an awl, a set of needles for heavy repair, a whetstone in a fitted pocket. The leather had protected them from the worst of the water. The rust was surface. The tools were still essentially whole.

He spread them on the bank beside the runner and the rope. He looked at them for a long time. Then he went back into the mud.

The sled's storage compartments had been built into the frame — Hakiia's design. The main compartment had broken open in the fall and its contents had scattered across the ravine, destroyed or dispersed by weather and animals over the intervening months. But the sled had secondary compartments. Smaller. Built into the frame's cross-members, accessible only if you knew where to look, sealed against moisture with wax and treated leather.

Hurkuzak had not packed those compartments. Hakiia had packed them himself, the morning they left, while Hurkuzak loaded the dogs. The division had seemed ordinary — Hakiia always kept the important work for himself, so consistently that the keeping was invisible.

He found the first compartment by feel. His hands moved along the cross-member — swollen, mud-caked, the wood soft enough that his fingers left impressions — and found the seam where the compartment lid met the frame. The wax seal had held. The treated leather gasket underneath had held. He pried the lid open with his knife and the wood split along the grain and inside was a space the size of two fists and inside the space was a bundle wrapped in oiled cloth.

He pulled it out. The oiling had preserved the cloth, and when he unwrapped it his hands were holding something that belonged to the life on the other side of the cliff. The bundle contained a small wooden box, sealed with wax, and inside the box were three containers. Ceramic. Small — each the size of his thumb. Stopped with wax seals that bore no mark he recognized. He held one up to the light. The light did not penetrate the ceramic. The contents did not shift when he tilted it.

The voice stirred. Not as a word. As a dense attention behind his eyes, the way a sound barely audible becomes loud when the listener recognizes what it is. The presence knew these objects. Not as ideas. As things it had touched.

He set the containers aside and went back to the wreck.

The second compartment was harder to find. It was built into a different cross-member, lower in the frame, and the frame had partially collapsed onto it. He had to lever a section of broken wood away with the hatchet, working carefully because the compartment underneath was intact — and the care was already evidence that he had crossed from salvage into investigation, from accident into intent.

This compartment was longer, narrower, built to hold something flat. He opened it and inside was an oiled leather tube, the kind used for carrying documents in wet conditions. The tube was sealed at both ends with wax. He broke one seal and slid the contents out.

Papers. Not the casual notes of a hunting trip — weather observations, game counts, rough maps. These were written in Hakiia's hand, but smaller, more careful, more deliberate. Documentation, not notation.

Hurkuzak held the papers and did not read them. The handwriting was Hakiia's, and the care in every letter was Hakiia's — the same refusal to do anything carelessly.

He began to read.

The first page was a diagram. Concentric circles bisected by straight lines, notations at the intersections in a shorthand that was not language but something between language and mathematics. Hurkuzak knew neither the system nor its purpose. He turned the page.

The second page was a list. Items, quantities, preparation methods. Dried materials — some named, some described by effect rather than identity. Powders derived from biological sources, described by origin tissue with the specificity of someone who understood anatomy. Preserved substances in sealed containers, their shelf lives noted, their active properties catalogued. A kit assembled for a specific procedure, each component checked against a requirement, substitutions noted where the primary material was unavailable.

The physician's jars in Merrath. The notation on the parcels. The grading language, the preparation methods, the specificity of which tissues produced which effects. The list in his hands used the same vocabulary. Not identical — Hakiia's notes predated the market by months — but adjacent.

The voice was no longer stirring. It was pressing. A weight behind his eyes, in his chest, at the base of his skull. A hand pushing against the inside of his forehead. Not words. The presence was a man watching his own diary read aloud in a room full of strangers. Not commentary. Panic.

He turned another page. More diagrams. These were anatomical — demon anatomy, rendered with a precision that required direct observation or detailed source materials. Tissues, organs, connections between systems. Arrows indicated transfer pathways. Notations described what was absorbed and what was lost. The diagrams were studying the mechanism by which one body's properties could enter another through consumption.

He turned another page and the page was about siblings.

The notation changed. The clinical distance compressed into something urgent. The diagrams became specific to a pairing — two related bodies, shared lineage, overlapping physiological signatures. The notes described what happened when the consumption occurred between siblings: not merely physical properties but reflexive memory, instinctual patterns, fragments of identity. The word used was not transfer. The word was inheritance.

Hurkuzak's hands were shaking. He noticed because the papers trembled and there was no wind in the ravine. His body knew before his mind did — the same way it had known the cliff.

He found the correspondence at the bottom of the tube. Three fragments — not complete letters but portions, torn or cut from larger documents, preserved as a man preserves evidence or instructions. The handwriting was not Hakiia's. It was a different hand — precise, educated, the writing of someone accustomed to giving instructions and expecting them followed. The tone was transactional — arrangements, schedules, logistics. Materials supplied. A window of opportunity tied to season and location. And references to a subject that were clearly references to a person, though the word used was vessel.

The word vessel had a meaning in this context that Hurkuzak's mind was approaching the way a man approaches a cliff edge, knowing the drop is there.

He put the papers down. He picked them up.

He gathered the materials — the ceramic containers, the document tube, the loose pages — and held them against his chest the way he had held Hakiia against the travois, with the same grim obligation. He climbed out of the ravine. The mud gave way under his feet. His hands found holds in the eroded earth and he pulled himself upward with strength that belonged to the brother who had packed these materials into hidden compartments and carried them into the mountains, and the pulling felt like the sled going over the edge — the same sick lurch of ground becoming air.

He reached the top. The late afternoon light was golden and indifferent, warming the mud where he had dug.

He sat on the ridge above the ravine. He spread the materials before him on the ground — the containers in a row, the document tube beside them, the pages weighted with stones against the breeze. The light gave him enough clarity to read. He was not ready to read. He was going to read anyway, because the not-reading was no longer a shelter and the reading was the only direction left.

The voice inside him had gone still — not absent but held, the way breath is held before something breaks. Whether it was grief or the desperate energy of something caught and unable to flee, he could not tell. Hakiia had never been readable, alive or dead or discovered.

The light faded. Hurkuzak sat with the evidence of his brother's care spread before him and the evidence of his brother's intent folded inside it. The same materials. The same hand. The hand of the man who had loved him and the man who had brought him to this mountain. The explanation was a door, and the door was opening, and behind the door was not darkness but clarity.

The clarity was worse.
