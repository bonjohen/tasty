# Chapter 12: What the Cabin Holds

He woke to cold and dark and the smell of stone.

The cold was the first thing. Not the grief, not the memory, not the fact of what lay beside him on the floor — the cold. His body had its priorities and they were older than sorrow and they announced themselves in the language the body understood best: pain, stiffness, the sick desperate shivering that meant the temperature had dropped past the point where lying still was compatible with continuing to live.

He did not know how long he had been unconscious. The light through the gap in the shuttered window was gray, but it had been gray when he collapsed and it was gray now and the gray told him nothing about hours or days. His body told him something: he was dehydrated, weak, and stiff in every joint, and the wolf bite in his forearm throbbed with a deep, infectious heat that suggested it had been working on its own problems while the rest of him was absent.

He got up. The getting up took a long time and involved the same stages as before — hands, knees, a push, a second push, vertical — and when he was standing the cabin tilted and he put his hand against the wall and the wall was stone and the stone was cold and the cold traveled up his arm and settled into the thing that his bones had become.

Hakiia was on the floor where Hurkuzak had placed him. The body had not moved, because bodies do not move, and the not-moving was the fact of the room, the fact that occupied the space between the hearth and the door and between Hurkuzak's eyes and any direction he tried to look that was not directly at the still form under the bloodstained furs. He did not look at the body. He looked at the hearth.

Fire first. The hearth was stone, wide and deep, blackened by years of use. He found dry wood stacked against the wall outside the back door — not much, a pile that previous visitors had left and that the overhang of the roof had kept relatively dry. He carried an armload inside, shaved tinder with the knife, and worked the spark until it caught. The fire was small and reluctant and it was the most important thing that had happened since he woke because it meant warmth and warmth meant not dying today and not dying today was the only plan he was capable of making.

The cabin revealed itself in the firelight. One room, as he had seen before he collapsed. Stone walls, timber roof, stone floor. The hearth dominated the back wall. The sleeping platform along the right side was bare wood, but he found two blankets folded beneath it — thin, rough-woven, musty from storage, but whole. On the shelf above the platform: a clay pot with a cracked rim, a small iron pan, a wooden bowl. Against the left wall, a chest that he opened to find rope, a hand axe, a coil of wire, three candles, and a sack of dried grain that had gone partly to mold but was not entirely lost. He picked through it and separated what was edible from what was not. A double handful of clean grain. Maybe three days if he was careful.

Under the chest, shoved against the wall: a jar of rendered fat, sealed with wax. He opened it. The fat was old and slightly rancid but it was calories and calories were the currency his body was demanding. He set it on the table beside the grain.

He checked the back room through the smaller door. Not a room — a narrow alcove, partially open to the air where the roof joined the rock face behind the cabin. Cold enough to freeze water. Cold enough to preserve.

He carried Hakiia's body into the alcove.

The carrying was difficult. The body was stiff, the joints locked by cold and time, and Hurkuzak had to lift and angle and negotiate the narrow doorway while his bruised ribs screamed and his bitten forearm pulsed and his cut palm, which had healed badly and reopened during the march, left prints on Hakiia's clothing that were his own blood on his brother's chest. He set the body down on the stone floor of the alcove and straightened the limbs as well as the stiffness allowed and pulled the furs over the face and stood back.

The cold would preserve it. He told himself this was about dignity. About not leaving his brother on the floor of the room where he slept and cooked and existed. About the practical fact that a body in a living space was not a living arrangement. He told himself these things and they were true and the truth he did not tell himself was underneath them like water under ice, present and invisible and patient.

He closed the alcove door.

The cabin felt different with the body in the other room. Not better. Not worse. Different, the way a room feels different when a person leaves it — the shape of the space changes, the air redistributes, and the absence becomes a kind of presence that occupies the place the person used to fill. Hakiia was behind a door. Hakiia was still in the cabin. Both of these things were true and Hurkuzak could feel both of them at all times, the way you feel a sound even after the sound has stopped, in the ringing that follows.

He ate a portion of grain boiled in snowmelt. The taste was flat and faintly bitter and his stomach accepted it with the desperate gratitude of an organ that had been running on nothing for too long. He ate a small amount of the fat, smearing it on the edge of his hand and licking it. The fat was the kind of sustenance that the body recognized as real — dense, caloric, immediately convertible to heat and function — and he could feel it entering his system like fuel entering a stove, not warmth yet but the possibility of warmth.

He slept on the platform. The blankets were thin but they were better than furs soaked in blood, and the fire, small as it was, had raised the temperature in the cabin from killing cold to merely miserable. He slept and woke and slept and woke and each waking was the same dark room and the same cold and the same silence and the same knowledge of what was behind the alcove door.

Days passed. He did not count them at first, then he scratched marks on the wall above the platform with the knife because counting felt like the act of a person who expected to survive long enough for the count to matter, and he was not sure he was that person but the counting was something to do and doing was what kept the silence from becoming a pressure that his mind could not withstand.

His routine emerged the way routines emerge in confinement — not chosen, but accrued. Wake. Check the fire. Feed it if it needed feeding. Melt snow in the clay pot. Drink. Cook a small portion of grain. Eat it slowly, making the eating last, because the eating was an event and events were what structured time and time without structure was a kind of torture that had nothing to do with hunger or cold but with the mind's need to move from one thing to the next.

He checked his injuries. The wolf bite was infected — hot, swollen, leaking a thin fluid that smelled wrong. He cleaned it with snowmelt and wrapped it and the cleaning helped and the wrapping helped and neither of them solved the problem because the problem was inside the flesh and he had no tools for inside. He watched the infection the way he watched the grain supply — as a number that moved in one direction and could not be reversed by attention alone. His ribs were bruised but mending. His knee worked, badly, with a grinding sensation that suggested something inside the joint had been rearranged by the cliff descent and had settled into a new configuration that was functional but wrong. His palm had scarred into a thick ridge that pulled when he flexed his hand. The scars were the only things that were healing. Everything else was either holding steady or getting worse, and the difference between holding steady and getting worse was measured in food, and the food was measured in days, and the days were running out.

He talked. At first under his breath, muttering while he worked — practical words, the language of tasks. Put that there. Needs more wood. Water's low. Then louder, to the room itself, as though the cabin were an audience that deserved narration. Then to Hakiia.

Not to the body. He did not open the alcove door and speak to what was inside. He spoke to the air, to the memory, to the version of Hakiia that still occupied the cabin in the form of absence and silence and the faint, persistent sense of being observed.

"You would hate this," he said one evening, sitting by the fire with the last of the grain cooking in the pot. "You would hate the waiting. You never waited for anything. You decided and you acted and the time between the decision and the act was so small it might as well not have existed." He stirred the grain. "I am waiting. I don't know what for. The snow to stop. The spring to come. Something to change. Nothing is changing. The fire burns and the snow falls and the grain gets less and I am still here and you are still in the other room and nothing is changing."

The silence was his answer. It was always his answer.

He argued with it sometimes. Replayed conversations from the trail and revised his own responses and gave himself the words he had not had when Hakiia was alive. The pressure of his brother's certainty had always compressed everything Hurkuzak wanted to say into one inadequate syllable — no, or fine, or nothing at all. Here in the cabin, alone with the absence, the words came more easily. He was more articulate without an audience. He could build whole arguments, carry them to their conclusions, hear himself say the things he had never said to the face that had always been harder than his own.

"You never let me finish a sentence," he told the fire one night. "You heard the first three words and decided the rest were wrong and you corrected me before I could find out whether you were right. And I let you. Every time. Because arguing with you was like arguing with weather. It was happening whether I participated or not."

He paused. The fire cracked. The wind pressed against the shutters and the shutters held and the sound was the sound of something trying to get in and being told no.

"I miss you," he said. The words surprised him. They were true and they were insufficient and they did not make anything better, and he said them to a room that could not hear and a body that could not answer and the saying of them changed nothing except that now they existed in the air instead of only in his chest, and the air was a slightly better place for them than his chest had been.

Snow fell. It fell for two days without stopping and when it stopped the world outside the cabin had risen by another foot and the door opened against a wall of white that came to his chest. He dug a path. The path filled in overnight. He dug again. The path filled again. He stopped digging. The trap had closed. Not with violence, not with drama. The snow had simply accumulated until the world outside the cabin became a theoretical place that he could see through the window but could not reach, and the cabin became the entire world, and the entire world was one room and an alcove and a fire and a diminishing supply of food.

The grain ran out on the seventh day. Or the eighth. He had been careful — smaller portions each day, stretching what was there until what was there was nothing — and the last meal was so small it was more ritual than sustenance, a few spoonfuls of thin paste that his body absorbed without acknowledgment because the amount was too small to register as food.

The fat lasted two more days. He rationed it in slivers, cutting thin strips with the knife and holding them on his tongue until they dissolved. The calories were real but they were not enough. His body was eating itself now — muscle first, then the fat it had managed to store from the grain, then the deeper reserves that a body hoards for the exact kind of emergency that the word starvation was invented to describe.

He lay on the platform in the dark and listened to the fire and felt the hunger. Not the hunger of a missed meal, which is an inconvenience. Not even the hunger of the trail, which had been sharp and immediate and attached to the possibility of resolution. This was the hunger that had moved in. It occupied his stomach and his chest and his head and it was not leaving because there was nothing coming to replace it. It was permanent. It was the new resident of his body, and it would remain until either something fed it or the body that housed it stopped being a body.

He lay in the dark and the dark was complete because the fire had burned low and the candles were too precious to waste and the only light in the room was the faint glow of embers. The cabin was quiet. The wind had stopped. The snow had stopped. Everything had stopped except the hunger and the cold and the sound of his own breathing and the knowledge — held at the distance of an arm's length, not yet admitted into the center of his thought, not yet looked at directly — that behind the alcove door was the only food left in the world.

He did not think the thought. He lay in the dark and felt the hunger and felt the cold and felt the presence of what was in the other room and the thought existed at the edge of his mind the way a wolf exists at the edge of a clearing: visible, patient, and waiting for the moment when resistance became thinner than what pressed against it.

He did not sleep. The hunger watched. The cold watched. Behind the door, in the dark of the alcove, something that was not watching watched too, because the dead do not watch but the living cannot stop feeling watched by them, and the feeling was enough.

He did not sleep.
