# Chapter 48: What Was Passed On

Weeks later.

Hurkuzak sat in a market town he had not intended to reach, eating food that tasted like food again — not the flat nothing of the early months after the cabin, but actual flavor, texture, the ordinary pleasure of a body receiving what it needed. The town was small, east of the hill country, surrounded by farms and orchards just beginning to fruit. He had arrived because the road led here and he had no reason to go elsewhere.

He was not hiding. He was simply present — buying bread, sitting on a stone bench near the well, watching the commerce of a place that had nothing to do with him.

His body was healed. The burns on his forearms had closed and faded to smooth, tight skin — the accelerated healing that would never stop being strange and would never stop being useful. The cut across his palm was a white line. The bolt wound in his shoulder was a dimple of scar tissue that ached in cold weather and did not ache in warm weather.

He noticed, as he had been noticing for days, the small scar on the back of his left hand.

He did not remember receiving it. It was not from the fight with Vark or the confrontation with Tessivane or the fire or the guards. It was new and it was wrong — wrong in the way that a wound is wrong when it has been made by teeth rather than by a blade. Small, crescented, the shape of a bite that had broken the surface and taken something away.

It had happened during the destruction. He was certain of that because the scar's healing stage matched the timeline of the fire and the chaos and the carrying of the unresponsive captive through the burning corridors. Someone had bitten him. In the smoke, in the confusion, someone had put their mouth on his hand and taken a piece of him. The bite had been so small and the chaos so large that he had not registered it at the time. He had only begun to notice the scar days later, when the larger wounds had healed and the smaller one remained.

A piece of him, taken. Flesh, consumed. The rite activated the way a drowning person takes air — without thought, without intent, because the conditions permitted it.

He looked at the scar. He could not identify who had bitten him. He could not undo what the bite had transferred. He could not control what the rite did in the world any more than he could control the children chasing each other through the square.

He let it pass. The ancient text, pressed against his ribs in its wrapping of torn cloth, had described the rite's propagation with the same directness it described everything else: the transfer was not contained by intent. It moved through flesh the way fire moved through dry wood — wherever the conditions permitted.

He did not know if the bite had been sufficient. He did not know what a new carrier would hear — one voice or two, the original brothers or the composite they had become, something recognizable or something changed by the passage through another body. He knew only that the rite was not his to control.

He looked away from his hand. Ate his bread, watched the square, felt the sun on his face.

The woman's name was Ila. She did not know this would matter.

She lived in a village three weeks' walk from the market town where the man with the scars had eaten bread. Her village was coastal — salt air, fishing boats, the rhythm of a community built around the tides. She mended nets. Had mended them since she was old enough to hold a needle. It was the only skill she possessed that the village valued, and it was enough.

She was ordinary. She was thirty-one years old. She had a house that was small and adequate and that smelled of salt and dried seaweed and something mineral beneath. She had a sister who lived in a larger village to the north. She had hands that were strong and callused at the fingertips from years of cord and needle, and her fingers moved through the netting without consulting her. She had never left the coast for more than a few days. She had never killed anything larger than a fish.

She was mending nets on the shore when it happened.

The afternoon was warm and the water was calm and the boats were out. The village behind her was quiet — gulls and rope-creak and nothing else.

She was reaching for a new length of cord when she stopped.

She held still. The cord hung between her fingers. The net lay across her lap. The shore stretched in both directions, empty and indifferent.

Something had shifted inside her. The way a room feels different when someone has entered it while your back was turned. The way the air changes when it is no longer your air alone.

She listened.

She was a woman who mended nets in a coastal village and who had, three weeks ago, found herself in a burning building. She had been traveling. She had been near the stronghold when the fire started, and she had gone inside because she heard voices — someone might need help, and the instinct to help was the only instinct she had that operated faster than the instinct to flee. In the burning and the smoke and the terror she had stumbled against a man who was carrying someone. Her mouth had struck his hand. Her teeth had closed reflexively. Skin had broken. A fragment of the man's flesh had landed on her tongue, and the chaos had been so total that she had not thought about it again until now.

Until this.

She listened. The listening was not a choice.

What she heard was not one voice.

It was two.

The first was rough. Sharp. It assessed the shoreline with a precision she had never possessed — the angle of the rocks, the distance to cover, the wind direction and what it meant for anyone approaching from the water. It noticed the way the water moved against the boat hulls and calculated weight and displacement and structural integrity. It was a voice that understood danger the way she understood netting — automatically, comprehensively, as a permanent feature of how the world was perceived.

The second was quieter. Steadier. It noticed the light on the water with an attention that had nothing to do with tactics — the color the afternoon made when it hit the surface at this angle, the boats resting heavy in their moorings, the cord between her fingers. It was a voice that attended to the world the way she attended to a difficult knot — carefully, patiently.

The two voices were not arguing. They were present. They were both looking out through her eyes at a world they had not seen before, through a body they had not occupied before, and the looking was entangled the way two colors of thread are entangled in a weave — distinct in origin, inseparable in practice.

Ila sat on the shore. The net lay across her lap. The cord hung between her fingers. The water moved against the hulls. The village waited behind her. The world continued, ordinary and indifferent and entirely changed.

She pressed a hand to her chest. Her breath caught. Her eyes widened. Her body held still — a creature that has realized it is no longer alone in its own territory.

Not terror. Not yet. Something before terror — a mind discovering it contains rooms it did not build and voices it did not invite and a presence that is not going to leave.

The cord fell from her fingers. The net slipped from her lap. The shore continued. The tide came in. Everything continued except the thing inside her, which had just begun.

The inheritance had moved on. What it carried now was not two brothers, not one — something else, layered and entangled, passing through the world in a body that had never asked for it and could not give it back.
