# Chapter 48: What Was Passed On

Weeks later.

Hurkuzak sat in a market town he had not intended to reach, eating food that tasted like food again — not the flat nothing of the early months after the cabin, but actual flavor, texture, the ordinary pleasure of a body receiving what it needed. The town was small, east of the hill country, surrounded by farms and orchards just beginning to fruit. He had arrived because the road led here and the road was where his feet had been taking him and the feet knew what they were doing even when the mind did not offer a destination.

He was not hiding. He was not performing normalcy. He was simply present, the way any traveler is present in a market town — buying bread, sitting on a stone bench near the well, watching the commerce of a place that existed for the exchange of goods and conversation and the small transactions that kept a community functioning.

His body was healed. The burns on his forearms had closed and faded to smooth, tight skin — the accelerated healing that would never stop being strange and would never stop being useful. The cut across his palm was a white line. The bolt wound in his shoulder was a dimple of scar tissue that ached in cold weather and did not ache in warm weather and the aching was so ordinary, so human in its pattern, that it almost made him forget what the shoulder had been through.

He noticed, as he had been noticing for days, the small scar on the back of his left hand.

He did not remember receiving it. It was not from the fight with Vark or the confrontation with Tessivane or the fire or the guards. It was new and it was wrong — wrong in the particular way that a wound is wrong when it has been made by teeth rather than by a blade. Small, crescented, the shape of a human mouth pressed hard against skin and the pressing accompanied by the kind of bite that breaks the surface and takes something away.

It had happened during the destruction. He was certain of that because the scar's healing stage matched the timeline of the fire and the chaos and the carrying of the unresponsive captive through the burning corridors. Someone had bitten him. In the smoke, in the confusion, in the particular madness of a building collapsing around desperate people — someone had put their mouth on his hand and taken a piece of him and the taking had been so small and the chaos had been so large that he had not registered it at the time and had only begun to notice the scar days later when the larger wounds had healed and the smaller one remained as a question he could not answer.

A piece of him, taken. Flesh, consumed. The rite's mechanism, activated by accident or desperation or the blind survival instinct of a person in a burning building who saw flesh and took it the way a drowning person takes air.

He looked at the scar. He felt nothing about it that was useful. He could not identify who had bitten him. He could not undo what the bite had transferred. He could not control what the rite did in the world any more than he could control the wind or the season or the way the market town's children chased each other through the square with the oblivious velocity of creatures who had not yet learned that the world contained things worth being afraid of.

He let it pass. He could not control what the rite did in the world. He could only control what he did with what he carried. The ancient text, pressed against his ribs in its wrapping of torn cloth, had described the rite's propagation with the same directness it described everything else: the transfer was not contained by intent. It moved through flesh the way fire moved through dry wood — wherever the conditions permitted, the conditions being consumption and the consumption being sufficient to introduce the biochemistry of inheritance into a new body.

He did not know if the bite had been sufficient. He did not know what a new carrier would hear — one voice or two, the original brothers or the composite they had become, something recognizable or something changed by the passage through another transfer. He knew only that his body held the mechanism and the mechanism was not his to control, and the not-controlling was one more thing to carry, and the carrying was what he did now, and the doing was his life.

He looked away from his hand and ate his bread and watched the square and felt the sun on his face.

The woman's name was Ila. She did not know this would matter.

She lived in a village three weeks' walk from the market town where the man with the scars had eaten bread, and the distance was the kind of distance that made the two places exist in entirely separate worlds. Her village was coastal — salt air, fishing boats, the particular rhythm of a community built around the tides. She mended nets. She had mended nets since she was old enough to hold a needle, and the mending was the only skill she possessed that the village valued, and the valuing was sufficient, and the sufficient was the shape of her life.

She was ordinary. She was thirty-one years old. She had a house that was small and adequate and that smelled of salt and dried seaweed and the particular mineral smell that coastal buildings acquire over years of exposure to the air that comes off the water. She had a sister who lived in a larger village to the north. She had hands that were strong and quick and callused at the fingertips from years of cord and needle and that moved through the netting with the automatic precision of a body that had performed the same motion thousands of times. She had never left the coast for more than a few days. She had never killed anything larger than a fish. She had never heard of carriers or the taboo or the ancient rite that the world had buried and that kept surfacing through the cracks in the burial.

She was mending nets on the shore when it happened.

The afternoon was warm and the water was calm and the boats were out and the village behind her was quiet in the particular way that coastal villages are quiet when the fleet is working — a held-breath quality, a waiting, the sound of a community that exists between departures and returns.

She was reaching for a new length of cord when she stopped.

The stopping was not a decision. It was an interruption — the way a hand stops moving when the mind that controls it suddenly has something else to process. She held still. The cord hung between her fingers. The net lay across her lap. The shore stretched in both directions, empty and indifferent.

Something had shifted inside her. Not pain. Not illness. Not the dizziness of sun or hunger or the disorientation of standing too quickly. Something else. The way a room feels different when someone has entered it while your back was turned. The way the air changes when it is no longer your air alone.

She listened.

She did not know what she was listening for. She did not have a framework for what she was hearing. She had no knowledge of rites or taboos or carriers or the mechanism by which one person's flesh, consumed, could transfer fragments of identity into another body. She was a woman who mended nets in a coastal village and who had, three weeks ago, found herself in a burning building — she had been traveling, she had been near the stronghold when the fire started, she had gone inside because she heard voices, because someone might need help, because the instinct to help was the only instinct she had that operated faster than the instinct to flee — and in the burning and the smoke and the terror she had stumbled against a man who was carrying someone, and the stumbling had brought her mouth against his hand, and the bringing had been involuntary, and her teeth had closed reflexively, and the closing had broken skin, and the breaking had deposited a fragment of the man's flesh on her tongue, and the depositing had been so small and so chaotic and so surrounded by the larger fact of the building being on fire that she had not thought about it again until now.

Until this.

She listened. The listening was involuntary. The listening was the first act of a process she did not understand and could not stop and had not consented to.

What she heard was not one voice.

It was two.

The first was rough. Sharp. It assessed the shoreline with a tactical precision she had never possessed — the angle of the rocks, the distance to cover, the wind direction and what it meant for approaching threats. It noticed the way the water moved against the boat hulls and calculated weight and displacement and structural integrity. It was a voice that understood danger the way she understood netting — automatically, comprehensively, as a permanent feature of how the world was perceived.

The second was quieter. Steadier. It noticed the light on the water with an attention that had nothing to do with tactics and everything to do with the quality of seeing — the specific color the afternoon made when it hit the surface at this angle, the way the boats rested in their moorings with the patient weight of things that existed between uses, the small perfection of the cord between her fingers. It was a voice that attended to the world the way she attended to a difficult knot — carefully, patiently, with the specific gravity of a person who believed that attention itself was a form of love.

The two voices were not arguing. They were present. They were aware. They were both looking out through her eyes at a world they had not seen before, through a body they had not occupied before, and the looking was layered and strange and impossible to separate into its components because the components were entangled the way two colors of thread are entangled in a weave — distinct in origin, inseparable in practice.

Ila sat on the shore. The net lay across her lap. The cord hung between her fingers. The water moved against the hulls. The village waited behind her. The world continued in every direction, ordinary and indifferent and entirely changed.

She pressed a hand to her chest. The pressing was involuntary — the gesture of a person who feels something new in the place where the self lives and who does not yet know what the newness is or what it will become or what it will cost. Her breath caught. Her eyes widened. Her body held still with the particular stillness of a creature that has just realized it is no longer alone in its own territory.

Not terror. Not yet. Something before terror — the raw, unprocessed recognition of a boundary that has been crossed without permission, a self that is no longer singular, a mind that has just discovered it contains rooms it did not build and voices it did not invite and a presence that is not going to leave.

The cord fell from her fingers. The net slipped from her lap. The shore continued. The tide continued. The village behind her continued in its held-breath waiting for the boats to return. Everything continued except the thing inside her, which had just begun.

The inheritance had moved on. The chain continued. And what it carried now was more complicated than it had ever been.
