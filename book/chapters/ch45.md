# Chapter 45: The Oldest Inheritance

He read through the afternoon.

The hill held him and the sun moved and the smoke behind him thinned from a column to a haze to a stain on the western sky and finally to nothing. The stronghold had finished burning and become a ruin.

The text continued to open.

Not easily — the pages were damaged, the language resistant, the sequence uncertain despite the prince's marginal numbering. But Hurkuzak had nowhere to be and nothing to flee. His only obligation was to understand the thing he carried, and the understanding came in fragments, with gaps that required inference and passages that would never be legible because the damage was too complete.

In the earliest period — the crisis that had produced the rite — the practice had been rare, desperate, and surrounded by what the text described as observance. Not ritual in the sense of ceremony, but attention. The community watched. The carrier was held close, accompanied by kin, and when the composite grew unstable, physically restrained by those who loved them.

That watchfulness had been the first thing to erode.

As the crisis passed and the communities recovered, the practice continued. It had worked. It had saved lineages that would have vanished. But the text described the carriers with a mixture of reverence and unease that Hurkuzak had no need to read twice — the distance between gratitude and dread was one he knew by the way people stepped back when he turned too fast, or stopped talking when his voice changed without warning. A carrier might speak in a voice that had belonged to someone dead for twenty years, using phrases and cadences that the living remembered and the carrier had never been taught. A carrier might demonstrate a skill — setting a bone, reading weather, navigating by stars — that belonged to the consumed rather than the consumer. The carriers were not shunned. They were held at the distance between gratitude and dread.

Some carriers held their composites for decades. The text described one who had consumed three siblings over the course of a prolonged famine — each consumption a last resort, each sibling willing, each transfer deepening the composite until the carrier held four selves in a single body. She had been the community's most capable member — healer, hunter, navigator, builder. She had also been, by the text's account, a woman no one could look at without seeing the three faces she had swallowed.

And the proof of the rite's power attracted ambition.

The text described the corruption not as a single act but as a drift. A family patriarch who saw the rite's potential and wondered if it could be applied outside of crisis. A leader who wanted a carrier's capabilities without the catastrophe that produced them. A community that began to decide which of its members were the kind that could be taken and which were the kind worth keeping. The moment they divided — those who would eat and those who would be eaten, those who carried the dead and those who became the dead — the rite was broken.

The controlled consumptions began. The text did not describe them with the clinical detachment of the prince's trial records. It described them with horror. Siblings paired by authority rather than grief. Consumptions performed under supervision rather than in desperate isolation. The consumed chosen not for what they carried but for what the consumer was meant to acquire. The results were consistent and consistently terrible: transfers that produced physical properties without identity. Composites that fought internally from the first hour. Carriers who lost coherence within months, degrading into confusion, violence, or silence.

The ancient corruptors had made the prince's mistake two thousand years before the prince was born. They had treated the rite as a technology, tried to separate the flesh from the grief. Without the grief, the flesh was only meat. They had not believed that. They had believed the consumption was the thing and treated the love as accident, and the failures accumulated until failure was the rite's only legacy.

The prohibition followed the failures the way a scar follows a wound.

The text described the decision-makers as people who understood what they were forbidding and who forbade it anyway because the alternative was to watch the rite consume more of the living than it preserved of the dead. The prohibition was born in knowledge — the knowledge that the rite worked and was necessary under specific conditions. But those conditions could not be protected, because every person who wanted the rite badly enough would find a catastrophe sufficient to justify it. The only safe answer was no. Not "no, unless." Not "no, except in cases of." No. Always. The text articulated this with the weary certainty of people who had argued the exceptions and lost.

He set the page down and looked at nothing for a while.

What the text described of the early corruptions — the patriarch who saw the potential, the careful selection of who would be taken, the materials prepared in advance, the method designed to optimize the result — none of it was the language of grief. It was the language of acquisition. And the ancient carriers produced this way had shattered because the rite was not a technology. It had never been a technology. The grief was not incidental to the mechanism. The grief was the mechanism.

Hakiia had packed the compartments before they left. He had written the word *vessel* in his careful hand. He had brought materials and instruction supplied by the prince's network, by someone who had made the same mistake forty-two times and had not learned from any of them. The plan was meticulous. The plan was exactly what the ancient text described: the rite attempted as acquisition, without the condition that made it work.

But the cliff had broken the plan. What followed — the cabin, the grief, the collapse of every barrier between love and horror — had been the original conditions, arriving by catastrophe rather than design. The world had imposed on the brothers the same circumstances that had produced the rite's first carriers: impossible loss, no alternatives, and love that could not be separated from its damage.

The reframing did not make Hakiia innocent. Nothing could make Hakiia innocent. He had planned the trip. He had packed the compartments. He had written the word *vessel* in his careful hand. The ancient context deepened the betrayal. Hakiia had used a practice the world had already condemned and learned nothing from the condemnation. He was not uniquely evil. He was the latest in a line of people who believed they could control what could only be endured. That he had been recruited, supplied with materials, given a method designed by someone who had made the same mistake forty-two times — that did not excuse. It explained.

Hurkuzak was not the product of Hakiia's plan. He was not the product of the prince's protocol. He was the product of a cliff and a winter and a knife and the weeks that followed. The mechanism was grief, and the grief was his.

He put the text down.

The afternoon was ending. The light had changed — lower, warmer, the shadows of the hill's few trees stretching east across the grass. The air held the balance between the cold that had been and the warmth that was arriving. He had sat for hours. His body was stiff. The burns on his arms had begun to tighten as the healing progressed, the skin drawing together faster than any human wound should close.

He stood. The standing required effort that had nothing to do with fatigue.

The ancient carrier of three siblings had been a woman no one could face without grief. Hurkuzak was a carrier alone, walking through a world that did not know what carriers were, that had buried the knowledge so thoroughly that even the word had been forgotten. He carried no title. He belonged to no tradition.

Yet the integration was stable. The text's descriptions of failure — the interior wars, the identity erosion, the layered composites that shattered — none of those described what he felt. The war had happened and had ended. The erosion had been resisted by the thing Hakiia himself had named: the stubborn gentleness that refused to let Hakiia become an invader. The successful carriers in the ancient accounts were the ones who loved the consumed deeply enough to hold them as persons rather than properties.

He wrapped the text in the fabric of his shirt — torn now, burned in places, adequate for protection against the damp — and held it against his body and looked at the landscape.

East. The road. The world. Nothing except continuation — which was what the rite had always been for.

He walked toward it.

The road was empty in the late afternoon light. The air smelled of growth and the faintest trace of char from the west, already thinning. The ground under his feet was solid and dry and marked by the passage of carts and feet and animals — the ordinary commerce of a world that continued its business while strongholds burned.

Behind him, the western sky carried the last trace of smoke, a faint gray against the fading blue. Before him, the road stretched east into country he had never seen.

The ancient text pressed against his ribs as he walked. The knowledge it held was not comfort. It was not absolution. It was the oldest truth the rite could offer: that what had happened to him had happened before, would happen again, and meant exactly what it cost.

He was the oldest inheritance, walking east, carrying the dead.
