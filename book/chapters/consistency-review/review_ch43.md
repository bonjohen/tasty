# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 43: What the Stronghold Kept

**Reviewed against:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy setting (wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps; no engines, firearms, or electricity). Voice and world rules per `book/CLAUDE.md`. Section 20 outline per `book/docs/section_20_outline.md`.

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "The records first. The shelves held twenty-three years of documentation — trial records, correspondence, dosage protocols, anatomical analyses, supplier networks, client registries, financial accounts, shipping routes, contact names."

**Problem:** Bureaucratic and corporate inventory language.

**Explanation:** "Dosage protocols," "supplier networks," "client registries," "financial accounts," and "shipping routes" all belong to the vocabulary of modern bureaucratic, corporate, and medical institutions. A pre-industrial flesh trade would have records, ledgers, and tallies — not "dosage protocols" (clinical pharmacology language) or "supplier networks" (logistics/business management language) or "client registries" (administrative/healthcare language). The accumulation of these terms in a single sentence lands the reader squarely in modernity.

**Suggested direction:** Anchor to physical objects and pre-industrial categories: ledger books, tallies, lists of names, correspondence, route maps, and marks in ink. The systemic scale can be conveyed through the physical quantity of paper, not through the modern categories applied to them.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "dosage protocols"

**Problem:** Clinical pharmacology term.

**Explanation:** "Protocol" as a formal, standardized procedural document is a modern institutional word. In its clinical sense — a prescribed method for administering a measured substance — it belongs to medicine and pharmacology, not to a pre-industrial apothecary or flesh merchant. The prince may well have measured and tracked his consumptions, but the language of those records would be different: quantities, timings, measures, instructions, proportions.

**Suggested direction:** "records of amounts and timings" or "measures and instructions" or simply "the records of his methods."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "supplier networks"

**Problem:** Modern logistics and business management vocabulary.

**Explanation:** "Network" in the sense of a coordinated commercial supply chain is a modern organizational concept. Pre-industrial trade had suppliers, contacts, traders, and routes — but the word "network" applied to supplier relationships belongs to modern systems thinking. Combined with "supplier," which itself leans modern, the phrase produces a corporate register that breaks the world.

**Suggested direction:** "the names of his suppliers," "lists of traders and hunters," "routes and contacts."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "client registries"

**Problem:** Administrative/bureaucratic institutional language.

**Explanation:** A "registry" in the modern sense — a formal, organized database of client records — belongs to institutional and administrative contexts (medical registries, property registries, voter registries). A pre-industrial merchant would have account books, lists of buyers, correspondence with patrons, or records of transactions — not registries. The word implies a standardized, formal record-keeping system that does not fit the era.

**Suggested direction:** "lists of buyers," "accounts of his patrons," "records of those who had paid."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "financial accounts, shipping routes"

**Problem:** Modern financial and logistics terminology.

**Explanation:** "Financial accounts" as a formal category of business record implies modern accounting practice. "Shipping routes" implies a modern logistical infrastructure (shipping as a commercial category associated with modern trade). Pre-industrial alternatives would be: ledger books, tallies of coin and debt, road and river markings, route maps, or records of transport.

**Suggested direction:** "ledgers, route maps, records of transport." Keep it concrete and material rather than categorical.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "anatomical analyses"

**Problem:** Modern scientific/medical classification language.

**Explanation:** "Analysis" in the scientific sense — systematic examination and classification of structure — belongs to the vocabulary of modern natural philosophy and medicine. A pre-industrial flesh merchant's records would more likely be described as diagrams, notes, observations, or drawings. "Anatomical analyses" sounds like a forensic pathologist's report, not a merchant's private documentation.

**Suggested direction:** "diagrams of cut and organ," "notes on which parts carried which effect," "observations on the flesh."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "The supply chain made visible."

**Problem:** Modern logistics and economic management vocabulary.

**Explanation:** "Supply chain" is a term from modern industrial economics and logistics management. It implies a coordinated, systematized flow of goods through multiple nodes — a concept that emerged with industrial production. The prince's operation certainly functions like a supply chain, but describing it in those terms pulls the narrator out of the world and into a modern economic frame. This is a case where the author's vocabulary betrays the modern frame of reference.

**Suggested direction:** "The trade made visible," "the whole operation laid out in containers and labels," "the goods waiting to move."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "preservation fluid"

**Problem:** Modern chemical/industrial terminology.

**Explanation:** "Preservation fluid" sounds like embalming fluid or chemical fixative — terminology from modern mortuary science, laboratory practice, or industrial food preservation. A pre-industrial setting would have specific substances used to preserve flesh: salt, oil, certain resins, brine, smoke. Even if the prince uses some alchemical or magical preparation unique to this world, calling it "preservation fluid" is generic and modern in register. The term appears twice in the chapter.

**Suggested direction:** Name or describe the substance in terms the world knows: brine, rendered fat, resin, oil, or a world-specific preparation. If it must remain unnamed, "the fluid that preserved them" or simply describing the smell (chemical, mineral, sweet) without applying the clinical category.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "The bolt wound in his shoulder, still closed, still healing."

**Problem:** Possible anachronism worth examining.

**Explanation:** "Bolt" in the context of a crossbow wound is period-appropriate and fits the pre-industrial setting. This is not a flag for anachronism — it is consistent with the world (crossbows are plausible in this era). Noted here only to confirm it as acceptable, since "bolt" could be misread as anachronistic by a quick reviewer. No change needed.

**Priority: Not an issue — confirmed consistent.**

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "the smooth surface of a river revealing the weight of current beneath"

**Problem:** Potential register concern — simile examination.

**Explanation:** This simile is naturalistic and draws from phenomena (river current, surface appearance) that exist in any pre-industrial world. It is not anachronistic. It fits the literary register of the prose. No change needed; noted only for completeness.

**Priority: Not an issue — confirmed consistent.**

### Issue 11

**Passage:**
> "the acrid smell of preservation fluid igniting"

**Problem:** Second instance of "preservation fluid" — same issue as Issue 8.

**Explanation:** See Issue 8. The repetition of the term increases the immersion cost. On second occurrence it is especially visible.

**Suggested direction:** Describe the smell and the substance without the clinical category. "The smell of the stored fluids catching," "the brine and oil igniting," or simply the smell: "something sharp and chemical."

**Priority: Medium**

### Issue 12

**Passage:**
> "Rendered fat in ceramic crocks. Bone meal in cloth sacks."

**Problem:** Minor register check.

**Explanation:** "Bone meal" is technically a pre-modern substance (ground bone used in agriculture and other trades), but in this context it reads slightly like a modern product category. "Rendered fat" is period-appropriate. Neither is high-priority, but "bone meal" may land as slightly modern for some readers.

**Suggested direction:** "Ground bone in cloth sacks" or "bone dust in cloth sacks" maintains the same meaning with slightly more period texture.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 13

**Passage:**
> "the clients would not receive their next shipment"

**Problem:** "Shipment" implies modern commercial logistics.

**Explanation:** "Shipment" as a unit of commercial goods in transit carries modern commercial connotations, particularly in the context of organized supply chains. Pre-industrial equivalents would be "delivery," "consignment," "the goods," "their order," or "what had been promised them."

**Suggested direction:** "the clients would not receive their next delivery," "what had been promised them would not come," "their orders would not be filled."

**Priority: Low**

It is not anachronistic. "Empire" is pre-industrial vocabulary. This phrase works. No change needed.

**Priority: Not an issue — confirmed consistent.**

**Passage:**

**Problem:** None. Confirmed consistent.

**Explanation:** This is controlled, period-neutral prose. No anachronism.

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 43 is largely well-controlled. The prose voice, the violence, the physical descriptions of the stronghold, and the emotional register of the destruction sequence all hold to the pre-industrial dark fantasy setting. The chapter's most significant immersion problems are concentrated in one passage: the inventory of the prince's records (paragraph beginning "The records first"). That paragraph introduces a cluster of modern bureaucratic, corporate, and clinical vocabulary — "dosage protocols," "supplier networks," "client registries," "anatomical analyses," "financial accounts," "shipping routes" — that collectively drops the reader into a modern institutional frame at the precise moment the prose is describing the scale of the prince's operation. This is the chapter's primary consistency failure.

The secondary problem is "supply chain," which appears later and repeats the same category of modern economic language.

"Preservation fluid" appears twice and is a lower-stakes but still noticeable anachronism — clinical and industrial in register where concrete period-appropriate substance names would serve better.

The rest of the chapter is in good shape. The action, the destruction sequence, the emotional beats, and the closing image all work within the setting. The failures are in the narrator's vocabulary for institutional and organizational categories, not in the world-building itself.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is institutional and organizational vocabulary: the language of modern bureaucracy, business, logistics, and clinical science applied to pre-industrial structures. When the narrative describes the physical world — rooms, weapons, fire, bodies, stone — the language is controlled and period-appropriate. When the narrative shifts to describing systems, organizations, categories, and records, the author's modern frame of reference intrudes. The vocabulary for "how a large operation is organized and documented" defaults to contemporary business and clinical English rather than to the categories and language of the world. This is a narrator-voice drift problem, not a worldbuilding inconsistency problem: the world is well-constructed, but the narrator occasionally describes it from outside it.

## Revision Plan

**Priority 1 — Fix the records inventory paragraph.** Replace "dosage protocols," "supplier networks," "client registries," "anatomical analyses," "financial accounts," and "shipping routes" with concrete, period-appropriate equivalents (ledger books, lists of names, diagrams of cut and organ, route maps, records of transport, accounts of coin). This single paragraph repair resolves the chapter's most serious consistency failure.

**Priority 2 — Replace "supply chain."** Change "The supply chain made visible" to "The trade made visible" or equivalent. One word change, high impact.

**Priority 3 — Replace "preservation fluid" (both instances).** Describe the preserving substance concretely or by its smell/effect rather than by its clinical category. Small change, removes a recurring clinical register problem.

**Priority 4 — Replace "shipment."** Change to "delivery" or "what had been promised them." Low effort, removes the final logistics-vocabulary anachronism.

**Priority 5 — Consider "bone meal."** Optional low-priority change to "ground bone" or "bone dust" if desired.

---

## Root Cause Note

The chapter's failures are **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift across the full chapter. The world itself is coherent and well-realized. The setting logic holds. The failures cluster in one paragraph and one subsequent phrase, both describing the same thing: the institutional scale of the prince's record-keeping. The author knows the world. The vocabulary for pre-industrial organizational and documentary categories is the gap. Fix the record inventory passage and the chapter is clean.
