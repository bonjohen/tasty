# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 27: What the Hunter Eats

**Chapter:** Ch27 — Captain Sella Vark POV
**Section:** 12 (The Wider Economy of Demon Flesh)
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Review standard:** poor-consistency.md

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "She maintained the way a physician maintained a patient on medication: consistent dosing, reliable supply, attention to diminishing returns."

**Problem:** Modern clinical and pharmacological language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Dosing" is a modern medical/pharmaceutical term tied to standardized, quantified drug administration. "Diminishing returns" is an economics and pharmacology concept that entered disciplined analytical language in the industrial era. "Medication" is also clinical in register — the setting has physicians, but the framing here belongs to modern pharmaceutical thinking, not pre-industrial medical practice. The metaphor imports a precision and institutional vocabulary the world has not established.

**Better direction:** Keep the physician comparison but use terminology that fits a pre-industrial healer. Something like: "She maintained the way a physician maintained a tonic regimen — the same interval, the same measure, the same attention to whether the body still answered." Or replace "dosing" with "portion" or "measure" and "diminishing returns" with "the body's lessening answer" or "the flesh's fading yield."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "She thought about the subject as she tracked."
> (and later)
> "She thought about the subject as a person."

**Problem:** Clinical bureaucratic register — "the subject."

**Why it breaks immersion:** "The subject" is language drawn from modern scientific, legal, and clinical institutions — experiments, case reports, official documentation. It belongs to a world with formalized research protocols and institutional distance. Vark is a professional hunter, not a researcher filing a report. She thinks efficiently and categories clearly, but the word "subject" imports a modern institutional framing that doesn't fit a pre-industrial context. Even a cold professional in this world would use language native to the trade: "quarry," "the demon," "the specimen," "the demon-man," "the thing she tracked," or simply "him" following a prior reference.

**Better direction:** Replace "the subject" with "the quarry," "the demon," "the specimen," or "him." The hunter's taxonomy is already well-established in the chapter — lean on its existing vocabulary.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "a behavioral overlay"

**Problem:** Modern psychological/technical terminology.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Behavioral overlay" is drawn from modern psychology, behavioral science, and possibly software/systems thinking — the language of professional analysis of observable conduct patterns. A pre-industrial hunter would not reach for this abstraction. Vark is perceptive and clinical, but her clinical register should be drawn from tracking, anatomy, and the trade — not from behavioral science.

**Better direction:** Something like "a second habit layered over the first," "a second will wearing his movements," or "a pattern set over his pattern like a hand over a hand." Vark's perception is sharp; the image just needs to be grounded in physical or craft vocabulary rather than academic psychology.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "a control mechanism — chemical, mechanical, or both"

**Problem:** Modern technical categorization language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Mechanism" is acceptable in period registers, but "chemical, mechanical, or both" as a systematic taxonomy of control options reads as modern analytical shorthand — the kind of categorization a modern technician or logistician would produce. In a pre-industrial world, Vark would think in terms of the actual tools of capture: rope, a soporific, a snare, a bolt, restraints. The abstract taxonomy of options by type ("chemical, mechanical") belongs to industrial-era systems thinking.

**Better direction:** Name the actual pre-industrial means: "she would need rope and a soporific — something from the buyer's apothecary or prepared herself — and somewhere to hold him before the transfer." Or: "she would need something to drop him and something to bind him, and a place to keep him alive until the buyer's people arrived." Concrete, trade-specific, no abstract taxonomy.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "her records put the number between sixty and seventy"

**Problem:** Record-keeping implied with modern precision and institutional feel.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The implication of formal records is not impossible in this world — merchants and professionals keep tallies and ledgers in many pre-industrial contexts. But the phrasing "her records put the number between sixty and seventy" sounds like a modern professional consulting a database or file system. The qualifier "between sixty and seventy" paired with "her records" suggests the records themselves are approximate, which is odd — if she's consulting records, the records would give a precise number. What's more likely is that she has kept a tally or a ledger, and her memory of the count is approximate. The phrasing should foreground memory or a physical object (a tally mark, a journal, a count), not the abstract concept of "records."

**Better direction:** "She had kept a tally — marks in the back of her field journal, one per kill — and the count stood somewhere between sixty and seventy." Or simply: "she had lost the precise count somewhere past fifty — the tally marks in her journal had faded on one page — but sixty was close enough."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "a coded message that said enough to motivate without saying enough to steal"

**Problem:** Modern idiom — "saying enough to steal."

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Saying enough to steal" in the sense of preventing information theft through competitive intelligence is a modern business and espionage idiom. The idea is not anachronistic — secrecy in commerce is ancient — but the specific idiomatic compression belongs to modern transactional thinking. A pre-industrial hunter with trade secrets would more naturally frame this in terms of what the message revealed and what it withheld, without the modern idiom.

**Better direction:** "A coded message that named enough to whet his interest without naming enough to move without her." Or: "A message that told Koss what kind of quarry without telling him what kind of buyer, and left the rest to his inference." The idea is the same; the phrasing is grounded in what a trade-minded person would actually think about.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "the matching of supply to demand"

**Problem:** Modern economics idiom.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Supply to demand" as a paired concept is a product of modern economic theory. The flesh economy is intentionally structured and civilizational, and commercial thinking is appropriate for this world — but the specific phrase "supply and demand" (or here, "supply to demand") is a modern academic economics formulation. A pre-industrial merchant or professional would think about buyers and sellers, what was available and what was sought, not the abstracted dyad of supply/demand.

**Better direction:** "the matching of what was available to what buyers needed" or "knowing what the wealthy wanted and finding who could provide it." Keeps the commercial register without importing the specific modern economic idiom.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "Her consumption was mechanical. His was alchemical."

**Problem:** "Mechanical" and "alchemical" used as modern oppositional metaphor.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Alchemical" is at least historically plausible in a fantasy register. "Mechanical" as a metaphor for routine, transactional, and emotionally uninvested behavior is a modern usage — it imports the machine age. Before industrial machinery, "mechanical" did not carry this metaphorical load. In a pre-industrial world, you would not naturally reach for "mechanical" to describe something rote and efficient. The contrast being made is strong and appropriate, but the words chosen betray the author's modern frame.

**Better direction:** "Her consumption was trade. His was inheritance." Or: "Her consumption was a transaction. His was a transformation." Or: "Her method was craft and discipline. His was something older — grief made flesh, love made strength." Keep the contrast but ground both terms in the world's own vocabulary.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "a crossbow within reach"

**Problem:** Potential immersion note — crossbow is period-appropriate but worth flagging.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is not a setting violation — crossbows exist in many pre-industrial contexts and are consistent with the world's established technology. However, it is worth flagging as a reference point: the chapter mentions a crossbow as Vark's primary ranged weapon. This is fine and consistent. No change needed.

**Priority:** Low (no change needed — noted for completeness)

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "the particular focus of someone who is very good at what she does"

**Problem:** Throat-clearing and modern-idiomatic phrasing.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Someone who is very good at what she does" is a colloquial modern English phrasing that sounds slightly deflated in the context of literary dark fantasy prose. It does not violate the setting per se, but it belongs to a modern conversational register and weakens the prose at a moment where Vark's character is being established. It reads as the author's shorthand rather than the world's voice.

**Better direction:** Cut or sharpen: "Where Hurkuzak's narration carried grief and confusion, Vark's carried clarity, discipline, and the cold attention of mastery." Or restructure to show rather than name the quality.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 11

**Passage:**
> "installed rather than grown"

**Problem:** Technical/technological metaphor that subtly imports modern associations.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Installed" as a metaphor for skills or traits that have been artificially placed rather than organically developed belongs primarily to the age of technology — software installation, hardware installation, implanted devices. In a pre-industrial context, this metaphor is not available in the same way. A craftsperson or hunter of this world would more likely think in terms of what was taught versus what grew naturally, what was given versus what was won.

**Better direction:** "Learned rather than born into, placed rather than grown." Or: "Taught rather than grown, worn rather than native." Or: "The fieldcraft was still good. Still better than it should have been. And still carrying the quality she had identified from the beginning — shaped by another hand, not native to the one who carried it."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 12

**Passage:**
> "the real money was always in the rare physiological variants — unusual healing, sensory gifts, extreme resilience"

**Problem:** "Physiological variants" is modern scientific/biological terminology.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Physiological variants" is language drawn from modern biological and medical science. A professional hunter in a pre-industrial world would categorize demons by their abilities, traits, and rarity — but in trade vocabulary, not in biological taxonomy. She might think of "the rare gifts," "the high-grade properties," "unusual flesh," "strong-blooded ones," or the like. "Physiological variants" is the vocabulary of a modern researcher or clinical analyst.

**Better direction:** "the rare ones — the fast healers, the ones with gifts in their senses, the ones cold and pain couldn't slow — those paid better than any baseline kill." Keep the taxonomy but in the vocabulary of the trade rather than of science.

**Priority:** High

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 27 is well-executed in structure, voice differentiation, and thematic purpose. Vark's POV is tonally distinct from Hurkuzak's, cooler and more professionally attentive, and the chapter successfully establishes her as the living face of a normalized economic system. The prose is controlled and purposeful.

The setting consistency problems are almost entirely confined to vocabulary drift: the author's modern analytical frame bleeding into Vark's internal monologue through clinical, scientific, psychological, and economic terminology that does not belong to a pre-industrial world. The world itself is coherently imagined — the flesh economy, the professional hierarchy, the consumption-as-maintenance routine — but the language used to describe Vark's thinking occasionally imports modern institutional registers.

The issues are mostly isolated wording problems rather than deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The world is solid. The voice is mostly solid. A targeted vocabulary pass would resolve the majority of problems without touching the chapter's structure or tone.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is **professional vocabulary drift**: reaching for modern clinical, scientific, economic, and psychological terminology when writing a character who thinks in disciplined, analytical terms. Vark's clarity of thought is the right instinct — she is precise and efficient. But the words chosen to render that precision are consistently drawn from modern institutions (pharmacology, behavioral science, economics, industrial mechanics) rather than from the pre-industrial world the story inhabits. The underlying concepts are period-appropriate — careful dosing of a treatment, categorizing prey by rarity, assessing capture methods — but the vocabulary is not.

A secondary pattern is **modern idiomatic compression**: phrases like "saying enough to steal," "supply to demand," and "very good at what she does" that are fluent modern English but register slightly out of key for the world's voice.

The world itself is not the problem. The prose is not the problem. The specific vocabulary chosen to render a disciplined pre-industrial professional's inner thoughts is importing too much modern institutional language.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace all instances of "the subject"** with trade-appropriate vocabulary: "the quarry," "the demon," "the specimen," or "him." Two instances; two targeted replacements.
2. **Replace "a behavioral overlay"** with a physically grounded image — something drawn from tracking, anatomy, or craft vocabulary rather than behavioral science.
3. **Replace "Her consumption was mechanical. His was alchemical."** — Recast the contrast using vocabulary native to the world: transaction vs. transformation, trade vs. inheritance, craft vs. something older.
4. **Replace "chemical, mechanical, or both"** — Name the actual pre-industrial means of capture and restraint.
5. **Replace "consistent dosing, reliable supply, attention to diminishing returns"** — Retain the physician metaphor but replace pharmacological vocabulary with period-appropriate equivalents.
6. **Replace "rare physiological variants"** — Use the trade's own vocabulary: "the rare gifts," "the strong-blooded ones," "the ones worth premium prices," etc.
7. **Replace "installed rather than grown"** — Substitute with vocabulary of teaching, shaping, or giving vs. growing natively.
8. **Replace "the matching of supply to demand"** — Rephrase in terms of buyers and sellers, want and availability.
9. **Revise "saying enough to motivate without saying enough to steal"** — Name the information logic concretely rather than using the modern compression.
10. **Revise "her records put the number between sixty and seventy"** — Anchor in a physical object (a tally, a journal) and foreground memory rather than abstract "records."
