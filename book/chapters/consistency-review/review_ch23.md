# Setting Consistency Review: Chapter 23 — The Familiar Tongue

**Reviewed against:** `book/CLAUDE.md` (story rules), `book/docs/01_story_beats.md`, `book/docs/01_story_analysis.md`, `book/docs/section_11_outline.md`
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Review rules:** `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`

---

## Individual Issues

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "It contained a violence that activated under pressure with the efficiency of a mechanism — total, precise, and it did not consult him before engaging."

**Problem:** Modern mechanical/engineering metaphor — "efficiency of a mechanism" and "activate."

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Mechanism" in the sense of a precision machine (clockwork, industrial device) carries a post-industrial connotation. In a pre-industrial world, characters might know levers, gears, and simple machines, but the abstract concept of a "mechanism" as a metaphor for internal process — particularly with the word "efficiency" and the verb "activated" — belongs to a framework of thinking shaped by mechanized industry and, later, psychology. The phrasing reads as a modern writer reaching for a metaphor, not a character whose mental vocabulary is pre-industrial. "Activated" in particular imports the language of switches and electrical circuits.

**Better direction:** Use an organic or craft metaphor — something fired, something that springs, something trained into a body like a reflex in a hunting dog or a blade edge catching a groove. "...with the sureness of a drawn bow — total, precise, and it did not wait for him." Or simply: "...with a sureness that was not his and did not consult him."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "it contained a violence that activated under pressure with the efficiency of a mechanism"

**Problem (secondary):** "Activated" as a verb applied to internal violence.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Activated" is the vocabulary of machinery, electrical systems, and later of behavioral psychology ("trigger" / "activate" framing). It also appears once more implicitly in the surrounding logic. A person in this world would experience violence erupting, surging, taking over — not activating.

**Better direction:** "...a violence that came up under pressure like a trained reflex, total and precise, and it did not consult him before it moved." Or: "...a violence that rose under pressure without warning or permission."

**Priority:** High (absorbed into Issue 1 fix)

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "The food was still wrong — flat, textureless, his body accepting it the way a machine accepts inferior fuel."

**Problem:** Industrial machine/fuel metaphor.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This simile explicitly compares Hurkuzak's body to a machine and food to fuel — a framing that belongs to the age of engines and combustion. A pre-industrial character might understand their body as a forge, as a laboring animal, as something that burns or feeds — but not as a machine processing fuel. The fuel metaphor is specifically post-industrial in its mechanical precision.

**Better direction:** Retain the alienation of the sensation without the machine frame. "...his body accepting it the way a sick animal eats — joylessly, by obligation, because stopping was worse." Or: "...flat, textureless, his body taking it in without acknowledgment the way dry earth takes rain — no pleasure, no waste, nothing recognized."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "inside him, regardless of what was outside, was the company of one"

**Problem:** Mildly clinical/modern phrasing — "the company of one" as an abstract formulation.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is borderline — the phrasing is somewhat archaic-feeling and idiomatic. However, "company of one" as a self-conscious paradox reads as a modern essayistic turn of phrase rather than natural narration from inside a pre-industrial consciousness. It sounds like a person who has read contemporary writing reflecting on solitude.

**Better direction:** The sentiment is good; the framing could be made more concrete and less self-consciously clever. "Inside him, regardless of what was outside, something occupied the space that solitude should have." Or keep it but let it stay closer to lived experience rather than aphorism.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "The explanation was plausible. Minds did this."

**Problem:** Modern psychological register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Minds did this" as a generalized claim about mental functioning is the vocabulary of psychological thinking — the idea of the mind as a category of study, a thing that operates in documented, classifiable ways ("minds do X under Y conditions"). A pre-industrial character dealing with a terrifying experience would more likely frame this in terms of spirits, illness, exhaustion, grief, or the will of something external. The casual appeal to how "minds" work as a known, generalizable system is anachronistic in register, even if the thought is plausible for any human.

**Better direction:** Keep the rationalizing gesture but ground it in more immediate, embodied experience rather than psychological generalization. "He told himself this. Exhaustion made things. Grief invented what it needed." Or: "He told himself this. Men lost in the mountains saw things. Hunger put voices in the air."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "He told himself it was stress."

**Problem:** "Stress" as a psychological/clinical term.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Stress" in its psychological sense — meaning mental or emotional strain producing symptoms — is a modern clinical coinage. The pre-industrial equivalent is exhaustion, strain, grief, fever-mind, wrack, distress — all of which are available and more period-appropriate. The bare word "stress," applied to mental state in this explanatory way, pulls from modern psychological vocabulary.

**Better direction:** "He told himself it was exhaustion." Or "He told himself his mind was starved of rest." Or simply fold it into the already-present "exhaustion" in the following sentence.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "draft at the base, angled wood, the configuration that maximized heat retention"

**Problem:** Technical/optimization language — "configuration," "maximized."

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Configuration" is a technical and somewhat scientific word — in common pre-industrial usage you would speak of the arrangement, the lay, the set. "Maximized" is the language of optimization and efficiency calculation, a modern framing applied retroactively to practical knowledge that would simply be known as the right way — not as a maximization problem solved. This phrasing sounds like the narrator is describing fire-building as an engineer, not as someone practicing inherited craft.

**Better direction:** "...draft at the base, wood angled the way Hakiia had drilled into him — the arrangement that held heat longest." Or: "...the lay that gave the most heat, the way the voice had shown him."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "reading terrain, choosing footfall, navigating by calculations his mind performed without being asked"

**Problem:** "Calculations" as a cognitive metaphor.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Calculations" implies mathematical computation, an abstraction that belongs to a world with formalized mathematics, accounting, or engineering. The pre-industrial equivalent for unconscious skill-reading would be judgment, reading, sense, instinct. "Navigating by calculations" particularly sounds modern — like describing a medieval hunter's movement in the language of dead reckoning on a navigational chart.

**Better direction:** "...reading terrain, choosing footfall, moving by a judgment his mind issued without being consulted." Or: "...the ground-reading and footfall-choosing that happened ahead of thought, by some borrowed instinct."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "They were not his calculations."

**Problem:** Continuation of the calculations metaphor (see Issue 8).

**Priority:** Medium (fix together with Issue 8)

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "He did not speak. He did not acknowledge the company."

**Problem:** Minor — "acknowledge" in a clinical/bureaucratic sense.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Acknowledge" here has a mild modern administrative flavor — as if recognizing a notification. In context it is not severely jarring, but paired with the surrounding modern-register words, it accumulates. "He did not speak. He did not name what sat beside the fire with him." Or: "He did not speak to it. He was not ready."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 11

**Passage:**
> "The way his own hands had moved with a fluency that was not learned but inherited — and the crowd had seen it."

**Problem:** "Fluency" as applied to physical movement is a modern linguistic metaphor applied to the body.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Fluency" in the sense of ease with a skill ("she speaks French fluently") derives from the concept of flowing speech. Its extension to physical movement — "moved with a fluency" — is a relatively modern metaphorical use, and it carries a slightly contemporary, workshop-prose flavor. It is not severely breaking, but it is tonally slightly off for this register.

**Better direction:** "...the way his own hands had moved with a sureness that was not learned but inherited." Or "...a readiness that was not his."

**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 23 is atmospheric, psychologically precise, and largely successful in its literary register. The prose sustains the weighted, McCarthy-inflected darkness the story calls for. The setting consistency problems are not severe — there are no anachronistic objects, no impossible technology, no outright historical contradictions. The issues are at the level of word choice and metaphor: the author's modern frame of reference slipping through in comparisons (machines, fuel, mechanisms, calculations, stress) and in the psychological vocabulary used to explain Hurkuzak's inner experience ("stress," "minds did this," "configuration," "maximized").

These are the kinds of errors that read fine in first pass and become quiet immersion breaks on rereading — the reader is not pulled sharply out of the world, but the worldbuilding voice does not feel fully native to a pre-industrial consciousness.

No single failure is catastrophic. The cumulative effect of five or six such slippages, however, creates a narrator who sounds like a modern person imagining a pre-industrial world rather than a narrator whose consciousness lives inside it.

---

## Top 10 Most Important Issues (Ranked by Impact)

1. **"efficiency of a mechanism" / "activated"** — Most jarring single phrase. Industrial/electrical vocabulary applied to inner violence is the sharpest register break. (High)
2. **"machine accepts inferior fuel"** — Explicit machine/fuel metaphor. Fully post-industrial framing of the body; stands alone as a visible anachronism. (High)
3. **"He told himself it was stress"** — "Stress" as psychological term imports clinical modernity into a scene that otherwise earns its terror through pre-modern dread. (High)
4. **"calculations his mind performed" / "They were not his calculations"** — Mathematical/computational framing of intuitive bodily knowledge. (Medium-High)
5. **"Minds did this"** — Generalized psychological claim. Anachronistic register of the mind as a classifiable, documented system. (Medium)
6. **"configuration that maximized heat retention"** — Engineering/optimization language for fire-craft. (Medium)
7. **"fluency that was not learned but inherited"** — Modern metaphorical extension; accumulates with other issues. (Low-Medium)
8. **"the company of one"** — Essayistic modern aphorism for a pre-industrial narrator. (Low)
9. **"acknowledge the company"** — Minor clinical register slippage. (Low)
10. **"navigating by calculations"** — Secondary instance of the calculations issue; lower impact but should be corrected alongside the primary instance. (Medium)

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is the unconscious reach for the language of mechanism, calculation, and psychology when describing internal experience. The prose is otherwise well-controlled and period-sensitive — there are no jarring object-level anachronisms (no clocks, no glass windows, no gunpowder). The failures are all at the cognitive and metaphorical level: the author's modern mind describes Hurkuzak's pre-industrial experience using the frameworks available to a contemporary writer — machine efficiency, fuel economics, stress response, cognitive optimization. The world is correctly built; the narrator's language quietly imports the wrong frame of reference when reaching for explanation.

A secondary pattern is the occasional essayistic generalization ("Minds did this," "the company of one") that sounds more like a contemporary literary novelist summarizing internal experience than a narrator who lives inside it.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace "efficiency of a mechanism" and "activated."** Single most important fix. Substitute an organic or bodily metaphor — something that surges, rises, takes hold. Affects the second paragraph.
2. **Replace "machine accepts inferior fuel."** Rewrite the fire-camp food observation using an animal or elemental comparison rather than a mechanical one.
3. **Replace "He told himself it was stress."** Substitute "exhaustion," "wrack," or a more grounded phrase like "He told himself his mind was starved."
4. **Replace "calculations his mind performed" and "They were not his calculations."** Use "judgment," "ground-reading," or "instinct" — something that belongs to a craftsman's or hunter's vocabulary rather than a mathematician's.
5. **Replace "configuration that maximized heat retention."** Rewrite as "the arrangement that held heat longest" or "the lay he had been drilled on" — preserving the Hakiia association while dropping the optimization language.
6. **Replace "Minds did this."** Substitute "Grief invented what it needed" or a similarly embodied and less clinical framing.
7. **Lower-priority:** Reconsider "the company of one," "fluency," and "acknowledge." These are style refinements, not immersion breaks, but they accumulate.

---

## Failure Mode Note

This chapter is not failing because of deep worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift as a sustained problem. The world rules are intact; the POV stays coherent; the emotional register is correct and controlled. The failures are **isolated wording problems** — specific words and metaphors that import a modern cognitive framework into otherwise sound prose. The fix is surgical: targeted substitutions at six to seven points, not a structural or voice-level revision.

The narrator's overall voice is appropriate to the story. These are the seams showing through in moments of explanatory reach, where the author's modern vocabulary overtakes the narrator's pre-industrial one.
