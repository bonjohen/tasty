# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 17: The Descent

**Source:** `book/chapters/ch17.md`
**Section:** 8 — Return to the World
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Review standard:** `C:\Projects\tasty\.claude\rules\poor-consistency.md`

---

## Individual Issues

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "the smell of old commerce — leather, grain, rendered fat, dried meat"

**Problem:** The phrase "old commerce" imports a mildly modern abstraction. "Commerce" as an economic category belongs to a conceptual register that sits slightly above the pre-industrial world's self-understanding. The trading post keeper would think in terms of trade, goods, stock — not a Latin-derived term of economic discourse.

**Why it feels out of place:** "Commerce" is not impossible in a medieval-adjacent world, but the phrase "old commerce" — used as an atmosphere descriptor rather than dialogue — carries a slightly Latinate, economistic flavor that edges toward the modern narrator's vocabulary rather than the world's native language.

**Better direction:** "the smell of old trade," "the smell of goods kept too long," or simply "the layered smell of a building that had bought and sold for years."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "He could separate the components the way he had separated the sounds in the forest near the cabin, each one distinct, arriving in a flood."

**Problem:** "Components" is modern scientific/analytical vocabulary. In sensory description it implies a model of decomposable inputs — a word that belongs to engineering and chemistry rather than a pre-industrial demon's experience of smell.

**Why it feels out of place:** A character from this world would not naturally think of smell as "components." He might think of threads, layers, strands, notes — terms available to anyone who works with complex materials. "Component" carries the flavor of systematic analysis that belongs to post-Enlightenment technical culture.

**Better direction:** "He could pull the strands apart the way he had pulled sounds from silence near the cabin" or "He could separate each thread of it."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "the specific brightness that people use when they are steering away from a subject that should not have been raised"

**Problem:** This is psychologically precise in a modern, observational-social-science way. "Specific brightness" as a recognizable behavioral category — named, analyzed, and described as a social strategy — sounds like a narrator drawing on contemporary awareness of conversational deflection tactics.

**Why it feels out of place:** The observation itself is entirely plausible for any era — people have always noticed when a topic is being steered away from. The problem is the clinical precision of its framing. "Specific brightness" as a named social phenomenon reads like a narrator who has absorbed modern behavioral vocabulary. The insight should feel felt and observed, not catalogued.

**Better direction:** "She spoke of other things then, and the brightness in her voice was the kind that closes a door without touching it." Or simply render the behavior without the meta-analysis of why people use it.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "He could feel it the way he felt his own heartbeat — a set of eyes behind his eyes, watching through his sight."

**Problem:** Not a setting-consistency error in vocabulary — this is strong writing — but "a set of eyes behind his eyes" is a mildly modern metaphorical construction. The deeper issue is "watching through his sight," which subtly echoes surveillance or monitoring language (watching through a lens or feed) that has no direct equivalent in this world.

**Why it feels out of place:** Minimally. This is the lowest-grade flag in the chapter. The metaphor works in context and is not technically anachronistic. However, "watching through his sight" has a slightly optically clinical quality — watching through a device rather than simply inhabiting a shared perspective. A native of this world might instead think of a presence riding behind the eyes, or looking out from within the skull.

**Better direction:** "a presence that rode behind his eyes and looked out through them" or "something looking out through his face."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "He made himself walk slowly. His body wanted to run and he had to hold it back the way you hold back a horse."

**Problem:** The second-person construction "the way you hold back a horse" momentarily breaks narrative voice by shifting from third-person past to a generalizing present-tense you. This is a register slip rather than a world-consistency failure, but in the context of a pre-industrial dark fantasy with a strong literary register, the casual generalizing "you" feels like a modern conversational tic — specifically the kind of colloquial aside a contemporary narrator might use.

**Why it feels out of place:** The rest of the chapter maintains a disciplined third-person past. "The way you hold back a horse" imports the informal, generalizing second person of modern spoken English and essays. A person from this world would not necessarily think in that generalizing register; the comparison is available but the framing is modern.

**Better direction:** "the way a rider holds back a horse" or "the way you bridle a horse that has smelled danger" (acceptable if kept as brief simile) — or rephrase to avoid the generalizing you entirely: "He had to hold it back the way a horse is held at a gate."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "She sold him dried meat and a sack of grain. He paid with a coin he had found in the cabin — old, worn, of uncertain value, but she took it with the pragmatism of a merchant who would rather take uncertain money than no money."

**Problem:** "The pragmatism of a merchant" is an analytical, slightly modern framing. "Pragmatism" is a Latinate abstraction that in this usage characterizes the woman's economic reasoning in a voice that sounds like a modern narrator glossing a character's behavior, not a narrator embedded in the world.

**Why it feels out of place:** The world has merchants and trade, so the concept is real — but "pragmatism" as the narrating word belongs to a philosophical and analytical register (it is the name of an entire philosophical tradition in modern thought). In the pre-industrial register, the narrator would be more likely to say she had the sense of a merchant, or the habit of a trader who knew the value of a coin in hand, or simply describe the action without the Latinate abstraction.

**Better direction:** "she took it with a trader's sense — uncertain money was better than none" or "she took it because a coin in hand was a coin regardless of its face."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "the road was the road to the rest of his life"

**Problem:** This is a well-written line, but "the rest of his life" is a modern sentimental formulation — an emotional closure phrase that carries the flavor of contemporary reflective writing. It is not anachronistic in any technical sense (the concept of one's remaining life is universal), but in the context of a literary dark fantasy in a pre-industrial register, it reads like a modern novelistic valediction rather than a thought or perception native to this world.

**Why it feels out of place:** It is the kind of line that would appear in contemporary literary fiction as a chapter-ending emotional release. In a world where this character would not think in terms of "the rest of my life" as a framing concept, it rings slightly imported.

**Better direction:** The line could simply be cut or replaced with something that accomplishes the same emotional release through more concrete imagery: "The road went east and he followed it into the noise of the world" — or let the following paragraph (which works well) carry the closing weight.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "the world that was not interested in finding out what he carried"

**Problem:** "Interested in finding out" is a mildly modern psychological construction — specifically the idea of the world as an agent with interest and curiosity, framed in that casual idiomatic way. This is a very minor register issue: the sentence works and is not technically broken, but "not interested in finding out" sounds slightly contemporary in its informality.

**Why it feels out of place:** The phrasing is closer to contemporary prose than to a literary dark fantasy register. In context — a final closing line — the modern informality slightly undercuts the tonal weight the chapter has earned.

**Better direction:** "a world that had no interest in what he carried" or "a world that would not ask and would not want to know."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "the smell of old commerce — leather, grain, rendered fat, dried meat. A woman behind the counter watched him enter."

**Sub-issue:** "Counter" as the sole description of the trading post's service surface is fine for the setting, but "counter" combined with "shelves" in the same sentence creates a very modern retail-space image — a layout that belongs more to a 19th-century general store than to a pre-industrial trading post.

**Why it feels out of place:** Trading posts in pre-industrial settings had benches, boards, trestle tables, and shelves of various kinds — but the word "counter" paired with "shelves" in that configuration reads as a modern shop interior being projected back. The specific image of a woman behind the counter watching him enter is a contemporary shop scene.

**Better direction:** "A long table ran the length of the wall behind her, stacked with bundled goods and open sacks" or a board on trestles rather than a counter. Or simply "A woman behind the board watched him enter."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "the layered smell of human proximity — cooking, sweat, leather, dung"

**Problem:** "Human proximity" is a minor modern abstraction. "Proximity" as a named quality of human presence — the smell of being near other people — is a slightly clinical description.

**Why it feels out of place:** It is not severely wrong, but in a pre-industrial register the narrator would be more likely to describe the smell of people gathered, or simply name the smells without abstracting them under a category. The abstraction "human proximity" sounds like it belongs to a novel of the 20th century or later.

**Better direction:** "the thick smell of people gathered close — cooking, sweat, leather, dung." Or remove the abstraction entirely and just run the list.

**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 17 is a strong, disciplined chapter with a well-maintained pre-industrial register for most of its length. The prose earns its literary weight. Setting-consistency failures are not numerous, not severe, and not structural — they are isolated vocabulary and framing choices where the narrator's modern sensibility surfaces briefly in otherwise native prose. The chapter does not introduce anachronistic objects, technologies, or institutions. No firearms, no engines, no electricity, no modern social structures appear. The world is physically coherent throughout.

The failures that exist are almost entirely at the level of word-register and analytical framing — the modern narrator occasionally reaches for a Latinate abstraction ("pragmatism," "components," "commerce") or a contemporary observational formulation ("the specific brightness that people use when steering away from a subject") that reveals the author's 21st-century vocabulary rather than a voice native to the world.

## Pattern Summary

The recurring weakness is **Latinate abstraction at moments of emotional or sensory intensity.** When the narrator moves to generalize, categorize, or frame a character's perception analytically, he tends to reach for vocabulary that belongs to modern economic, psychological, or scientific discourse ("components," "pragmatism," "commerce," "proximity," "brightness"). This is not a deep structural problem — it is a surface-level habit of a modern literary narrator. The fixes are local.

A second recurring pattern is **generalizing-you constructions and contemporary observational framing** ("the way you hold back a horse," "the specific brightness that people use when...") where the narrator steps slightly outside the world to describe human behavior in a modern analytical register.

Neither pattern affects the world's physical integrity, the character's voice, or the story's emotional logic. They are seams between the author's modern vocabulary and the world's native register.

## Revision Plan

1. **Replace "the specific brightness that people use when steering away from a subject"** with concrete behavioral rendering. This is the most immersion-breaking phrase because it most explicitly imports modern observational-social framing. Render what the woman does, not the category of what she is doing.

2. **Replace "components" with "threads" or "strands"** in the sensory passage. One word swap restores register throughout the paragraph.

3. **Replace "the pragmatism of a merchant"** with a concrete trade-register formulation that removes the Latinate abstraction.

4. **Replace the generalizing "you"** in the horse-holding simile with "a rider" or rephrase to maintain third-person discipline.

5. **Revise "old commerce"** to "old trade" or a concrete alternative. Minor, but consistent with the above pattern.

6. **Revise "human proximity"** to remove the abstraction or replace with "people gathered close."

7. **Consider revising "counter" and "shelves"** to a slightly more pre-industrial image — trestle board, benches, open sacks — if the author wants stricter world-picture fidelity. This is the lowest-priority structural item.

8. **Leave the chapter's closing lines** ("the road went east" paragraph) largely intact. The line "the road was the road to the rest of his life" is mildly modern in register but not damaging; if revised, replace with something concrete rather than cutting the valedictory feeling entirely.

---

## Failure Mode Assessment

The chapter is failing at **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The physical world is sound. The character's interiority is consistent with the setting. The story logic is native to the world. The fixes are local, targeted, and will not require structural revision. This is a line-edit problem, not a worldbuilding problem.
