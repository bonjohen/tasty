# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 2: What the Captain Keeps

**Reviewed against:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity. Literary dark register.

**POV:** Sella Vark — professional hunter and butcher of demon remains.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "one careless nick through the underlayer would start the enzymes that turned good leather into paste within a day"

**Problem:** Modern biochemical vocabulary — "enzymes" is a word belonging to 19th-century laboratory science. It implies a character who conceptualizes biological processes in systematic, categorized, scientific terms that do not exist in this world.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Vark is a skilled practitioner. She knows what happens through experience. She would describe the effect — the hide rots, the leather curdles, the skin goes bad — not name the mechanism in a term that implies formal biological training.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with experiential language: "one careless nick through the underlayer and the hide would begin to spoil within a day" or "the underlayer would corrupt, turn the leather to rot within a day." The knowledge is identical; the frame of reference is grounded.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "Learning the map had cost her three kills and the loss of a liver that would have covered two months of supplies."

**Problem:** Not a hard immersion break, but "learning the map" as a cognitive metaphor carries a faint modern clinical flavor — it belongs to the vocabulary of procedural training documentation, occupational onboarding. Very mild.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Marginally. The metaphor is natural enough that most readers will not notice. Worth flagging because the pattern of clinical/systematic framing recurs.

**Suggested direction:** "It had cost her three kills and the ruin of a liver that would have covered two months of supplies before she understood the body." Minor if changed at all.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "something about the interaction of properties when consumed together"

**Problem:** "Interaction of properties" is mild scientific framing — the word "interaction" in this sense belongs to pharmaceutical or chemical analysis discourse. More importantly, "properties" used this way (as in measurable, categorized attributes) carries a naturalist or alchemist register that tilts slightly toward early-modern scientific classification.

**Why it breaks immersion:** It is close to the line but not definitively wrong for a world that has physicians and structured flesh economy. This could be how physicians of this world actually speak, which would make Vark's internalization of it plausible. Flag as low priority unless the world is meant to be pre-literate-science.

**Suggested direction:** "something about the combined effect when eaten together" or "physicians in the southern cities prescribed the pair together — the liver and heart worked better in combination, they said." Keeps the knowledge without the clinical phrasing.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "she suspected superstition more than pharmacology"

**Problem:** "Pharmacology" is directly anachronistic. It is a modern academic discipline name, coined in the 19th century, referring to the systematic scientific study of drugs. A pre-industrial character — even one operating inside a sophisticated flesh economy — would not have this word.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is the clearest single anachronism in the chapter. A hunter-butcher in a pre-industrial world might distinguish between "what the physicians say" and "what people believe," or between "craft knowledge" and "old story," but she would not reach for a word that did not exist in any form until centuries after the era this world implies.

**Suggested direction:** "she suspected it was more old story than any real effect" or "she suspected custom more than any real virtue" or "she suspected the price was sentiment, not medicine." All preserve the skepticism without the anachronism.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "His labor was conducted at desks and in counting rooms."

**Problem:** "Counting rooms" is period-appropriate. "Desks" as a piece of furniture is not inherently wrong — writing surfaces have existed in various forms — but "at desks" paired with "counting rooms" creates a specific early-modern mercantile-office atmosphere that leans toward a Dickensian or early commercial world rather than the pre-industrial dark fantasy register the world rules imply.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Mildly. The problem is tonal, not strictly anachronistic. The flesh economy as described is sophisticated and systemic, so mercantile infrastructure is consistent. But the phrasing has a slightly administrative-modern ring.

**Suggested direction:** This is borderline. If the world has trading houses, factors, and mercantile hierarchy, desks and counting rooms are reasonable. Consider "a man whose work was ledgers and locked rooms" for slight tonal improvement if revision is warranted.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "did not renegotiate after agreeing to a price"

**Problem:** "Renegotiate" is a modern business term. It belongs to the vocabulary of contracts, corporate dealings, and formal commercial agreements. In a pre-industrial trade world, a merchant or factor who changed the agreed price after the fact would be described differently — as a man who went back on his word, who tried to reduce the price at delivery, who was a cheat or had weak handshake.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The word is not catastrophically wrong, but it carries a faint modern commercial-contract flavor that jars against the setting.

**Suggested direction:** "did not go back on a price once set" or "never tried to reduce the price at delivery" or "kept his word on price, which was worth more than either of the other two qualities combined."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "He is a factor for one of the southern trading houses."

**Problem:** Not an anachronism — "factor" as a commercial agent is period-appropriate, and trading houses exist in many pre-industrial trade economies. No issue here. Included only to note it is correctly set.

**Priority: Not flagged**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "But the number Koss had named was not a collector's number. It was an investor's number. Investors did not pay four times the market rate unless they had already seen returns."

**Problem:** "Investor" and "returns" are modern financial vocabulary. "Returns" in the sense of financial return on investment is a modern economic concept. "Investor" as a category is early-modern at the earliest — it implies a rationalized, formalized understanding of capital deployment that is post-mercantile in register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is the second-highest priority issue in the chapter. The sentence is sharp and effective as written, and the thought is correct for Vark's intelligence, but the words belong to a financial-market vocabulary that this world has not yet developed. The underlying thought — that someone paying this much has already profited from similar spending — is completely valid for the setting. The specific words are not.

**Suggested direction:** "The number Koss had named was not a collector's number. A collector spent on desire. This was different — someone who had already made back what they spent, and wanted more." Or: "The price a patron sets when he already knows the answer is worth what he is paying." The shrewdness stays; the anachronistic financial vocabulary goes.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "she had stopped thinking about it in moral terms years ago"

**Problem:** "Moral terms" is a slightly modern philosophical register. It is not catastrophically wrong, but "moral" as an abstract category one "thinks in" belongs to a self-reflexive, philosophically literate discourse. Vark is established as efficient and unsentimental, but the phrasing implies a specific kind of modern ethical self-consciousness — as though she has done the mental work of classifying her eating as a moral question and resolved it.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Subtly. A character in this world might more naturally be written as simply not thinking of it at all, or as having been raised in a tradition that had no language for it as wrong, or as someone who stopped caring what the priests said about it. The abstraction "moral terms" is the problem, not the thought.

**Suggested direction:** "She had stopped wondering about it years ago." Or: "She did not think of it as sin anymore, if she ever had." These preserve the character without importing a modern ethical self-reflection frame.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "A physician with a new theory. A noble with a new craving. A collector convinced the right combination of parts could grant more than strength."

**Problem:** "A physician with a new theory" is fine for the world. "A noble with a new craving" is fine. "A collector convinced the right combination of parts could grant more than strength" is fine. No issue here — this passage is well-grounded.

**Priority: Not flagged**

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 2 is largely well-executed and tonally consistent with the world it is trying to inhabit. Vark's professional detachment is effectively rendered, the flesh economy is introduced without didactic explanation, and the prose sustains the cooler, more efficient register specified for her POV. The physical detail is grounded throughout, and the chapter's central risk — making normalized atrocity feel real without either glamorizing or moralizing it — is handled with considerable skill.

The setting-consistency problems are isolated and fall into two clusters:

1. **Modern scientific/biochemical vocabulary:** "enzymes," "pharmacology," and to a lesser degree "interaction of properties" all import a naturalist-scientific register that belongs to 19th-century laboratory culture. The world has physicians and a sophisticated flesh economy, but neither implies formal biochemical science. These are the easiest fixes: experiential or craft-language replacements preserve all the meaning.

2. **Modern financial/commercial vocabulary:** "investor," "returns," and "renegotiate" are the more significant problem. "Investor" and "returns" in particular belong to a post-mercantile, rationalized-capital vocabulary that the world does not support. The underlying thoughts are sharp and right for Vark's intelligence; the words are wrong.

The third issue — "stopped thinking about it in moral terms" — is smaller, a matter of philosophical register rather than strict anachronism, but worth smoothing because it gives Vark a slightly modern self-reflective quality that undercuts her otherwise well-rendered professional blankness.

No deeper worldbuilding inconsistency is present. The chapter does not contradict the established setting. These are narrator-voice problems: isolated word choices where the author's modern vocabulary slips through.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is **scientific and commercial register drift**. When describing Vark's professional knowledge, the prose reaches for precise, systematic, categorized language — "enzymes," "pharmacology," "investor," "returns" — that implies a modern or early-modern scientific and financial literacy the world does not support. This is a common authorial trap: the writer understands the mechanism being described and unconsciously supplies the accurate modern word for it, when the character would describe the same thing through effect, experience, and craft knowledge rather than formal category names.

The underlying intelligence of the writing is not the problem. Vark is correctly portrayed as sharp and professionally knowledgeable. The problem is that her knowledge is currently expressed in the author's vocabulary rather than in the vocabulary her world would have developed for the same phenomena.

There is no narrator-voice drift in the emotional register — Vark's POV is consistently cooler and more efficient than Hurkuzak's, as specified. The tonal register is correct. Only the vocabulary layer needs attention.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace "pharmacology"** with craft-language or skeptical-vernacular equivalent. One word, clear fix.
2. **Replace "investor's number" and "returns"** with world-grounded equivalents that preserve Vark's analytical sharpness. Two words; the surrounding sentence needs slight reconstruction.
3. **Replace "enzymes"** with experiential description of what actually happens to the hide.
4. **Replace "renegotiate"** with pre-industrial equivalent for changing an agreed price.
5. **Revise "stopped thinking about it in moral terms"** to remove the philosophical self-reflection frame while preserving the character beat.
6. **Optional:** Revisit "interaction of properties" and "desks and counting rooms" if tonal consistency becomes a priority in later revision passes.

---

## Diagnosis Note

The chapter's problems are **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The setting is correctly imagined. The character's profession, the flesh economy, the physical environment, and the emotional register are all well-grounded. The fixes are surgical and limited to specific word choices where the author's modern vocabulary briefly surfaces. No structural revision is needed.
