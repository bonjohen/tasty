# Chapter 27: What the Hunter Eats

Vark ate her morning meal in the gray light before dawn, crouching beside a cold camp with her back to a tree and her crossbow within reach. The meal was a strip of dried demon flesh from her kit — a thigh cut, aged three weeks, preserved in salt and rendered fat and wrapped in treated cloth. She chewed it slowly, not because she savored it but because thorough chewing extracted more from the tissue and the extraction mattered. The flesh was fuel. Her body converted it with the efficiency of a system that had been consuming this specific fuel for years and had optimized for the conversion.

The taste was clean. Faintly gamey, carrying the particular mineral quality that demon muscle carried regardless of the source specimen's age or diet. She had eaten enough of it to know the variations: young demons tasted leaner, sharper, with a higher iron content that she could identify by the metallic film it left on her teeth. Older specimens were richer, denser, with a depth of flavor that reflected decades of accumulated physiological change. The piece she was eating now was from a borderland demon she had killed two months prior — middling age, moderate quality, adequate for maintenance.

She was maintaining. That was the professional term. The benefits of consumption degraded over time if not sustained through regular intake. Strength declined over months. Sensory enhancement faded. The cold tolerance that let her work the mountain ranges receded if she went too long between meals. She maintained the way a physician maintained a patient on medication: consistent dosing, reliable supply, attention to the diminishing returns that suggested it was time to increase the amount or find a higher-quality source.

She finished the strip and packed her kit and checked the trail.

Hurkuzak's track was two days old now. He had passed through a town called Merrath — her network of informants, such as it was, had confirmed his presence from a dry-goods vendor and a physician who had noticed a man loitering outside his shop with the particular intensity of someone who was seeing the trade for the first time. The physician had not been alarmed. People discovered the flesh economy at their own pace, and the discovery was usually followed by either a purchase or a departure, and the departure was more common, and the man had departed.

She followed. The track led east from Merrath along a secondary road, then north into the forest, the same pattern: road to tree line, exposure to concealment, the practiced oscillation of someone who understood that roads were useful and dangerous in equal measure. The fieldcraft was still good. It was still better than it should have been. And it was still operating with the specific quality she had identified from the beginning: learned rather than inherent, installed rather than grown, the capabilities of someone else expressed through a body that had not originally possessed them.

She thought about the subject as she tracked. Professional thinking, not emotional — the kind of assessment that organized data into categories and categories into value. The physical profile was now well established: augmented strength, enhanced senses, accelerated healing, cold tolerance, and a combat capability that emerged under stress with the specific quality of a second behavioral set overriding the first. The behavioral profile was developing: avoidant baseline, crisis-capable, showing increasing integration of the transferred capabilities into daily function. The market profile was unprecedented: a stable sibling-consumption case, alive, demonstrating full transfer of physical and — based on the behavioral evidence — psychological properties.

The hierarchy of the flesh economy was clear in her mind the way the hierarchy of any industry is clear to someone who occupies a specific position within it and understands both the positions above and below. At the bottom were the trappers — opportunistic, poorly equipped, working the borderlands where weak or young demons made easy kills and the product was low-grade. Above them were hunters like herself — professional, augmented, capable of taking mature specimens in difficult terrain and delivering product that met the standards the upper market demanded. Above the hunters were the factors and merchants — Koss and his kind, who never touched a body but who understood the logistics of preservation, transport, and the matching of supply to demand. Above the merchants were the physicians, the chefs, the ritualists — the professionals who converted raw material into refined product, who knew which tissues produced which effects and how to prepare them for consumption by clients who would never see a carcass and would not want to. And above them all, invisible and powerful, were the patrons. The collectors, the nobles, the men and women with enough wealth to fund the entire chain from hunter to table and enough appetite to consume what the chain produced.

She had never met a patron. She had worked for them through intermediaries so numerous that the original source of a contract was untraceable by the time it reached her. But she knew they existed the way she knew the weather existed: by its effects. The premiums, the specific requirements, the contracts that paid multiples of the standard rate for product that met criteria only a patron would specify — these were the evidence of appetite operating at a scale that transcended individual consumption and entered the territory of philosophy, of theory, of the belief that flesh, properly harvested and properly consumed, could produce not merely strength but transformation.

She had sent word to Koss. A courier she trusted, carrying a coded message that said enough to motivate without saying enough to steal. Koss would understand. Koss would contact the buyer. The buyer would respond, because the buyer had been looking for exactly this, and the looking had been funded by the kind of money that did not accept failure as an outcome.

The morning warmed. She moved through the forest with the ease of a body that had been built for exactly this work — each step placed by years of practice and augmented by years of consumption, the combination producing a woman who moved through hostile terrain the way water moved through a channel: without wasted effort, without unnecessary resistance, finding the path of least resistance because the path of least resistance was the path her body had been designed to find.

She thought about the taxonomy of her kills. Over fifteen years she had hunted and processed more demons than she could count precisely, though she maintained records and the records suggested the number was between sixty and seventy. Most had been unremarkable — baseline specimens, carrying the standard properties, worth the standard prices. Some had been notable: unusually resilient, unusually fast, carrying a sensory gift or a healing factor that placed them above the median and into the premium range.

Three had been dangerous. She remembered those clearly because the remembering was what kept her alive — the dangerous ones were the ones who taught you the limits of your own augmentation and the consequences of underestimating a creature that was faster or stronger or more violent than your professional experience had prepared you for. One had nearly killed her. The scar on her left shoulder blade was the reminder. She had consumed that one with particular thoroughness afterward, partly for the enhanced properties the tissues carried and partly for the professional satisfaction of incorporating the thing that had almost ended her.

She was proof that the system worked. Her body was the evidence. Every augmentation she carried — the strength, the speed, the night vision, the healing — was the product of systematic consumption sustained over years. She was not a natural predator. She was a manufactured one, built from the materials of her kills, and the building was ongoing, and the building required maintenance, and the maintenance required supply.

Hurkuzak was not supply. Not yet. He was something else — a proof of a different system, a deeper mechanism, a transfer protocol that produced results her own method of consumption could not match. She maintained. He integrated. The difference was the difference between a person who eats a particular food regularly and a person who has become the food — not consumed but transformed, not augmented but rebuilt.

The distinction mattered economically. Her augmentations could be replicated by any hunter willing to do the work and accept the risk. What Hurkuzak represented could not be replicated through the standard channels because the standard channels did not include killing your brother, consuming him over weeks of isolation, and surviving the psychological and physiological integration intact. The standard channels did not include love.

That thought surprised her. Not the word itself — she understood that the subject had consumed a family member and that the emotional context was significant. What surprised her was that she had identified the mechanism's key variable: the relationship. Commercial consumption was transactional. What the subject had done was something else, and the something else was what produced the depth of integration that made him unprecedented.

She did not dwell on it. Dwelling was not professional. She noted the insight, filed it alongside the physical data and the behavioral data and the market data, and continued tracking. The trail was fresh enough now that she could smell him — the mixed scent that her augmented senses had identified from the blood on the knife, the scent that was both demon and not-demon and occupied the space between categories that the market had not yet learned to price.

She would help the market learn. And the learning would come at a price she intended to name, and the naming would be the most significant professional act of her career, and the career had been built from the ground up through violence and discipline and the consistent willingness to do what the trade required without flinching, and the not-flinching was her version of strength, and the version was effective, and the effectiveness was what made her the right person to be tracking the most valuable specimen the trade had ever produced.

She paused at midday and examined the trail more closely. His pace had changed. He was slowing — not from injury, she could see no evidence of distress in the track, but from something else. The stride was shorter, the placement more deliberate, as though the subject was thinking while walking and the thinking was affecting the walking. She had seen this before in quarry: the moment where the animal stops fleeing and starts processing, where the flight response gives way to something more considered. It could mean he was planning. It could mean he was despairing. Either way, it meant his pattern was shifting, and pattern shifts were opportunities for someone who knew how to read them.

The afternoon passed. She moved east, closing the gap by the small increments that professional patience allowed. She was not rushing. Rushing produced mistakes, and mistakes in this context — engaging a subject whose combat capabilities exceeded her own — could be fatal. She was positioning. She was waiting. She was building the information base that would allow her to choose the optimal moment to act, and when that moment arrived, she would act with the precision and commitment that had kept her alive for fifteen years and would keep her alive for many more.

She considered the capture logistics. The subject could not be taken in a direct engagement — his combat capability under pressure exceeded anything she could match alone, even with her augmentations. She would need infrastructure: a contained space, a control mechanism (chemical, mechanical, or both), and ideally the support of the buyer's own personnel, who would have the resources and the expertise to manage a specimen this valuable. The buyer would want to be involved. A specimen of this significance was not something you trusted to a courier. The buyer would send people, or would come himself, and the sending or the coming would tell her something about the buyer's resources and priorities that she did not yet know.

She ate again at midday. Another strip of dried flesh, chewed thoroughly, the fuel entering her system with the familiar efficiency of a transaction her body had performed thousands of times. The fuel was adequate. It was always adequate. It kept her strong, kept her sharp, kept her operating at the level the trade required.

She thought, briefly, about the subject as a person. Not from empathy — from professional curiosity. He had killed his brother. He had consumed his brother. He had survived the integration and emerged carrying his brother's capabilities and, apparently, aspects of his brother's mind. What did that feel like from the inside? She had consumed demon flesh for years, and the consumption had never produced anything like what the witnesses described — a second presence, a behavioral overlay, a voice that shaped action. Her consumption was mechanical. His was alchemical. The difference was the difference that made him worth more than anything she had ever tracked.

The curiosity passed. It was not useful. Useful was the trail, the data, the courier to Koss, and the buyer's response that would arrive through channels she had established and trusted.

She packed her kit. She checked her equipment. She looked east along the trail that led to the most valuable thing she had ever tracked, and the looking was the looking of a professional at the top of her trade contemplating a transaction that would redefine her place in the hierarchy she had spent a career navigating.

The forest was quiet. The quarry was ahead. The day continued, and Vark continued with it, patient and capable and certain that the thing she was following was worth every hour she spent following it.
