# Chapter 27: What the Hunter Eats

Vark ate her morning meal in the gray light before dawn, crouching beside a cold camp with her back to a tree and her crossbow within reach. The meal was a strip of dried demon flesh from her kit — a thigh cut, aged three weeks, preserved in salt and rendered fat and wrapped in treated cloth. She chewed it slowly. Thorough chewing extracted more from the tissue. The flesh was fuel.

The taste was clean. Faintly gamey, carrying the mineral quality that demon muscle carried regardless of the kill. She had eaten enough of it to know the variations: young demons tasted leaner, sharper, with a metallic bite she could feel on her teeth. Older ones were richer, denser, with a depth that came only with age. The piece she was eating now was from a borderland demon she had killed two months prior — middling age, moderate quality, adequate for maintenance.

She was maintaining. The benefits of consumption degraded over time if not sustained through regular intake. Strength declined over months. Sensory enhancement faded. The cold tolerance that let her work the mountain ranges receded if she went too long between meals. She maintained the way a physician maintained a patient on medication: consistent dosing, reliable supply, attention to diminishing returns.

She finished the strip and packed her kit and checked the trail.

Hurkuzak's track was two days old now. He had passed through a town called Merrath — a dry-goods vendor confirmed his presence, and a physician had noticed a man loitering outside his shop with the stare of someone seeing the trade for the first time. The physician had not been alarmed. People discovered the flesh economy at their own pace, and the discovery was usually followed by either a purchase or a departure. The man had departed.

She followed. The track led east from Merrath along a secondary road, then north into the forest, the same pattern: road to tree line, exposure to concealment, the practiced oscillation of someone who understood that roads were useful and dangerous. The fieldcraft was still good. Still better than it should have been. And still carrying the quality she had identified from the beginning: learned rather than inherent, installed rather than grown.

She thought about the subject as she tracked. The physical profile was established — strength, senses, healing, cold tolerance, and a combat capability that emerged under stress like a second self overriding the first. What interested her now was the behavioral shift: the avoidance giving way to something more integrated. He was learning to use what he carried. And what he carried, by every measure she knew, was unprecedented.

She knew where she fit in the hierarchy. At the bottom were the trappers — opportunistic, poorly equipped, working the borderlands for low-grade product. Above them were hunters like herself, professional and augmented, capable of taking mature specimens in difficult terrain. Above the hunters were the factors and merchants — Koss and his kind, who never touched a body but who understood logistics, preservation, and the matching of supply to demand. Above the merchants were the professionals who converted raw material into refined product: physicians, chefs, ritualists who knew which tissues produced which effects and how to prepare them for clients who would never see a carcass. And above them all, invisible, were the patrons.

She had never met a patron. She had worked for them through so many intermediaries that the original source of a contract was untraceable by the time it reached her. But she knew they existed the way she knew the weather existed: by its effects. The premiums. The specific requirements. The contracts that paid multiples of the standard rate for product only a patron would specify. Evidence of an appetite that had become philosophy — the belief that flesh, properly consumed, could produce not merely strength but transformation.

She had sent word to Koss. A courier she trusted, carrying a coded message that said enough to motivate without saying enough to steal. Koss would understand. Koss would contact the buyer. The buyer would respond, because the buyer had been looking for exactly this, backed by the kind of money that did not accept failure.

The morning warmed. She moved through the forest the way water moved through a channel — without wasted effort, without resistance, finding the path her body and her years of consumption had built her to find.

She thought about her kills. Over fifteen years she had hunted and processed more demons than she could count precisely, though her records put the number between sixty and seventy. Most had been unremarkable — baseline specimens, standard properties, standard prices. Some had been notable: unusually resilient, unusually fast, carrying a sensory gift or a healing factor that placed them into the premium range.

Three had been dangerous. She remembered those clearly because the dangerous ones taught you where your augmentations ended. One had nearly killed her. The scar on her left shoulder blade was the reminder. She had consumed that one with particular thoroughness afterward, partly for the enhanced properties the tissues carried and partly for the professional satisfaction of incorporating the thing that had almost ended her.

She was a manufactured predator, built from the materials of her kills. Every augmentation she carried — the strength, the speed, the night vision, the healing — was the product of systematic consumption sustained over years.

Hurkuzak was not supply. Not yet. He was proof of a deeper mechanism, one her own method could not match. She maintained. He integrated. The difference mattered economically. Her augmentations could be replicated by any hunter willing to do the work and accept the risk. What Hurkuzak represented could not be replicated through the standard channels, because the standard channels did not include killing your brother, consuming him over weeks of isolation, and surviving the integration intact. The standard channels did not include love.

The insight was not one she would have predicted. Commercial consumption was transactional. What the subject carried was something else — kinship threaded through the flesh, a bond the standard consumption could never replicate. She noted it, filed it alongside the rest, and continued tracking. The trail was fresh enough now that she could smell him — the mixed scent her augmented senses had identified from the blood on the knife, both demon and not-demon, occupying a space between categories the market had not yet learned to price.

She would help the market learn. The price she intended to name would be the most significant act of her career.

She paused at midday and examined the trail more closely. His pace had changed. He was slowing — not from injury, she could see no evidence of distress in the track. The stride was shorter, the placement more deliberate, as though his attention had turned inward. She had seen this before in quarry: the moment where the animal stops fleeing and starts processing. It could mean he was planning. It could mean he was despairing. Either way, his pattern was shifting.

The afternoon passed. She moved east, closing the gap by the small increments that professional patience allowed. She was positioning. She was waiting.

She considered the capture logistics. She would need infrastructure: a contained space, a control mechanism — chemical, mechanical, or both — and ideally the support of the buyer's own personnel, who would have the resources to manage a specimen this valuable. The buyer would want to be involved. A specimen of this significance was not something you trusted to a courier. The buyer would send people, or come himself, and she would learn something about the resources behind the contract.

She thought about the subject as a person. He had killed his brother and consumed him and survived the integration carrying capabilities and, apparently, aspects of a mind that were not his own. What did that feel like from the inside? She had consumed demon flesh for years, and the consumption had never produced anything like what the witnesses described — a second presence, a behavioral overlay, a voice that shaped action. Her consumption was mechanical. His was alchemical.

The curiosity passed. She packed her kit and looked east along the trail that led to the most valuable thing she had ever tracked.

The forest was quiet. The quarry was ahead.
