# Chapter 9: The Line He Crosses

He stopped pulling when the light failed because there was nothing left in his body that could pull in the dark. He found a hollow between rocks where the wind broke and the snow was shallow. He dragged the travois into it and built a fire that took him four attempts and most of the feeling in his fingers. The flame when it came was small and uncertain, and it was all there was.

Hakiia had not spoken since the demand. Hours of silence, broken only by the wrong breathing and the occasional small sound that was not a word but was the body's response to being moved over uneven ground with two shattered legs. Hurkuzak had pulled through that silence as though the silence were the medium they were traveling through — thicker than air, heavier than snow. The demand and the refusal and the knowledge that the refusal was temporary filled the space between them.

He sat beside the fire and watched his brother's face. The lucidity from earlier was gone. Hakiia's eyes were closed and his features had settled into something that was no longer the mask Hurkuzak had known his entire life. The face was loose. The jaw hung slightly open. The lines that had always held Hakiia in the posture of command had softened, and what remained was the face of a man being weathered, the way stone is weathered — not through resistance but through erosion.

Hurkuzak did not sleep. He sat beside the fire and watched the embers and watched his brother's breathing and counted the pauses between breaths. The pauses were longer. Five heartbeats between one breath and the next. Then six. Then a stretch of seven that made his own heart stumble before Hakiia's chest rose again, shallow and reluctant.

He thought about what Hakiia had asked. He thought about it the way a man thinks about a wound he has been told to look at. The demand was there. It had been there since the afternoon. It occupied the space between them the way the fire occupied the space between the rocks — present, impossible to ignore.

He thought about the dog. The quiet one, the left rear, with its cracked pad and its patient eyes and the wrongness in its spine. He had put his hand on its head and drawn his knife and done what needed to be done. The dog had died quickly and the doing had been clean, and afterward he had climbed down from the ledge and continued working.

He thought about the elk. The dark eye, the held breath, the moment where the arrow was drawn and the animal was alive and the decision not to release had felt like mercy, and Hakiia had said it was weakness.

And now Hakiia was asking him not to flinch.

The fire burned lower. He fed it with the last of the deadwood he had gathered, and the flames brightened and then settled, and he sat inside the small warmth and felt the hours pass.

Sometime in the deep part of the night, Hakiia woke.

Hurkuzak knew it because the breathing changed. Not improved — it could not improve — but it shifted, and Hakiia's eyes opened and they were clear again, as clear as they had been that afternoon, and they found Hurkuzak's face across the fire and held it.

Neither of them spoke for a long time. The fire cracked. An ember popped and a flare of light moved across Hakiia's face and the blood crust on his forehead caught the light and held it and for a moment the wound looked fresh.

"You have been sitting there all night," Hakiia said.

"Yes."

"Have you slept?"

"No."

Hakiia looked at the fire. His voice when he spoke again was different from the afternoon. The contempt was nearly gone. What remained underneath it was something Hurkuzak had never heard before.

"I am not going to argue again," Hakiia said. "I said what I said. The facts have not changed. If anything they are worse. The right leg is further gone. I can feel less of it now than I could this afternoon, and that is not recovery."

"I know."

"I know you know." He paused. "I know what I am asking you to carry. After. I know that the weight of it does not end when my weight ends. I know that you will walk away from this with something inside you that will not leave."

Hurkuzak's throat tightened. Hakiia had never said anything like this.

"I would not ask if there were another way," Hakiia said. "There is not. I have looked for one. I spent more hours looking than you know, because there was no asking this. I looked first. I found nothing."

The words were quiet. They came without force or cunning. They came the way a stone falls — straight down, without art.

"You are the only one here," Hakiia said. "And what I am asking is the hardest thing I have ever asked anyone. And I am asking you because you are the one who will do it with mercy, and because I trust you to carry it afterward, and because if I am going to die I want it to be by the hand of someone who will remember that it mattered."

The fire burned. The wind moved through the trees above them.

"Please," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak had never heard that word from his brother. Not in the worst moments of the last three days.

Hurkuzak felt the refusal inside him give way the way ice gives way in spring — from beneath, the structure standing a moment after the support was gone, then the water moving through with quiet certainty.

"Yes," he said.

The word was small. It was the largest thing he had ever done.

Hakiia closed his eyes. When he opened them again, there was something in them that Hurkuzak had no word for. He did not say thank you. He did not need to.

Hurkuzak stood. His legs held him, though he could not have said how. He stood beside the fire and looked down at his brother.

He picked up the knife. Hakiia's knife. The handle was cold. The blade was clean.

He knelt beside the travois. He put his free hand on Hakiia's chest. Under his palm he could feel the heartbeat — fast, shallow, irregular, but present. Still present. Still his brother.

Hakiia looked at him. The look was steady. It left no question.

"Don't hesitate," he said.

Hurkuzak did not hesitate.

The knife went where mercy lived. He felt the blade enter and felt the resistance and felt the giving way, and Hakiia's right hand came up — what was left of it, the ruined thing, the thumb stump and the broken fingers — and the movement was slow, effortful. The thumb stump found Hurkuzak's wrist. It pressed against the pulse point, against the place where the blood still moved. Not a grip — the hand could not grip. A pressure. Everything it had left, which was almost nothing. The hand trembled. The thumb stump slipped and found the wrist again. To be touching his brother at the moment of crossing. And then the pressure eased, and then it was gone.

Hakiia's eyes stayed open. They held Hurkuzak's face, and then the eyes were still open but they were not seeing anymore, and the hand that had pressed against his wrist lay on the furs — the thumb stump, the missing fingers, the broken ones fixed at their wrong angles, the cauterized surfaces dark against the pale fur. What relaxed was not the fingers but the effort. The hand was just a shape now.

The breathing stopped. Not gradually — completely. The fire still burned. The wind still moved through the trees. Hakiia was dead, and Hurkuzak had killed him.

He did not withdraw the knife immediately. He knelt beside his brother's body with his hand on the handle and his other hand still on Hakiia's chest where the heartbeat had been and he stayed there. The fire lit them both. The blood that came from the wound was dark and slow and it pooled in the furs.

He pulled the knife free. He cleaned it. He cleaned it because Hakiia kept his tools clean, and the keeping had always fallen to him. The habit survived the killing. The hands did not know yet what the hands had done.

He closed Hakiia's eyes. The lids came down easily, as though they had been waiting for permission. The face underneath was the face from sleep — unclenched, unheld, the face of someone who had finally held still long enough to be loved without complication. It was the face Hurkuzak had watched by firelight two nights ago and thought looked like someone who might have been easy to love if he had ever stopped moving.

He was stopped now. He would not move again.

Hurkuzak sat beside his brother's body in the cold. The fire was dying. He did not feed it. The wind moved through the trees and the snow fell in small, intermittent flurries that landed on Hakiia's face and did not melt because the face was cooling and the warmth that had melted snow was leaving it in the slow, invisible way that warmth leaves all things when the source is gone.

He did not think about the cabin. He did not think about the morning or the march or the question of what came next. He did not think about survival or food or the cold or the wolves or the distance still to cover. He sat beside the body that had been his brother and the knife that had been his brother's and the furs that were soaked with his brother's blood and he felt the weight of what he had done settle into him like something swallowed, something that would live inside him now, something that would not dissolve or diminish or grow lighter with time.

The killing had also been real. His hand on the knife. The blade going in. The resistance and the giving way. The pressure on his wrist and the easing of the pressure. The last breath and then the absence. He had done that. His hand. His choice. His brother's death lived in his muscles and his bones and behind his eyes and it would live there for as long as he lived, which might not be very long, but would feel long enough.

The fire died. The cold came in. The snow fell on his brother's face and on his hands and on the furs and on the blood that was freezing now, turning dark and solid, becoming part of the winter the way everything became part of the winter eventually — the elk, the dogs, the wolves, the sled, the trail, the brothers.

Hurkuzak sat beside Hakiia's body and did not move. He would move later. He would have to. The cabin was ahead and the cold would kill him too if he stayed, and his body would stand him up when it was ready.

But not yet. For now he sat. For now the only thing in the world was his brother's face, unclenched and still, and the silence that had replaced the wrong breathing, and the snow that fell without opinion on the living and the dead alike, and the cold, patient and absolute, continuing its work on everything.
