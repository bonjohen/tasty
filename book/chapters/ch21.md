# Chapter 21: What the Body Knows

The bolt wound had closed by morning. He unwrapped the cloth from his thigh and the wound was a raised line of new skin, pink and tight, where yesterday there had been a hole with a crossbow bolt in it. He had pulled the bolt out himself — an act that should have produced a scream and instead produced a grunt and a quantity of blood that stopped flowing before he finished binding it. Now the blood was dried and the binding was unnecessary and the skin underneath was whole.

He stared at the healed wound and felt the distance between what his body was doing and what his body should have been doing widen into a gap he could no longer bridge with explanations. This was not nutrition. This was not rest. This was not the natural resilience of a healthy body managing an injury within normal parameters. This was something else, and the something else was the thing behind the alcove door that he had eaten and the eating was continuing to produce results that could not be accounted for by any framework he was willing to accept.

He needed supplies. He needed to move. He needed information about whoever had shot him — the bolt had been aimed with professional precision, which meant the shooter was not a bandit but something worse, something that chose its targets for reasons beyond simple robbery. He needed to know whether the shooter was still following.

He was still following. He could feel it. Not through any specific sense — not hearing, not sight, not the enhanced smell that could separate individual scents at distances that should have been impossible. Through something else. A pressure at his back that the voice registered as attention and that his body processed as threat, constant and patient, the kind of threat that did not announce itself with noise but with the specific quality of silence that a professional tracker produced.

Move. The voice was not suggesting. It was pushing, the way it had pushed during the bandit fight — not through words but through the body's own systems, a tightening in the chest that said go, a surge of clarity that illuminated exits and routes and the comparative safety of each. The voice wanted him off the forest trails and into a settlement where proximity to other people would complicate the tracker's options. Hurkuzak wanted the same thing, though his reasons were different: he wanted witnesses. The tracker would not shoot again with witnesses.

He found a settlement by midday. Larger than the trading post where he had bought the tasteless meat — this was a proper town, with a market and a garrison and the commercial infrastructure that trade routes generated where they intersected. People everywhere. The noise was a wall of sound that his enhanced hearing decomposed into individual sources before he could stop it: conversation, hammering, the call of vendors, children, dogs, the grinding of a mill somewhere west of the market square, the sound of metal on metal from a smithy.

He moved through the crowd and tried to be small. The trying was an act of will that required him to override everything his body wanted to do — the body wanted to scan, assess, position itself for advantage, track the movements of every person within engagement range. The body operated on the assumption that the world was hostile and the assumption was Hakiia's assumption, installed alongside the reflexes and the strength and the senses, and overriding it was like holding his breath: possible for a time, but not sustainable, and the body would eventually insist.

He bought food. He ate standing up beside a water trough, chewing the dried meat that tasted of salt and nothing else, watching the market move around him with the hyper-attentive gaze he could not shut off. The voice cataloged every face: that man is carrying a weapon under his coat, left side; that woman is watching you, not with interest but with the general wariness of a merchant tracking strangers near her goods; those two by the farrier are not waiting for a horse, they are waiting for something else, their attention is wrong.

He told the voice to stop. The voice did not stop. The voice operated on a principle that did not include off, only louder and quieter, and in a crowd it defaulted to louder because a crowd was a target-rich environment and target-rich environments required assessment.

He was resupplying at a dry goods vendor when the situation built.

It started with a dispute at a neighboring stall — two men arguing about a transaction, their voices rising in the specific pitch that anger gives to commercial disagreements. One of the men was large and confident in the way that men are confident when their size has always been sufficient to resolve disputes. The other was smaller, older, and was refusing to yield with the stubbornness of a person who knew he was right and had decided that being right was worth the risk of being hurt.

Hurkuzak looked away. The dispute was not his concern. His concern was the dried meat in his hand and the coins in his other hand and the transaction that would allow him to leave this settlement and continue moving east.

The large man hit the small man.

It was not a killing blow. It was a dominance blow — open-handed, across the face, the kind of violence that says I am larger and you will do what I say because I am larger. The small man staggered. The people around the stall backed away with the practiced geometry of bystanders who knew how these things went and had already calculated the safe distance.

Hurkuzak did not move. He stood at the neighboring stall with the meat in his hand and did not move because moving was involvement and involvement was exposure and exposure was the thing that had already cost him twice and would cost him again.

Two more men materialized from the crowd. They were with the large man. The three of them surrounded the smaller man and the surrounding had the practiced coordination of people who did this regularly — enforcers, collectors, men whose trade was the conversion of proximity into compliance.

They began to beat him.

Not viciously — not yet. Methodically. A blow to the body, a kick when he went down, a verbal demand that accompanied each impact. The crowd watched. The crowd did not intervene. The crowd had calculated the risk of intervention against the risk of observation and had arrived at the universal answer of crowds, which is that watching is free and acting is expensive and the expense might include your own body.

Hurkuzak's body was making a decision.

He could feel it happening — the same process as the bandit fight, the same acceleration of something that operated below conscious thought. His weight was shifting. His hands were emptying themselves of meat and coins. His breathing was changing from the shallow, concealed breathing of a man trying to be invisible to the deep, regular breathing of a body preparing for expenditure. The voice was not speaking in words. It was present as a current, as a direction, as a set of priorities that aligned his body toward the threat the way a compass needle aligns toward north.

He tried to stop it. He tried the way he had tried to stop the flow during the bandit fight — through conscious resistance, through the assertion of a will that said this is not me, this is not what I do, I do not move toward violence. The resistance met something stronger. Not the voice. The body itself, which had been rebuilt from the inside by months of consumption and which carried in its muscles and its nerves a set of reflexes that had been calibrated by a lifetime of experience that was not Hurkuzak's lifetime, and those reflexes had assessed the situation and concluded that the correct response was immediate, and the correct response was happening whether Hurkuzak authorized it or not.

He moved.

The first enforcer saw him coming and did not process what he saw quickly enough. Hurkuzak's hand found the man's collar and the hand closed and pulled and the man's balance broke and the man went down and Hurkuzak was past him before the man's body reached the ground. The second enforcer turned and swung and the swing was competent and it missed because Hurkuzak was not where the swing expected him to be — he had moved inside the arc, inside the range where the fist had power, and his elbow found the man's jaw and the connection was solid and the man's consciousness departed with the specific abruptness of a machine being switched off.

The large man — the one who had started it, the one whose size had always been sufficient — looked at Hurkuzak and saw what the wagon traders had seen and what the third bandit had seen: a person who had changed category from bystander to force, whose body moved with a speed and precision that did not match his frame or his face or anything about the tired, thin-featured traveler who had been buying dried meat two seconds ago.

The large man reached for something at his belt — a weapon, a tool, something metal — and the voice delivered a single instruction that Hurkuzak felt in his hands rather than his mind: end it.

Hurkuzak struck him. Once. The blow landed in a place that the voice knew and Hurkuzak did not — a point where the neck met the jaw that contained vulnerabilities invisible to anyone who had not spent years learning where bodies broke most efficiently. The large man dropped. Not slowly, not in stages. He went from standing to horizontal with the committed totality of something that had been switched off at the source.

The silence that followed was the silence of a crowd that had stopped breathing.

Hurkuzak stood among three fallen men and felt the voice settle into quiet satisfaction. Not joy — the voice did not experience joy, or if it did, the joy was a hunter's joy, the grim professional pleasure of a problem solved with the minimum necessary force. The quiet was worse than noise would have been. It was the quiet of something that had gotten what it wanted and was content to rest until the next time the wanting arose.

The small man was on the ground, bleeding from the mouth, curled against the base of the stall. Hurkuzak stood over him for a moment and the standing-over was wrong because the posture was dominant, territorial, the posture of a man who had claimed a space through violence and was surveying the result with the satisfaction of ownership. He made himself step back. The stepping back was hard — the body did not want to yield ground it had taken, and the not-wanting was a physical sensation, a tension in the legs and the spine that said stay, hold, this is yours now.

He forced himself back. The small man looked up at him and the look was not gratitude. It was the look of a person who had been rescued by something more frightening than the thing he was rescued from.

He looked at his hands. They were steady. They should not have been steady. They should have been shaking the way they had shaken after the wolves, because what had just happened was violence that his mind had not chosen and his body had executed and the execution had been flawless and the flawlessness was the terrifying thing, more terrifying than the violence itself, because flawless meant practiced and practiced meant the body carried a library of violence that could be accessed at any time under sufficient pressure.

The crowd was staring at him. Every face held the same expression: the look of people who had just witnessed something they would describe to others and that the others would struggle to believe, because the something did not fit into the categories available for describing what a person could do. The faces were not grateful. They were afraid. He had helped someone and the helping had made them afraid.

He saw himself in their faces. Not a reflection — a reading. They did not see a traveler who had intervened in a beating. They saw something that had emerged from the shape of a person and done things that persons do not do. The speed. The precision. The single blow that had dropped the largest man in the market with the surgical efficiency of someone who knew exactly where the body's architecture failed under impact. They saw what Vark would see when she arrived, what the stories would carry when they spread: a creature wearing a man's clothes, moving with a predator's training, and looking — when they met his eyes — as though the thing looking back through those eyes was not entirely the person standing behind them.

He left. He did not run — running would draw more attention — but he walked fast, through the market, past the garrison, toward the eastern road, and every step carried him away from the eyes and closer to the understanding that the eyes would follow him in the form of stories, and the stories would travel faster than he could, and the stories were about a man who moved like something inhuman and fought like something trained and healed like something that did not obey the ordinary rules.

The voice was quiet. It had done its work. It was resting inside him with the patience of something that knew it would be needed again and was perfectly willing to wait.

He thought about Hakiia. Not the dead brother in the alcove, not the voice that corrected and commanded and had just used his body as a weapon. The Hakiia who had killed the elk with effortless violence and then mocked Hurkuzak for hesitating. The Hakiia who had killed the wolf from the ground with a fury that should not have been possible. The Hakiia who had believed, with the certainty of a man who had never been wrong about the world's essential nature, that strength was the only honest answer to a hostile world.

That Hakiia was inside him now. Not as memory. As function. As a library of violence that his body could access without his permission and deploy without his consent. The inheritance was not a gift. It was a colonization. And the colony was growing stronger with each engagement, each eruption, each moment when the world pressed hard enough that the man Hurkuzak had been stepped aside and the man Hakiia had been stepped forward.

Hurkuzak walked east. The terror he carried was not behind him. It was in his hands, in his muscles, in the borrowed violence that lived in his body and would express itself whenever the world pressed hard enough against the man who carried it. And the world, he was learning, pressed constantly.
