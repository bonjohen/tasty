# Chapter 21: What the Body Knows

The bolt wound had closed by morning. He unwrapped the cloth from his thigh and the wound was a raised line of new skin, pink and tight, where yesterday there had been a hole with a crossbow bolt in it. He had pulled the bolt out himself — an act that should have produced a scream and instead produced a grunt and a quantity of blood that stopped flowing before he finished binding it. Now the blood was dried and the binding was unnecessary and the skin underneath was whole.

He touched the new skin. Flinched. Looked away.

He needed supplies. He needed to move. The bolt had been aimed with professional precision. He needed to know whether the shooter was still following.

He was still following. Hurkuzak could feel it — not through hearing or sight, but a pressure at his back. Patient. Constant. The kind of silence a tracker makes.

Move. Not a suggestion — a tightening in the chest, a surge of clarity that mapped every exit. The voice wanted a settlement. Hurkuzak wanted witnesses.

He found a settlement by midday. Larger than the trading post — a proper town, with a market and a garrison and the bustle of two roads meeting. People everywhere. His hearing pulled the noise apart before he could stop it: conversation, hammering, vendors calling, children, dogs, the grinding of a mill west of the square, a smithy ringing.

He moved through the crowd and tried to be small. The body wanted to scan, assess, position for advantage. Holding it back was like holding his breath.

He bought food. He ate standing up beside a water trough, chewing the dried meat that tasted of salt and nothing else, watching the market with the sharp gaze he could not shut off. The voice cataloged every face: that man is carrying a weapon under his coat, left side; that woman is watching you — merchant wariness, not interest; those two by the farrier are not waiting for a horse, they are waiting for something else, their attention is wrong.

He told the voice to stop. The voice did not have an off — only louder and quieter. In a crowd, it defaulted to louder.

He was resupplying at a dry goods vendor when the situation built.

It started at the neighboring stall — two men arguing about a transaction, voices rising. One of the men was large, standing with his weight forward, the posture of someone who had never lost an argument at close range. The other was smaller, older, chin up, feet planted.

Hurkuzak looked away. The dispute was not his concern. His concern was the dried meat in his hand and the coins in his other hand and the transaction that would allow him to leave this settlement and continue moving east.

The large man hit the small man.

It was a dominance blow — open-handed, across the face. The small man staggered. The people around the stall backed away with practiced geometry.

Hurkuzak did not move. He stood at the neighboring stall with the meat in his hand. Moving had cost him twice already.

Two more men materialized from the crowd. They were with the large man. The three of them surrounded the smaller man with practiced coordination — enforcers, collectors.

They began to beat him. Not viciously — not yet. Methodically. A blow to the body, a kick when he went down, a verbal demand that accompanied each impact.

The crowd watched. The crowd did not intervene.

Hurkuzak's body was making a decision.

He could feel it happening. His weight was shifting. His hands were emptying themselves of meat and coins. His breathing was changing — the shallow concealment of a bystander becoming the deep rhythm of a body preparing for effort. The voice was not speaking in words. It aligned his body toward the threat the way a compass aligns toward north.

He tried to stop it. The resistance met something stronger — not the voice, but the body itself, rebuilt by months of consumption, carrying reflexes calibrated by a lifetime that was not his. Those reflexes had already decided.

He moved.

The first enforcer saw him coming and did not process what he saw quickly enough. Hurkuzak's hand found the man's collar, closed, pulled. Balance broke. The man went down and Hurkuzak was past him before the body reached the ground. The second enforcer turned and swung and the swing was competent and it missed because Hurkuzak was not where the swing expected him to be — he had moved inside the arc, inside the range where the fist had power. His elbow found the man's jaw. The connection was solid. The man dropped — instant, total, like a rope cut.

The large man — the one who had started it, the one whose size had always been sufficient — looked at Hurkuzak and saw what the wagon traders had seen and what the third bandit had seen: someone whose body moved with a speed that did not match his face.

The large man reached for something at his belt, and the voice delivered a single instruction that Hurkuzak felt in his hands rather than his mind: end it.

Hurkuzak struck him. Once. The blow landed where the neck met the jaw — a point the voice knew. He dropped with the totality of a felled tree.

The crowd had stopped breathing.

Hurkuzak stood among three fallen men and felt the voice settle into quiet satisfaction — the grim pleasure of a problem solved with minimum force. The quiet was worse than noise. It was the quiet of something content to wait.

The small man was on the ground, bleeding from the mouth, curled against the base of the stall. Hurkuzak stood over him and the standing-over was wrong. He made himself step back. His legs resisted. Stay. Hold. This is yours now.

He forced himself back. The small man looked up at him and the look was not gratitude. It was the look of a person who had been rescued by something more frightening than the thing he was rescued from.

He looked at his hands. They were steady. After the wolves, they had shaken. Now — nothing. Steady as a craftsman's.

Every face held the same expression. The faces were not grateful. They were afraid.

He saw himself in their faces. They did not see a traveler who had intervened in a beating. They saw something that had emerged from the shape of a person and done things that persons do not do. The speed. The precision. The single blow. They saw what Vark would see when she arrived, what the stories would carry when they spread: a creature wearing a man's clothes, moving with a predator's training, and looking as though the thing behind those eyes was not entirely the person standing behind them.

He did not run — running would draw more attention. He walked fast, through the market, past the garrison, toward the eastern road. The eyes would follow him as stories. The stories would travel faster than he could — a man who moved like something inhuman, healed like something that did not obey the rules.

He thought about Hakiia. Not the dead brother in the alcove, not the voice that corrected and commanded and had just used his body as a weapon. The Hakiia who had killed the elk with effortless violence and then mocked Hurkuzak for hesitating. The Hakiia who had killed the wolf from the ground with a fury that should not have been possible. The Hakiia who broke a man's arm for touching Hurkuzak's shoulder and called it education.

That Hakiia was inside him now. Not as memory. As function. The inheritance was not a gift. It was a colonization — growing stronger with each eruption, each moment the world pressed hard enough for the man Hakiia had been to step forward.

Hurkuzak walked east. The world, he was learning, pressed constantly.
