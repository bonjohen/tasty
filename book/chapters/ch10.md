# Chapter 10: The Carrying

He did not know how long he sat. The fire died and the cold replaced it and the cold was absolute. The snow fell on Hakiia's face and on his own hands and on the furs between them. It was the same snow it had always been. It did not know what had happened here.

At some point his body began to shiver. The shivering was not a decision. It was the body announcing that it had a separate opinion about what happened next, and the body's opinion was survival, and it did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely.

Hurkuzak stood. The standing took a long time — hands on the ground, knees braced, a push that failed and a second push that succeeded. The world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out and waited and the balance came, not through strength but through stubbornness. He looked down at what was on the travois.

Hakiia was dead. The word was simple. The fact of it was not. His brother lay with his eyes closed and his face still and the blood on the furs frozen. The body was no longer the body of a man who was dying or a man who was in pain or a man who was commanding or arguing or saying please. The body was only a body. What remained was mass without will, shaped like a man.

The first problem was the travois. The wolf fight and the long dragging had wrecked it — the lashings had loosened, one of the poles had cracked near the crosspiece, and the furs underneath were stiff with frozen blood and no longer slid cleanly. Hurkuzak knelt and retied what he could retie and reinforced the cracked pole with a strip of leather and repositioned the furs and tested the frame by lifting the drag poles and pulling. It moved. Barely. The resistance was different now and it took him several steps to understand why.

A living body on a travois cooperates in small ways the living person does not notice — muscles adjust, weight shifts, the body finds its center on the frame without being told to. A dead body is only mass, and the travois that had carried a wounded man with difficulty now carried a dead man with more difficulty, because the dead man did not help.

Hurkuzak pulled. The poles bit into the grooves they had already worn in his palms. The pain was not new but the context was — yesterday these hands had pulled a living man. The poles did not know the difference. His mind did, and his mind was the least useful part of him.

The valley continued. The snow was deep and the trees were sparse and the dawn was coming in the slow, reluctant way winter dawns arrive — not as light but as a lessening of dark, a flat gray that showed the landscape without warming it. He pulled through this half-light and the travois scraped and caught and scraped again and his arms burned and his ribs burned and his bitten forearm pulsed with every pull, so regular it replaced the hours.

He pulled for what might have been an hour. The travois caught on roots buried under the snow and on rocks hidden beneath the white surface and each time it caught he had to stop and pull it free and each time he pulled it free the jolt traveled through the frame and the body shifted and the shifting was the movement of something that was not choosing to move.

He stopped and rested and checked the travois. One of Hakiia's arms had fallen off the side and was dragging in the snow. Leaving it to drag felt like something Hakiia would have criticized. He lifted the arm — heavy, stiff, the cold had begun its work on the joints — and laid it across the chest and the hand rested there the way it had rested after the pressure on his wrist had eased. The thumb stump, the missing fingers, the broken ones fixed at their wrong angles, the dark sealed surfaces where the cauterization had done its work. He could not look at that hand for long.

He apologized.

The word came out of him without thought. "Sorry," he said, and the silence that followed was the specific emptiness that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear.

He pulled again. The landscape became a single texture — white, cold, featureless. The trees were bare poles. The sky was a ceiling. The rhythm of the march was the only thing between him and the full arrival of what he had done.

After another hour, the hallucinations began.

They were small. The first was a voice — or the memory of one — that had been part of his life so long its absence was a presence. It said: Lower your shoulder. You are pulling from the arms. Pull from the legs and the back.

He heard it. He almost turned around. Then the voice was gone and there was only the wind and the scraping of the travois and the sound of his own breathing, which was loud and labored and not unlike the wrong breathing Hakiia had made in the hours before the end.

The second hallucination was visual. He looked ahead and saw, through the trees, a shape that was the cabin — the straight lines, the right angles, the suggestion of a roof under snow. He walked toward it for ten minutes before the shape resolved into a rock face with a shelf of ice along its top edge that had caught the light in a way that mimicked walls and windows. The cabin was not there. The cabin was still ahead, in the same indeterminate distance it had always occupied, receding as he approached.

The third was a feeling. A warmth on his left side, the side closest to the travois, as though the body behind him were still radiating heat. It was not. The body was cold.

More came after that. A tree that had not been there and then was. A sound like a voice carried on the wind that resolved, when he listened, into nothing. A moment where the ground seemed to tilt and the snow slid sideways and he braced himself against a fall that was not happening because the ground was level and his mind was not. He felt Hakiia's hand on his shoulder — the rough, corrective grip that had steered him a thousand times, the grip that belonged to a hand that no longer existed — and he turned and there was only the travois and the body and the cold, and the phantom grip dissolved into the wind's pressure on his coat.

These were not visions. They were gaps.

He stopped in the early afternoon, if it was the afternoon — the light had not changed, the gray was the same gray it had been since dawn, and the only reason he believed it was later was that his body was more exhausted than it had been. He sat beside the travois and ate the last strip of dried meat and chewed it mechanically and swallowed it without tasting it.

He looked at Hakiia. The face had changed. The relationship between Hurkuzak and the face had changed. In the hours since the mercy killing, he had looked at the face and seen his brother. Now, after the mechanical repetition of effort that had ground his mind down to its functional minimum, he looked at the face and saw weight. Not only weight. But weight first, and brother second, and the order of those two perceptions had reversed without his permission.

He was carrying weight. He was carrying his brother.

He adjusted the body on the travois. The adjustment was efficient, mechanical — a repositioning of mass for better balance, a straightening of the limbs so they did not catch on the frame. He noticed that his hands moved differently now. Earlier, he had touched Hakiia's body with the care of a man handling something sacred. Now his hands were solving a problem.

He pulled.

The afternoon wore into something that might have been evening. The light did not change — winter light did not change, it simply was and then it was not — but the temperature dropped in the small, measurable increments that meant the sun, wherever it hid behind the gray, was leaving.

He stopped again. He did not build a fire. A fire required hands and focus and material. He had barely the first, none of the second. Searching for the third meant stopping the march, and stopping was something he could not afford.

He spoke to Hakiia.

"You were right," he said. "About the leg. About the cold. About the mathematics of it. You were right about all of it and I knew you were right and I refused because I could not bear to be the one who made it real."

The silence listened. The wind moved through the trees and the snow fell and the travois sat behind him with its burden and the burden did not respond.

"You were wrong about the rest," he said. "You were wrong about father and about me and about what softness means. You were wrong that the only honest love is hard love. You were wrong that seeing is the same as flinching." He paused. His voice was thinning. "But you were right about the leg. And that is what I will carry. Not that I did it. That you were right. That the cruel thing and the kind thing were the same thing, and I still did not want to do it, and I did it anyway, and it does not feel like either cruelty or kindness. It feels like what it was. A killing."

The silence absorbed the words the way the snow absorbed the blood.

He picked up the drag poles. His hands closed around them and the wood was cold and the grooves in his palms had deepened, the dried blood holding the wood in place. He pulled. The valley went on. The trees went on. The snow went on. Behind him, the body of his brother slid through the world on a broken travois, changing categories with every mile — from person to weight to burden — and Hurkuzak pulled because the cabin was ahead, and because stopping meant something he was not yet ready to become.
