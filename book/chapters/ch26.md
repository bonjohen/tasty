# Chapter 26: The Market of Parts

The town was called Merrath. It sat at the junction of three trade roads, large enough that one more face was not a face but a number — the particular anonymity Hurkuzak needed.

He entered from the north road in midmorning and the market was already working. Stalls lined the central street, awnings of stretched hide and cloth shading tables of goods. Haggling, hammering, the clatter of wheels, the complaints of animals.

He moved through it the way the voice had taught him to move through populated spaces: peripheral, unhurried, eyes forward but attention wide, reading the crowd's currents for the gaps that would let him pass without generating friction. The voice offered its usual commentary — that man is armed, the alley on the left has two exits, the garrison presence is light — and Hurkuzak received it the way he received weather.

He noticed the merchant's stall because the smell was wrong.

Not bad — wrong. Salted, dried, rendered flesh, and the smell made his stomach clench because he knew it. The cabin. The pan over the hearth. The specific scent that demon flesh produced when it was cooked.

He stopped. He looked at the parcels. They were neatly wrapped and labeled with a notation system he did not recognize — symbols and numbers, a grading language, the shorthand of a trade that had developed its own vocabulary. The merchant behind the stall was a small man with careful hands who watched Hurkuzak look and said nothing, because the customer either knew what they were looking at or they did not, and the knowing was its own qualification.

Hurkuzak did not buy anything. He walked on. But the stall stayed with him — the smell, the parcels, the notation — and as he moved through the market the stall multiplied. A physician's shop with jars on shelves, the jars containing materials he could not see clearly but could smell through the glass. The labels on the jars used terms that the voice recognized before Hurkuzak did.

Liver extract, rendered. Marrow suspension. Sinew concentrate, dried.

The terms were the terms of a trade, and the trade was the trade of bodies like his, disassembled with the same care he had used in the cabin.

He stood outside the physician's shop and felt the voice supply information in a continuous stream: the preparation methods, the shelf life, the interaction effects when different tissues were consumed in combination. The information arrived without source attribution, as though a library had been opened inside his mind and the catalogue was being browsed by a reader who knew exactly what to look for.

The reader was Hakiia. The knowledge was Hakiia's. How his brother had come to know the flesh trade's workings with this specificity — a hunter, a mountain man, not a merchant or a physician — was a question that lodged and did not leave.

He stood outside the physician's shop and the understanding arrived. Not that demon flesh was consumed — he had known that since the silence in the trading post, since the conversation that was steered away. The understanding was that the consumption was organized. It was an industry.

He walked further. The market's structure became visible once he knew what he was looking at. Most stalls sold ordinary goods — grain and cloth and tools. But woven through the ordinary commerce was a second economy, visible to those who knew the signs and invisible to those who did not. The signs were the smell, first. The notation, second. A quality of discretion that was not secrecy but tact.

A butcher's stall sat at the market's edge, slightly removed from the main traffic, shaded by a canvas awning that was cleaner than its neighbors. He cleaned his blade between cuts with a thoroughness that suggested the residue mattered. He weighed each portion on a scale finer than any mutton seller would own. Each cut was wrapped in treated cloth and marked with the same notation system the preserved-meat merchant had used.

A woman was purchasing from the butcher. She discussed her order the way a person discusses a prescription. This cut was from the upper limb, good for general vitality. That preparation was concentrated marrow, recommended for injuries that would not close on their own. The rendering was done within twelve hours of harvest, which preserved the active properties. She paid without negotiating. The price was established.

Two men stood beside a wagon discussing shipment in the tone that grain merchants used to discuss grain. The numbers were quantities. The descriptors were tissue types: long muscle, dense bone, organ suite. One mentioned freshness and the other mentioned price and the discussion carried the same matter-of-fact specificity that any commodity price carries among people who trade in it daily.

A man at a nearby stall complained about import tariffs on preserved organ shipments from the eastern provinces. The complaint sounded the same whether the product was wool or wheat or the liver of a creature that had been alive and thinking a week ago.

Children ran past the wagon. A woman at the adjacent stall called to them — something about supper, something ordinary. No one in this marketplace was horrified. No one was performing a secret.

The voice stirred. Contempt. Not for the merchants — for the system itself. The contempt of the material for the merchant.

And underneath the contempt, something colder. Hakiia recognized the vocabulary. Not all of it, but enough. The terms for preparations, the grading system, the notation on the physician's jars. Some of it was familiar to the presence inside him in a way that Hurkuzak's own experience could not account for. Hakiia had known something about this system. How much, from what angle, with what intent — those questions had no answers yet. But the familiarity was there.

He had already done what these people paid for. He had consumed his brother completely — every tissue, every organ, every part. The effects they purchased in careful doses, he had inherited in total. The market was a mirror.

The voice reacted with something that was almost laughter — not mirth but recognition. Hakiia had always understood the world in terms of what it could take from you if you were not hard enough to prevent the taking. Now Hurkuzak was standing in a marketplace that had turned the taking into an industry, and the industry had proved Hakiia right: the world did not kill gentle creatures first. It priced them.

Hurkuzak left Merrath in the late afternoon carrying provisions and a new understanding of his own body. He was not merely hunted because he was strange. Every tissue in his body had a market price. The total integration — the strength, the reflexes, the healing, the voice — was what the market's wealthiest buyers had been trying to achieve through crude commercial doses. He was not an anomaly. He was a proof of concept. The parts were merely meat. The integration was power.

Hakiia had known about the rite. Hakiia had known about the preparations. Hakiia had led them to the cabin with a purpose that the dream had hinted at — a certainty that organized everything else. If the flesh economy was this organized, this wealthy in its upper tiers, then the people at the top would not merely wait for the rite to occur naturally. They would commission it. They would recruit participants. They would find the right demons, the right pairing, the right circumstances.

They would find a pair of brothers traveling to a remote cabin in winter.

He did not follow the thought to its end. The reconciliation was too fragile and the love he had just reopened too new. But the thought existed now, and it would not go away.

He walked east. The voice was quiet, but the quiet was not empty.

Hakiia had known. Not everything. But something.
