# Chapter 25: Two Sets of Eyes

He woke from a dream that had belonged to someone else.

The details were precise and wrong. He was standing in a place he had never been — a high meadow with the tree line far below and the sky wide and clear and the wind carrying the smell of ice and stone. His body was larger than his body. His hands, when he looked at them, were broader, the knuckles thicker, the palms scarred from work he had never done. He was looking at the landscape the way a man looks at a problem he intends to solve — and the whole of his attention was organized around a single question: how do I get there from here?

The question was so characteristically Hakiia that waking from it felt like waking from a visit.

He lay on the ground where he had slept — no fire, a cold camp, the spring night mild enough that the blanket was sufficient — and waited for the presence to settle. The waiting had become a practice. Each morning now the boundary between his thoughts and the other thoughts took longer to reestablish, and each day the border was less clear.

The voice had not spoken during the dream. But it had been present — as the quality of attention, as the certainty that permeated every detail. The voice was the dream. A way of expressing what could not be compressed into terse corrections.

He moved through the day with the new awareness that the reconciliation of the previous days had produced. Not peace. Proximity — a closeness to the presence inside him that he had stopped fighting and had not yet accepted. He did not know what to do with the space between those two states except to watch.

The voice was more continuous now. Not louder — more frequent, and more various. It reacted to the world with emotions that were not his. A well-built stone wall along the road produced a flush of approval that settled in him like warmth — someone else's standards, someone else's eye. A poorly maintained road produced irritation out of proportion to the inconvenience, a tightening in his jaw that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with a man who had never forgiven laziness in any form. A bird of prey circling above the road produced a warmth in his chest — admiration, focused and predatory, that he recognized as belonging to someone who understood killing as craft.

He had stopped pretending these were his responses. The privacy of his own mind was no longer entirely private. The corrections, the dreams, the physical capabilities, the combat reflexes — all Hakiia, transferred through the consumption and integrated at a level deeper than conscious access could reach.

He moved through the morning with this knowledge, and the knowledge changed everything. When the voice directed him to take a higher path along a ridge, it was not an anonymous instinct. It was Hakiia, telling his younger brother where to walk, the way Hakiia had always told his younger brother where to walk, with the same certainty and the same contempt for hesitation. The dynamic was the dynamic. It had survived death and consumption. Hakiia corrected. Hurkuzak followed. The choreography was ancient and it was theirs.

He did not say the name aloud. Not yet. The saying aloud would make it irreversible — public, even if the only audience was the trees and the road and the spring air. He was not ready for irreversible.

He thought about the dream's purpose — the fixed point the dreaming mind had been moving toward. The certainty of it was Hakiia's. Arguing with Hakiia about direction was like arguing with water about the way downhill.

What had the destination been? The cabin? Hakiia had known the route. Hakiia had led them there. The dream's perspective — standing in a high meadow, planning a journey he intended to make — was consistent with the trip that became the place where the brother became the meal.

The thought circled something he could not yet see. He let it circle.

He passed through a camp midday — not a settlement but a temporary gathering of travelers sharing a fire. He bought bread from a woman who baked it in the coals. The bread was warm and tasted of grain and ash — the first food in weeks that tasted of anything at all. He ate slowly. The flavor surprised him, and the surprise itself was a recovery, small and specific, of something the cabin had taken.

He handled the interaction competently. The competence was not entirely his. He saw the tension in the woman's shoulders ease when he kept his distance. He saw her hand move from the knife at her belt to the cloth over the bread. He chose the right words — measured, ordinary, designed to dissolve from memory the moment he walked away.

He noticed himself doing this and the noticing was the strangeness: acting and being acted through, choosing and being chosen for.

He noticed, as he left the camp, that the woman watched him go with an expression he had seen before — the slightly unsettled look of someone who has spoken with a thing that passed for normal but did not quite fit the passing. She did not look afraid. She looked uncertain.

The afternoon was quiet. He walked through woodland that was coming fully into leaf, the canopy closing overhead, the light filtering green and gold. He stopped in a clearing where the sun warmed the ground, sat with his back against a tree. Sun on his face. Wind in the new leaves. The presence inside him, steady, permanent.

"Hakiia," he said.

He said the name once. Quietly, without inflection — acknowledgment, not address. He did not expect a response. He did not receive one in words. But something shifted. A focusing of the presence, as though the name had been a call that turned something's head. The presence did not speak. But it listened.

He sat with the name in the air, the presence in his mind, the spring around him — and the grief that was not only guilt anymore. The grief had changed shape. It was no longer the locked room of the cabin. It had become something with weather in it, something that contained the killing and the eating and the love and the damage and did not try to separate them.

The name, spoken into the quiet of a spring afternoon, was the first honest thing he had said since the night he said yes to the knife.

He was something harder than peace or healing or resolution: he was carrying his brother with open eyes. Acceptance without absolution. Love without excuse. The person inside him was exactly what he had always been, and what he had always been could not be held in a single judgment.

He thought about the dream again. The thought circled closer this time and almost touched the thing it was circling — a question he had not yet asked: if Hakiia had planned the journey to the cabin with such certainty, what had the purpose been? The trip had been presented as a hardening exercise. But the dream's purpose had felt deeper than that. The feeling was a seed. He did not know what it would grow into. He would.

He stood. He walked east. The name stayed in the air behind him and the presence walked with him — two beings in one body, one alive and one dead, both real. The spring did not care about either of them. It went on, and they went with it.
