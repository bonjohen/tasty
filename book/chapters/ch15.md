# Chapter 15: The Body Remembers

He reached for the axe and his hand arrived before he expected it.

The motion was small — a stretch from the sleeping platform to the wall where the hand axe hung on its peg. The distance was familiar. This time the hand arrived early. Not by much. A fraction of a breath. But the hand he had carried his entire life did not move that way.

He held the axe and looked at his hand on the handle. The grip was different. Not in its position — he was holding the tool the same way he had always held it. The difference was underneath — in the density of the fingers, the way the hand closed around the wood with a sureness that was not his. He had never held anything that way. Someone else had.

He put the axe down and did not think about it.

But the wrongnesses continued.

He stood that afternoon and the cabin felt smaller. His body was occupying the space differently. He moved through the room and his shoulders found the distances between objects the way a large animal moves through its den — automatic, mapped below thought. He had banged his hip on the table for weeks. Now the miscalculations had stopped.

He went outside. The snow had compacted in the weeks since the last storm, and the surface held his weight in ways it had not held it before. He walked to the tree line and the walking was easy in a way it should not have been. His legs carried him with a steadiness that felt borrowed. His breathing was controlled. The cold reached him and he acknowledged it and it did not reach further.

He tested himself. Not deliberately — the testing came from curiosity that he could not suppress, a need to know where the edges of the new capacity lay. He found a fallen tree, half-buried in the snow, and he gripped the trunk and lifted. It came up — not with Hakiia's casual power, but with a strength his arms should not have contained. The trunk was thick and wet with freeze and should have been beyond him. The strength that lifted it was specific. It mapped to a body he knew.

The strength was Hakiia's kind — dense, explosive, built for application rather than endurance. None of it had been his before.

He set the trunk down and stood in the snow and listened. The forest was making its winter sounds — the creak of branches under ice, the settling of snow, the small movements of unseen things in the underbrush. He could hear them separately. That bird in the pine, forty paces to the right, adjusting its grip on a branch. That sound below the snow, closer, the tunneling of something small and warm through the compacted layer. The wind shifting direction at the top of the granite face, carrying a scent — stone, ice, the absence of smoke, the absence of anything living at that altitude.

He had never heard the world this way. Hakiia had. Hakiia had moved through the wilderness hearing everything — each sound separate, each sound placed. Hurkuzak had walked through the same landscapes hearing only noise.

He reached inside himself and found someone else's hand already there.

He recoiled from the knowledge. Not from the changes — those kept him alive. He had eaten his brother. Now his body was becoming his brother's body. The line between those two facts was straight and bright and he did not want to see it.

He went back inside. He built up the fire. He sat on the platform and stared at the flames. The looking would come later.

He ate that evening. The alcove was nearly empty — what remained of Hakiia was bone, sinew, and the parts that required more preparation than a simple cut. The meals had become sparser, the portions smaller. His body had begun to register the approaching end — not hunger yet, but the knowledge of hunger coming. The balance held. It was shifting. Whatever the cabin winter had made him, it would need to sustain itself on something other than what was in the alcove, and soon.

That night, the correction came.

He was banking the fire for sleep — pushing the coals together, laying a larger piece of wood across them to burn slow through the dark hours. His hands placed the wood in the usual pattern. Adequate. Wrong.

Not there. At the back. Angle it so the draft feeds the base, not the top. You are wasting half your heat.

The thought arrived with a texture he knew. He had lived beneath it for his entire life.

He adjusted the wood. He angled it toward the back of the firebox and positioned it so the gap beneath caught the draft from the gap in the wall. The fire responded immediately — the draft pulled clean, the flame steadied, and the heat it gave changed from a broad, unfocused warmth to something directed and steady. The correction had been right.

He sat back and looked at the fire. It was the kind of thing he could have figured out himself. He had built fires every day.

He told himself this. The telling was adequate.

More corrections followed. They came over the next days in the same register — terse, tactical, practical. Move the water pot to the left side of the hearth where the stone retains heat longer. Check the roof beam over the door; the snow weight has shifted and the wood is under stress. When he went outside: walk on the compacted surface, not the fresh — less noise, less effort, less trace. When he slept: face the door, not the wall.

Sleep facing the door. That one arrived at night — a flat imperative, no justification. The justification came a moment later as his own thought, and it was so basic he should have known it already.

The explanation held.

But the corrections had a quality — a texture of thought different from his own. His thoughts wandered and circled. These arrived straight — conclusions without the preceding argument. They were the way Hakiia thought.

He was not hearing a voice. He was hearing himself, changed. The person who had killed and consumed and survived was not the person who had fumbled the elk shot.

He told himself this. The telling held. Mostly.

The last night of the chapter of his life that he would later think of as the cabin winter, he lay on the platform with the fire banked properly, the draft feeding the base, the heat directed and steady. The cabin was warmer than it had been on any previous night. Every correction had built on the last.

The corrections had gone quiet. Not absent — present but silent. The difference between an empty room and one with someone in it who has chosen not to speak.

He lay in the dark and felt the silence and the silence felt like watching.

This was the silence of something that had settled into the same space he occupied. It was the patience of something that was already there and not going away.

Hurkuzak closed his eyes. His body was warm. His body was strong. His body was becoming something he did not understand, and the shape of the not-understanding was familiar, and the familiar thing had a name, and the name was behind the alcove door and also behind his own eyes.

He slept. The fire burned. The cabin held its heat. And the silence continued its work — not quite empty.
