# Chapter 35: What the Net Holds

The compound looked like refuge and that was the first lie.

Hurkuzak saw it from the road in the late afternoon — stone walls, a timber gate standing open, the smoke of a cooking fire rising from within. A trading compound of the kind that sat along every major road in the eastern territories: a walled enclosure where merchants and travelers stopped to water animals, trade news, and sleep behind something stronger than a tent. He had passed similar compounds since the cabin. He had avoided some and used others and the using had been unremarkable.

The voice assessed the compound before Hurkuzak had crossed the field between the road and the gate. Terrain, cover, exits. The compound had stone walls high enough to discourage climbing, a single gated entrance wide enough for a wagon, and interior buildings that blocked sight lines from the gate to the far wall. The voice filed the details and offered no objection. Everything ordinary.

That was the second lie. The ordinary was the trap.

He entered through the open gate into a visible courtyard. A well, a water trough, hitching posts, and the quiet evidence of recent occupation. Packs against a wall. A cart with its tongue resting on the ground. The cooking fire was in a pit near the center, tended by no one, burning with the low steady heat of a fire that had been maintained for hours.

The men were in the buildings. He did not see them when he entered. He saw them later, after the gate closed, and the seeing was already too late.

The gate mechanism was quiet. Well-maintained — the hinges oiled, the crossbar mounted on a track that allowed it to slide into position with a single motion. A low, heavy thud. The options he thought he carried had been closed days ago. Weeks ago. Shaped by someone who understood how hunted men move and had designed the ground to funnel this particular hunted man to this particular place.

The voice screamed.

Not a word. A wave of compressed attention that slammed against the inside of Hurkuzak's skull. The pattern snapping into focus the way a face snaps into focus when you realize the stranger across the room is someone you know. The drive. The funnel. The systematic closing of options that had felt like chance and terrain but was the work of someone who had shaped the ground to move him here.

Hurkuzak turned toward the gate. The gate was barred. The first man was already moving.

He came from the building on the left — fast, professional, carrying a small ceramic vial in his right hand. A second man emerged from the building on the right. A third dropped from the wall itself, landing in a crouch near the well, blocking the courtyard's center.

The voice took over. The transition was not gradual this time — not the slow bleed of instinct into action that Hurkuzak had felt during the bandit fight or the public eruption. This was immediate, total, the way a rider seizes the reins when the hand that holds them goes slack. Hurkuzak's body moved with Hakiia's precision, Hakiia's economy of violence.

He hit the first man before the vial could be opened. The strike was to the wrist — fast, accurate, the kind of targeted violence that disarms without killing because killing costs time he did not have. The vial flew from the man's hand and shattered against the courtyard stones. The liquid spread in a dark stain and the smell that rose from it was chemical and sweet and wrong: a sedation draught, concentrated, designed for a body stronger than a normal man's.

They were not trying to kill him. The understanding arrived with the second man's charge. An opponent trying to capture is constrained in ways a killer is not.

He moved through the second man the way water moves through a gap — not around but through, using the man's forward momentum against him, redirecting the charge into the stone wall with a sound that was half impact and half breath leaving a body. The second vial broke in the man's pocket and the sedation soaked through his clothes and the man's legs buckled as his own weapon did to him what it had been meant to do to Hurkuzak.

Two down. The third was different. Patient, positioned, waiting with the stillness of someone who had been told to wait. He held his vial in his left hand and a short blade in his right. The blade was for cutting, for opening skin so the sedation could enter the blood directly. Whoever built this trap knew what Hurkuzak was and what it would take to bring him down.

The fight with the third man was longer. The man was trained — not at the level the voice brought to combat, but trained enough to survive the first exchange and the second and to keep the blade viable. Hurkuzak — Hakiia — drove him back across the courtyard with strikes that were efficient and frightening and that left bruises and cuts on the man's arms and torso without finishing him. Every time Hurkuzak committed to the killing stroke the blade came up, angled toward exposed skin, the vial ready. He could not afford the opening.

The crossbow bolt hit him from above.

A line of fire across his right shoulder — a placement shot, delivered from the wall. The pain did not stop him. But it pulled his attention upward for a fraction of a second, and the fraction was enough.

The third man closed the distance. The blade opened a cut across Hurkuzak's forearm — shallow, precise — and the vial followed, the liquid poured into the wound, and what entered his blood was wrong.

The sedation hit him like a tide. Not a wall — a rising. His legs did not buckle at once. His vision did not go dark at once. The first dose was in his blood. The second was in the courtyard stones, wasted. The third was on the man he had driven into the wall. But the first was enough to begin it — a distance opening between his intention and his body's response.

He hit the third man. The hit was slower than it should have been. He hit him again. The man went down. Hurkuzak turned toward the wall where the bolt had come from. The turning took too long. The chemical was spreading through his blood with the patience of something designed for exactly this.

A figure on the wall. A woman. The crossbow reloaded, aimed, lowered — not fired again, because the firing was no longer necessary. She watched from the wall, evaluating the progress of her design. Below her, Hurkuzak's body was beginning to disagree with his mind about whether to keep fighting or to stop.

Vark. He knew her the way prey knows its predator — not by name but by the shape of the threat. The bolt in his thigh. The blood she had collected. The professional patience that had brought him here, to this courtyard, to this mechanism built by a woman who understood how to absorb individual strength into a system.

He went to his knees. The sedation had reached his legs. What waited at the end was not death but something else — containment, transport, delivery. He was being shipped. He was product entering the supply chain. He was meat with a consciousness, a specimen with a value, and the value was why they had sedated him rather than killed him. Somewhere at the end of the chain was a man — the patron — and the chain was pulling him in.

The voice fought. Inside the chemical fog closing around his mind, the voice raged with a fury that was entirely Hakiia's — the fury of a man who had never submitted to a force he did not choose. The rage was useless against the chemistry. The voice could not metabolize the sedation. The voice could not override the body's mechanical failure. But the voice could refuse to stop, and the refusal was a sound inside Hurkuzak's dimming mind, a pressure against the chemical darkness. Hakiia had never surrendered. Not even when the dying was his own choice.

Hands on him. The treated cord around his wrists — he felt the chemical suppression enter through his skin, the muscular contraction in his arms reducing, his grip loosening from iron to something merely strong and then from strong to human and then from human to something less. More cord around his ankles. The restraint was methodical — the work of people who had done this before, who knew the sequence: sedate, then restrain while the sedation held, then reinforce before the body began to burn the draught away.

His vision was narrowing. The courtyard was becoming smaller, the walls closing in, the sky above contracting to a point of light that was receding. The woman on the wall was still watching. She had not descended. She had not needed to. Her mechanism was performing as designed.

The voice spoke.

Not as pressure. Not as instinct. Not as surfacing memory or emotional reaction or the wordless certainties that had been crossing the boundary with increasing frequency. The voice spoke as a voice. As words. As Hakiia's — the particular cadence, the clipped authority, the tone of a man addressing another man in a room they both occupied.

Hold on.

Two words. Spoken the way one person speaks to another.

The barrier had broken. The sedation, the capture, the rage — all of it had pushed the Bleed past its final threshold. What stood on the other side was Hakiia. Speaking. Present. No longer a voice but a self, trapped inside the same body being tied and sedated and loaded into a supply chain that would carry them both to the man who had designed this. The man who did not know that what he was receiving was not one consciousness but two.

Hold on.

Hurkuzak held. The darkness came in and the holding continued and the two words were the last thing he heard before the sedation won. Hakiia was still there. Still fighting. The trap had been built for a body. What it held was a body and a brother, and the brother was not going to stop.
