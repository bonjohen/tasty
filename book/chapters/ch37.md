# Chapter 37: The Prince's Table

The door opened and the man who entered was not what the architecture had prepared Hurkuzak to expect.

The stronghold was a machine — brutal in purpose, refined in execution, built to process bodies with the impersonal thoroughness of a slaughterhouse that had never needed to name what it slaughtered. But the man who owned the machine was gentle in his movements, unhurried, carrying himself with the ease of someone who has never needed to rush. He was middle-aged, lean-faced, with dark eyes that moved across the room the way hands move across familiar surfaces — touching everything, lingering on nothing. His clothing was tailored and clean, the kind of fabric that communicated wealth by fitting as though it had been grown onto the body rather than draped over it. He was not tall. He was not imposing. He looked like a scholar with resources and a collector with taste.

He carried two plates from the sideboard to the table with his own hands. He placed one at the head and one before Hurkuzak, and the placing was courteous, domestic — the merchant prince serving his prisoner the way a host serves a guest. The plates held food that Hurkuzak's enhanced senses identified before his eyes could process the presentation: demon flesh. Thin cuts arranged in overlapping layers, dark at the center and seared pale at the edges, dressed with a reduction that smelled of juniper and char and something underneath both that Hurkuzak's body recognized before his mind did. The preparation was extraordinary. A meal made for pleasure, not strength.

He sat at the head of the table. He looked at Hurkuzak the way a man who has spent years searching for a particular piece of art looks at the piece when he finally finds it.

"You are remarkable," he said. His voice was warm and educated. "I want you to understand that I mean that without reservation. What you carry is something I have spent a significant portion of my life trying to create, and the fact that you created it through means I could not have designed is part of what makes it remarkable."

He paused.

"My name is Orath Tessivane," he said. "I believe you have found some of my correspondence."

The name landed with the weight of accumulated knowledge — the fragments from the wreck, the methods, the word vessel, the patron who funded experiments, the system-builder who sat above the flesh economy and directed its highest purposes. The name converted the architecture into a person. The person was sitting across the table from Hurkuzak and looking at him with genuine curiosity, and the curiosity was more frightening than any threat because it meant Tessivane did not see a prisoner to be broken. He saw a phenomenon to be understood.

The voice erupted.

Not as words — not yet, not here, not in front of this man. The eruption was physical: Hurkuzak's jaw clenched, his pulse climbed, his hands closed into fists inside the restraints with a force that tested the chemical suppression. The reaction was Hakiia's. Hakiia had known this man. Had sat in a room with this man. Had listened to this man explain the design and the purpose and the expected outcome and had accepted it all because the acceptance had seemed like strength and the strength had seemed like survival and the survival had been the only thing Hakiia had ever understood as love.

Tessivane noticed the reaction the way a scholar notices a result. "Ah," he said. "Both of you."

He leaned forward slightly. "Your hands clenched, but the pattern was his, not yours. His reactions move through your body without your direction." He sat back. "Extraordinary."

"You designed this," Hurkuzak said. His voice was rough from disuse and sedation. "The trip. The cabin. The materials Hakiia carried. You designed all of it."

Tessivane did not deny it. He did not hesitate. He simply nodded, the way a man nods when a colleague states an established fact. "I designed the method, yes. Your brother volunteered. The distinction matters to me, if not to you."

"He volunteered to kill me."

"He volunteered to participate in a process that, had it succeeded as designed, would have made him something unprecedented. He understood the costs. He accepted them." His voice carried no apology and no cruelty. "The design called for the stronger sibling to survive. Your brother was the stronger sibling. He was meant to endure. You were meant to contribute."

Contribute. The word sat in the room the way the word vessel had sat on the page — a euphemism so precise that the precision was its own cruelty. Contribute. Die. Be eaten. Become material.

"What interests me," Tessivane continued, "is that the method failed and the failure produced a superior result. You were the weaker brother. You survived. You consumed. And the integration you carry is more complete, more stable, and more persistent than anything my attempts achieved." He looked at Hurkuzak. "I have been studying how demon flesh passes from the consumed into the consumer for twenty-three years. I have funded forty-one trials. None of them produced what you produced. Do you understand how significant that makes you?"

"I understand what you did to my family."

"I offered your brother an opportunity. He took it. The world he feared — the world that kills gentle creatures, as he put it — that world is real. Your brother believed the rite was the answer. I provided the means." He gestured at the food on Hurkuzak's plate. "Please eat. It is not your kind, if that concerns you. Mountain demon, from the eastern range. The preparation is excellent."

Hurkuzak did not touch the plate. The refusal was not principled. It was physical — the idea of eating demon flesh prepared as cuisine in a room built for the celebration of consumption while restrained in a chair by the man who had designed his brother's death. His stomach closed against it.

Tessivane accepted the refusal without comment, the way a courteous host accepts any guest's preference. He picked up his own utensil and began to eat. The eating was casual, practiced. "What I did not provide, and what I now need to understand, is what happened differently. The required conditions were met — isolation, duration, total consumption. The condition that differed was the nature of the bond. Your brother's death was willing. His consent was explicit. The consumption occurred in a context of grief, love, and prolonged intimate contact with the dead. These are not elements I can manufacture through method."

"How many?" Hurkuzak asked.

Tessivane looked up. The question had interrupted his thought and the interruption surprised him.

"How many brothers. How many sisters. How many pairs did you send to cabins."

Tessivane considered the question. He did not evade it. "Forty-one trials over twenty-three years. Not all were sibling pairs — some were parent-child, some were twins, some involved cousins. The sibling pairs produced the most promising results. Yours was the forty-second."

Forty-two. The mathematics of appetite: eighty-four lives fed the work.

"Most did not survive the joining," Tessivane added, as though the clarification were helpful. "Some lost themselves entirely — the stronger presence swallowed the weaker and left a broken thing behind. Some became composites that could not hold together. Some simply collapsed. The ways to fail are numerous. That is the nature of research." He ate another bite. He chewed thoughtfully. "Love," he said. "Your brother's love for you, your love for him. The rite's working was activated not by dominance but by attachment. The transfer went deeper because you consumed a person you loved, not a person you overpowered. That is the element I have been unable to produce." His eyes had not left Hurkuzak's face. "I intend to understand it. I intend to learn how two selves come to share one body and one mind. I intend to build a method from that understanding. And from the method, a way of producing the same result."

The voice pushed against the inside of Hurkuzak's skull with a fury that was entirely Hakiia's — the fury of hearing his own worst act described as a finding, his own monstrous choice analyzed as a condition, the love he had twisted into violence reduced to a variable in a method that a man at a table planned to optimize while eating the flesh of beings like both of them.

"You want to industrialize it," Hurkuzak said.

Tessivane heard it. The dual quality — observation and contempt braided into a single sentence from a single mouth. He set down his utensil. "Yes," he said. "That is exactly what I intend. And the fact that I could hear both of you in that sentence is the most compelling evidence I have ever received that the integration is real, that it is stable, and that it is the most significant development in the history of demon physiology."

"You will fail," Hurkuzak said. "You cannot replicate what happened in the cabin. You cannot manufacture grief. You cannot produce consent through method. The thing that made the transfer work is the thing your system is designed to eliminate."

Tessivane's eyes narrowed — not in anger, but in the recalculation of a man revising his assessment upward. "That," he said, "is a more sophisticated objection than I expected. The secondary presence is contributing to your analysis. Fascinating." He paused. "You may be right that direct replication is impossible. But understanding the working is sufficient. Once I understand why love and grief deepen the transfer, I can learn what the body does under attachment and find another way to make the body do the same. The grief is the condition. What the body does under grief is the process. I do not need to manufacture love. I need its consequence in the flesh."

He stood. He was not threatened. He was not disturbed. He was excited, and the excitement was contained — a man whose lifetime of work had just been vindicated by the thing sitting in his chair.

"You will be housed here," he said. "You will not be harmed. The integration must remain intact for my work to proceed. Your body is the archive. Damage the archive, damage the knowledge." He moved toward the door. "We will speak again. There is much to discuss."

He left. The door closed. The guards remained at their positions. The candles continued to burn. The food on Hurkuzak's plate cooled, untouched — demon flesh refined into cuisine, sitting before a man who had once consumed his brother raw in a cabin in winter.

The voice spoke.

I sat where you are sitting. He said the same things. I believed him.

Hurkuzak sat in the prince's chair in the prince's room in the prince's stronghold and carried his brother inside him. The brother was speaking. The speaking was the weapon the prince did not know he had finished building. The prince wanted to map the integration. He did not understand that the two selves had been coexisting toward this moment since the cabin, since the wreck, since the correspondence, since the word hold on crossed the barrier between them and made the barrier rubble.

The only way out of this place ran through its heart. Hurkuzak looked at the room — the table, the candles, the tapestries, the architecture of appetite that surrounded him — and began, with the brother inside him, to think about how to break it.
