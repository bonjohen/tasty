# Chapter 37: The Prince's Table

The door opened and the man who entered was not what the architecture had prepared Hurkuzak to expect.

The stronghold was a machine — brutal in purpose, refined in execution, built to process bodies with the impersonal efficiency of industry. The man who owned the machine should have matched it: hard, cold, the physical expression of a system that turned beings into product. Instead, the man who entered was gentle in his movements, unhurried, carrying himself with the particular ease of someone who has never needed to rush because the world has always arranged itself around his schedule. He was middle-aged. His hair was dark and well-kept. His face was lean and alert, the face of a man who paid attention to things because paying attention was a pleasure rather than a necessity. His clothing was excellent — tailored, clean, the kind of clothing that communicated wealth not through ostentation but through the quiet authority of fabric that fit because it had been made to fit. He was not tall. He was not imposing. He looked like what he was: a scholar with resources, a collector with taste, a man whose appetites had been educated into sophistication and whose sophistication had been funded into empire.

He carried two plates from the sideboard to the table with his own hands. He placed one at the head and one before Hurkuzak and the placing was courteous, domestic, the gesture of a host who takes pleasure in serving because the serving is a form of intimacy with the meal. The plates held food that Hurkuzak's enhanced senses identified before his eyes could fully process the presentation: demon flesh, prepared with extraordinary care, rendered into a cuisine so refined that the brutality of the source material had been completely absorbed into the artistry of the preparation. The smell was rich, complex, layered with spices and techniques that elevated the base material into something a person could consume not merely for strength but for pleasure.

The man sat at the head of the table. He looked at Hurkuzak with an expression that was not predatory but appreciative, the way a man who has spent years searching for a particular piece of art looks at the piece when he finally finds it — not with greed but with the satisfaction of confirmation. The search had been real. The piece was real. The satisfaction was earned.

"You are remarkable," he said. His voice was warm and educated, carrying the cadence of a man who had spoken to many people about many things and had found most of those conversations interesting. "I want you to understand that I mean that without reservation. What you carry is something I have spent a significant portion of my life trying to create, and the fact that you created it through means I could not have designed is part of what makes it remarkable."

He paused. The pause was not dramatic. It was the pause of a man who was organizing his thoughts because his thoughts deserved organization.

"My name is Orath Tessivane," he said. "I believe you have found some of my correspondence."

The name landed with the weight of accumulated knowledge — the fragments from the wreck, the protocols, the word vessel, the patron who funded experiments, the system-builder who sat above the flesh economy and directed its highest purposes. The name converted the architecture into a person and the person was sitting across the table from Hurkuzak and looking at him with genuine curiosity and the curiosity was more frightening than any threat because the curiosity was real and the realness meant that Tessivane did not see Hurkuzak as a prisoner to be broken but as a phenomenon to be understood and the understanding was what made him dangerous.

The voice erupted.

Not as words — not yet, not here, not in front of this man. The eruption was physical: Hurkuzak's jaw clenched, his pulse climbed, his hands closed into fists inside the restraints with a force that tested the chemical suppression. The reaction was Hakiia's. The reaction was the rage and shame and recognition of a man confronting the person who had recruited him and used him and designed the experiment that had sent him into the mountains to kill his own brother. Hakiia had known this man. Had sat in a room with this man. Had listened to this man explain the protocol and the purpose and the expected outcome and had accepted the explanation and the purpose and the expectation because the acceptance had seemed like strength and the strength had seemed like survival and the survival had been the only thing Hakiia had ever understood as love.

Tessivane noticed the reaction. He noticed it the way a scientist notices a result — with the careful attention of someone for whom observation is a professional skill. "Ah," he said. "Both of you."

He leaned forward slightly. "That is what I was hoping to see. The dual response. Your hands clenched, but the clenching was not yours — the pattern was different from your baseline physiological stress response. It was his. The integration is deep enough that his emotional reactions produce physical manifestation through your body without your conscious direction." He sat back. "Extraordinary."

"You designed this," Hurkuzak said. His voice was rough from disuse and sedation and the particular hoarseness of a man who has not spoken aloud in days because the person he has been speaking to lives inside his skull. "The trip. The cabin. The materials Hakiia carried. You designed all of it."

Tessivane did not deny it. He did not hesitate. He simply nodded, the way a man nods when a colleague states an established fact that requires no debate. "I designed the experimental framework, yes. Your brother volunteered. The distinction matters to me, if not to you."

"He volunteered to kill me."

"He volunteered to participate in a process that, had it succeeded as designed, would have made him something unprecedented. He understood the costs. He accepted them." Tessivane's voice carried no apology and no cruelty. It carried the specific tone of a man who had thought carefully about the ethics of his work and had arrived at conclusions he was willing to defend. "The protocol specified the stronger sibling as the consumer. Your brother was the stronger sibling. He was meant to survive. You were meant to contribute."

Contribute. The word sat in the room the way the word vessel had sat on the page — a euphemism so precise that the precision was its own cruelty. Contribute. Die. Be eaten. Become material.

"What interests me," Tessivane continued, "is that the protocol failed and the failure produced a superior result. You were the weaker brother. You survived. You consumed. And the integration you carry is more complete, more stable, and more persistent than anything my controlled experiments achieved." He looked at Hurkuzak with the same appreciative attention he had shown when he entered. "I have been studying the mechanism of demon-flesh transfer for twenty-three years. I have funded forty-one controlled trials. None of them produced what you produced. Do you understand how significant that makes you?"

"I understand what you did to my family."

"I offered your brother an opportunity. He took it. The world he feared — the world that kills gentle creatures, as he put it — that world is real. Your brother believed the rite was the answer. I provided the means." He gestured at the food on Hurkuzak's plate. "Please eat. It is not your kind, if that concerns you. Mountain demon, from the eastern range. The preparation is excellent."

Hurkuzak did not touch the plate. The refusal was not principled. It was physical — the idea of eating demon flesh prepared as cuisine in a room built for the celebration of consumption while restrained in a chair by the man who had designed his brother's death produced a revulsion that went beyond the moral and into the cellular.

Tessivane accepted the refusal without comment, the way a courteous host accepts any guest's preference. He picked up his own utensil and began to eat. The eating was casual, practiced, the gesture of a man for whom demon flesh was a daily meal and the daily meal was a communion with his own philosophy. "What I did not provide, and what I now need to understand, is what happened differently. The clinical variables were met — isolation, duration, total consumption. The variable that differed was the emotional architecture. Your brother's death was willing. His consent was explicit. The consumption occurred in a context of grief, love, and prolonged intimate contact with the dead. These are not variables I can replicate through protocol."

"How many?" Hurkuzak asked.

Tessivane looked up. The question had interrupted his thought and the interruption was noted with the mild surprise of a man who was not accustomed to being interrupted.

"How many brothers. How many sisters. How many pairs did you send to cabins."

Tessivane considered the question. He did not evade it. "Forty-one controlled trials over twenty-three years. Not all were sibling pairs — some were parent-child, some were twins, some involved cousins. The sibling pairs produced the most promising results. Yours was the forty-second."

Forty-two. The number sat in the room like an object. Forty-two pairs of people sent to remote locations with sealed compartments and protocols and the expectation that one would kill and consume the other. The mathematics of appetite: eighty-four lives entered the machine, and however many survived had survived as something other than what they were when they entered, and the otherness was what the man at the head of the table studied and catalogued and tried to perfect.

"Most did not survive the integration," Tessivane added, as though the clarification were helpful. "The transfer mechanism is aggressive. Personality erosion, composite instability, cognitive degradation — the failure modes are numerous. I lost more than I gained. That is the nature of research." He ate another bite. He chewed thoughtfully. "Love," he said. "Your brother's love for you, your love for him. The rite's mechanism was activated not by dominance but by attachment. The transfer went deeper because you consumed a person you loved, not a person you overpowered. That is the variable I have been unable to produce." He looked at Hurkuzak and the looking was the looking of a man who has identified a problem and is already thinking about solutions. "I intend to understand it. I intend to map the architecture of your integration — how the two of you coexist, how memory surfaces, how instinct operates across the boundary, how will and identity interact. I intend to produce a model. And from the model, I intend to develop a method that replicates the result."

The voice pushed against the inside of Hurkuzak's skull with a fury that was entirely Hakiia's — the fury of hearing his own worst act described as a data point, his own monstrous choice analyzed as a variable, the love he had twisted into violence reduced to a parameter in a model that a man at a table planned to optimize while eating the flesh of beings like both of them.

"You want to industrialize it," Hurkuzak said. The words were his and not his — the observation was Hurkuzak's, the contempt was Hakiia's, and the two carried the sentence together with the particular weight of a statement made by two people speaking through one mouth.

Tessivane heard it. The dual quality. He set down his utensil and looked at Hurkuzak with an attention that was no longer merely scientific but was the attention of a man who has just seen something that confirms his most ambitious hypothesis. "Yes," he said. "That is exactly what I intend. And the fact that you said that — the fact that the observation was yours and the contempt was his and I could hear both — is the most compelling evidence I have ever received that the integration is real, that it is stable, and that it is the most significant development in the history of demon physiology."

"You will fail," Hurkuzak said. The voice carried the weight of both of them — Hurkuzak's certainty, Hakiia's knowledge. "You cannot replicate what happened in the cabin. You cannot manufacture grief. You cannot produce consent through protocol. The thing that made the transfer work is the thing your system is designed to eliminate."

Tessivane regarded him. The regard was careful, thorough, the attention of a man who was accustomed to assessing the intelligence of his opponents and had just revised his assessment upward. "That," he said, "is a more sophisticated objection than I expected. The secondary presence is contributing to your analysis. Fascinating." He paused. "You may be right that direct replication is impossible. But understanding the mechanism is sufficient. Once I understand why love and grief deepen the transfer, I can identify the physiological pathways they activate and find alternative means of activating them. The emotion is the trigger. The biochemistry is the mechanism. I do not need the emotion. I need the biochemistry."

He stood. He was not threatened. He was not disturbed. He was excited in the contained, disciplined way that a scholar is excited when a lifetime of work is suddenly vindicated. He looked at Hurkuzak the way the collector looked at the acquisition: with the knowledge that what he possessed exceeded his original expectations and that the exceeding was a gift and the gift would be studied and understood and replicated until the singular became the systematic.

"You will be housed here," he said. "You will not be harmed. The integration must remain intact for my work to proceed. Your body is the archive. Damage the archive, damage the knowledge." He moved toward the door. "We will speak again. There is much to discuss."

He left. The door closed. The guards remained at their positions. The candles continued to burn. The food on Hurkuzak's plate cooled, untouched, the refined preparation of demon flesh sitting before a man who had once consumed his brother in a cabin in winter and was now sitting in the cabin's civilizational mirror — the place where the private taboo had been turned into a public institution and the institution had been turned into a philosophy and the philosophy was being articulated by a man who ate people with the mannered ease of a connoisseur.

The voice spoke. Inside, not aloud. The words were Hakiia's, recognizably his, directed at Hurkuzak with the specific address of one person speaking to another person in a shared space.

I sat where you are sitting. He said the same things. I believed him.

The words were quiet. The words were the closest thing to an apology the voice had ever produced. The words were also an admission — of complicity, of failure, of the specific kind of shame that attaches to a man who was persuaded to do something terrible by a man who made the terrible thing sound like wisdom.

Hurkuzak sat in the prince's chair in the prince's room in the prince's stronghold and carried his brother inside him and the brother was speaking to him and the speaking was the weapon the prince did not know he had finished building. The prince wanted to map the integration. The prince wanted to understand how two selves coexisted. The prince did not understand that the two selves had been coexisting toward this moment since the cabin, since the wreck, since the correspondence, since the word hold on crossed the barrier between them and made the barrier permanent rubble.

The only way out of this place ran through its heart. Hurkuzak looked at the room — the table, the candles, the tapestries, the architecture of appetite that surrounded him — and began, with the brother inside him, to think about how to break it.
