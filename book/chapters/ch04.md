# Chapter 4: The Road Gives Way

They set out at first light and the cabin was close enough now that Hurkuzak could almost believe in it. The trail had narrowed overnight, or the snow had narrowed it, and they moved in a single line — Hakiia ahead, the dogs behind him, the sled behind the dogs, and Hurkuzak at the rear with one hand on the rail and his eyes on the ground. The sky was low and gray and the wind had died to nothing.

Hakiia was moving well. Better than the terrain justified. He picked the line the way he chose his words — quickly, precisely, with no apparent awareness that another choice existed. The dogs followed him because the dogs had learned what Hurkuzak had always known: where Hakiia went, the way was correct.

The trail ran along the edge of a drop. Hurkuzak could see it through the trees on their left — a long fall through open air to a snow-covered basin below, the rock face jagged where erosion and frost had pulled it apart in layers. The drop was simply there, a fact of the terrain that required no attention as long as the ground beneath your feet remained ground.

There was no warning. No crack, no settling, no sound. The trail simply ended. One moment there was ground. The next there was a sound like the world breaking a bone — deep, structural, final — and the outer edge of the trail dropped away in a mass of snow and frozen earth and loose stone, and Hakiia was on it, and the dogs were on it, and the sled was on it, and Hurkuzak was not.

He felt the ground move under his boots. He threw himself backward and to the right, away from the edge, and his shoulders hit packed snow and the impact drove the air from his lungs and he lay on his back staring at the gray sky while the world fell apart beside him. The sound was enormous — a long tearing separation, stone from stone, a hundred winters of stability giving way in a single breath.

Then silence. Then his own breathing. Then a dog screaming.

He rolled onto his hands and knees and crawled to the edge. The edge was raw now, the stone broken in a clean face where the trail had been, and below it was ruin. The collapse had taken a section of the cliff face twenty paces wide and dropped it thirty paces or more into the basin. Snow and rock and earth were still settling, small slides running down the debris field, and through the dust and powder Hurkuzak could see shapes that did not belong to the landscape.

The sled was destroyed. It lay on its side at the base of the fall, one runner snapped free, the crossbars shattered, gear scattered across the snow in a radius of wreckage. Furs, tools, bundles of dried meat, the traps — everything they had carried was strewn across the broken ground.

The dogs. Two were still in harness, tangled in the traces and the wreckage, and they were not moving. A third lay on the snow apart from the others with its neck at an angle that did not allow for life. The fourth — the left rear, the quiet one — was alive and screaming. It had landed on a shelf of rock below the main debris field and its back legs were wrong and it was trying to pull itself forward with its front legs and the sound it made cut across the basin without pause or variation.

Hakiia.

Hurkuzak could not see him. The wreckage was a confusion of snow and stone and broken wood and dead animals and scattered gear, and his brother was somewhere inside it or beneath it or beyond it, and the silence from that direction was worse than the dog's screaming.

"Hakiia."

Nothing.

"Hakiia!"

Then, from somewhere below and to the right, a sound. Not a word. A cough, or a groan, or the kind of noise a body makes when breathing requires a decision. Then a voice, low and tight with pain but unmistakably his brother's.

"Stop shouting. Come down."

He pressed his hands against the broken edge and breathed until the shaking settled enough that his legs would carry him.

Then the wolves.

Not close. Not yet. But somewhere behind them, back down the trail they had come, a howl rose — long, even, rising at the end. Then another, answering. Then a third, from a different direction, and the three voices braided together across the distance.

Hurkuzak looked at the cliff. It was not sheer — the collapse had left a rough slope of broken rock and debris that angled down to the basin, steep but climbable if he was careful. He swung his legs over the edge and began to descend.

The climb down was ugly. He moved on his hands and his seat, grabbing at whatever held — jutting stone, frozen roots, the edge of a buried boulder — and twice his grip failed and he slid half-again his height before catching himself on something that might hold or might not. His left hand caught a sharp edge and the skin split across his palm, the blood was bright and hot against the cold stone. His ribs took a blow against a rock shelf that left him gasping. His knee struck rock and the pain shot through his thigh.

He reached the bottom bruised, bleeding, and shaking. The basin was a mess of broken earth and scattered snow and wreckage. He found Hakiia twenty paces from the sled, half-buried in the debris, trying to pull himself free with his arms.

His legs were wrong. Both of them. Hurkuzak could see it before he was close enough to touch him — the angles were not angles that legs were meant to make. The left was bent below the knee in a direction that made Hurkuzak's stomach lurch. The right was straighter but the foot hung loose and the boot was dark with blood. There were other injuries — a gash across his forehead that had sheeted blood down the left side of his face, something wrong with his left shoulder — but the legs were the disaster. The legs were the end of everything the sled and the dogs and the trail had been carrying them toward.

Hakiia looked up at him. His face was white with pain and his eyes were sharp and clear and furious.

"Get me out of this," he said. His voice was compressed. "Then deal with the dog."

"Your legs — "

"I know about my legs. Get me free."

Hurkuzak dug. He pulled stone and snow and pieces of the sled away from Hakiia's body, working around the broken legs, trying not to move anything that would shift the weight onto what was already shattered. Hakiia did not cry out. He gripped Hurkuzak's arm once, hard enough to bruise, when a shifting rock pressed against his left shin, and his breath came in short controlled bursts that sounded like someone counting, and then his grip loosened and his breathing steadied and he said:

"The dog first. Then the gear. What can you see of the sled?"

"Destroyed."

"How much of the gear is loose?"

"Most of it. It's everywhere."

"Gather what you can reach. Food first, then the furs, then the tools. Leave the traps." He closed his eyes. Opened them. "Kill the dog."

Hurkuzak looked at the left rear dog on the ledge above. It had stopped screaming and was lying on its side, panting, watching them.

"I'll do it," Hurkuzak said.

"I know you will. Do it now."

He climbed the short distance to the ledge. The dog watched him come. He knelt beside it and put his hand on its head and felt the heat of its body and the trembling in its muscles and the wrongness in its spine where something had broken that would not unbreak. He drew his knife. He did not hesitate.

The dog died under his hand. Quickly.

He climbed back down. The other three dogs were dead. The traces he cut free from the wreckage and bundled. The harnesses he left. There was nothing they would pull now.

Below him, Hakiia had pulled himself to a sitting position against a rock and was watching the cliff face above them. His hands were flat on the ground at his sides, steadying his body.

"How bad is the fall?" Hakiia asked. "Can you climb back up?"

Hurkuzak looked at the cliff. It was climbable — barely — but not while carrying anything. And not while carrying anyone.

"I can get up. I can't carry you."

"I didn't ask about carrying me. I asked about you."

"I can climb it."

"Good. Then we go forward, not up." Hakiia turned his head, slowly, and looked out across the basin toward where the valley continued below them. "The cabin is ahead. Below us now, but still ahead. How far?"

"I don't know. A day, maybe more."

"Then that is the direction we go." He looked at his legs the way he'd look at a damaged tool. "You'll need to make something to move me. Drag, not carry. Use the sled rails if they're intact."

"One is broken."

"Use the one that isn't."

Somewhere above them, from the trail they could no longer reach, the wolves howled again. Closer now. The sound rolled down the cliff face and spread across the basin. The dogs' blood was in the snow. Their bodies lay in the open, and the smell rose into the cold air like a signal.

Hakiia heard it too. His eyes went to the cliff edge, then the dead dogs, then Hurkuzak.

"Gather the gear," he said. "Quickly now."
