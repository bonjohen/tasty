# Chapter 44: What the Ashes Remember

He stopped walking when the smoke was behind him and the air was clean.

A low hill, covered in spring grass, with a view east toward the road and west toward the column of black and gray that climbed from what had been the stronghold. The column leaned in the wind and spread and thinned and became part of the sky.

He sat. His body demanded it — limbs heavy past their reserves, done with the business of carrying him. The burns on his forearms throbbed in the spring air. The cut across his palm had stopped bleeding but had not yet closed. His eyes were raw from the smoke.

He pulled the text from inside his shirt and held it.

The paper was heavier than the prince's, darker, with a texture like woven plant fiber — not the thin pale sheets the prince used for his records, but something made by different hands for different purposes. The edges were worn. Some had crumbled. The binding — stitched cord, not glue — had loosened in places, and some of the interior pages had shifted out of their original order. The prince had added page numbers in his own hand, small numbers in the corners, marking the order he believed was correct. His annotations crowded the margins like commentary on a sacred text — the scholar's utility, not the believer's reverence.

Hurkuzak opened the text and began to read.

The reading was slow. The language was archaic — not a different tongue, but an older version of the same words, shaped by usage he did not fully recognize. Some terms he understood through context. Others he understood because the knowledge inside him recognized roots and word-kin the way a hand recognizes a tool it has held before.

The text was fragmentary. Not a complete document — a surviving section of something larger, something that had been copied, damaged, partially reconstructed. It read like the testimony of witnesses who knew their words would be the only record.

The rite was old.

Not old the way the prince's enterprise was old — two decades, a single lifetime of systematic refinement. Old the way stone is old. The text described a period that had no name in any vocabulary Hurkuzak carried, a time identified only by its conditions: famine, the breaking of old boundaries, communities that had held together for generations coming apart under hunger. Starvation, exposure, neighbors turning on neighbors, the violence that emerged when survival required choosing who lived and who did not.

In those conditions, the rite had been born.

When a family was dying — when the knowledge that had sustained a lineage for generations was about to vanish because the people who held it were about to die — one member could consume another to carry forward what would otherwise be lost.

The mechanism was specific. Sibling consumption was preferred because the kinship produced deeper transfer. The ancient practitioners had observed this and recorded it: rites performed between strangers or between those who bore each other ill will produced fractured, unstable results. Rites performed between those who had shared a life — who had shaped each other across years of proximity — produced transfers of another order. The relationship between the carrier and the consumed was not incidental. It was the thing the practitioners kept returning to, in record after record, as the difference between what worked and what did not.

A healer whose body was failing could pass her knowledge to a surviving sibling. A warrior killed in defense of the family could continue through a brother or sister who consumed the body and inherited the instinct to fight. The accumulated skill of a lineage — tracking, building, healing, navigating, reading weather, terrain, animal behavior — could survive the death of every member except one, carried forward in a single composite being who held the voices of the dead alongside their own.

The text called these beings by a term he could not fully translate. The closest equivalent was something like carriers or those who hold. Not a title of honor. A description of function.

One passage described a carrier who had consumed his sister during a winter siege. She had been the family's healer — the one who knew which roots drew poison, which bark eased fever, which pressure points could slow bleeding. When she was dying from a wound that her own knowledge could not mend, her brother consumed her body over three days and walked out of the siege carrying her knowledge in his hands. He healed people for forty years with skills he had never learned. The text noted that he spoke to her sometimes, in the privacy of his own mind, and that the speaking was not madness but the ordinary conversation of a man who shared his skull with someone he had loved and eaten.

Another passage described a carrier who had failed.

Two brothers during a famine. The consumption was willing. The transfer was deep. But the consumed brother had been the stronger personality — commanding, certain, accustomed to authority — and the carrier had been quiet, yielding, shaped by a lifetime of deference.

Hurkuzak stopped reading.

He looked at the burned skin on his forearms. The cut on his palm. The smoke still climbing to the west.

He looked back down at the page.

By the third year, the carrier spoke only in the dead brother's voice. By the fifth year, the carrier did not remember his own name. The ancient writer called it a grief.

He continued through the text. More cases. The war within a single body — two selves in permanent contest, neither willing to yield, until nothing coherent remained of either. The layered ruin of a carrier who had consumed more than one — too many inherited instincts competing for the same body, capable of extraordinary things and unable to remain one person long enough to do any of them. The ancient practitioners wrote about these outcomes with the precision of people who had watched them happen more than once, who were tired of watching them happen, who needed future readers to understand the cost before attempting the rite.

The taboo followed.

The text did not describe the taboo's origin as a single event or decree. It described a recognition — gradual, reluctant, born of accumulated failure. The rite worked. The rite was necessary. The rite also destroyed people with terrible regularity, and the destruction was not limited to the willing. Once the rite's power was known, those who sought power found reasons to use it. The text's language shifted here — from the direct reportage of witnesses to the harder, angrier tone of people who had watched something sacred become a tool.

Kin consumed against their will. Families broken apart for what their blood might pass. Bonded pairs made to grief and death by arrangement. The same pattern the prince had perfected — but older, cruder, performed without his clinical infrastructure and producing the same results: fractured composites, unstable transfers, beings who degraded and suffered and died because the conditions of the rite had been manufactured rather than endured. The ancient corruptors had believed they could replicate the mechanism without the relationship. They had been wrong in exactly the same way the prince had been wrong.

The prohibition was total. Not "only in emergencies." Not "only between willing participants." Total. Because the practitioners understood what the prince had refused to understand: that "only in emergencies" was a door that every ambitious person would find a way to open. Every war became an emergency. The only safe prohibition was absolute, and so the prohibition became absolute, and the knowledge was suppressed with the severity of people who understood exactly what they were forbidding and why.

The knowledge survived anyway. In fragments. In texts like the one Hurkuzak held. In families who passed the information down as forbidden history, as cautionary tale, as something to know and never use. The carrying was its own kind of inheritance: not flesh but paper, not instinct but instruction, not a voice in the skull but a warning on the page.

Hurkuzak sat on the hill with the text open on his knees and the smoke climbing behind him.

He was not the first. He was not an accident or an aberration. He was a new instance of something the world had tried to bury because it could not be trusted. The burying had failed. Its failure produced the prince, his enterprise, forty-two names in the prince's ledger. Three people carried out of the holding rooms. A column of smoke that was the enterprise's only monument.

He closed the text. The closing was careful — the pages fragile, the binding loose, the document older than anything he had ever held. He did not hold it reverently. He held it the way he held everything now: with the practical care of a man who understood what things cost and what they were worth.

The smoke had thinned to a gray smear against the afternoon sky. Below the road the creek ran full with snowmelt, sound without source from this height. The spring grass on the hill bent and recovered in the wind. He sat with the text in his hands and did not move, and the hill held him the way the world holds things that have endured past their reason to stop.
