# Chapter 44: What the Ashes Remember

He stopped walking when the smoke was behind him and the air was clean.

A low hill, covered in spring grass, with a view east toward the road and west toward the column of black and gray that climbed from what had been the stronghold. The column leaned in the wind and spread and thinned and became part of the sky.

He sat. His body demanded it — limbs heavy past their reserves, done with the business of carrying him. The burns on his forearms throbbed in the spring air. The cut across his palm had stopped bleeding but had not yet closed. His eyes were raw from the smoke.

He pulled the text from inside his shirt and held it.

The paper was heavier than the prince's, darker, with a texture like woven plant fiber — not the smooth, processed sheets used for trial records, but something made by different hands for different purposes. The edges were worn. Some had crumbled. The binding — stitched cord, not glue — had loosened in places, and some of the interior pages had shifted out of their original order. The prince had added pagination in his own hand, small numbers in the corners, mapping the sequence he believed was correct. His annotations crowded the margins like commentary on a sacred text — the scholar's utility, not the believer's reverence.

Hurkuzak opened the text and began to read.

The reading was slow. The language was archaic — not a different tongue, but an older version of the same words, shaped by usage he did not fully recognize. Some terms he understood through context. Others he understood because the knowledge inside him recognized roots and cognates the way a hand recognizes a tool it has held before.

The text was fragmentary. Not a complete document — a surviving section of something larger, something that had been copied, damaged, partially reconstructed. It read like the testimony of witnesses who knew their words would be the only record.

The rite was old.

Not old the way the prince's enterprise was old — two decades, a single lifetime of systematic refinement. Old the way stone is old. The text described a period that had no name in any vocabulary Hurkuzak carried, a time identified only by its conditions: famine, territorial collapse, communities that had held together for generations breaking apart under hunger. Starvation, exposure, neighbors turning on neighbors, the violence that emerged when survival required choosing who lived and who did not.

In those conditions, the rite had been born.

When a family was dying — when the knowledge that had sustained a lineage for generations was about to vanish because the people who held it were about to die — one member could consume another to carry forward what would otherwise be lost.

The mechanism was specific. Sibling consumption was preferred because the kinship produced deeper transfer. The relationship between the consumer and the consumed mattered — not as feeling but as a fact the ancient practitioners had observed and recorded. The closer the bond, the more complete the transfer. The more the consumer grieved the consumed, the more durable the integration. Grief carried the transfer. Love made it last. The rite was not designed for strangers or enemies or the indifferent. It was designed for people who loved each other enough that the consumption was an act of devastating preservation rather than predation.

A healer whose body was failing could pass her knowledge to a surviving sibling. A warrior killed in defense of the family could continue through a brother or sister who consumed the body and inherited the instinct to fight. The accumulated skill of a lineage — tracking, building, healing, navigating, reading weather, terrain, animal behavior — could survive the death of every member except one, carried forward in a single composite being who held the voices of the dead alongside their own.

The text called these beings by a term he could not fully translate. The closest equivalent was something like carriers or those who hold. Not a title of honor. A description of function.

One passage described a carrier who had consumed his sister during a winter siege. She had been the family's healer — the one who knew which roots drew poison, which bark eased fever, which pressure points could slow bleeding. When she was dying from a wound that her own knowledge could not mend, her brother consumed her body over three days and walked out of the siege carrying her knowledge in his hands. He healed people for forty years with skills he had never learned. The text noted that he spoke to her sometimes, in the privacy of his own mind, and that the speaking was not madness but the ordinary conversation of a man who shared his skull with someone he had loved and eaten and could not stop carrying.

Another passage described a carrier who had failed. Two brothers during a famine. The consumption was willing. The transfer was deep. But the consumed brother had been the stronger personality — commanding, certain, accustomed to authority — and the carrier had been quiet, yielding, shaped by a lifetime of deference. By the third year, the carrier spoke only in the dead brother's voice. By the fifth year, the carrier did not remember his own name. The text described this with grief. The preservation had succeeded in the wrong direction: the dead had consumed the living from the inside.

The costs were documented with the same directness as the benefits.

Composite instability. Cases where the consumed self did not integrate but fought — a permanent interior war between the carrier and the carried, each personality asserting dominance, the composite being degrading into confusion and eventual collapse.

Identity erosion, where the consumed personality overwhelmed the carrier's own self. The carrier became the consumed, speaking in their voice, losing the original personality entirely.

Layered composites. A carrier who consumed one sibling and then another, or who consumed a sibling who had already consumed someone else. The text described these beings with something approaching fear. Too many voices. Too many instincts. Too many memories competing for the same attention. The composites became capable of extraordinary things and incapable of sustaining coherence. They broke. Sometimes violently.

Hurkuzak read these descriptions and felt each one resonate through his own experience. The interior war — he had lived it. From the cabin through the wreck, from the first terse correction to the night-long argument in the prince's study. The voice fighting for space, the personality asserting itself, the months of conflict between two selves competing for one body. He had endured the war the ancient text described as the most common cause of composite failure, and he had survived it not through strength but through acceptance.

The ancient practitioners had known this. The rite's success depended not on the power of the consumer but on their capacity to welcome the consumed — to grieve fully, to hold the dead as a permanent presence rather than an intruder. Everything the prince had tried to manufacture through protocol was what the rite required by nature, and the requiring had been understood by people who had watched it succeed and watched it fail and had learned the cost of confusing the two.

The taboo followed.

The text did not describe the taboo's origin as a single event or decree. It described a recognition — gradual, reluctant, born of accumulated failure. The rite worked. The rite was necessary. The rite also destroyed people with terrible regularity, and the destruction was not limited to the willing. Once the rite's power was known, those who sought power found reasons to use it. The text's language shifted here — from the direct reportage of witnesses to the harder, angrier tone of people who had watched something sacred become a tool.

Controlled consumptions. Forced pairings. The harvesting of kin for their properties. The same pattern the prince had perfected — but older, cruder, performed without his clinical infrastructure and producing the same results: fractured composites, unstable transfers, beings who degraded and suffered and died because the conditions of the rite had been manufactured rather than endured. The ancient corruptors had believed they could replicate the mechanism without the relationship. They had been wrong in exactly the same way the prince had been wrong.

The prohibition was total. Not "only in emergencies." Not "only between willing participants." Total. Because the practitioners understood what the prince had refused to understand: that "only in emergencies" was a door that every ambitious person would find a way to open. Every war became an emergency. Every succession crisis. Every powerful family's desire to preserve its line. The only safe prohibition was absolute, and so the prohibition became absolute, and the knowledge was suppressed with the severity of people who understood exactly what they were forbidding and why.

The knowledge survived anyway. In fragments. In texts like the one Hurkuzak held. In families who passed the information down as forbidden history, as cautionary tale, as something to know and never use. The carrying was its own kind of inheritance: not flesh but paper, not instinct but instruction, not a voice in the skull but a warning on the page.

The prince had found such fragments. He had studied them. He had spent twenty-three years trying to rebuild the technology without understanding that the technology was not the point. The rite was not a technique. It was a relationship pushed to its absolute limit by conditions that could not be chosen. And Hakiia had repeated the pattern — believed the rite could be used as a tool, the same mistake the ancient practitioners had already condemned. The failure would have repeated too, except that a cliff broke the design and grief replaced it with the original conditions: catastrophe, love, necessity. The result was the original result — a carrier. A being who holds.

Hurkuzak sat on the hill with the text open on his knees and the smoke climbing behind him and felt the weight of the history settle over his own experience.

He was not the first. He was not an accident or an aberration. He was a new instance of something the world had tried to bury because it could not be trusted. The burying had failed. Its failure produced the prince, his enterprise, forty-two bodies in the trial records. Three people carried out of cells. A column of smoke that was the enterprise's only monument.

He was what the rite was for. Not because the rite had been planned for him — it had not. Because the conditions that produced him were the rite's original conditions. Catastrophe. A cliff, a blizzard, shattered legs, dying wolves, a brother who could not hold a knife. Love. The love that had been tangled with cruelty for their entire lives but that had been love nonetheless. Grief. Weeks in a cabin with a body that had been his brother and became his sustenance and then his inheritance. The ancient practitioners would have recognized him.

He closed the text. The closing was careful — the pages fragile, the binding loose, the document older than anything he had ever held. He did not hold it reverently. He held it the way he held everything now: with the practical care of a man who understood what things cost and what they were worth.

The smoke thinned behind him. The spring air moved across the hill. Grass bent in it, and somewhere below the road a creek ran full with snowmelt. He sat carrying the oldest version of the truth that had shaped his life, and the truth was not a comfort. It was a history. It was the knowledge that what had happened to him in the cabin was a repetition of something the world had endured before and would endure again, and the enduring was the inheritance, and the inheritance was his.
