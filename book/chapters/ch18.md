# Chapter 18: The Hunter's Patience

Vark's winter had been profitable.

Three kills between the first snowfall and the thaw. Two borderland demons — young, alone, moving through the passes with the carelessness of creatures who did not know the trade routes had been mapped — and one older specimen taken from a settlement's edge under contract, quietly, in the hours before dawn, with the precision that contracts required. The older one had been worth the most. Mature organs, dense musculature, liver intact and the bile sac unruptured. Koss had paid above the agreed price without complaint, which meant the final buyer had paid considerably more, which meant the product had been exceptional and Vark's cut had been, as always, a fraction of what it generated downstream.

She did not resent the math. Resentment was an emotion that produced nothing of value. She understood the math instead. Her function in the system was extraction, and extraction sat at the bottom of the value chain because extraction involved risk and labor while the profits rose with each subsequent handler who assumed neither. She could have resented this. Instead she maintained her tools, improved her methods, and conducted each operation with the discipline of a professional who understood that reputation was the only leverage the bottom of a system could hold.

Spring found her east of Cassavon, working a contract that had come through channels she did not normally use. A trading house she had not dealt with before, operating through intermediaries who carried the careful anonymity of money that did not want to be traced. The contract was for reconnaissance, not kill — a departure from her usual work, and one that paid enough to make the departure interesting. Someone wanted information about demon activity in the eastern mountain ranges. Movement patterns, population density, and anything unusual that the spring thaw might reveal.

Unusual was a professional word. In Vark's trade it meant commercially significant. Demons who behaved normally had normal value. Demons who behaved abnormally — faster, stronger, more resilient, harder to corner — suggested augmentation, and augmentation suggested either exceptional lineage or ingestion, and either of those raised the price.

She had traveled east through the passes as the snow receded, working on foot because the terrain above the trade roads was impassable to horses and because she preferred to move on her own body, which did not tire, did not complain, and did not require feeding at the rate that horses required feeding. Her body was, in its way, her primary tool. It carried the accumulated benefits of years of professional consumption: strength that exceeded her frame, senses that operated at ranges no unaugmented human could match, a healing factor that turned injuries into inconveniences, and a cold tolerance that let her work the mountain ranges while lesser hunters waited in the lowlands for conditions they could survive.

She was in the settlement three days before Hurkuzak arrived. She did not see him. She heard about him afterward, from the woman at the trading post, who mentioned the mountain man to a supplier who mentioned him to a traveler who mentioned him within Vark's hearing at a cook fire two settlements east. The chain of mentions was unremarkable. People talked about strangers. Strangers on mountain roads in early spring were worth noting — most were trappers, traders, or prospectors, and all of them carried information about conditions in the passes that had commercial value.

But the details were wrong.

The mountain man had been alone. Had come from above the treeline, from somewhere high enough that the woman at the trading post had commented on it — nobody wintered that high, not voluntarily. He had been gaunt in the face but strong in the body, which was a contradiction that ordinary starvation did not produce. He had moved strangely — too fast for his condition, too aware, with the twitchy alertness of something that was either afraid or dangerous, and the woman had not been able to determine which. He had paid with a coin she did not recognize, which suggested either foreign origin or scavenged currency.

Vark noted the details and filed them in the professional inventory she maintained without conscious effort — the running catalog of anomalies, tracks, reports, and coincidences that accumulated in the field until enough of them pointed in the same direction to constitute a lead. One mountain man was not a lead. One mountain man with a body that did not match his condition was an entry in the catalog. But it was the kind of entry that stayed near the top of the list, because the contradiction — gaunt face, strong body — was the specific contradiction that augmented demons produced, and augmented demons were the specific product that commanded premiums, and premiums were what kept Vark in the field instead of behind a counter taking other people's money for other people's kills.

She had encountered a problem two days prior that had reminded her why she worked alone. A pair of local hunters had crossed her trail and decided she was competition and decided competition required addressing. They had approached her at a campsite with the confidence of men who saw a woman alone and calculated their odds from the outside. The outside was misleading. The first one reached for her arm and she broke his wrist with a rotation that used his own grip as the lever. The second drew a knife and she took it from him with a speed that left his hand empty and his mind several seconds behind the event. She held the knife — a poor one, badly maintained — and looked at them and said nothing, because saying nothing was more instructive than any threat, and the instruction took. They left. She ate her meal. The wrist she had broken would heal badly. She did not care about it because caring about the consequences of other people's miscalculations was not part of her professional scope.

She continued east. The contract's reconnaissance requirements were broad enough to accommodate her own inquiries, which ran parallel to the contract's goals and occasionally intersected. She checked the standard markers: game trails for demon spoor, settlements for reports of attacks or sightings, trade routes for the particular disruptions that a demon presence produced in commercial traffic. The spring was early. The passes were opening ahead of the usual schedule. The eastern ranges were emptier than she expected, which either meant the winter had been hard enough to thin the population or something had moved the population elsewhere.

Three days later, she found the track.

It was on a logging trail east of the settlement where Hurkuzak had been reported, and it was wrong. The footprint was human in shape but the depth was excessive for the width, which meant the body that made it weighed more than its frame suggested, which meant dense muscle, which meant either a very large man or a man who was more than a man. The stride length was long and even and the placement was economical — no wasted steps, no hesitation marks, no adjustments for terrain that would have required adjustment from an ordinary walker. The track read like the track of someone who understood the ground the way a hunter understands the ground, and the understanding was in the feet, not the eyes.

Vark followed the track for half a day. It led east through the forest, staying below the ridgelines, avoiding open ground, choosing routes that minimized visibility from above. The route selection was tactical — not the random path of a traveler but the deliberate line of someone who expected to be followed and was managing that expectation through terrain. The management was good. Better than most demons she had tracked, who relied on speed and durability rather than fieldcraft. This one used the landscape the way she used it: as a tool.

She found a campsite where the track paused overnight. Cold fire, no shelter, minimal disturbance of the ground. He had slept — the compression in the earth said so — but he had slept lightly and on his side, facing the direction of approach. She found the place where he had eaten — a ring of cleaned bone from a rabbit, caught and consumed on the trail. The catch itself was notable. Rabbits in this terrain were wary and fast and required either snares, which she found no evidence of, or a speed and reflexes that suggested capabilities beyond the ordinary.

She knelt beside the fire ring and thought. The track was demon. The route selection was trained. The campcraft was disciplined. The physical indicators — stride, depth, the speed implied by the rabbit catch — suggested augmentation well beyond the baseline. And the direction was east, which was the direction the contract's money had pointed her, which was the direction the premium buyer Koss had mentioned in the winter wanted material from.

She picked up a piece of the rabbit bone and turned it in her fingers. The break was clean — not gnawed, not cracked with a rock, but separated at the joint with a twist that required significant grip strength applied with anatomical precision. She knew that technique. She had used it herself, when field conditions required eating without fire and she needed to access the marrow without tools. It was not a technique most demons used. It was a technique that hunters used. Professionals.

She set the bone down. She was not yet certain what she was following. The profile did not match a standard borderland demon — too skilled, too careful, too physically capable. It did not match a feral, which would have been messier, less tactical, more direct. And it did not match anything commercially augmented — the consumption signature was wrong. Augmented demons showed their enhancement in specific ways: sustained speed, damage resistance, accelerated healing. This one showed all of those plus something else — a quality of movement and decision that felt learned rather than inherited. As though the body's capabilities had been acquired recently and the mind was still integrating them.

That profile had a name. Not a common name. Not one used in casual professional conversation. But a name that existed in the deeper literature of the trade, in the texts that wealthy buyers circulated among themselves and that occasionally filtered down to hunters like Vark through the same channels that premium contracts arrived: sibling consumption. The rite. The mechanism by which one demon body absorbed another's capabilities not through commercial processing but through direct, prolonged, total ingestion.

It was rare enough that Vark had never confirmed a case in the field. Rare enough that most of her professional peers considered it theoretical — a story the aristocratic buyers told each other to justify the prices they paid for certain organs, certain combinations, certain freshness requirements that made no sense unless you believed the mechanism was real.

But the track was real. The campsite was real. The bone technique was real. And the body that had left them was moving east through the mountains with a capability set that did not match any standard category in Vark's professional experience.

The implications, if the profile held, were significant. A sibling-consumed demon was not a standard commodity. The organs would carry layered properties — the subject's own baseline plus whatever the consumed sibling had contributed. The muscle tissue would be denser, the bone harder, the blood richer in the markers that wealthy buyers tested for. And the subject would be alive, which meant the properties were active, which meant the tissue could be harvested fresh, which meant the prices would be multiples of anything Koss had ever offered for her standard kills.

The number Koss had mentioned in the winter — the premium buyer, the factor of three or four — suddenly made more sense. If that buyer had already suspected a sibling consumption case in the eastern ranges, the premium was not speculative. It was informed.

She stood. She checked her equipment — the crossbow, the bolts, the blade, the field kit she carried for exactly this kind of extended pursuit. Everything was in order. It was always in order. Maintenance was not a habit. It was a discipline, and the discipline was what separated professionals from enthusiasts and the living from the dead.

She looked east. The track continued, clear enough in the soft spring earth that following it would require attention but not difficulty. The subject was moving at a pace she could match, along a route she could read, toward a destination she would identify when the route revealed it.

She did not know yet what she was following. Not with certainty. But the catalog of anomalies had stopped accumulating entries and had started pointing in a single direction, and the direction was the track, and the track was the person, and the person was something she had never encountered and might never encounter again.

Vark started walking. She was patient. She had always been patient. Patience was the most valuable skill in the trade — more valuable than strength, more valuable than speed, more valuable than the enhanced senses she carried from years of consuming what she killed. Patience was what let you track without being seen, wound without being reckless, and wait for the moment when the value of a specimen became clear enough to act on with precision rather than impulse.

The forest received her the way it received everything: without opinion, without preference, with the indifference of a system that served predator and prey with equal hospitality. She moved through it with the economy of a woman who had been doing this for a long time and intended to keep doing it for a long time more. The track led east. She followed. The spring continued its slow, indifferent work of turning winter into something else, and the turning did not care about the hunter or the hunted or the price that one of them would eventually pay the other.

The light was good. The terrain was navigable. The air smelled of wet earth and pine and the faint, persistent trace of something that was not quite human and not quite animal that she had been trained to detect and that her augmented senses could follow the way a dog follows blood — not through concentration but through the simple physical reality of scent particles in moving air.

She was in no hurry. The quarry was ahead. The quarry would stay ahead until she decided otherwise. And when she decided otherwise, she would be ready, and the readiness was the thing that mattered most, because the thing she was following — if it was what the profile suggested — was worth more alive and studied than dead and processed, and the alive required a different kind of patience than the dead, and Vark had both kinds in abundance.
