# Chapter 18: The Hunter's Patience

Vark's winter had been profitable.

Three kills between the first snowfall and the thaw. Two borderland demons — young, alone, moving through the passes with the carelessness of creatures who did not know the trade routes had been mapped — and one older specimen taken from a settlement's edge under contract, quietly, in the hours before dawn, with the precision that contracts required. The older one had been worth the most. Mature organs, dense musculature, liver intact and the bile sac unruptured. Koss had paid above the agreed price without complaint, which meant the final buyer had paid considerably more, which meant the product had been exceptional and Vark's cut had been, as always, a fraction of what it generated downstream.

She did not resent the math. Her work sat at the base of it — risk and labor with no claim on what came after. The profits climbed with each set of hands the product passed through. She maintained her equipment, tracked her reputation, and took every contract clean.

Spring found her east of Cassavon, working a contract that had come through channels she did not normally use. A trading house she had not dealt with before, operating through intermediaries whose names were kept out of it — money that moved without a face attached. The contract was for reconnaissance, not kill — a departure from her usual work, and one that paid enough to make the departure interesting. Someone wanted information about demon activity in the eastern mountain ranges. Movement patterns, population density, and anything unusual that the spring thaw might reveal.

Unusual was a professional word. In Vark's trade it meant worth money. Significant money.

She had traveled east through the passes as the snow receded, on foot because the terrain was impassable to horses and because her body was a better instrument than any animal she could ride.

She was in the settlement three days before Hurkuzak arrived. She did not see him. She heard about him days later, through the talk that travels ahead of strangers on mountain roads — mention passed from the trading post through two settlements before it reached her at a cook fire.

But the details were wrong.

The mountain man had been alone. Had come from above the treeline, from somewhere high enough that the woman at the trading post had commented on it — nobody wintered that high, not voluntarily. He had been gaunt in the face but strong in the body. He had moved strangely — too fast for his condition, too aware. The woman could not tell whether his alertness was fear or threat. He had paid with a coin she did not recognize, which suggested either foreign origin or scavenged currency.

One mountain man was not a lead. One with a body that contradicted his condition was. The contradiction — gaunt face, strong body — was the specific mark of demons who had taken on unusual properties, the kind of product that commanded premiums and kept Vark in the field.

Two days prior, a pair of local hunters had crossed her trail and decided she was competition and decided competition required addressing. They had approached her at a campsite with the confidence of men who saw a woman alone and calculated their odds from the outside. The first one reached for her arm and she broke his wrist with a rotation that used his own grip as the lever. The second drew a knife and she took it from him with a speed that left his hand empty and his mind several seconds behind the event. She held the knife — a poor one, badly maintained — and looked at them and said nothing. They left. She ate her meal. The wrist she had broken would heal badly. She did not care about it.

She continued east. The contract's scope was broad enough to accommodate her own inquiries, which ran parallel to the contract's goals and occasionally intersected. She checked the standard markers — trails, settlements, trade routes — for signs of demon activity. The passes were opening ahead of schedule. The eastern ranges were emptier than she expected, which either meant the winter had been hard enough to thin the population or something had moved the population elsewhere.

Three days later, she found the track.

It was on a logging trail east of the settlement where Hurkuzak had been reported, and it was wrong. The depth was excessive for the width — the body that made it weighed more than its frame suggested. Dense muscle. A man who was more than a man. The stride was long and even, the placement economical — no wasted steps, no hesitation marks. The track read like a hunter's. The understanding was in the feet, not the eyes.

Vark followed the track for half a day. It led east through the forest, staying below the ridgelines, avoiding open ground, choosing routes that minimized visibility from above. The route selection was tactical — the deliberate line of someone who expected to be followed. Better than most demons she had tracked.

She found a campsite where the track paused overnight. Cold fire, no shelter, minimal disturbance of the ground. He had slept — the compression in the earth said so — but he had slept lightly and on his side, facing the direction of approach. She found the place where he had eaten — a ring of cleaned bone from a rabbit, caught and consumed on the trail. Rabbits in this terrain required either snares — she found no evidence of snares — or speed beyond the ordinary.

She knelt beside the fire ring and thought. The track was demon. The route selection was trained. The campcraft was disciplined. The physical signs — stride, depth, the speed implied by the rabbit catch — said more than a standard demon body could account for. And the direction was east, which was the direction the contract's money had pointed her, which was the direction the premium buyer Koss had mentioned in the winter wanted material from.

She picked up a piece of the rabbit bone and turned it in her fingers. The break was clean — not gnawed, not cracked with a rock, but separated at the joint with a twist that required knowing the joint, and significant grip applied by someone who had taken animals apart many times. She had used that technique herself, in the field, when she needed marrow without tools. It was a hunter's technique, not a demon's.

She set the bone down. The picture didn't fit a standard borderland demon — too skilled, too careful. Not a feral either, which would have been messier. And the signs of it were wrong for anything she had tracked out of the ordinary trade. This one moved differently — the capabilities felt learned rather than inherited. As though the body's abilities had come recently and the mind had not yet caught up to them.

There was a category for this, one that circulated in the trade without being named in open talk. She had never confirmed a case in the field. Most of her peers considered it theoretical — a story aristocratic buyers used to justify the prices they paid for specific organs and freshness requirements. She had dismissed it herself, more than once.

But the track was real. The campsite was real. The bone technique was real.

What she was assembling was not a standard borderland kill. The capabilities had arrived recently — the body's knowledge outrunning the mind's adjustment. The profile was specific enough that buyers of a certain class paid in advance to be told if it surfaced. She had a buyer of that class. She had a number he had offered.

The number began to look less like speculation.

She stood. She checked her equipment — the crossbow, the bolts, the blade, the gear she carried for exactly this kind of extended pursuit. Everything was in order. It was always in order.

She looked east. The track continued, clear enough in the soft spring earth that following it would require attention but not difficulty. He was moving at a pace she could match, along a route she could read.

The pieces had stopped adding up to nothing and started adding up to something. She did not yet know what she was following. But it was nothing she had tracked before.

Vark started walking.

The forest received her without preference. The track led east. She followed.

The light was good. The terrain was navigable. The air smelled of wet earth and pine and the faint trace of something not quite human — a scent she could follow the way a dog follows blood, without effort, through the body alone.
