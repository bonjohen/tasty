# Chapter 22: The Value of the Unprecedented

She arrived at the settlement four hours after the incident. It was still talking about it.

Vark moved through the market with the unhurried attention of a woman shopping for information rather than goods. She bought a meal she did not need at a stall near the altercation and ate it slowly while the vendor talked. Vendors talked.

The vendor had seen the whole thing. He described it the way witnesses describe events that exceeded their comprehension — emphasis on the wrong details, because the right ones had happened too fast. The man had been fast. The vendor kept returning to that word. Fast. Not running-fast or fighting-fast but the kind of fast that emptied the space around it, that turned a populated market square into a ring with one figure at the center and everyone else outside.

Three enforcers had been beating a smaller man. The quarry had intervened. The intervention had lasted — the vendor guessed — ten seconds. Possibly less. In that time the quarry had disabled all three with a combination of speed, precision, and striking power the vendor kept circling back to without landing on.

She visited the garrison. Not the soldiers — the physician's room, where the enforcer with the jaw injury had been brought. His jaw was not broken. The strike had hit with enough force to drop him where jaw meets neck but not enough to fracture bone. Calibrated. Not a brawler's work.

She examined the ground where the fight had occurred. Boot prints. The quarry's prints were distinctive — the same narrow cut, the same economical placement she had identified on the logging trail. The enforcers' prints were scattered — men caught by something faster than they'd prepared for. One set ended in a long slide mark where the first enforcer had been pulled off his feet. Another showed the body's impact impression — the second man, dropped. The third set simply stopped. The large man, struck once, dropped where he stood.

She tracked the quarry's path through the settlement before the fight. He had entered from the west road, stopped at the dry goods vendor, and moved through the market in a pattern she recognized because she used the same pattern: peripheral movement, edge-walking. He had been trying to be invisible. The trying had failed. She could see it in the witnesses' memories: a man who walked like a traveler but watched like a predator, who bought food like a customer but angled his body toward every exit in the room.

She gathered five witness accounts. The accounts agreed on the essentials — speed, precision, damage far beyond the provocation — and disagreed on the details. One witness described the quarry as young. Another said middle-aged. One said he had moved like a trained soldier. Another said he had moved like a demon. The last assessment was closest.

Two details mattered more than the rest.

First: the small man the quarry had protected described his eyes during the fight as wrong. When pressed, he could not articulate what wrong meant. He said the eyes had already finished the fight before the body started. The eyes already knew.

Second: a woman selling cloth at an adjacent stall had noticed the quarry before the fight, during the minutes when he was eating at the dry goods vendor. She described him as nervous, flinching, moving his head too often. Like a rabbit, she said. Then, during the fight, the rabbit was gone. Something else was there, and the something else was not nervous at all.

Two beings in one body. The witnesses did not have the words for what they were describing, but Vark did.

Vark sat in the garrison courtyard and assembled what she had. Sibling consumption. Stable transfer — physical capabilities and a behavioral overlay that emerged under pressure. This was not commercial augmentation. This was a second intelligence — a full personality transfer.

The value of such a quarry was not calculable by standard methods. Standard methods assessed demon parts — liver, heart, bone, sinew — as discrete commodities with established market prices. What the quarry represented was not parts. It was proof. Proof that the sibling rite produced stable transfer. Proof that the ancient mechanism worked. Proof that what the wealthy had paid to learn for decades was real.

That proof, alive and demonstrable, would command a price that dwarfed everything she had earned in twenty years of hunting.

She knew the tier. Above the hunters, the factors, the trading houses and physicians and chefs — a level that did not advertise and did not negotiate through middlemen like Koss. This tier operated through private contracts, sealed auctions, patronage that funded the research turning flesh into theory and theory into power. They did not buy parts. They bought what parts could not give them. And what Hurkuzak represented — a living demonstration of the deepest form of inherited power — was worth anything they had to spend.

She thought about Koss. She thought about the premium buyer he had mentioned — the anonymous money, the factor of three or four, the requirement for fresh mountain stock. The buyer had known, or suspected, that a sibling case existed in the eastern ranges — and had seeded the market with enough money to draw the best hunters.

Vark was the best hunter. She had found it.

The question was not whether to proceed. The question was how. The straightforward approach — kill, harvest, sell parts — would generate significant revenue. The quarry's tissue would carry layered properties that would interest every buyer in the chain. But killing him would destroy the proof. Dead tissue degraded. A living quarry — tested, observed, studied — was worth not more than the parts but a category above them.

She thought about the men she had worked for who operated at that level. Not Koss — Koss was a middleman, a factor. The people above Koss. The ones who commissioned the contracts she occasionally received through channels that had been deliberately obscured, the contracts that paid five times the standard rate and came with specific requirements — live delivery, no damage to spine or organs, full faculties intact, still thinking when handed over. Those were the people who would want this quarry. Those were the people whose appetite for the sibling rite had built the premium market Koss described. The premium had existed before Vark found the quarry. The buyer already knew, or suspected. The confirmation would be worth whatever she had the courage to demand.

She chose the sophisticated play. Capture, delivery, and sale of a living quarry to the right buyer at the right price. The play was more dangerous — the quarry was extraordinarily capable, and capture would require either overwhelming force or strategic patience. She chose patience. She had always chosen patience.

The capture would require planning. She could not corner him the way she cornered ordinary quarry — approach, engage, overpower. His speed, reflexes, and resilience exceeded her own by more than she could yet judge. A direct engagement meant injury to a body that healed faster than hers. Fatigue against a system that recovered faster. And the second behavioral set — the one the witnesses had described. She had no desire to meet it at close quarters.

She would need help. Not muscle — muscle was what the three enforcers had brought, and muscle had lasted ten seconds. She would need infrastructure. Containment. Resources only the upper tier could provide. Acquiring that support meant contacting the buyer. Contacting the buyer meant revealing what she had found. And revealing it meant negotiating from the weaker position.

She accepted the terms. The upward always took the larger share. But the smaller share of something unprecedented could change a life, and this was unprecedented.

She stood. She packed her equipment. She walked east, following the trail that was now a day old but that her augmented senses could still read in the disturbed ground, the fading scent, and the stories spreading ahead of the quarry.

The stories would help. They would reach merchants, hunters, factor networks — people who would connect them to the money already circulating. The convergence would deliver the quarry to the buyer. Vark intended to be the mechanism of that delivery.

She would send word to Koss at the next settlement — find a reliable courier, someone headed east. The message would be coded — she and Koss had a cipher, words that meant something else on the surface. The message would contain enough to motivate the buyer to respond: confirmed augmented specimen, eastern mountain range, consistent with sibling rite profile, recommend live acquisition. She would not name a price in the message. That required seeing the buyer's face.

She moved east. The market was behind her. The quarry was ahead. Between them, money was flowing toward the rarest thing, and Vark was closest. She intended to stay that way. And the thing she was closest to — the grieving man carrying his dead brother's violence inside him — had no idea what he was worth, or what the world would do when it found out.
