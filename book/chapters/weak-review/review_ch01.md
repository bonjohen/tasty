# Editorial Review: Chapter 1 — The Older Brother's Country

---

## Issue Log

### 1.
**Passage:** "each time the shock traveled up through his ankles and into the meat of his calves"
**Problem:** Wordiness. "Up through his ankles and into" is three prepositional phrases stacked for a simple physical sensation.
**Why it weakens:** Slows the opening sentence, which should move with the cold.
**Revision approach:** Cut to one anatomical anchor. "each time the shock climbed into his calves" does the same work.
**Priority:** Low

### 2.
**Passage:** "That was the way of things between them."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing. This sentence announces the metaphor that the next two sentences already deliver.
**Why it weakens:** The next line — "Hakiia moved over the world as though it had been laid for his particular weight" — is strong enough to carry the comparison without a preamble telling the reader a comparison is coming.
**Revision approach:** Cut the line. Let the contrast speak for itself.
**Priority:** Medium

### 3.
**Passage:** "The runners hissed over the packed snow. The trees on either side were black pillars crusted white, and the sky held the color of spent ash, and nothing in any direction suggested that anyone had ever been here before or would come here after."
**Problem:** Sentence rhythm monotony. The second sentence chains three independent clauses with "and...and...and." Combined with the short sentence before it, this creates a pattern of short → sprawling that repeats throughout the chapter.
**Why it weakens:** The triple-and construction flattens the landscape description into a list. Each image is strong individually but they arrive at the same pace and the same syntactic weight.
**Revision approach:** Break the chain. Give "nothing in any direction suggested" its own sentence — that's the most important image (the emptiness). The trees and sky can share one.
**Priority:** Medium

### 4.
**Passage:** "He simply was no longer moving, and Hurkuzak had to catch up and halt beside him and pretend the sudden change of pace had not made him stumble."
**Problem:** Wordiness. "Catch up and halt beside him and pretend" is three actions chained with "and" where the significant one (the pretending) gets buried at the end.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence's emphasis falls on the wrong element. The pretending is the character beat; the catching up is mechanics.
**Revision approach:** Subordinate the physical action, emphasize the psychological one. "Hurkuzak caught up and stopped beside him, pretending the sudden halt hadn't made him stumble."
**Priority:** Low

### 5.
**Passage:** "something between a cough and a dismissal"
**Problem:** Vague language. "Something between" is a hedge. The reader needs to feel the sound, not be told it exists in an undefined space between two categories.
**Why it weakens:** Abstract where concrete would land harder. What does the sound actually sound like?
**Revision approach:** Commit to one description. A cough that carried dismissal. Or just a cough — the context already makes it dismissive.
**Priority:** Low

### 6.
**Passage:** "This was old choreography between them. Hakiia noticed. Hakiia corrected. Hurkuzak adjusted. Hakiia found the adjustment insufficient. The world continued."
**Problem:** Telling instead of showing. The staccato summary is effective as rhythm, but the word "choreography" explicitly names the pattern that the preceding scene already demonstrated.
**Why it weakens:** The reader just watched the choreography. Naming it diminishes the scene's work.
**Revision approach:** Cut "This was old choreography between them." Start at "Hakiia noticed." The short declaratives already convey the repetition without announcing it.
**Priority:** Medium

### 7.
**Passage:** "Somewhere in the sequence there was supposed to be learning, and after years of these corrections Hurkuzak had learned mainly that his brother's standards existed in a territory he could approach but never inhabit."
**Problem:** Explaining emotion. The observation is sharp ("territory he could approach but never inhabit") but it's preceded by a setup clause ("somewhere in the sequence there was supposed to be learning") that pre-explains the punchline.
**Why it weakens:** The strong metaphor does the work. The throat-clearing weakens it.
**Revision approach:** Cut the first clause. "After years of these corrections, Hurkuzak had learned mainly that his brother's standards existed in a territory he could approach but never inhabit."
**Priority:** Medium

### 8.
**Passage:** "The cold deepened as the trees thinned and the wind found room to move. Hurkuzak's face went numb at the edges first, then the numbness crept inward until his cheeks felt like something he was wearing rather than something he was."
**Problem:** Over-paralleled construction. "Something he was wearing rather than something he was" is a clever turn, but the sentence gets there through a methodical description (numb at edges → crept inward → simile) that feels effortful.
**Why it weakens:** The payoff image is good but the setup is procedural. The reader tracks the numbness step by step when the destination is what matters.
**Revision approach:** Compress the approach. "His face went numb until his cheeks felt like something he was wearing rather than something he was." The inward creep is implied.
**Priority:** Low

### 9.
**Passage:** "Hakiia's face was bare and Hakiia's face was always bare and mentioning the cold was the same as mentioning weakness."
**Problem:** Repetition for emphasis that over-insists. "Hakiia's face was bare and Hakiia's face was always bare" repeats the name and the phrase. The triple-and construction again.
**Why it weakens:** The reader already understands Hakiia doesn't cover his face. The repetition doesn't add — it underlines.
**Revision approach:** "Hakiia's face was bare. It was always bare. Mentioning the cold was the same as mentioning weakness." Short sentences match the character's bluntness better than the chained construction.
**Priority:** Medium

### 10.
**Passage:** "something in the quality of his attention changed when he tracked, a stillness that tightened the air around him, as though the cold itself drew closer to watch what he would do"
**Problem:** Overwriting. Three layers of metaphor stacked: the quality of attention → stillness that tightens air → cold drawing closer to watch. Each escalates the mystification of a simple action (Hakiia goes still to listen).
**Why it weakens:** The first image is the strongest. Each addition dilutes it. "The cold itself drew closer to watch" strains for effect — it personifies the environment in a way that tips toward fantasy-prose ornamentation the project specifically prohibits.
**Revision approach:** Keep "a stillness that tightened the air around him." Cut the rest. Trust the image.
**Priority:** High

### 11.
**Passage:** "It was a good weapon — Hakiia had strung it himself, had tested the draw against his own arm and found it acceptable, which meant it was better than anything Hurkuzak could have made in three lifetimes."
**Problem:** Unnecessary explanation. "Which meant" spells out a connection the reader already makes. If Hakiia found it "acceptable," the reader understands — from the dynamic established in the previous page — that acceptable from Hakiia means excellent by any other standard.
**Why it weakens:** Explaining the joke kills the joke.
**Revision approach:** End at "found it acceptable." The implication is stronger than the explanation.
**Priority:** High

### 12.
**Passage:** "and the snow cracked under him like something brittle and resentful"
**Problem:** Overwriting / strained personification. "Brittle" works. "Resentful" pushes into pathetic fallacy that doesn't earn its keep — the snow doesn't need emotions to be an obstacle.
**Why it weakens:** Personifying the snow as resentful is a reach. The rest of the landscape writing in this chapter is precise and physical. This word breaks the register.
**Revision approach:** "Like something brittle" is enough. Or find a concrete comparison — what brittle thing does it sound like?
**Priority:** Medium

### 13.
**Passage:** "Maybe the dogs. Maybe Hurkuzak's breathing. Maybe just the wrongness that another body introduced into a silence that had been managing fine without him."
**Problem:** The third "maybe" overextends. The first two are concrete (dogs, breathing). The third shifts into abstract commentary ("the wrongness that another body introduced") and then personifies silence ("managing fine without him").
**Why it weakens:** The triple-maybe is a good structure, but the third item is doing different work than the first two — it's editorializing, not speculating. The shift from concrete to philosophical within the same list is jarring.
**Revision approach:** Either make all three concrete ("Maybe just the weight of a body in a space that had been empty") or cut the third and let the two concretes stand.
**Priority:** Medium

### 14.
**Passage:** "He could see the eye now — dark, wet, tremendously alive — and the eye saw him and did not understand what it saw, and in that moment of confusion Hurkuzak was supposed to release."
**Problem:** "Tremendously alive" is an empty intensifier. "Tremendously" does no descriptive work.
**Why it weakens:** "Dark, wet, alive" is stronger. The eye's aliveness is self-evident; intensifying it with an adverb weakens rather than strengthens.
**Revision approach:** Cut "tremendously." Or replace with a concrete detail: "dark, wet, unblinking."
**Priority:** Medium

### 15.
**Passage:** "Hakiia was going to look at him the way Hakiia always looked at him when he proved that mercy and hesitation were the same failure wearing different clothes."
**Problem:** Explaining emotion / telling. "Mercy and hesitation were the same failure wearing different clothes" is an aphorism masquerading as interior thought. It's too polished for the panic of a missed shot.
**Why it weakens:** In the moment of a fumbled kill, Hurkuzak wouldn't be composing epigrams. The observation is authorial, not character-native.
**Revision approach:** Stay in the body. "Hakiia was going to look at him the way Hakiia always looked at him when he hesitated." The word "hesitated" already carries the failure. The aphorism can go.
**Priority:** High

### 16.
**Passage:** "Not a killing shot. Not even a clean wounding shot. Just enough to make the animal stagger and cry out and run badly"
**Problem:** "Not...Not even...Just enough" is a three-tier rhetorical descent that draws too much attention to its structure.
**Why it weakens:** The fragmented emphasis works once (the first "Not a killing shot"). The second and third steps feel like the prose is grading the failure for the reader instead of letting the image land.
**Revision approach:** "Not a killing shot. The arrow struck behind the shoulder and too high, and the animal staggered and ran badly..." Fold the judgment into the action.
**Priority:** Low

### 17.
**Passage:** "Hakiia came from the right side of the tree line like something that had always been there and had simply decided to move."
**Problem:** This is a strong simile. No issue — noting for the pattern summary that the chapter's best images are physical and simple.
**Priority:** N/A — this works.

### 18.
**Passage:** "His forearms were red to the elbow. Steam rose from the cavity like something escaping."
**Problem:** "Like something escaping" is vague. Something what? The simile gestures at meaning without delivering it.
**Why it weakens:** "Steam rose from the cavity" is already vivid. The simile adds fog, not clarity.
**Revision approach:** Cut "like something escaping" or make it concrete: "like breath from a mouth."
**Priority:** Medium

### 19.
**Passage:** "separating and sorting with the efficiency of a man who had taken apart a hundred animals and regarded each one as a problem of geometry"
**Problem:** Overwriting. "Problem of geometry" is trying too hard. Butchery is a problem of anatomy and practice, not geometry. The metaphor doesn't map accurately.
**Why it weakens:** Pulls the reader out of the physical scene to admire the metaphor, and the metaphor doesn't hold up under scrutiny.
**Revision approach:** "Separating and sorting with the efficiency of long practice." Simple. True. Or a better metaphor that maps to what butchery actually involves.
**Priority:** Medium

### 20.
**Passage:** "He had hesitated because the elk had been alive and beautiful and afraid, and he had not wanted, in that particular moment, to be the reason it stopped being any of those things."
**Problem:** This is strong as interior thought. The "any of those things" turn works because it resists ranking the three qualities. No issue.
**Priority:** N/A — this works.

### 21.
**Passage:** "It would earn the silence Hakiia reserved for observations so profoundly weak they did not deserve language."
**Problem:** "So profoundly weak they did not deserve language" is an overwritten intensification. "Profoundly" is an empty intensifier. "Did not deserve language" strains.
**Why it weakens:** The idea (Hakiia's worst disapproval is silence) is good. The decoration weakens it.
**Revision approach:** "The silence Hakiia kept for the weakest observations — the ones that didn't deserve a response."
**Priority:** Medium

### 22.
**Passage:** "as though the animal had been designed with these disassembly lines already drawn into its body"
**Problem:** Overwriting again. Same register as "problem of geometry" — the prose is trying to make butchery intellectual. The image is clever but it sounds like the author, not the character.
**Why it weakens:** Hurkuzak, watching his brother dress an elk, would not think "disassembly lines." He'd notice the speed, the sureness, the absence of waste.
**Revision approach:** Stay physical. "As though the animal came apart along lines only Hakiia could see."
**Priority:** Medium

### 23.
**Passage:** "It was warm and heavy and dense in a way that always surprised him, as though organs carried a different kind of weight than the bodies that housed them."
**Problem:** Explaining the sensation. "In a way that always surprised him" is a tell. The surprise should be in the description, not announced.
**Why it weakens:** The observation about organs carrying different weight is interesting. "In a way that always surprised him" adds nothing — if it surprises him, show the surprise in how he handles it.
**Revision approach:** "It was warm and heavier than it should have been, as though organs carried a different weight than the bodies that housed them." The unexpectedness is in "heavier than it should have been."
**Priority:** Low

### 24.
**Passage:** The entire paragraph beginning "They loaded the sled in silence" through "in a different family might have been called care."
**Problem:** Paragraph without clear compression. The paragraph makes one point (Hakiia's care is hidden inside his efficiency) but takes 8 lines to get there, walking through the mechanics of sled-packing to arrive at the observation.
**Why it weakens:** The destination is strong ("in a different family might have been called care") but the journey is procedural. The reader tracks sled balance → dog strain → travel distance → lighter pieces → haunches → placement → refusal to delegate → care. Too many intermediate steps.
**Revision approach:** Cut the sled-mechanics explanation (balance, dogs, distance). Keep: the silence, the division of labor, the fact that Hakiia took the heavy pieces and gave Hurkuzak the light ones, and the closing observation. The reader doesn't need to understand sled physics to understand the gesture.
**Priority:** High

### 25.
**Passage:** "His brother could draw heat from wet wood the way he drew compliance from other people — through pressure, patience, and the absolute certainty that what he wanted would happen because he wanted it."
**Problem:** Overwriting / strained parallel. Comparing fire-building to social dominance via a three-item list ("pressure, patience, and the absolute certainty") is a construction that calls attention to its own cleverness.
**Why it weakens:** The simile doesn't map cleanly. You draw heat from wood through technique and material knowledge, not through certainty. The comparison serves the author's thesis about Hakiia more than it serves the scene.
**Revision approach:** Cut the simile. "The fire came fast under Hakiia's hands. It always did." That's enough. The character is already established as effortlessly competent — the reader fills in the rest.
**Priority:** High

### 26.
**Passage:** "the offer would be refused, and the refusal would arrive in a way that made him feel he had proposed something foolish"
**Problem:** Wordiness. "The refusal would arrive in a way that made him feel" is a circuitous construction for a simple idea.
**Why it weakens:** Takes too long to say: Hakiia would make him feel stupid for offering.
**Revision approach:** "The offer would be refused in a way that made offering feel foolish."
**Priority:** Low

### 27.
**Passage:** "with an expression that was either gratitude or hunger and was probably both"
**Problem:** Cliche territory. The "was it X or Y — probably both" construction is common in contemporary literary fiction. Feels like a borrowed move.
**Why it weakens:** The observation itself is fine, but the syntactic frame ("either...or...probably both") is a recognizable pattern that undercuts the prose's individuality.
**Revision approach:** "Watching him with something that might have been gratitude if dogs were capable of it." Or just describe what the dog's face actually does — ears forward, eyes steady, whatever is true.
**Priority:** Low

### 28.
**Passage:** "because it pulled without complaint and ate last and watched everything with the patience of a creature that had decided the world was too large to argue with"
**Problem:** Overwriting. The dog is being given a philosophical position ("decided the world was too large to argue with") that's doing thematic work about Hurkuzak's own temperament. The parallel is too neat.
**Why it weakens:** The dog becomes a metaphor instead of a dog. The reader can feel the author's hand arranging the symbol.
**Revision approach:** Keep it physical. "Because it pulled without complaint and ate last." That's already character. Cut the philosophy.
**Priority:** Medium

### 29.
**Passage:** "the smell of a body's workings turned into food, and Hurkuzak's stomach pulled tight with something that was hunger but was also the recognition that the animal had been breathing a few hours ago and was now becoming part of them"
**Problem:** Explaining emotion. The sentence names "the recognition" explicitly. The scene already shows it — he watched the elk die, he's now eating the liver.
**Why it weakens:** The reader has the recognition. Spelling it out is redundant.
**Revision approach:** "The smell was rich and dark, and his stomach pulled tight." Let the reader supply the complexity. They've earned it by now.
**Priority:** Medium

### 30.
**Passage:** "He sat. Hakiia handed him a piece on the point of his knife and Hurkuzak took it and ate it and it was good. It was always good. That never helped."
**Problem:** None. This is the chapter's best passage. Four short sentences. The rhythm is perfect. "That never helped" lands because nothing explains it.
**Priority:** N/A — this is the standard the rest of the chapter should meet.

### 31.
**Passage:** "She could move through snow the way water moves through rock. Without asking permission."
**Problem:** Mixed metaphor. Water through rock is erosion — slow, geological, destructive. But the context implies grace and ease. The metaphor maps to the wrong quality.
**Why it weakens:** The fragment "Without asking permission" saves it somewhat, but the water-through-rock image is confusing. Water doesn't move through rock effortlessly; it takes millennia.
**Revision approach:** Find an image that conveys effortless movement through snow. "She could move through snow the way wind moves through trees" — or better yet, something specific to this world.
**Priority:** Medium

### 32.
**Passage:** "as though the memory of their mother was one of the few things he could hold without squeezing"
**Problem:** Strong image. Works well. The "squeezing" conveys Hakiia's essential nature — his grip on everything is too tight.
**Priority:** N/A — this works.

### 33.
**Passage:** "You eat well, you sleep warm, you dress against the weather, and you think that means you understand it. You don't understand it. You survive it."
**Problem:** Hakiia's dialogue is strong throughout the chapter. This speech is the best example — it's specific, rhythmic, and reveals character through argument, not description.
**Priority:** N/A — this works.

### 34.
**Passage:** "he was tired of lessons, and he was tired of learning from them mainly that his brother saw him as a problem that the right amount of suffering might fix"
**Problem:** Repetition ("tired of...tired of") is intentional and works, but "a problem that the right amount of suffering might fix" is another aphorism that sounds authorial rather than character-native.
**Why it weakens:** The exhaustion is real. The epigrammatic framing of it is too polished for a character who is tired and sitting by a fire.
**Revision approach:** "He was tired of lessons, tired of learning from them mainly that his brother thought suffering could fix him." Simpler. Same meaning. Less writer-visible.
**Priority:** Low

### 35.
**Passage:** The paragraph beginning "And the worst of it" through "though he could not have said what."
**Problem:** Over-length. This is the chapter's thesis paragraph — the one that articulates Hurkuzak's quiet strengths versus Hakiia's loud ones. It's important. But at 10 lines it tries to do too much: list Hurkuzak's knowledge, contrast it with Hakiia's blindness, and then retreat into uncertainty ("he could not have said what"). The retreat undermines the catalog.
**Why it weakens:** The paragraph builds a case and then shrugs at the end. The shrug is meant to show Hurkuzak's uncertainty, but it reads as the prose losing confidence in its own argument.
**Revision approach:** Cut the final sentence ("though he could not have said what"). End on "Hakiia did not notice them." The silence is more powerful than the uncertainty.
**Priority:** Medium

### 36.
**Passage:** "He loved his brother. He feared him. He envied the ease with which Hakiia inhabited the world. He resented the way that ease was used against him..."
**Problem:** Telling / catalog of emotions. Five sentences in a row that directly name emotions: loved, feared, envied, resented, and then "something worse." This is a summary paragraph, not a scene.
**Why it weakens:** After an entire chapter of scenes that show these emotions, this paragraph names them all in sequence. The reader already knows. This passage trusts the reader less than the rest of the chapter does.
**Revision approach:** Cut it entirely. Or keep only the last sentence ("beneath all of it... there was something worse") and let the chapter's scenes carry the rest. The reader felt the love in the liver scene, the fear in the corrections, the envy in the walking. They don't need the inventory.
**Priority:** High

### 37.
**Passage:** "Some territory without doubt or hesitation, where every grip was right and every arrow flew true and nothing alive had reason to pause before killing."
**Problem:** Overwriting. Three parallel "where/every/nothing" clauses paint Hakiia's dream-self as an action figure. The image is too symmetrical to feel real.
**Why it weakens:** The prose has been carefully human about Hakiia all chapter. This passage tips him into archetype.
**Revision approach:** "Some territory without doubt or hesitation." Stop there. The specifics that follow diminish the mystery.
**Priority:** Medium

### 38.
**Passage:** "the stars were scattered across it like the debris of something that had broken long ago"
**Problem:** Cliche-adjacent. Stars as broken debris is a well-worn image in literary fiction.
**Why it weakens:** Serviceable but not distinctive. This chapter's best images are specific to its world (snow crust, elk eye, liver on stone). This one could appear in any novel.
**Revision approach:** Find an image native to this world. What do these characters know that they'd compare stars to? Scattered bones. Spilled grain. Something from their experience.
**Priority:** Medium

### 39.
**Passage:** "the warmth of it in his mouth and the way that warmth had felt like something borrowed from a life that no longer needed it"
**Problem:** Repetition of an earlier explained emotion. The "liver as borrowed life" theme was already stated in paragraph 77 ("the recognition that the animal had been breathing a few hours ago and was now becoming part of them"). This restates it with a slightly different metaphor.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter is closing. It should be gathering, not repeating. The liver image has already done its work.
**Revision approach:** Cut "and the way that warmth had felt like something borrowed from a life that no longer needed it." End the list on the physical: "the warmth of it in his mouth."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

This is strong first-draft writing with a clear voice, a well-constructed emotional architecture, and several passages that are genuinely excellent ("It was always good. That never helped." / "Hakiia moved over the world as though it had been laid for his particular weight."). The chapter accomplishes its structural goals: the brother dynamic is established, the cruelty and care coexist, and the reader enters the relationship feeling both brothers clearly.

The primary weakness is **over-explanation**. The chapter frequently delivers a strong image or scene, then follows it with a sentence or clause that tells the reader what the image meant. The best moments are the ones where the prose trusts the reader — the terse dialogue, the liver scene, the final line. The weakest moments are the ones where the prose circles back to explain the emotion it just showed.

The secondary weakness is **overwriting**. Similes and metaphors are sometimes stacked two or three deep when one would land harder. The prose reaches for philosophical abstraction ("problem of geometry," "disassembly lines," "choreography") when the physical and concrete images are consistently stronger.

## Top 10 Problems, Ranked by Impact

1. **Emotion-inventory paragraph** (issue #36) — names every emotion the chapter already showed. Trust the scenes.
2. **Overwritten tracking stillness** (issue #10) — three metaphors where one suffices. "The cold drew closer to watch" breaks register.
3. **Sled-packing paragraph** (issue #24) — 8 lines of logistics to reach a one-line emotional payoff. Compress.
4. **Fire-building simile** (issue #25) — strained parallel between fire and social dominance. The comparison is too clever.
5. **"Which meant" explanation on the bow** (issue #11) — explains a joke the reader already got.
6. **Mercy/hesitation aphorism** (issue #15) — too polished for the panic of the moment. Authorial, not character-native.
7. **Naming the choreography** (issue #6) — "This was old choreography between them" tells the reader what the scene just showed.
8. **Triple-and snow/resentful** (issues #3, #9, #12) — recurring syntactic monotony and strained personification.
9. **Liver-as-borrowed-life repetition** (issue #39) — the closing restates what was already said mid-chapter.
10. **Stars-as-debris cliche** (issue #38) — the chapter's only image that could belong to any novel instead of this one.

## Pattern Summary: Recurring Weaknesses

**1. Explaining after showing.** The chapter's most persistent habit is following a strong scene with a sentence that names the emotion or identifies the theme. The liver scene, the grip correction, the elk hesitation — all work as rendered experience. The explanatory sentences that follow them work against the chapter's best impulses.

**2. Stacked metaphors.** When one comparison lands, the prose tends to add a second or third. The tracking stillness gets three layers. The fire-building gets a social-dominance parallel. The butchery gets both "geometry" and "disassembly lines." The strongest images in the chapter are the simplest ones.

**3. Triple-and constructions.** "And...and...and" appears multiple times as a sentence-chaining device. It creates a rhythmic monotone — each clause arrives at the same weight, flattening emphasis. The prose already has strong short-sentence rhythm (the dialogue, the correction sequence); the triple-and passages work against that.

**4. Authorial aphorisms in character thought.** Several of Hurkuzak's interior observations sound like the author making a thematic point rather than a cold, tired young man thinking. "Mercy and hesitation were the same failure wearing different clothes" and "a problem that the right amount of suffering might fix" are both too epigrammatic for the character as rendered.

**5. Vague similes.** "Like something escaping," "like something brittle and resentful," "something between a cough and a dismissal" — the "something" construction appears repeatedly and signals imprecision. The chapter is at its best when comparisons are concrete and specific.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut the emotion-inventory paragraph** (lines 95-96 area). Let the scenes carry the emotional summary. If anything survives, keep only the final observation about dependency.

2. **Trim stacked metaphors to single images.** For each overwritten passage (tracking stillness, fire-building, butchery, dog philosophy), keep the first and strongest image. Cut the elaborations.

3. **Remove explanatory follow-ups.** After each strong scene beat, check: is the next sentence explaining what just happened? If yes, cut it. Apply to: "That was the way of things," "This was old choreography," "which meant," the liver recognition, the borrowed-warmth repetition.

4. **Replace vague similes.** Anywhere the text says "like something [adjective]," find a concrete image native to this world.

5. **Break triple-and sentences.** Each instance: decide which clause carries the most weight, give it its own sentence, subordinate or cut the rest.

6. **Ground aphorisms in the body.** Where Hurkuzak's thoughts sound authorial, rewrite to stay physical and immediate. He notices things. He doesn't compose thesis statements.

7. **Replace the stars image** with something from this world's texture.
