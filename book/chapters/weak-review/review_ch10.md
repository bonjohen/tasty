# Editorial Review: Chapter 10 — The Carrying

---

## Issue Log

### 1.
**Passage:** "the cold replaced it and the cold was absolute, the way facts are absolute — not an argument, not a threat, just the condition of the world with no fire in it"
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. "The cold was absolute" is strong. Then the dash opens a three-part gloss that explains what "absolute" means. The simile "the way facts are absolute" is abstract-to-abstract comparison, which lands nowhere concrete. The follow-up triple ("not an argument, not a threat, just the condition") then re-explains the simile.
**Why it weakens:** Three layers of explanation for one word. The reader understood "absolute" the first time.
**Revision approach:** Keep "the cold replaced it and the cold was absolute." Cut the dash and everything after it through "fire in it." The next sentence about the snow already establishes the cold's indifference concretely.
**Priority:** High

### 2.
**Passage:** "The snow fell on Hakiia's face and on his own hands and on the furs between them and the snow was the same snow it had always been and it did not know what had happened here and would not have changed its behavior if it did."
**Problem:** Triple-and chain (six "and" conjunctions). The sentence stacks three locations, then three observations about the snow's indifference. The cumulative rhythm works in moderation, but six chained clauses flatten every image to equal weight.
**Why it weakens:** The strongest idea — the snow's indifference — arrives exhausted at the end of a list. The reader has to hold too many clauses in mind before reaching the payoff.
**Revision approach:** Break into two sentences. One for where the snow falls. One for its indifference. "The snow fell on Hakiia's face and on his own hands and on the furs between them. It was the same snow it had always been. It did not know what had happened here."
**Priority:** High

### 3.
**Passage:** "It was the body announcing that it had a separate opinion about what happened next, and the body's opinion was that sitting still in this temperature with no fire would result in a second death, and the body was not interested in dying, and the body did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely."
**Problem:** Repetition / over-insistence. "The body" appears four times in one sentence. The personification of the body as having opinions is effective the first time, but the sentence keeps restating the same point — the body wants to live, the mind doesn't care — in four parallel clauses.
**Why it weakens:** The first clause ("the body announcing that it had a separate opinion") does the work. The next three explain the joke.
**Revision approach:** "It was the body announcing that it had a separate opinion about what happened next, and the body's opinion was survival, and it did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely."
**Priority:** Medium

### 4.
**Passage:** "The standing took a long time and required several intermediate stages — hands on the ground, knees braced, a push that failed and a second push that succeeded — and when he was upright the world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out for balance and the balance came eventually, not through strength but through stubbornness"
**Problem:** Run-on sentence / exhaustive sequencing. The sentence tracks every micro-stage of standing up, then chains four more clauses about balance. The reader is forced through a procedural inventory that delays the important turn: the looking.
**Why it weakens:** The physical detail is earned, but the sentence refuses to stop. By the time we reach "not through strength but through stubbornness," the reader is fatigued.
**Revision approach:** Break after the parenthetical. "He pushed once and failed. Pushed again and stood. The world tilted and steadied and tilted again." Then the looking gets its own sentence with full weight.
**Priority:** Medium

### 5.
**Passage:** "and he looked down at what was on the travois and the looking was the hardest thing he had done since the knife"
**Problem:** Explaining emotion. "The looking was the hardest thing he had done since the knife" is a direct statement of emotional difficulty. The reader should feel the difficulty through the preceding physical struggle. Naming it as "the hardest thing" tells us what to feel.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence explains the weight of the moment instead of trusting the scene to carry it.
**Revision approach:** Cut "and the looking was the hardest thing he had done since the knife." End on "he looked down at what was on the travois." The silence after that sentence does the work.
**Priority:** High

### 6.
**Passage:** "The body was only a body. The person who had lived in it was gone, and what remained was form without function, mass without will, the physical residue of someone who had been the largest presence in Hurkuzak's life and was now the absence of that presence shaped like a man."
**Problem:** Stacked metaphors / continuing after the point is made. "Form without function, mass without will" is a paired abstraction that works. But then the sentence keeps going: "the physical residue" re-says it, and "the absence of that presence shaped like a man" re-says it again. Three ways of saying "the body is no longer a person."
**Why it weakens:** Each restatement dilutes the previous one. "Mass without will" is the strongest. Everything after it is diminishing returns.
**Revision approach:** Cut after "mass without will." If "shaped like a man" is needed, attach it there: "mass without will, shaped like a man." One sentence, one image.
**Priority:** High

### 7.
**Passage:** "A living body on a travois cooperates in small ways the living person does not notice. Muscles adjust. Weight shifts. The body finds its center on the frame without being told to. A dead body does none of these things. A dead body is only mass, and mass obeys gravity and friction and nothing else"
**Problem:** Over-explaining a concrete observation. The paragraph spends six sentences establishing that dead weight is harder to drag than a living person. This is a simple physical fact that the reader grasps immediately. The writer methodically catalogs what a living body does, then says the dead body doesn't do those things, then explains mass.
**Why it weakens:** The insight is good but the delivery is a lecture. The reader understood after "a dead body does none of these things."
**Revision approach:** Compress to two sentences. "A living body on a travois cooperates in small ways the living person does not notice — muscles adjust, weight shifts, the body finds its center. A dead body is only mass." Cut "and mass obeys gravity and friction and nothing else" — that's physics class.
**Priority:** High

### 8.
**Passage:** "yesterday these hands had pulled a living man, and now they pulled a dead one, and the poles did not know the difference and his palms did not know the difference and the only thing that knew was his mind, and his mind was the least useful part of him"
**Problem:** Triple-and chain with repeated structure. "Did not know the difference" appears twice, followed by "the only thing that knew" — three variations on the same observation. The concluding turn ("his mind was the least useful part of him") is strong but arrives after redundancy.
**Why it weakens:** The reader gets the contrast (objects don't know, only the mind knows) after the first "did not know the difference." Saying it twice delays the good line.
**Revision approach:** "The poles did not know the difference. His mind did, and his mind was the least useful part of him."
**Priority:** Medium

### 9.
**Passage:** "not as light but as a lessening of dark, a gradual dilution of the black into gray and the gray into a flat, colorless illumination that showed the landscape without warming it"
**Problem:** Overwriting / stacked synonyms. "A lessening of dark" and "a gradual dilution of the black into gray" say the same thing. "Colorless illumination" is a contradiction played for effect but reads as straining.
**Why it weakens:** The observation is accurate but over-described. One image would land. Three synonyms for "dim" diffuse the effect.
**Revision approach:** "Not as light but as a lessening of dark — a flat gray that showed the landscape without warming it."
**Priority:** Medium

### 10.
**Passage:** "his bitten forearm sent a pulse of pain with every pull that was so regular it became a kind of clock, marking time in units of hurt"
**Problem:** Overwriting. "A kind of clock, marking time in units of hurt" strains for metaphorical effect. "Units of hurt" in particular feels like a writer's flourish rather than a character's experience.
**Why it weakens:** The image of regular pain-pulses is good. Calling it a clock is already a stretch. "Units of hurt" pushes into preciousness.
**Revision approach:** "His bitten forearm pulsed with every pull, so regular it replaced the hours." Simpler, same idea.
**Priority:** Medium

### 11.
**Passage:** "the shifting was the movement of something that was not choosing to move, that was only responding to force, and every shift reminded him that the cooperation was gone and what remained was physics"
**Problem:** Repetition / explaining after showing. The paragraph already established that the body shifts when the travois catches. "The movement of something that was not choosing to move" restates what the reader already knows. "What remained was physics" restates the dead-weight concept from paragraph 5.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter has already made this point eloquently. Repeating it dilutes the earlier version.
**Revision approach:** Cut "the shifting was the movement of something that was not choosing to move, that was only responding to force, and every shift reminded him that the cooperation was gone and what remained was physics." The body shifting on the frame is enough. The reader carries the dead-weight knowledge forward.
**Priority:** High

### 12.
**Passage:** "The arm had to be placed back because leaving it to drag felt wrong, felt like carelessness, felt like something Hakiia would have criticized."
**Problem:** Triple construction ("felt wrong, felt like carelessness, felt like something Hakiia would have criticized"). Each "felt" escalates but the third is the only one that matters — it's the character-specific beat.
**Why it weakens:** "Felt wrong" and "felt like carelessness" are generic. The Hakiia detail is where the sentence earns its place.
**Revision approach:** "Leaving it to drag felt like something Hakiia would have criticized." One clause, the right one.
**Priority:** Medium

### 13.
**Passage:** "the sound of his own voice in the silent landscape was startling and strange, and the silence that followed it was worse than the silence before, because the silence before had simply been empty and the silence now was the specific emptiness that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear"
**Problem:** Over-explanation / "silence" repeated five times. The observation is emotionally true but the sentence explains it exhaustively. "Startling and strange" is vague. Then the sentence explains why the new silence is worse than the old silence, which the reader would feel without the comparison being spelled out.
**Why it weakens:** The word "Sorry" spoken to a dead body in a snowfield is devastating on its own. The analysis that follows reduces it to a concept.
**Revision approach:** "The sound of his own voice in the silence was a thing he wished he could take back." Or simply end the paragraph at "Sorry," he said." and start the next paragraph with "He pulled again." Trust the reader.
**Priority:** High

### 14.
**Passage:** "The trees were bare poles. The sky was a ceiling. The ground was a problem that repeated itself with every step"
**Problem:** Mixed quality — diminishing returns. "Trees were bare poles" is precise. "The sky was a ceiling" is good. "The ground was a problem that repeated itself with every step" is abstract and vague by comparison.
**Why it weakens:** After two concrete images, the third goes abstract, breaking the pattern.
**Revision approach:** Either commit to concrete for all three or cut the weakest. "The trees were bare poles. The sky was a ceiling." Then move to the rhythm.
**Priority:** Low

### 15.
**Passage:** "and so he held onto the rhythm the way a drowning man holds onto a plank, not because it was taking him anywhere but because letting go meant sinking"
**Problem:** Cliche. Drowning man / plank is a stock simile. The gloss ("not because it was taking him anywhere but because letting go meant sinking") explains the simile, compounding the problem.
**Why it weakens:** The rest of the paragraph earns its abstraction. The simile defaults to a generic image.
**Revision approach:** Cut the simile entirely. "The rhythm of the march was the only thing between him and the full arrival of what he had done." That sentence is already strong enough to close the paragraph.
**Priority:** High

### 16.
**Passage:** "After another hour — or what felt like another hour, though time had lost its edges and the distinction between minutes and hours had dissolved into the general texture of endurance — the hallucinations began."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing parenthetical. The qualification ("or what felt like another hour, though time had lost its edges...") delays the important word: "hallucinations." The reader already understands from context that time is unreliable.
**Why it weakens:** The parenthetical explains what the chapter has been showing for paragraphs. The payoff word gets buried at the end of a long aside.
**Revision approach:** "The hallucinations began sometime after that." Or: "After another hour, the hallucinations began." Simple, direct.
**Priority:** Medium

### 17.
**Passage:** "They were small. The first was a voice, or the memory of a voice, or the imagined continuation of a voice that had been part of his life so long that its absence was an active presence."
**Problem:** Over-qualifying. "A voice, or the memory of a voice, or the imagined continuation of a voice" — three nested hedges. The observation about absence-as-presence is strong, but the triple "or" construction delays it.
**Why it weakens:** The hedging is meant to convey uncertainty but reads as indecision. Pick one.
**Revision approach:** "The first was a voice — or the memory of one — that had been part of his life so long its absence was a presence." One hedge, not three.
**Priority:** Medium

### 18.
**Passage:** "The warmth was a fabrication of his exhausted mind, conjuring the sensation of his brother's proximity because his body was not yet willing to accept that the proximity had ended."
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. The phantom warmth is already established as not real ("It was not. The body was cold."). Then the sentence explains why the hallucination happens — exhausted mind, unwilling body. The reader already understands this.
**Why it weakens:** The explanation removes the eerie quality of the phantom warmth by rationalizing it.
**Revision approach:** Cut the explanatory sentence. "It was not. The body was cold." is the right ending for that beat.
**Priority:** Medium

### 19.
**Passage:** "These were not visions. They were gaps. Places where his mind had worn thin from labor and loss and the unprocessed weight of what his hands had done, and through those thin places the past was leaking in, filling the present with fragments that had the shape and texture of Hakiia but none of his substance. They were memories replayed as sensation. Grief translated into perception. The mind's desperate insistence that the man who had filled every space in Hurkuzak's life could not simply be absent from it."
**Problem:** Summarizing emotion / explaining the hallucinations after they've been rendered. This entire paragraph is meta-commentary on the hallucination sequence. The reader just experienced the hallucinations — the phantom voice, the false cabin, the phantom grip. Now the writer steps in to explain what they mean.
**Why it weakens:** The hallucinations spoke for themselves. This paragraph tells the reader what to think about them. "Grief translated into perception" is thesis statement, not fiction.
**Revision approach:** Cut the entire paragraph. The hallucinations are their own explanation. If anything must remain, keep only "These were not visions. They were gaps." — that earns its place as a perception, not an analysis.
**Priority:** High

### 20.
**Passage:** "and exhaustion, like pain, was a form of time"
**Problem:** Aphorism ungrounded in the body. The comparison (exhaustion = time) is abstract and tries to sound profound. "A form of time" doesn't mean anything concrete.
**Why it weakens:** It reads like a writer's notebook entry, not a character's experience. The chapter's best moments are physical and specific. This is neither.
**Revision approach:** Cut. The reader already understands he's guessing at time by how tired he is. Show it: "the only reason he believed it was later was that his body was more exhausted than it had been."
**Priority:** Medium

### 21.
**Passage:** "Not in the way faces change through decay — the cold was preserving everything, holding the body in the state it had been in at death, frozen and still. But the relationship between Hurkuzak and the face had changed."
**Problem:** Unnecessary clarification. The reader does not assume decay in a freezing environment. The parenthetical about preservation addresses a question no one asked, and delays the real observation (the relationship to the face has changed).
**Why it weakens:** The writer anticipates a misreading and corrects for it, which slows the paragraph and weakens the real point.
**Revision approach:** Cut "Not in the way faces change through decay — the cold was preserving everything, holding the body in the state it had been in at death, frozen and still." Start at "But the relationship between Hurkuzak and the face had changed" — or better, show the change directly.
**Priority:** Medium

### 22.
**Passage:** "and the order of those two perceptions had reversed without his permission and the reversing felt like a betrayal and also like the truth"
**Problem:** Explaining emotion after showing it. The paragraph has already rendered the shift: he sees weight first, brother second. That's the show. "The reversing felt like a betrayal and also like the truth" tells us how to feel about what we just read.
**Why it weakens:** The emotional content is already in the reversal. Naming it "betrayal" and "truth" pins it down where ambiguity would be stronger.
**Revision approach:** Cut "and the reversing felt like a betrayal and also like the truth." End the sentence at "had reversed without his permission." Let the reader supply the emotional weight.
**Priority:** Medium

### 23.
**Passage:** "He was carrying weight. He was carrying his brother. Both of these things were true, the way the mercy and the killing had been true"
**Problem:** Redundancy / callback that over-insists. The mercy/killing duality was established in Chapter 9. Referencing it here as a structural parallel ("the way X had been true") feels like the writer ensuring the reader notices the pattern.
**Why it weakens:** The parallel between weight/brother and mercy/killing is obvious. Pointing to it reduces the reader's work and the story's resonance.
**Revision approach:** Cut "the way the mercy and the killing had been true." The two opening sentences are strong enough alone. "He was carrying weight. He was carrying his brother." Period.
**Priority:** Medium

### 24.
**Passage:** "and the weight was winning because weight was the thing his body measured and his body was the only part of him still functioning"
**Problem:** Triple-and chain / explaining after showing. The paragraph just rendered the weight-vs-brother shift. This sentence explains why weight is winning, which the reader already understands from the preceding description of his mechanical handling of the body.
**Why it weakens:** The reader is ahead of the sentence.
**Revision approach:** Cut. The paragraph's power is in "He was carrying weight. He was carrying his brother." Everything after that weakens it.
**Priority:** Medium

### 25.
**Passage:** "Earlier, he had touched Hakiia's body with the care of a man handling something sacred. Now his hands were solving a problem. The sacred thing was still there, underneath the problem, but the problem was what his hands were for and the hands had made their choice."
**Problem:** Over-explaining a well-rendered observation. "His hands were solving a problem" is excellent. The next sentence ("The sacred thing was still there, underneath the problem") explains the coexistence of reverence and pragmatism that the reader already feels.
**Why it weakens:** The best line in the paragraph gets undercut by the gloss.
**Revision approach:** Cut "The sacred thing was still there, underneath the problem, but the problem was what his hands were for and the hands had made their choice." End on "Now his hands were solving a problem."
**Priority:** Medium

### 26.
**Passage:** "He did not build a fire because building a fire required hands and focus and material and he had the first of these barely and the second not at all and the third would require searching and searching would require stopping the march and stopping the march was something he did not want to do because the march was the rhythm and the rhythm was the plank and the plank was all that was between him and the water."
**Problem:** Run-on chain / repeated cliche. This single sentence contains ten "and" conjunctions and returns to the drowning-man/plank metaphor from earlier. The chain tries to replicate the mechanical quality of his thinking but reads as an exercise in clausal stacking.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence calls attention to its own construction. And the plank metaphor, already weak the first time, is weaker on reuse.
**Revision approach:** Break into shorter sentences that convey the same mental narrowing. "He did not build a fire. A fire required hands and focus and material. He had barely the first, none of the second. Searching for the third meant stopping the march, and stopping was something he could not afford." Cut the plank callback.
**Priority:** High

### 27.
**Passage:** "Not to the body. To the space where Hakiia had been."
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. The reader understands from context that speaking to a dead person means speaking to the memory, not the corpse. The clarification is unnecessary.
**Why it weakens:** It pre-interprets the emotional gesture for the reader.
**Revision approach:** Cut. "He spoke to Hakiia." is the right sentence. Let the dialogue do the rest.
**Priority:** Low

### 28.
**Passage:** "He spoke aloud because the silence had become a thing with mass and he needed to push against it with something, and the only something he had was words."
**Problem:** Explaining motivation for dialogue. The reader does not need to be told why a grieving man speaks aloud to his dead brother. The motivation is self-evident.
**Why it weakens:** It delays the dialogue, which is where the chapter's emotional core lives.
**Revision approach:** Cut the entire sentence. Go from "He spoke to Hakiia." directly to his words.
**Priority:** Medium

### 29.
**Passage:** "His voice was the only warm thing in the landscape and even it was cooling, thinning, becoming harder to push out of his chest."
**Problem:** Overwriting. Voice as "the only warm thing" is a metaphor. "Cooling, thinning, becoming harder to push" stacks three descriptors. The sentence strains for poetic effect in a passage that should be raw.
**Why it weakens:** The monologue is strong. The prose around it keeps reaching for lyricism where restraint would serve better.
**Revision approach:** Cut entirely, or compress: "His voice was thinning." One physical detail.
**Priority:** Low

### 30.
**Passage:** "The silence absorbed the words the way the snow absorbed the blood — without response, without judgment, without any indication that the words had changed anything in the world they had been spoken into."
**Problem:** Over-extended simile / explaining after showing. The silence/snow comparison works, but then the sentence explains the comparison three times: "without response, without judgment, without any indication..." The triple "without" is redundant.
**Why it weakens:** "The silence absorbed the words the way the snow absorbed the blood" is a strong line. Everything after the dash weakens it.
**Revision approach:** End the sentence at "blood." Period. Trust the simile.
**Priority:** Medium

### 31.
**Passage:** "and the grooves in his palms had deepened and the blood in the grooves had dried and the dried blood held the wood the way the wood held his weight and the system of hand and pole and pain and forward motion was the only system left that worked"
**Problem:** Triple-and chain / overwriting. Six chained clauses. "The dried blood held the wood the way the wood held his weight" is a chiasmus that calls attention to its craft. "The system of hand and pole and pain and forward motion" is a list pretending to be insight.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence is about his hands on the poles. It doesn't need to be a philosophical construction.
**Revision approach:** "His hands closed around the poles. The grooves in his palms had deepened, the dried blood holding the wood in place." Physical, specific, done.
**Priority:** Medium

### 32.
**Passage:** "changing categories with every mile — from person to weight to burden, from beloved to cargo, from the thing he could not leave to the thing he was not sure he could carry"
**Problem:** Triple parallel list / continuing after the point is made. Three paired progressions that all say the same thing: Hakiia's body is becoming an object. The first pair ("person to weight to burden") does the work. The second and third restate it.
**Why it weakens:** The final sentence of the chapter should land once, hard. Instead it lands three times, each softer.
**Revision approach:** Keep one. "From person to weight to burden" is the strongest because it's the most concrete. Cut the other two.
**Priority:** High

### 33.
**Passage:** "and Hurkuzak pulled because pulling was the only verb his body had left, and because the cabin was ahead, and because stopping meant something he was not yet ready to become"
**Problem:** Explaining motivation at the close. The chapter has shown us why he pulls. These three reasons ("only verb," "cabin was ahead," "stopping meant something") are thesis statements. The final clause ("something he was not yet ready to become") is vague — become what?
**Why it weakens:** The chapter earns its ending through action and physical detail. The final sentence defaults to abstraction.
**Revision approach:** End the chapter on action, not explanation. "Hurkuzak pulled." Or if more is needed: "Hurkuzak pulled, and the valley went on, and the cabin was somewhere ahead." Physical, forward-facing, unresolved.
**Priority:** High

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 10 is structurally sound and emotionally authentic. The central concept — carrying a dead brother through a frozen landscape while the act of carrying transforms the relationship from sacred to mechanical — is powerful and well-chosen. The physical detail is often excellent: the travois mechanics, the shifted arm, the frozen hand. The monologue to Hakiia is the chapter's emotional peak and mostly earns its place.

The chapter's primary weakness is **compulsive explanation**. Nearly every strong image or rendered moment is followed by a sentence or clause that tells the reader what the image means. The writer does not trust the scene to carry its own weight. This creates a consistent pattern: show, then explain, then explain the explanation. The result is a chapter that is roughly 30% longer than it needs to be, with the excess almost entirely composed of analytical commentary on its own images.

The secondary weakness is **chain syntax**. The chapter relies heavily on sentences built from four to ten clauses joined by "and." While this can convey mechanical exhaustion, it is used so uniformly that it becomes the chapter's default rhythm rather than a deliberate choice. The reader cannot feel the intended flatness because there is no contrasting rhythm to measure it against.

The tertiary weakness is **stacked restatements**. The chapter frequently states an idea, then restates it in different metaphorical language, then restates it again. This is most damaging in the final sentence, where three parallel progressions all describe the same transformation.

The chapter's best moments are its simplest: "He apologized." "He pulled." "Sorry." The monologue. These moments trust the reader. The revisions should extend that trust to the rest of the chapter.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. **Explaining after showing** (Issues 1, 5, 11, 13, 19, 22, 25, 27, 28, 33) — The most pervasive problem. Nearly every strong moment is followed by analytical commentary. Cutting these glosses would immediately improve the chapter.

2. **Summary paragraph after the hallucination sequence** (Issue 19) — The paragraph beginning "These were not visions" is the single most damaging passage. It converts rendered experience into essay.

3. **Drowning-man/plank cliche used twice** (Issues 15, 26) — A stock simile that appears once and then returns. Both instances should be cut.

4. **Triple parallel lists in the final sentence** (Issue 32) — The chapter's closing image repeats itself three times. One progression would land with power; three land with diminishing returns.

5. **Run-on chain sentences** (Issues 2, 4, 8, 26, 31) — Sentences with five to ten "and" conjunctions flatten the prose into monotone. Breaking these would add rhythmic variation.

6. **Over-explaining the dead-weight concept** (Issues 7, 11) — The insight about dead bodies not cooperating is explained in a full paragraph and then restated later. Once is enough.

7. **Over-explained silence after "Sorry"** (Issue 13) — The word spoken to a dead brother is powerful. The five-fold repetition of "silence" that follows drowns it.

8. **Ungrounded aphorisms** (Issue 20) — "Exhaustion, like pain, was a form of time" and similar constructions reach for profundity without physical grounding.

9. **Stacked metaphors for the dead body** (Issue 6) — "Form without function, mass without will, the physical residue..." — three metaphors for one idea. Keep the strongest, cut the rest.

10. **Explaining motivation before dialogue** (Issue 28) — The monologue to Hakiia is delayed by a sentence explaining why he speaks aloud. The reader already knows.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weaknesses in this chapter:

1. **The gloss reflex.** Every concrete image triggers an abstract explanation. The writer renders a moment, then steps outside it to tell the reader what it means. This is the chapter's dominant flaw.

2. **Chain syntax as default.** The "and...and...and" construction is used for almost every long sentence, regardless of whether the moment calls for flatness, urgency, or lyricism. The chapter has one rhythm.

3. **Triple restatement.** Ideas arrive in threes — three metaphors, three "felt like" clauses, three parallel progressions. The first is usually the strongest. The second is tolerable. The third weakens the first.

4. **Distrust of silence.** The chapter's best moments are its shortest ("He apologized." / "He pulled."). But the writer cannot resist filling the silence around them with commentary. The impulse to explain is strongest precisely when restraint would be most powerful.

5. **Returning to spent images.** The drowning/plank simile, the dead-weight observation, the mercy/killing duality — each is used, then returned to, weakening both instances.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut all explaining-after-showing.** Go through every paragraph and identify the sentence that explains what the preceding image means. Cut it. This single pass will remove roughly 25% of the chapter's word count and will strengthen almost every passage.

2. **Delete the hallucination meta-commentary paragraph** ("These were not visions. They were gaps..."). The hallucinations speak for themselves.

3. **Remove both instances of the drowning/plank simile.** Replace with nothing. The sentences before each simile are strong enough.

4. **Trim the final sentence to one progression.** "From person to weight to burden" — cut the other two parallel lists.

5. **Break the longest chain sentences.** Target any sentence with more than four "and" conjunctions. Break into two or three sentences. Vary rhythm.

6. **Cut the dead-weight lecture paragraph down to two sentences.** Keep the insight. Remove the physics lesson.

7. **Trim triple constructions to singles.** "Felt wrong, felt like carelessness, felt like something Hakiia would have criticized" becomes just the last one. Apply throughout.

8. **Remove ungrounded aphorisms.** "Exhaustion was a form of time" and similar. If the line doesn't live in the body, cut it.

9. **Simplify the silence after "Sorry."** Let the word land. Cut the five-fold "silence" analysis.

10. **Preserve what works.** The monologue. "He apologized." The hand on the travois. The mechanical adjustment of the body. The face-as-weight reversal. These are the chapter's spine. Build the revision around them.
