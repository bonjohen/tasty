# Chapter 16: What Winter Leaves Behind

The snow did not melt. It compacted. The ice did not thaw. It thinned at the edges. The sky, which had been a solid gray for weeks, began to show occasional breaks where a color that was not quite blue appeared and vanished. Winter was not ending. But it was loosening.

Hurkuzak felt it in the cabin. The walls held heat longer. The fire demanded less. The draft through the gap in the door frame was still cold but had lost its killing edge — something in it now carried the smell of wet stone, of earth not quite thawed but no longer frozen solid. He did not know when spring would come, or whether it would come in time.

The voice had continued.

It was more frequent now and harder to dismiss. The corrections had graduated from practical adjustments — the fire, the door, the sleeping position — to opinions about how he moved. He walked too carefully, it informed him. He placed his feet with the deliberation of a person who expected the ground to betray him, and the placement wasted energy and announced his presence to anything listening. A man in the mountains moved by committing to each step, not by testing each step. Hurkuzak moved like prey.

He did not argue with the voice. Arguing required acknowledging it as something separate from himself, and the acknowledgment was a door he was not ready to open.

But the voice had more to say.

He was sitting by the hearth on a day when the wind was quiet, and his mind drifted to the journey — the trail before the cliff, the path Hakiia had chosen along the ridgeline. He was thinking about the argument — the trees and the ridge, Hakiia's insistence on the exposed path. The memory was his own. It carried the texture of his own thinking: hurt, bewildered, the old confusion of a man trying to understand why his brother chose speed over mercy.

Then the correction came.

The ridge gave clear eyes in every direction — far enough to see wolves before they saw us. The trees cut that to nothing. The dog was a cost. I chose the cost that included seeing what was coming.

Hakiia had said nothing about sightlines on the ridge. The justification was new.

He had not thought about sightlines. He had not looked at the ridge as a hunter's position. He had thought about the dog.

Someone else had thought about the wolves.

He sat beside the fire. The knowledge was specific — open ground, the dog dismissed as a cost. Survival instinct did not supply tactical reasoning about terrain choices made weeks ago. The voice knew things only the person who had made those decisions would know — decisions made for reasons that person had never shared.

He pushed back. He told the voice that it was not a voice. That it was exhaustion, isolation, the wreckage of a winter that had ground him down to whatever was left at the bottom. That what he had just heard was his own mind filling the silence with shapes — his own mind reconstructing Hakiia's reasoning from what he already knew, the same knowledge any man who'd walked that ridge could have pieced together.

The voice did not argue back. It waited. The patience was Hakiia's — the certainty that did not need to argue because it knew the other person would come to it. Hurkuzak recognized that patience the way a body recognizes a fist.

He did not name it. To name it would be to say his dead brother's mind was alive inside his own, and he did not want to think the thing he was starting to think — because if he thought it, there was no way to unthink it. Because it might be true.

The voice had other opinions. It disliked the way Hurkuzak ate — slowly, carefully, with the deliberation of a person who was still thinking about each bite. Eat or don't eat. The hesitation is worse than the act. One evening he pressed his hand against the granite wall behind the hearth and found it warm — the rock holding the day's heat and giving it back slowly in the dark. The thought arrived, unbidden, that whoever built this cabin had known that. Had chosen the granite face for that reason. Had placed the hearth against it so the stone would work through the night after the fire burned low. Hurkuzak had never thought about a cabin this way.

He caught his reflection in the water pot one morning. The features were the same — the jaw, the cheekbones, the scar on his palm reflected in the water beside his face. But the softness Hakiia had tried to burn away was diminished. Not gone, but overlaid with something harder — a stillness that watched. He held his face the way Hakiia had held his face: as a surface that gave nothing away.

He looked away.

He left the cabin.

Not permanently. Not as a departure. As a test. He walked past the tree line, down the slope toward the valley where he'd dragged the travois. The walking was easy. The landscape was readable — the terrain speaking in a language he now understood, where the wind had carved the surface into shapes that told you which way the wind always came from.

He could track. He had never learned to. The skills felt less like knowledge and more like someone else's memory. The logic was circular. The circle was a trap, and the trap was his life.

He caught himself moving like Hakiia — not the same gait, but the same principle: the absolute refusal to apologize to the landscape for occupying it.

He stopped in the snow. The world was readable. His body was capable. He thought about the alcove — what it had contained, what it had cost.

He walked for an hour. When he returned, he stood in the doorway and looked at the room that had been his world. The last of Hakiia's body was gone. He had used everything — bone for broth, marrow for sustenance, the final sinew boiled soft. The alcove was empty. He looked at the bare stone floor where his brother's body had lain. He had cleaned it himself — on his knees, methodical, final. The alcove was just a room again. Cold stone, empty air, the smell of nothing.

His brother had passed through him entirely — gone from the floor, carried in the body that stood in the doorway. Hakiia was gone from the cabin. Hakiia was not gone from Hurkuzak.

He closed the alcove door for the last time.

The days continued. The light grew. The snow compacted further. The first sounds of running water came from somewhere below the cabin — a stream or a seep released by the earliest loosening of ice.

The season was turning. The cabin's grip was loosening. Somewhere beyond the mountains was a world he would need to reenter — carrying Hakiia inside his body and a name that no longer fit. He did not know what he was becoming. Flesh and grief were what it had cost.

He stood in the light and felt the warmth on his face and the watchfulness behind his eyes. He would leave soon. He did not yet know what he would carry with him. But he knew it was more than he had brought.
