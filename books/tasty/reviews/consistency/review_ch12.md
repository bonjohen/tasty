# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 12: What the Cabin Holds

**Chapter:** 12
**Section:** 6
**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Reviewer note:** Applied rules from `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`. Setting baseline: pre-industrial dark fantasy. No engines, firearms, or electricity. Voice register: literary dark, tending toward McCarthy/Morrison — precise, atmospheric, weighted; not ornamental archaic, not genre-default. Anachronism test applied to language, metaphor, imagery, social framing, and narratorial assumption.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "he could feel it enter him like fuel entering a stove"

**Problem:** Modern mechanical metaphor — fuel/stove system framing.

**Why it's out of place:** "Fuel" as an abstract noun denoting combustible material fed to a consuming system is a post-industrial register word. In a pre-industrial cabin setting, the character would conceptualize this in terms of fire, wood, and heat, not the mechanized fuel-delivery metaphor of engines and furnaces. "Stove" is borderline — iron stoves existed in pre-industrial eras — but the phrase "fuel entering a stove" pairs a modern technical abstraction (fuel as generic combustible) with a mechanical loading concept that reads industrial. The whole metaphor feels like the author's modern framing rather than Hurkuzak's.

**Better direction:** Ground the metaphor in what a person of this world would feel and know. Something like: "He could feel it enter him the way dry wood catches — not warmth yet, only the promise of it" — or simply cut the metaphor and stay in pure physical sensation, which is what the rest of the prose does well.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "He watched the infection the way he watched the grain supply — as a number that moved in one direction and could not be reversed by attention alone."

**Problem:** Quantified/clinical framing — "a number that moved in one direction."

**Why it's out of place:** This is the language of metrics, data points, and managed systems. "A number that moved in one direction" is the cognitive frame of someone who tracks things on ledgers or spreadsheets — a modern administrative or scientific way of perceiving a biological process. Hurkuzak is a pre-industrial man alone in a hunting cabin. He would not perceive infection and grain as parallel quantities on an invisible dashboard. He might feel dread about both, might weigh them against each other intuitively, but the abstraction to "a number" imposes modern quantitative thinking onto his experience.

**Better direction:** Keep the parallel structure (infection / grain supply), which is effective, but render both in concrete sensory terms rather than metric abstraction. For example: "He watched the infection the way he watched the grain — as a thing that moved in one direction, worsening by degrees no care could stop."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "His body was eating itself — muscle first, then stored fat, then the deeper reserves a body hoards against starvation."

**Problem:** Clinical/physiological framing of starvation — "stored fat," "deeper reserves."

**Why it's out of place:** "Stored fat" and "deeper reserves" belong to modern nutritional science and physiology. The sequencing — muscle, then stored fat, then deeper reserves — reads like a summary of how a modern person understands the medical stages of starvation (catabolism, lipolysis, etc.). A pre-industrial character experiencing starvation would feel it, not diagnose it. He would know hunger, weakness, the shrinking of himself, the way his clothes hung differently, the way his hands shook — but not in the vocabulary of nutritional science.

**Better direction:** Stay in felt experience rather than physiological taxonomy. The prose surrounding this passage does this beautifully — "the hunger that had moved in" is excellent. This sentence should match that register. Something like: "His body was eating itself — he could feel it in the way his arms had gone thin, in the shaking, in the new hollows where there had been none."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "calories were what his body was demanding"
> "The calories were real but they were not enough."

**Problem:** Modern dietary/scientific term — "calories."

**Why it's out of place:** "Calorie" as a unit of food energy is a 19th-century scientific measurement concept. It belongs entirely to modern nutritional thinking. A pre-industrial character has no concept of calories. He would know sustenance, strength, what keeps a body alive — but not the abstracted energy-unit vocabulary of modern dietetics.

**Better direction:** Replace with sensory/functional language Hurkuzak would actually use. "The fat was old and slightly rancid but it was sustenance, and his body was demanding sustenance" — or more concisely, "it would keep him alive, or work toward it." The second instance ("The calories were real but they were not enough") could simply be cut or replaced: "The fat was there, and not enough."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "Time without structure was its own kind of torture."

**Problem:** Modern psychological framing — "time without structure."

**Why it's out of place:** "Structure" used to describe the psychological necessity of routine is the language of modern self-help, therapy, and behavioral psychology. The concept — that unstructured time produces psychological distress — is real and universal, but the specific phrasing "time without structure" imposes a modern managerial/clinical lens. Hurkuzak would not conceptualize this as a structural problem with time. He would simply feel the weight, the silence, the crawling formlessness of days with no shape.

**Better direction:** The sentence can go entirely. The prose around it does the work better — "doing kept the silence from becoming pressure" is the right register. Or rephrase entirely in felt terms: "Idle hours were their own weight."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "the thought existed at the edge of his mind the way a wolf exists at the edge of a clearing"

**Problem:** Not a consistency issue — this is one of the chapter's best lines. Flagging only to confirm it passes.

No issue. The wolf metaphor is setting-native, earned by the preceding wolf attack in Section 1, and psychologically precise. Do not change.

**Priority: N/A (no problem)**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "arguing with you was like arguing with weather"

**Problem:** Minor tonal check — modern idiom risk.

**Why it's borderline:** "Arguing with weather" is a natural, setting-plausible folk metaphor that could easily exist in a pre-industrial culture. Weather is universal. This is not a modern metaphor. However, the idiomatic deployment — the easy, almost conversational way Hurkuzak reaches for it — sits slightly closer to modern informal register than the surrounding prose does. This is a very low-stakes issue.

**Better direction:** No change required. The metaphor earns its place and reads as native to the world. Flagging only for completeness.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "he was not sure he was a person who expected to survive long enough for the count to matter"

**Problem:** Modern psychological self-distancing construction.

**Why it's out of place:** "A person who expected to survive" is self-referential in a slightly modern, therapeutic framing — the kind of detached self-analysis ("I am not sure I am the kind of person who...") that belongs to contemporary introspective idiom. The emotional reality is correct, but the framing is mildly anachronistic in register.

**Better direction:** Collapse it into something more direct and less self-analytical: "He scratched marks on the wall, not because he expected to survive, but because counting was something to do." The current construction is not egregious but it inflates a simple moment with modern psychological vocabulary.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "deep, infectious heat"

**Problem:** Minor check — "infectious" as an adjective for the wound's heat.

**Why it's borderline:** "Infectious" is technically correct for the pre-industrial setting — infection is observable without modern germ theory. A person can know a wound has gone bad by touch, smell, and feel. The word does not require scientific framing. This is not an anachronism. Flagging only because it sits close to clinical register.

**Better direction:** No change required. The phrase "infectious heat" is setting-appropriate — it names a felt quality, not a microbiological diagnosis.

**Priority: Low (no change needed)**

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "The scars were the only things healing. Everything else was holding steady or getting worse, and the difference was food, and the food was days, and the days were running out."

**Problem:** Minor check — "holding steady."

**Why it's borderline:** "Holding steady" has a faint modern clinical/navigational ring (as in instrument readings holding steady, or a patient holding steady). In this context it reads more as idiomatic expression of stability, which any pre-industrial person would understand. Not a true anachronism.

**Better direction:** No change strictly required, but could be replaced with something like "everything else was worsening or not yet worsened" if the slight clinical tinge is a concern. Very low priority.

**Priority: Low**

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 12 is a well-controlled piece of prose that largely succeeds at maintaining its pre-industrial, psychologically grounded register. The voice is literary and restrained; the physical details (stone hearth, dry wood, clay pot, iron pan, rough-woven blankets, rendered fat, moldy grain) are convincingly pre-modern and specific. The chapter's emotional architecture is strong: the cold-before-grief opening, the practical carrying of Hakiia's body into the alcove, the monologue to the absent brother, and the final image of the thought-at-the-edge-of-the-clearing wolf are all excellent.

The consistency problems are concentrated in a small cluster of modern scientific and clinical terms — "fuel," "calories," "stored fat," "deeper reserves," "time without structure," "a number that moved in one direction" — that repeatedly import modern quantitative and analytical thinking into a character who would have no access to those frames. These are not voice problems (the chapter otherwise has a strong, unified voice) and not worldbuilding problems (the physical setting is accurate). They are isolated vocabulary intrusions where the author's modern frame of reference leaks into the narration.

The fix is surgical: replace the clinical/quantitative terms with sensory and functional language that Hurkuzak, as a pre-industrial man, would actually experience.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring world-language mismatch in this chapter is a specific and narrow one: modern scientific and quantitative vocabulary imported into a character's interiority. The problem is not in the physical setting (which is accurately pre-industrial) or in the dialogue (which is naturalistic and effective) or in the story beats. It is in a handful of moments where the narrator describes bodily or environmental processes using the analytical framework of someone who has read about nutrition, physiology, or systems management. The affected terms are: calories, fuel, stored fat, deeper reserves, a number that moved in one direction, and time without structure.

This is a second-draft problem, not a structural one. The author has the world, the voice, and the emotional architecture right. The intrusions are brief and correctable without any prose surgery beyond sentence-level substitution.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace both instances of "calories"** with functional/sensory substitutes ("sustenance," "enough to keep him alive," or cut). Two uses, both High.

2. **Revise "He could feel it enter him like fuel entering a stove"** — replace the industrial metaphor with setting-native sensation (fire/wood/warmth framing, or pure bodily feeling).

3. **Revise the starvation-taxonomy sentence** — cut "stored fat" and "deeper reserves" and replace with felt physical experience: thinning arms, shaking hands, hollows where fullness had been.

4. **Revise "a number that moved in one direction"** in the infection/grain comparison — keep the parallel structure but replace the metric abstraction with concrete worsening language.

5. **Cut or rephrase "Time without structure was its own kind of torture"** — the surrounding prose makes the point better; the sentence is expendable or needs register-appropriate rephrasing.

6. **Optional low-priority clean-up:** "he was not sure he was a person who expected to survive" — collapse into simpler direct statement.

---

## Failure Mode Note

This chapter is failing through **isolated vocabulary intrusions**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The narrator's voice is consistent and effective throughout. The world is rendered accurately. The character's emotional and physical experience is grounded. The problems are a small cluster of scientific and quantitative terms that belong to the author's modern English rather than to this world's language. No structural revision is needed. Surgical word-level changes to five or six phrases will resolve all High-priority issues.
