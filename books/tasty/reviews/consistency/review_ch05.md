# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 5: What the Wolves Smell

**World:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Section:** 2 — The Road Collapse and the Fall
**Reviewed against:** `poor-consistency.md` rules, `book/CLAUDE.md`, `section_02_outline.md`

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "He carried what he had back to Hakiia and laid it out in the snow and Hakiia looked at it the way a general looks at the remains of an army after a defeat."

**Problem:** Modern simile frame — "a general looks at the remains of an army after a defeat."

**Why it breaks immersion:** The simile imports a recognizably modern (or at minimum, literate-military) conceptual frame: the general as an abstracted strategic commander surveying organizational destruction. The world here is pre-industrial and the brothers are hunters and trappers on a wilderness trip, not soldiers. Even if a military caste exists in this world, "general" is a rank-title with modern organizational resonance (chain of command, coordinated armies, strategic command) that jars against the physical, immediate, blood-and-snow texture established throughout the chapter. More practically, the simile steps outside Hurkuzak's POV register — he thinks in the visceral and immediate; this metaphor imports an outsider's abstract frame.

**Suggested direction:** Ground the comparison in something Hurkuzak would actually know. Hunters and trappers read ruin in concrete terms: what can still be used, what is lost for good. A tight, behaviorally specific image — "the way a man reads damage and counts what remains" or simply the fact of Hakiia's eyes moving over the laid-out items one by one, face saying nothing — would serve the prose better than the generalship simile.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "The temperature drop was not gradual — it came in steps, each one lower than the last, as though the air were descending a staircase."

**Problem:** The staircase simile carries a structural/architectural register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A staircase is an indoor, constructed object associated with buildings of some permanence and design. While stairs exist in pre-industrial worlds, the simile reaches for something abstract and architectural — a measured descent in increments — that feels more modern in its conceptual precision than Hurkuzak's experience in a frozen wilderness basin warranted by blood loss and cold should allow. The staircase simile is not egregiously anachronistic, but it does subtly shift register from embodied cold to an image drawn from interior, built space.

**Suggested direction:** Keep the sense of step-change cold without the architectural frame. Options: the cold dropped in layers, each one harder than the last; or the cold tightened in degrees, each darker than the one before; or a purely physical description — the warmth that had been working against the cold ran out by stages, and when it was gone it was gone all at once.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "Use the rope in a figure pattern, not a spiral, because a spiral would work loose under load and drop the crosspiece and then the whole thing would fold."

**Problem:** "Figure pattern" is incomplete and implies a technical/craft vocabulary that may be slightly modern in register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The instruction "figure pattern" appears to be a fragment of "figure-eight pattern" or "figure-of-eight lashing" — a specific knot and lashing terminology that exists in the craft tradition of rope work but, as rendered here, sounds truncated and faintly modern-technical. Hakiia, even as a practical outdoorsman with precise knowledge, would likely describe the pattern in physical terms natural to the world: cross it twice, double back, reverse the loop — rather than by naming it as a named pattern type. The phrasing suggests the author is gesturing at technical vocabulary without grounding it fully in the world's idiom.

**Suggested direction:** Describe the lashing in physical-action terms rather than pattern-name terms. "Cross it twice and double back, not a long spiral — a spiral works loose under load." This preserves Hakiia's competence and precision without importing a craft-catalog naming convention.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "His fingers drove into the frozen ground."

**Problem:** Minor physical implausibility worth flagging.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Not strictly a vocabulary problem, but a sensory-consistency one. The snow has been established as crusted, frozen, blood-soaked — a surface described earlier as cracking "like thin glass." Fingers driving into frozen ground would require unusual force and implies the soil is loose or unfrozen, which contradicts the established terrain. This is a minor continuity crack: Hakiia in extremis is grabbing at the snow, which is the more likely surface. If the snow has crusted over frozen earth, "drove into" overstates penetration.

**Suggested direction:** "His fingers drove into the snow beside him" or "his fingers clawed into the crusted snow" — keeping the desperate grip without implying soil penetration through ice.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "The sound was the sound of tearing. Wet, muscular, deliberate."

**Problem:** "Muscular" as a sound descriptor.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Muscular" applied to a sound is a modern literary-stylistic usage — a transferred epithet that belongs to a specific contemporary prose register (literary fiction, MFA workshop sensibility). In context it is meant to communicate thickness, force, embodied effort — the sound has physical weight. But "muscular" applied to sound is an abstraction that does not arise naturally from within the world. Hurkuzak, watching wolves tear at frozen dog carcasses, would experience the sound in more immediate, less figurative terms: its wetness, its rhythm, its wrongness — not its metaphorical muscularity.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with a concrete sensory description: "The sound was tearing — wet, dense, rhythmic, the sound of something that ate without urgency." Or cut to two adjectives that stay physical: "Wet and deliberate." The existing three-fragment sentence loses nothing essential by trading "muscular" for something less stylistically self-aware.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "The feeding was systematic, the pack watching the living things at the center while the dead things lasted."

**Problem:** "Systematic" imports a modern organizational/behavioral-science register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Systematic" belongs to a vocabulary of planned, organized procedure — it is the word of a naturalist observer or a bureaucratic description, not a hunter or a man bleeding in a snow basin watching wolves eat his dogs. Hurkuzak would experience wolf feeding not as "systematic" but as purposeful, patient, and deeply frightening in its efficiency. The word pulls the narration up out of his body and into a cooler observational register that does not match his POV throughout the rest of the chapter.

**Suggested direction:** "The feeding was patient, each wolf holding its place while the dead things lasted" — or simply "They ate without rushing, the pack watching the living things while the dead things held." This preserves the observation without the behavioral-science vocabulary.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "his gaze held the steady appraisal of something deciding whether another thing was food."

**Problem:** "Appraisal" carries modern commercial/evaluative register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Appraisal" is a word from systems of assessment — property valuation, job performance review, formal evaluation. Applied to a wolf's gaze, it is a modern metaphor that aestheticizes predation into a deliberate economic act. The sentence is trying to convey the coldness of the wolf's attention — the calculating patience of something weighing risk against reward — but "appraisal" is the word of a market or a courtroom, not a wilderness. The sentence is otherwise strong; the single word is the problem.

**Suggested direction:** "his gaze held the patience of something deciding whether another thing was worth the cost" — or, more directly: "his gaze was the gaze of something that was deciding." The decision itself, not the formal word for the act of assessing, is what the prose needs.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "Hurkuzak felt the bruises forming in real time."

**Problem:** "In real time" is a modern technical idiom.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "In real time" comes from computing, broadcasting, and processing systems — it describes data or events occurring without lag relative to a reference clock. It is entirely modern in origin and has no place in pre-industrial diction, even as colloquialism. Its use here is jarring: it is a very precise anachronism, the kind of phrase that the author's modern frame of reference has slipped into the text unmarked. It tries to convey "as it was happening, moment by moment" — which is a legitimate thing to convey, but there are many ways to do so without importing this particular idiom.

**Suggested direction:** "Hurkuzak felt the bruises forming under the grip, slow and certain" — or "he felt his brother's fingers printing themselves into the muscle" — or simply "his fingers dug into the muscle and left something that would be there tomorrow."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "he could feel their attention on his back like the weight of something deciding how much longer it was willing to wait."

**Problem:** "Willing to wait" anthropomorphizes in a slightly modern psychological register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is a borderline issue. Predator attention is real, and Hurkuzak would feel it. "Willing to wait" projects a moral-psychological vocabulary onto animal patience — willingness implies choice framed in terms of consent and deliberation, which carries a modern interior-life assumption. It is not egregiously wrong, but "willing" subtly lifts the wolves into a human decisional framework rather than keeping them as purely instinctual things. The sentence is mostly strong; the word "willing" is doing slightly the wrong work.

**Suggested direction:** "like the weight of something measuring how much longer its patience would hold" — or "like the weight of something that had not yet decided." This preserves the menace and the wolf-attention without the consent-vocabulary.

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "The blood in the snow was a map that pointed in one direction, and the direction was them."

**Problem:** "A map that pointed" — minor register concern.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is the weakest flag in the chapter — the metaphor itself is not anachronistic, since maps exist in pre-industrial worlds. The slight concern is that "a map that pointed" is a somewhat modern-literary closing image, a rhetorical device that feels consciously literary rather than emerging from Hurkuzak's in-the-moment POV. The closing beat of the chapter is otherwise earned; this is a stylistic register note rather than a true consistency violation.

**Suggested direction:** This is a Low issue and may not need revision. If any adjustment: "The blood in the snow ran in one direction, and the direction was them" — removing the map metaphor and keeping the trailing sentence's rhythm, which is what gives it force.

**Priority: Low**

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 5 is one of the cleanest chapters in the manuscript from a setting-consistency standpoint. The prose is grounded in physical materials — rope, leather, bone, snow, blood, wood — and Hurkuzak's POV stays embodied and immediate throughout. Hakiia's direction of the travois-building is precisely rendered and tonally right. The wolf sequence is restrained and effective.

The consistency problems that do exist are vocabulary-level, not worldbuilding-level: the chapter is not confused about where it is or what technology is available. The failures are modern idioms and modern-register words that slip through because the author's frame of reference is present, not because the world is incoherent. None of the ten issues flagged above signal a structural problem. They are line-level corrections.

The most significant single anachronism is "in real time" (Issue 8), which is an exact and unambiguous modern import. The highest-impact prose failures are "muscular" as a sound descriptor (Issue 5), "systematic" as a wolf behavioral descriptor (Issue 6), and "in real time" (Issue 8) — all three impose modern observational or technical vocabulary onto a scene that otherwise maintains strong pre-industrial register.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring vulnerability in this chapter is the **modern observer slipping into third-person narration as a cool, detached voice**. When the prose is inside Hurkuzak's body — gripping bone, feeling cold, moving rope — it is excellent. When it pulls slightly back to describe something from outside his experience (wolf behavior, temperature change, the quality of a sound), it reaches for vocabulary and conceptual frames from the author's modern sensibility rather than from within the world. "Systematic," "muscular" (as applied to sound), "in real time," and "appraisal" are all words that belong to a modern observer making careful notes, not to a man bleeding in a snow basin.

The second pattern is **similes and metaphors that import modern infrastructure or institutional vocabulary** — the general and his army, the staircase. These are not anachronistic objects (generals and stairs exist in pre-industrial worlds) but the conceptual frame they carry — command-and-control military hierarchy, architectural precision — drifts modern.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace "in real time"** — this is an exact anachronism with no pre-industrial basis. Replace with language that conveys simultaneity and accumulation through sensation rather than temporal-system vocabulary.

2. **Replace "muscular" in the wolf-feeding passage** — trade the transferred epithet for a concrete sensory descriptor that stays inside Hurkuzak's embodied experience.

3. **Replace "systematic"** — find a word or construction that conveys the wolves' patient, purposeful feeding without importing a behavioral-science register.

4. **Revise the "general" simile** — ground the comparison in something native to Hurkuzak's experience and the world's material culture.

5. **Replace "appraisal"** — a one-word swap to something that conveys calculation without the evaluative-professional register.

6. **Fix "fingers drove into the frozen ground"** — correct to snow or crusted snow, consistent with the terrain established in the scene.

7. **Low-priority (optional):** Address the staircase simile, "figure pattern" phrasing, "willing to wait," and the closing map metaphor if a clean revision pass warrants it.

---

## Failure Type

This chapter's consistency problems are **isolated wording problems** — the worldbuilding is coherent, the setting logic is maintained, and the narrator-voice is largely stable. The failures are concentrated in a handful of vocabulary choices and one simile where the author's modern frame of reference briefly replaced the world's native register. These require line-level revision only. No structural or worldbuilding intervention is needed.
