# Setting Consistency Review: Chapter 7 — The Weight of What Is Left

**Reviewed against:** `book/CLAUDE.md` (story rules), `book/docs/section_03_outline.md`, `book/docs/01_story_beats.md`, `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Chapter function:** Aftermath of the wolf fight. Hurkuzak tends Hakiia, they share a near-tender moment, and Hurkuzak hauls the travois toward the cabin as Hakiia's authority and vitality decline.

---

## Individual Issues

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "a body borrowing from something deeper, something that would need to be repaid but not yet"

**Setting-consistency problem:** Financial/debt metaphor with modern economic framing.

**Why it feels out of place:** The phrase "needed to be repaid" frames the body's expenditure of reserves as a ledger transaction. This borrowing-and-repayment idiom is not inherently modern, but "repaid" is the specific word choice that carries a mercantile ledger register — a financial accounting frame rather than a bodily or natural one. The concept itself (the body drawing on reserves it cannot sustain) is valid for any era; the phrasing feels slightly commercial.

**Better direction:** "a body borrowing from something it would be made to answer for later" — or rephrase entirely toward physical sensation: "the slow grinding effort of a body drawing from something it had not earned and would eventually be made to give back in pain."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "the cold worked against the spark with patience older than patience"

**Setting-consistency problem:** Abstract philosophical formulation with a modern literary register.

**Why it feels out of place:** "Patience older than patience" is the kind of reflexive intensification that belongs to contemporary literary prose. It is not anachronistic in a technological sense, but it is an authorial construction that draws attention to itself in a way that interrupts the utilitarian, ground-level interiority that characterizes the rest of the chapter. The line aims for mythic but lands on decorative. More crucially, the formulation frames the cold's opposition as willed patience — an abstraction that feels imported from outside the scene rather than observed from within it.

**Better direction:** Keep the cold as physical fact rather than philosophical presence. "The cold killed the spark twice before the third caught." Or: "The wood was wet and the cold pressed against every small flame until he stopped trying to be fast about it."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "the messages between his mind and his fingers were traveling through something thicker than usual"

**Setting-consistency problem:** Neurological / communications-systems metaphor.

**Why it feels out of place:** "Messages traveling between the mind and the fingers" describes nerve conduction in terms that belong to a model of the nervous system as a signal-transmission network. This is a modern scientific framing. A person in a pre-industrial setting experiencing impaired grip from a bite wound would not think of the problem this way. He would feel the stiffness, the slowness, the wrong response — but would not conceptualize it as messages traveling through a medium.

**Better direction:** Render the sensation directly without the transmission metaphor. "When he flexed his hand the response came late and only partway, as though the fingers were deciding independently whether to answer." Or: "His fingers answered slowly, wrong, as if the bite had put something between his will and his hand."

**Priority:** High

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "authority required a mind that could track the situation"

**Setting-consistency problem:** "Track the situation" is light military/tactical management idiom.

**Why it feels out of place:** "Track the situation" is a phrase from tactical and operational contexts — modern managerial or military language for maintaining situational awareness. It is not a dramatic anachronism, but it does carry a contemporary register that slightly distances the narration from the pre-industrial physical world Hurkuzak inhabits. Hurkuzak would perceive Hakiia's lapsing authority in physical and relational terms, not situational-assessment terms.

**Better direction:** "authority required a mind that could still hold the ground between one moment and the next" — or: "Hakiia's authority had always required his attention, and his attention was no longer continuous."

**Priority:** Low

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "the cold worked against the spark with patience older than patience. But the fire caught, eventually, because he would not let it fail"

**Setting-consistency problem (additional):** "He would not let it fail" is a psychologically modern framing of willpower.

**Why it feels out of place:** The phrase "would not let it fail" personifies the fire as a thing that could be held by will — an agent-of-failure that Hurkuzak forbids. This is a recognizably modern motivational framing. It works in a contemporary literary register; in the world of this book, where things simply happen or do not happen and humans survive by material effort rather than psychological assertion, the phrasing feels slightly promotional. The chapter's strength elsewhere is in removing Hurkuzak's interiority from the abstract and keeping it physical.

**Better direction:** "the fire caught, eventually, because he kept trying past the point where trying felt useful." Or simply: "The fire caught."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "he understood this in the way he understood the cold — not as a thought but as a fact that had entered him and settled"

**Setting-consistency problem:** The epistemological distinction ("not as a thought but as a fact") is a modern phenomenological construction.

**Why it feels out of place:** Drawing a deliberate distinction between "a thought" and "a fact that enters and settles" is a philosophical frame about modes of knowing. It is not anachronistic in the way that firearms or electricity would be, but it is a narrator's metacognitive framing that sits slightly above the character's perspective in a way the rest of the chapter avoids. The observation is also not quite accurate to the character: earlier in the same paragraph Hurkuzak is clearly not articulating internal abstractions — he is simply pulling. Introducing a meta-observation about how he processes information at that moment breaks the chapter's established mode of narration-as-physical-experience.

**Better direction:** Cut the explanatory clause entirely. "He understood this in the way he understood the cold" is strong and sufficient. The parenthetical explanation weakens it. Let the simile carry the weight alone.

**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14

**Passage:**
> "the landscape still held the shape of their attention — empty now, but watchful"

**Setting-consistency problem:** "The shape of their attention" is a slightly abstracted perception metaphor.

**Why it feels out of place:** Describing a landscape as holding the "shape of attention" is a modern literary construction — the idea that space is semantically charged by prior event or focus. It is not anachronistic in a technological sense, but it belongs to the authorial register rather than Hurkuzak's physical perception. Hurkuzak is a pre-industrial demon hauling a travois; he would perceive the landscape as threatening or quiet, near or far, passable or not. Attributing a shape-of-attention to it is the author's observation projected onto the character's experience.

**Better direction:** "The landscape was quiet in the way that comes after predators leave — the silence of having already made their decision." Or simply: "Nothing moved. The wolves had gone, but Hurkuzak did not trust the stillness."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 15

**Passage:**
> "The world absorbs violence quickly."

**Setting-consistency problem:** General aphorism in authorial voice, mild modern register.

**Why it feels out of place:** The sentence is an authorial observation posed as universal truth, in present tense inside a past-tense chapter. It belongs to the essay register rather than the narrative register. It is not technologically anachronistic, but it is voiced from a position above the story. In a chapter that otherwise keeps the narration close to physical experience, this sentence steps back to a philosophical observation point. The observation is not wrong, but it is the author thinking rather than the scene showing.

**Better direction:** Cut it, or translate it into Hurkuzak's physical perception. "The snow had already half-covered the dead dogs. By morning, the valley would look clean." Let the scene make the point.

**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 7 is almost entirely consistent with its pre-industrial setting. There are no gross anachronisms — no technology, no modern idiom at the level of firearms or institutional language, no dialogue that sounds contemporary. The chapter is doing its structural work well and the prose is disciplined.

The setting-consistency problems that exist are all in the narrator's register rather than the scene's content. They fall into two types:

1. **Neurological / systems metaphors**: One clear instance where the narrator describes impaired nerve function in terms of signal transmission ("messages traveling between the mind and the fingers"). This is the only High-priority issue and it requires a direct fix.

2. **Author's metacognitive or philosophical voice entering from above the scene**: Several passages where the narration momentarily shifts from physical experience to conceptual observation — "patience older than patience," "not as a thought but as a fact," "the world absorbs violence quickly." These belong to a modern literary essayist register that sits slightly above where Hurkuzak's interiority should be.

There are no immersion-breaking idioms, no technology mismatches, no characters who sound contemporary. The chapter's prose is controlled and the physical detail is accurate to a pre-industrial winter wilderness. The problems are subtle and mostly in the Medium-to-Low range.

## Pattern Summary

The chapter's recurring weakness is **narrator-voice drift**: the author's modern literary sensibility briefly overrides Hurkuzak's physical, pre-intellectual perspective in several passages. These lapses all involve the narrator stepping above the scene to make an observation, draw a distinction, or reach for a striking formulation. The underlying issue is not technology mismatch but a tonal inconsistency between the chapter's predominantly physical, restrained narration and several moments where the prose reaches for contemporary literary effect.

The only genuine conceptual anachronism is the nerve-conduction metaphor (Issue 3 / Issue 1 in the ranking). Everything else is tonal and register-based.

## Revision Plan

**Priority 1 (required):**
- Replace the nerve-conduction metaphor. Render the impaired grip as physical sensation only, without the signal-transmission frame.

**Priority 2 (recommended):**
- Cut or rephrase "the cold worked against the spark with patience older than patience." Replace with physical fact.
- Cut the explanatory clause from "he understood this in the way he understood the cold." End the sentence at "the cold."
- Replace "The world absorbs violence quickly." with physical observation: translate the aphorism into what Hurkuzak sees happening in the snow.

**Priority 3 (optional polish):**
- Replace "track the situation" with period-neutral phrasing.
- Rephrase "the landscape still held the shape of their attention" as direct physical perception.
- Soften "a body borrowing from something that would need to be repaid" to remove the ledger register.
- Replace "he would not let it fail" with a behavioral description of persistence.

---

## Failure Mode Note

This chapter is failing at the level of **isolated narrator-voice drift** rather than deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or systemic anachronism. The world is consistent. The characters' dialogue and behavior are consistent. The technology and material culture are correct. The problem is a small number of passages where the modern author's literary voice briefly supplants the character's pre-industrial physical interiority. This is the easiest category of consistency problem to fix: it requires targeted substitution in five to eight phrases, not structural revision.
