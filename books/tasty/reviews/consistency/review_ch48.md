# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 48: What Was Passed On

**Reviewed against:** `book/CLAUDE.md` (world rules), `book/docs/01_story_beats.md`, `book/docs/01_story_analysis.md`, `book/docs/section_23_outline.md`
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Review criteria:** `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "It noticed the way the water moved against the boat hulls and calculated weight and displacement and structural integrity."

**Problem:** Modern engineering and naval architecture terminology in a pre-industrial setting.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Displacement" as a technical term refers to the specific physics measurement of a vessel's weight relative to the water it displaces — a concept from naval architecture formalized centuries after a pre-industrial setting would allow. "Structural integrity" is a compound noun from modern engineering, used in fields from aerospace to civil construction. Stacked together with "calculated," these three words import an entire modern technical vocabulary that the world of the novel cannot support. A pre-industrial character thinking in Hakiia's combat-sharpened idiom would assess boats in terms of how fast they could move, how many could hold, whether a hull was sound or rotted — not in formal engineering coefficients.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with observations grounded in practical, physical knowledge available to a pre-industrial fighter: load and how it would slow the boat, whether the hull sat high or low, which boats could hold the weight of a fleeing man. The assessment can still feel ruthlessly tactical and specific without importing modern measurement vocabulary.

> *Example revision:* "It noticed the way the water moved against the boat hulls and read the weight of each — which sat low and slow, which could carry a man at speed, which hull had already taken on water and would answer badly in a chase."

**Priority: HIGH**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "The rite activated the way a drowning person takes air — without thought, without intent, because the conditions permitted it."

**Problem:** "Activated" is mechanistic, modern language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Activated" belongs to the vocabulary of switches, machines, and engineered systems. It implies a designed mechanism that trips when conditions are met — the language of circuits, protocols, and technical triggers. In a world without engines or electricity, and in a passage describing an ancient taboo rite treated with dread and mystery, this word collapses the rite's ritual strangeness into a mechanical process. The whole passage is about the rite moving through flesh like something alive and ungoverned; "activated" contradicts that register.

**Suggested direction:** Use language drawn from the natural or organic world — something that moves, takes hold, wakes, or spreads. The fire simile later in the same paragraph ("the way fire moved through dry wood") is strong precisely because fire is organic, dangerous, and outside mechanical control. The rite itself deserves the same framing.

> *Example revision:* "The rite took hold the way a drowning person takes air — without thought, without intent, because the conditions permitted it."

**Priority: HIGH**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "the instinct to help was the only instinct she had that operated faster than the instinct to flee"

**Problem:** "Operated" applies mechanical-system language to a human instinct.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Operated" describes machinery, systems, and processes — a valve operates, an engine operates, a protocol operates. Applied to instinct in a pre-industrial setting, it introduces the kind of mechanistic thinking about human psychology that belongs to the post-industrial world. The sentence is otherwise well-crafted; the single verb imports a register the world cannot sustain.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with a verb rooted in the physical, immediate world — one that conveys speed and precedence without the mechanistic implication.

> *Example revision:* "the instinct to help was the only instinct she had that moved faster than the instinct to flee"

**Priority: HIGH**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "Her teeth had closed reflexively."

**Problem:** "Reflexively" is clinical, physiological register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Reflexively" is a term from physiology and psychology — the vocabulary of clinical observation and scientific description of involuntary response. In a pre-industrial setting where people understand their bodies through experience rather than formal physiology, this word sounds like a narrator who has read anatomy textbooks. The meaning is clear and the description is accurate, but the register is wrong: it describes the action from the outside, as a medical observer would, rather than from inside the experience of a panicked body.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with language that describes the action without clinical framing.

> *Example revision:* "Her teeth had closed before she thought to open them."
> Or: "Her teeth had caught, held, released — she had not willed it."

**Priority: MEDIUM**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "It was a voice that understood danger the way she understood netting — automatically, comprehensively, as a permanent feature of how the world was perceived."

**Problem:** "Comprehensively" and "a permanent feature of how the world was perceived" — both have a modern analytical and psychological register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Comprehensively" is formal, systematic language — the vocabulary of reports, assessments, and organized analysis. "A permanent feature of how the world was perceived" reads like cognitive-psychological description: it frames perception as a categorized system with identifiable features, which is the language of academic psychology, not of pre-industrial lived experience. The sentence is trying to convey that the voice reads danger the way a seasoned person reads their craft — fully, deeply, without effort. That idea is achievable without this register.

**Suggested direction:** Stay grounded in the physical and experiential. The netting comparison is strong; lean further into it rather than pivoting to abstract psychological framing.

> *Example revision:* "It was a voice that understood danger the way she understood netting — without thought, in full, reading each gap and tension before her eyes had finished moving."

**Priority: MEDIUM**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "distinct in origin, inseparable in practice"

**Problem:** "In practice" has a modern bureaucratic and analytical register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "In practice" is a phrase that belongs to the vocabulary of policy, law, analysis, and management — it describes the difference between what a rule says and what actually happens. Applied to two voices entangled in a weave, it imports a modern professional register into what should be a sensory and metaphorical description. The thread-in-a-weave simile that precedes it is exactly right for the setting; the abstract concluding phrase undercuts it.

**Suggested direction:** Complete the thought through the metaphor rather than with an analytical gloss.

> *Example revision:* "distinct in origin, impossible to pull apart without unraveling the whole"

**Priority: MEDIUM**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "She had a house that was small and adequate and that smelled of salt and dried seaweed and something mineral beneath."

**Problem:** "Something mineral" edges into geological/scientific register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Mineral" as a descriptor for smell is accurate in the modern sense — we use it to describe the smell of stone, wet rock, or certain water. But "mineral" as a standalone categorical term invokes geology and chemistry: it is how a scientist or educated modern person would name the category. A pre-industrial coastal villager is more likely to reach for concrete objects or comparisons — wet stone, old chalk, the smell of the low-tide rocks — rather than the abstract category "mineral." This is lower severity than the other issues because the word is not technically wrong, only slightly elevated in register.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with a specific concrete equivalent.

> *Example revision:* "smelled of salt and dried seaweed and the cold stone smell that came up from the floor when the tide was high"

**Priority: LOW**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "She did not know this would matter."

**Problem:** Omniscient narrator intrusion with a modern ironic-knowing register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is a stylistic issue as much as a setting issue, but it is relevant here because it introduces a narratorial self-consciousness — a knowingness about what will and will not "matter" — that belongs to modern literary fiction. The narrator is winking at the reader over the character's shoulder, using the word "matter" in the contemporary literary sense of significance-to-plot. In the context of a chapter striving for quiet inevitability, it reads as a slightly slick move that belongs to a different register than the rest of the prose. It also inadvertently signals the sequel hook too early, before the chapter has built toward it organically.

**Suggested direction:** Cut the line entirely. The chapter does not need it. Ila's name followed immediately by her introduction accomplishes the same grounding without the narratorial aside.

> *Example revision:* Cut. Begin: "She lived in a village three weeks' walk from the market town..."

**Priority: LOW**

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 48 is largely well-calibrated to its world and registers. The prose maintains the literary dark register the project requires, the setting details (fishing village, net-mending, tides, coastal geography) are internally consistent with a pre-industrial world, and the emotional architecture of the sequel hook lands with the right weight of strangeness. The chapter's setting-consistency problems are not structural. They are vocabulary intrusions — specific words and phrases where the author's modern frame of reference momentarily overrides the world's logic.

The most damaging cluster appears in the description of the first (Hakiia-derived) voice, where three modern engineering and analytical terms stack together in a single sentence and break the voice's otherwise effective characterization. Outside that cluster, the problems are isolated and individually recoverable.

---

## Top 10 Immersion-Breaking Issues (Ranked by Impact)

1. **"calculated weight and displacement and structural integrity"** — Three stacked modern technical terms in a single phrase. Displacement as a naval measurement and structural integrity as an engineering compound both belong to post-industrial vocabularies. This is the most concentrated anachronism in the chapter. (HIGH)

2. **"The rite activated"** — "Activated" collapses the ancient rite's organic strangeness into mechanical trigger-language. The surrounding passage is strong; the single verb undermines the register. (HIGH)

3. **"operated faster than the instinct to flee"** — "Operated" applies machine-system logic to human instinct. Replaceable with a single verb change. (HIGH)

4. **"Her teeth had closed reflexively"** — Clinical physiology register in a moment that should feel panicked and immediate. (MEDIUM)

5. **"comprehensively, as a permanent feature of how the world was perceived"** — Abstract psychological framing of perception, imported from modern cognitive vocabulary. (MEDIUM)

6. **"distinct in origin, inseparable in practice"** — "In practice" is analytical policy-speak; it breaks the thread metaphor it was intended to conclude. (MEDIUM)

7. **"something mineral beneath"** — Geological/scientific category rather than concrete sensory description. Isolated and mild, but the register is elevated for a coastal villager. (LOW)

8. **"She did not know this would matter."** — Omniscient narratorial irony in a modern-literary register. Cuts cleanly. (LOW)

9. **"automatically, comprehensively"** (as a pairing) — The double adverb construction is mildly formal. "Comprehensively" is the primary offender; "automatically" is borderline but acceptable in context. (LOW)

10. **"a permanent feature of how the world was perceived"** — On its own, this phrase sounds like a sociological or psychological description of cognition. It pairs with the other abstractions in the same sentence to elevate the register away from the setting. (LOW, flagged separately because its removal has independent effect even if "comprehensively" is revised)

## Pattern Summary

The chapter's recurring weakness is the intrusion of modern technical and analytical vocabulary in moments of abstract description. When the prose is grounded in physical, concrete action — bread, stone bench, net, cord, shore, boats, fire, smoke — it stays fully inside the world. When it reaches for abstractions or categories, it tends to reach for modern ones: engineering measurement, physiological terminology, analytical framing. The author's instincts for setting detail are strong; the drift happens at the level of the analytical gloss, the summary phrase, the abstract concluding clause.

A secondary pattern: the Hakiia-voice's tactical intelligence is described in the vocabulary of modern assessment rather than pre-industrial combat awareness. The distinction matters because the voice's nature is one of the chapter's most important elements. It should sound like a pre-industrial fighter's perception rendered as instinct, not like a modern tactical assessor.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Revise "calculated weight and displacement and structural integrity"** — Replace the three modern technical terms with physical, experiential equivalents grounded in what a pre-industrial fighter would actually observe about boats (load, draft, speed, soundness of hull). One sentence, one targeted replacement.

2. **Revise "The rite activated"** — Replace "activated" with organic language: "took hold," "moved," "woke," "caught." One word change.

3. **Revise "operated faster than"** — Replace "operated" with "moved" or an equivalent physical verb. One word change.

4. **Revise "Her teeth had closed reflexively"** — Replace "reflexively" with a phrase that describes the action without clinical framing. One clause revision.

5. **Revise "comprehensively, as a permanent feature of how the world was perceived"** — Replace the abstract psychological framing with a continuation of the netting comparison or another concrete equivalent. One clause revision.

6. **Revise "distinct in origin, inseparable in practice"** — Replace "in practice" with a phrase that completes the thread metaphor physically: "impossible to pull apart without unraveling the weave." One phrase revision.

7. **Cut "She did not know this would matter."** — The chapter does not need this line. Remove it and begin the paragraph with Ila's village.

8. **Revise "something mineral beneath"** — Replace with a concrete coastal sensory equivalent: "the cold stone smell that came up from the floor" or similar. One phrase revision.

---

## Failure Mode Note

The chapter is not failing because of deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The setting is coherent, the emotional register is calibrated, and the POV shift from Hurkuzak to Ila is executed cleanly. The problems are isolated wording intrusions — places where the author's modern vocabulary overrides the world's register in moments of abstract or technical description. All eight issues are recoverable with targeted revisions at the sentence or phrase level. None require structural changes to the chapter.
