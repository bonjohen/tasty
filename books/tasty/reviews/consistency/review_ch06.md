# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

**Chapter:** Ch 6 (Section 3, The Wolves)
**Reviewer role:** Developmental and line editor, setting consistency
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy — wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps; no engines, firearms, or electricity
**Date:** 2026-04-06

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:** "eyes that reflected the failing light without holding it"

**Problem:** Modern optical framing — the distinction between reflecting and retaining light is a perceptual category that belongs to optics-aware thinking. A character in a pre-industrial world would describe eyes that caught the light and gave it back cold, or eyes that shone in the dark like chips of frozen water.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Without holding it" performs a small, precise technical observation about light behavior. It is not wrong, but it carries the register of someone who thinks about light the way modern people do — as a phenomenon to be described with precision. A pre-industrial narrator would more likely describe what the eye looks like or what it does to the observer, not measure the physics of the reflection.

**Direction:** "eyes that caught the failing light and gave none of it back" or "eyes that held the light the way ice holds it — bright and cold and nothing behind it."

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:** "with the shared attention of animals that had hunted together long enough to communicate through position rather than sound"

**Problem:** Modern behavioral/ethological framing. Phrases like "communicate through position" belong to naturalist or scientific observation language — the kind of analytical vocabulary that emerged with formal study of animal behavior. It reads as the narrator stepping outside the world to describe the wolves as a biologist would.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A pre-industrial narrator — especially one under threat — would perceive the wolves' coordination as something threatening and purposeful, but would reach for different language. They would feel the pattern without naming it in clinical terms. "Communicate through position" is exactly how a naturalist paper or modern nature documentary would describe it.

**Direction:** "animals that had hunted together long enough that their silence was its own language" or simply showing the behavior and letting Hurkuzak feel its wrongness: "they spread and did not look at each other, and yet the spread was exact."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:** "the blind grinding force of bone and muscle operating without a brain to govern them"

**Problem:** The phrase "bone and muscle operating without a brain to govern them" is clinical anatomy language. "Govern" applied to brains, "bone and muscle" as named physiological components — this is the register of medical or anatomical text.

**Why it breaks immersion:** In a pre-industrial world, the narrator might know that a dead thing could still close its jaw, but would describe it in terms of its felt danger or uncanny persistence, not as a system whose components have been listed. The brain-governing-muscle framing assumes a mechanistic model of the body that is post-Enlightenment in register.

**Direction:** "the blind grinding force of a thing that was already dead but had not finished killing" or "the dead jaw that no longer knew it was dead."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:** "His right hand could not hold it. His right hand could not hold anything."

**Problem:** Not a consistency problem on its own — but the repetition pattern here, used twice in the same beat and then again with "His left arm lay at his side, motionless," creates a cataloguing structure that reads as clinical inventory.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The prose at this moment itemizes damage in a way that feels like a medical summary. In a pre-industrial dark fantasy, the narrator would feel the loss more than list it. The enumeration style belongs to assessment and documentation rather than witness.

**Direction:** This is a structural tendency rather than a single word problem. The damage should be rendered through Hurkuzak's perception of it, not through a damage report. The physical facts are correct — the framing belongs to a clinical mode. (See Pattern Summary below.)

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:** "Hurkuzak would only reconstruct it later, piecing together what he saw and what the evidence in the snow told him"

**Problem:** "Reconstruct" and "evidence" are terms that belong to investigative, forensic, or legal registers. "Reconstruct" in particular is post-Enlightenment diction — it implies a methodological procedure applied to fragmentary data.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A pre-industrial narrator piecing together memory would "find the shape of it later" or "remember it in pieces later, and the pieces did not line up." The idea is the same, but "reconstruct" and "evidence" are from a different world's vocabulary of understanding.

**Direction:** "What happened next was fast enough that he only understood it afterward, in pieces — what he saw, what the snow showed him, what the sounds matched to." Or more directly: "he only understood it afterward, from the blood in the snow and what his eyes had caught between one second and the next."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:** "The look was brief, clinical."

**Problem:** "Clinical" is a modern psychological or medical descriptor. In common usage it means detached, professionally neutral, emotionally suppressed — a word imported from medical practice. Characters in pre-industrial settings do not read each other as "clinical."

**Why it breaks immersion:** The word belongs to a world with clinical professions and a clinical manner as a named social mode. Even as a translation of Hakiia's detached self-assessment into modern English, it breaks the register precisely because it sounds like the narrator has stepped outside the world to apply a modern category.

**Direction:** "The look was brief and flat, the look of a man taking stock rather than grieving." Or: "The look was without grief. It assessed. It moved on."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:** "something has shifted in the way his body occupied the travois, a settling that did not look like rest"

**Problem:** "The way his body occupied the travois" is a phrasing that belongs to analytical, spatial, or architectural discourse. "Occupied" used in this sense — a body occupying a space in the way an object fills a container — is a modern abstraction.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A pre-industrial narrator watching a man collapse further into a litter would use different diction — not spatial occupation but something observed: he had gone slack in a way that did not look like sleep, or he had settled into the travois the way a man settles who is no longer resisting.

**Direction:** "something had changed in him — a slackening that did not look like rest, a settling that did not look like ease."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:** "Between them they held one working grip and the memory of what two good hands used to feel like."

**Problem:** Not a setting anachronism in vocabulary, but in emotional register: "the memory of what two good hands used to feel like" is a psychological interiority framing — reflective, slightly poetic, and self-aware in a way that feels more modern therapeutic than pre-industrial. It is also slightly sentimental in a chapter that is otherwise admirably unsentimental.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The problem is tonal rather than lexical. The prose elsewhere in this chapter is blunt and physical and trusts the reader. This line reaches for an emotional summary that the narrator elsewhere withholds. It briefly softens Hurkuzak in a way that does not match his established POV register in this chapter.

**Direction:** Cut "the memory of what two good hands used to feel like." The weight of Hurkuzak's hands is already present from context. Let the reader carry it: "Between them they held one working grip." Full stop.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:** "with the blank shaking where feeling should have been"

**Note on original:** The actual text reads "a shaking blank where feeling should have been" — the same problem.

**Problem:** "Where feeling should have been" is a psychological interiority construction that identifies an emotional vacancy by its absence, as if the narrator is doing psychological self-examination. This belongs to a self-analytical, therapeutic vocabulary.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A pre-industrial character in shock and pain would not experience or describe the absence of feeling as a named phenomenon. The body would just be wrong, or cold, or not his own. The phrasing betrays a modern reflective register.

**Direction:** "his body vibrating with something that was not shaking and not stillness and not anything he recognized." Or simply: "his body still vibrating from the effort, his hands wrong and cold and not quite his."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:** "Vessels needed closing. They were not closing."

**Problem:** "Vessels" as a term for blood vessels is anatomical terminology. In pre-industrial medical understanding, people spoke of veins, the flow of blood, wounds that would not stop — not "vessels" in the clinical anatomical sense.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Vessels needed closing" reads as a trauma-medicine assessment. A pre-industrial narrator watching someone bleed would describe the blood coming and not stopping, not identify the physiological category of the structures that require closure.

**Direction:** "The blood came from every surface and did not stop." Or: "He could not stop it — too many openings, and each one pouring freely."

**Priority:** High

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 6 is strong prose. The action is well-paced, the wolf tactics are convincing, the cauterization scene has the correct register of restrained horror, and the relationship between Hakiia's tactical brilliance and his physical destruction is handled with discipline. The chapter accomplishes what the section outline requires.

The setting-consistency problems are almost entirely in register rather than in specific anachronistic objects or technology. There are no engines, firearms, or electricity. The material world is correct. What slips is the narrator's vocabulary when describing bodies, damage, and behavior: several phrases carry the clinical, anatomical, behavioral-science, or forensic register of a modern educated narrator rather than a pre-industrial one. These are isolated failures of diction, not signs of deeper worldbuilding inconsistency.

The most persistent issue is clinical body-language: "vessels," "clinical," "bone and muscle operating without a brain to govern them," and the damage-inventory passages all suggest a narrator who thinks about bodies the way modern medicine trained us to think about them. This is a specific, fixable pattern.

## Pattern Summary

The chapter has one recurring weakness: **clinical and scientific register bleeding into narrative description of bodies, damage, and animal behavior.** The author consistently reaches for precise, analytical vocabulary when describing physical facts — anatomy ("vessels," "bone and muscle"), animal cognition ("communicate through position"), forensic process ("reconstruct," "evidence"), and medical assessment ("clinical"). These words are not wrong in isolation, but they belong to a world of categorized, systematized knowledge. The narrator's pre-industrial characters would not think in these terms. The problem is not gross anachronism (no guns, no electricity) — it is register drift at the level of diction when the narrative becomes technical or descriptive of bodies.

A secondary minor tendency: the narrator occasionally provides a psychological self-summary for Hurkuzak when the physical rendering would be stronger without it ("the memory of what two good hands used to feel like," "a shaking blank where feeling should have been"). These do not break setting consistency in the strict sense, but they weaken the chapter's otherwise effective restraint.

---

## Revision Plan

1. **Priority 1 — Fix the four High-priority phrases:**
   - Replace "reconstruct" / "evidence in the snow" with experiential, non-investigative language.
   - Replace "bone and muscle operating without a brain to govern them" with felt danger rather than anatomy.
   - Replace "The look was brief, clinical" with a behavioral description.
   - Replace "Vessels needed closing" with the experience of blood that will not stop.

2. **Priority 2 — Revise the Medium-priority register slips:**
   - "communicate through position rather than sound" → behavioral description without ethological framing.
   - "the way his body occupied the travois" → slackening/settling language.
   - "a shaking blank where feeling should have been" → physical wrongness without psychological category.
   - "the memory of what two good hands used to feel like" → cut the second clause; trust the context.

3. **Priority 3 — Review damage-inventory passages:**
   - Ensure damage is rendered through Hurkuzak's perception, not as a medical checklist.

4. **Low priority:**
   - "reflected the failing light without holding it" — can remain or be adjusted; does not significantly break immersion.

---

## Root Cause Note

The chapter is failing through **isolated wording problems, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift.** The fictional world, the technology, the social structure, the character voices, and the narrative logic are all consistent with the established pre-industrial dark fantasy setting. The slips are contained to specific vocabulary choices — primarily clinical/anatomical and behavioral-scientific language — when the narrator is describing bodies and animals under stress. These are fixable at the line level without restructuring.
