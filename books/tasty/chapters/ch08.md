Chapter 8: What Hakiia Asks

The march was failing. Hurkuzak knew it. The knowing changed nothing. His body moved on its own. The travois dragged behind him through the snow. Step and drag. Step and drag. The poles had worn grooves into his palms deeper than skin. The blood from them had frozen into the wood, and the wood had become part of his hands or his hands had become part of the wood.

The valley had narrowed into something close to a gorge. The trees pressed in on both sides, dark and heavy with snow, and the sky above was a strip of gray. The cabin was ahead. He had to believe it. He could not carry the alternative.

Behind him, Hakiia had been silent for hours. When Hurkuzak had checked the travois at the last stop, Hakiia's eyes had been open but unfocused, watching the sky.

Then Hakiia spoke.

“Stop.”

Hurkuzak stopped. He set down the drag poles. His shoulders screamed with the release. His knees tried to fold. He locked them and stood. He turned to look at his brother.

Hakiia was looking at him. Not the drifting, dulled eyes of the last several hours, but the sharp, certain eyes Hurkuzak had known his entire life. He was fully himself.

“Sit down,” Hakiia said. His voice was thin, but the authority in it was intact. “I have something to say to you.”

Hurkuzak sat in the snow beside the travois. The cold was no longer an event but a condition, like the weight of stone.

Hakiia looked at the sky for a moment. Then at his legs. Then at Hurkuzak. He looked at him the way he had looked at the tedik's body before cutting it open.

“My right leg is dead,” he said. “The flesh is dying from the ankle upward. By tonight or tomorrow it will be the knee. After that, the rot will move into the blood, if it hasn't already. I can feel it. It is warm in ways that warm should not be.”

“We can still —”

“We cannot. Listen to me.” He let the words drop like weight. “The left leg is broken in two places. Even if the right leg were whole, I would not walk for months. With both legs broken and one of them rotting, I will not walk again.” He paused. “And the hands. You have seen the hands.”

“I cannot hold a knife,” Hakiia said. “I cannot hold food. I cannot hold a waterskin. I cannot, if the need arose, do the thing myself.” He said this last part flatly. “I know this. You know this. We have been pretending otherwise because pretending was easier than what comes after we stop, and now we are going to stop.”

“The cabin may be a day away,” Hakiia said. “It may be two. You are injured, exhausted, and moving at a pace that is slower every hour. If you reach it with me, you arrive with a dying man who needs constant care, no food worth counting, no medicine, no help coming, and winter still at the door. You know what happens then. You spend your strength keeping me alive for days, maybe a week, and then I die anyway, and then you are alone in a cabin in the mountains in winter with nothing, and you die too.” He paused. “That is the reckoning. And we are done pretending it works out any other way.”

“What are you asking?”

“Kill me,” Hakiia said. “Here. Now. While I am still myself and the asking is mine.”

His stomach dropped. His throat tightened. His hands, ruined as they were, curled into themselves as though trying to hold something that was already gone.

“No.”

“Listen —”

“No.”

He stood too fast. His bad knee buckled. He caught himself and did not sit back down.

“Sit down,” Hakiia said.

“No.”

“You are standing in the snow refusing to listen because you are afraid of what you will hear. Sit down. I am not going to stop saying this because you are standing.”

Hurkuzak sat. Not because Hakiia told him to. Because his legs gave out.

Hakiia watched him settle into the snow. Then he spoke in the voice he used for hard lessons.

“I have thought about this,” Hakiia said. “Not for minutes. For hours. Since the wolves, since the fire, since I felt the leg stop hurting in the way that means the nerves are dying. I have read my body the way I read any ground, and the ground is against us. I am dying. The question is not whether. The question is how long it takes and how much it costs. If I die slowly — in the cabin, over days, while you watch — the cost is both of us. If I die here, now, by your hand, you walk to the cabin alone and lighter and you survive the winter.” He held Hurkuzak's gaze. “That is not cruelty. That is the honest accounting.”

“Don't call it accounting.”

“What would you call it?”

“I don't know. I don't care. You are asking me to kill my brother and I am not going to do it.”

Hakiia closed his eyes. When he opened them, the sharpness had shifted. Not softer. Older.

“You know what is asked,” he said. “You know the right. You know what our people do when the body will not carry the person any further.” He did not explain. He did not need to. The words called up the same thing they had talked about at the fire — the draught, the family's duty, the proverb Hurkuzak had heard since childhood. Never live so long that you become a burden. “There is no draught here. There is no mother to give it and no warm room to receive it. There is you and the knife. The cruelty is not what I am asking. The cruelty is that there is nothing gentler to ask for.”

The words settled in him and stayed. He thought of the draught. The gentle version of this act. A dying person in a familiar room, surrounded by family, the cup brought with care and received with dignity. That was what the practice looked like when the world was being kind. This — a knife in the snow, a brother with no hands, the cold pressing in from every direction — was what the practice looked like when it was not.

Hakiia's mouth moved in the shape that was not a smile. “You killed the dog.”

The words landed in a place that had no defense.

“The dog was different.”

“The dog was in pain. The dog could not survive. You looked at him clearly and you acted. That was not weakness. That was the first useful thing you did after the cliff.” Hakiia's voice softened. The softening was worse than the blade. It carried the almost-tenderness from the fire, the warmth that lived behind the correction. “Look at me the same way. Do the same.”

“You are not a dog.”

“No. I can still speak. And I am speaking.”

The silence after was long. The wind moved through the trees. Snow shifted on the branches above them. A small cascade of powder fell and landed on Hakiia's chest. He did not brush it away. The cold did not care what they were arguing about. The cold had its own work.

“You want me to die slowly,” Hakiia said. “You want to drag me to the cabin and watch me rot because watching is easier than acting. Because as long as I am breathing, you can pretend that what is happening is not what is happening, and the pretending is more important to you than my pain.”

“That is not what I want.”

“Then what do you want? Tell me. I am lying here with both legs broken and one of them dying and I am asking you to help me and you are refusing because it makes you feel something you do not want to feel. That is not mercy. That is not love. That is a man choosing his own comfort over his brother's suffering.”

There was truth in it. Not all of it — what he was protecting was not comfort. But there was enough that he could not dismiss it. Hakiia knew. He used the knowledge the way he used everything: precisely.

“Tell me the plan,” Hakiia said. His voice had dropped. The anger had changed into something colder. “Tell me the version of this where you haul me to a cabin with no food and no help and both of us survive. Tell me the plan that makes refusing me something other than selfishness.”

Hurkuzak opened his mouth and closed it. The plan did not exist. The cabin was shelter, not rescue. There was no help within reach. There was only winter and cold and two brothers, one dying, the other being asked. The word no was in his body like a bone, weight-bearing and immovable. He could not kill his brother.

“I don't have a plan,” he said. “I don't have an answer. I have that I don't want to do this, and I know that isn't enough, and I know you're right about the leg and the cold and what happens next, and I still cannot do what you are asking me to do.”

Hakiia looked at him. The look lasted a long time.

“You can,” Hakiia said. “You killed the wolf. You killed the dog. You have done hard things in the last three days that I did not believe you could do. Do not tell me you cannot do this. Tell me you will not. Those are different words and you know the difference.”

“I will not.”

“Yet.”

Hakiia closed his eyes. His breathing was wrong — shallow, irregular. He did not withdraw the demand. He simply stopped pressing it.

The snow fell. The trees creaked. The cold continued.

He picked up the drag poles and he pulled.

Behind him, Hakiia's breathing was wrong, and the silence between them was no longer the silence of a man who had stopped commanding.
