# Chapter 12: What the Cabin Holds

He woke to cold and dark and the smell of stone.

The cold was the first thing. Not the grief, not the memory, not the fact of what lay beside him on the floor — the cold. Pain, stiffness, the sick desperate shivering that meant the temperature had dropped past the point where lying still was compatible with continuing to live.

He did not know how long he had been unconscious. The light through the gap in the shuttered window was gray, but it had been gray when he collapsed and it was gray now. His body told him something: he was dehydrated, weak, and stiff in every joint, and the wolf bite in his forearm throbbed with a deep, infectious heat.

He got up. The getting up took a long time and involved the same stages as before — hands, knees, a push, a second push, vertical. The cabin tilted. He put his hand against the wall. Stone. Cold traveling up his arm and into his bones.

Hakiia was on the floor where Hurkuzak had placed him. The body had not moved. It occupied the room.

Fire first. The hearth was stone, wide and deep, blackened by years of use.

He found dry wood stacked against the wall outside the back door — not much, a pile that previous visitors had left and that the overhang of the roof had kept relatively dry. He carried an armload inside, shaved tinder with the knife, and worked the spark until it caught. The fire was small and reluctant, and not dying today was the only plan he was capable of making.

The cabin revealed itself in the firelight. One room, as he had seen before he collapsed. Stone walls, timber roof, stone floor. The hearth dominated the back wall. The sleeping platform along the right side was bare wood, but he found two blankets folded beneath it — thin, rough-woven, musty from storage, but whole. The remains of other travelers' stays lined the shelves and the chest against the left wall: a clay pot, an iron pan, rope, a hand axe, three candles, and a sack of dried grain that had gone partly to mold but was not entirely lost. He picked through it and separated what was edible from what was not. A double handful of clean grain. Maybe three days if he was careful.

Under the chest, shoved against the wall: a jar of rendered fat, sealed with wax. He opened it. The fat was old and slightly rancid but it would keep him alive, and keeping alive was what his body was demanding.

He checked the back room through the smaller door. Not a room — a narrow alcove, partially open to the air where the roof joined the rock face behind the cabin. Cold enough to freeze water. Cold enough to preserve.

He carried Hakiia's body into the alcove.

The carrying was difficult. The body was stiff, the joints locked by cold and time, and Hurkuzak had to lift and angle and negotiate the narrow doorway. His ribs screamed, his forearm pulsed, and the cut in his palm — healed badly, reopened — left prints of his own blood on his brother's chest. He set the body down on the stone floor of the alcove and straightened the limbs as well as the stiffness allowed and pulled the furs over the face and stood back.

The cold would preserve it. He told himself this was about dignity.

He closed the alcove door.

The cabin felt different with the body in the other room. Not better. Not worse. Different. Hakiia was behind a door. Hakiia was still in the cabin. Both of these things were true and Hurkuzak could feel both of them at all times.

He ate a portion of grain boiled in snowmelt. The taste was flat and faintly bitter and he ate it all, slowly, hands unsteady, the way a man eats who is not sure when he will eat again. He ate a small amount of the fat, smearing it on the edge of his hand and licking it. He could feel it enter him the way dry wood catches — not warmth yet, only the promise of it.

He slept on the platform. The blankets were thin but they were better than furs soaked in blood, and the fire, small as it was, had raised the temperature in the cabin from killing cold to merely miserable. He slept and woke and slept and woke — the same dark room, the same cold, the same silence, and the same knowledge of what was behind the alcove door.

Days passed. He did not count them at first. Then he scratched marks on the wall above the platform with the knife, not because he expected to survive long enough for the count to matter, but because counting was something to do, and doing kept the silence from becoming pressure.

Wake. Check the fire. Feed it if it needed feeding. Melt snow in the clay pot. Drink. Cook a small portion of grain. Eat it slowly, making the eating last. Idle hours were their own weight.

He checked his injuries. The wolf bite was infected — hot, swollen, leaking a thin fluid that smelled wrong. He cleaned it with snowmelt and wrapped it and the cleaning helped and the wrapping helped and neither solved the problem because the problem was inside the flesh and he had no tools for inside. He watched the infection the way he watched the grain — as a thing that moved in one direction, worsening by degrees that no care could stop. His ribs were bruised but mending. His knee worked, badly, with a grinding that said something inside had settled into a new configuration — functional but wrong. His palm had scarred into a thick ridge that pulled when he flexed his hand. The scars were the only things healing. Everything else was worsening or not yet worsened, and the difference was food, and the food was days, and the days were running out.

He talked. At first under his breath, muttering while he worked — practical words, the language of tasks. Put that there. Needs more wood. Water's low. Then louder, to the room itself, as though the cabin were an audience that deserved narration. Then to Hakiia.

Not to the body. He did not open the alcove door and speak to what was inside. He spoke to the air, to the memory.

"You would hate this," he said one evening, sitting by the fire with the last of the grain cooking in the pot. "You would hate the waiting. You never waited for anything. You decided and you acted and the time between the decision and the act was so small it might as well not have existed." He stirred the grain. "I am waiting. I don't know what for. The snow to stop. The spring to come. Something to change. Nothing is changing. The fire burns and the snow falls and the grain gets less and I am still here and you are still in the other room and nothing is changing."

The silence was his answer. It was always his answer.

He argued with it sometimes. Replayed conversations from the trail and revised his own responses and gave himself the words he had not had when Hakiia was alive. The pressure of his brother's certainty had always compressed everything Hurkuzak wanted to say into one inadequate syllable — no, or fine, or nothing at all. Here in the cabin, alone with the absence, the words came more easily. He was more articulate without an audience. He could build whole arguments, carry them to their conclusions, hear himself say the things he had never said to the face that had always been harder than his own.

"You never let me finish a sentence," he told the fire one night. "You heard the first three words and decided the rest were wrong and you corrected me before I could find out whether you were right. And I let you. Every time. Because arguing with you was like arguing with weather."

He paused. The fire cracked. Wind pressed against the shutters. The shutters held.

"I miss you," he said. The words surprised him. He said them to a room that could not hear.

Snow fell. It fell for two days without stopping and when it stopped the world outside the cabin had risen by another foot and the door opened against a wall of white that came to his chest. He dug a path. The path filled in overnight. He dug again. The path filled again. He stopped digging. The snow had accumulated until the cabin became the entire world: one room, an alcove, a fire, and a diminishing supply of food.

The grain ran out on the seventh day. Or the eighth. He had been careful — smaller portions each day, stretching what was there until what was there was nothing — and the last meal was so small it was more ritual than sustenance, a few spoonfuls of thin paste.

The fat lasted two more days. He rationed it in slivers, cutting thin strips with the knife and holding them on his tongue until they dissolved. It was not enough. His body was eating itself — he could feel it in the way his arms had gone thin, in the shaking, in the new hollows where there had been none.

He lay on the platform in the dark and listened to the fire and felt the hunger. This was the hunger that had moved in. It would remain until something fed it or the body that housed it stopped being a body.

He lay in the dark and the dark was complete because the fire had burned low and the candles were too precious to waste and the only light in the room was the faint glow of embers. The cabin was quiet. The wind had stopped. The snow had stopped. Everything had stopped except the hunger and the cold and the sound of his own breathing — and behind the alcove door was the only food left in the world.

He did not think the thought. He lay in the dark and felt the hunger and felt the cold and felt the presence of what was in the other room.

He did not sleep. The hunger watched. The cold watched. Behind the door, in the dark of the alcove, something watched too.

He did not sleep.
