Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

The first wolf came at dusk.

Hurkuzak had been pulling the travois for hours, or what felt like hours. The basin had narrowed into a shallow valley between rocky slopes, the trees thin and bent with altitude, the snow deep enough that each step was a labor of lifting and sinking and lifting again. His legs burned. His ribs flared with every breath that was not shallow. His cut palm had opened again under the friction of the drag poles. The blood made the wood slick and warm.

Behind him, Hakiia lay on the travois with his eyes open and his broken legs pointed at the sky. The sky was darkening from gray toward black — the kind of black that meant real cold.

The wolves had been following since the basin. Hurkuzak had seen them at intervals — gray shapes at the edge of sight, appearing between trees and disappearing, never close enough to count reliably but never far enough to forget. They moved in the same direction he moved. They maintained the same pace.

Then one of them was closer.

It came from the left, angling in from behind a stand of dead timber, and it stopped twenty paces away and stood there. Larger than the dogs had been, with a thick winter coat and a wide head and eyes that caught the failing light and gave none of it back.

Hurkuzak stopped pulling. He turned to face the wolf and reached for the hatchet on his belt, and the wolf watched his hand move and did not retreat.

“It’s testing,” Hakiia said from the travois. His voice was thin but steady. “It wants to see if you respond. Don’t move toward it. Don’t move away. Stand.”

Hurkuzak stood. The wolf stood.

After a time the wolf turned and walked back toward the trees. Not running. Walking. Unhurried.

“Two more,” Hakiia said. “Behind us, right side.”

Hurkuzak turned. Two wolves stood at the tree line to the right, thirty paces back. They were smaller than the first, but their positioning was different — angled, spread apart, covering different lines of approach. They were not watching Hurkuzak. They were watching the travois.

“They can see I’m down,” Hakiia said. “They know what that means.” He pushed himself up on his elbows. His face tightened. His breathing shortened. The muscles in his arms trembled with the effort of holding himself partially upright. “Pull me closer to those rocks. The ones at the base of the slope.”

Hurkuzak dragged the travois to the rocks — a low cluster of boulders at the foot of the valley wall, big enough to block approach from one side and put stone behind Hakiia’s back. He set the travois down with Hakiia’s back to the stone and his face toward the open ground.

“Give me the knife,” Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak gave him the knife. Hakiia held it the way he held all weapons — low, angled, the blade an extension of his forearm. Even lying down. Even broken. The knife in his hand looked like a threat.

“You take the hatchet,” Hakiia said. “Stand in front of me. When they come — and they will come — they’ll come from the sides first, testing. Then from the front. The first one through is not the attack. The first one through is the question. The attack comes from the one you’re not looking at.”

“How do you know —”

“Because that is how anything that hunts in groups kills something bigger than itself. They separate attention. They stretch the defense. Then they come through the gap.” He looked at Hurkuzak with eyes bright with pain and clear with a quality of focus that burned off everything unnecessary.

“I’ll be the bait,” Hakiia said. “I’m already down. They can see it. They’ll come for me because I’m the easy kill. When they commit to me, you hit whatever is closest and you hit it hard enough that it does not get up.”

“You can’t fight from the ground.”

“Watch me.”

Hurkuzak stood in front of the travois with the hatchet in his right hand and his body angled the way Hakiia had told him — weight forward, weapon high, feet wide. The wolves moved at the edge of sight. They circled. They came closer and pulled back and came closer again, and each approach was a little nearer than the last, and each retreat was a little shorter.

Five of them visible now. The three from before and two more that had joined from the basin, drawn by the commotion or the blood. They settled around the clearing in a loose formation that covered every approach, and they watched — none of them looking at each other, the spacing exact.

The wolves came.

They came the way Hakiia had said they would. One from the left, trotting forward in a half-circle that drew Hurkuzak’s eyes and shoulders with it. Another cut in from the right, faster, lower, closing the distance in quick, economical bursts. Hurkuzak shifted to meet the one on the right, and the wolf from the left lunged.

Hakiia’s voice cut through the cold.

“Left. Now.”

Hurkuzak swung. The hatchet caught the first wolf high on the skull instead of cleanly in the neck, but it was enough. Bone gave. The wolf crashed sideways into the snow and kicked once, twice, then lay still.

The second wolf hit Hurkuzak before he could recover his footing.

It came in low and hard, all weight and teeth and winter fur, striking his right side and taking him off balance. Claws raked down his coat. Teeth closed on his forearm above the wrist, grinding through cloth and skin into the muscle underneath. The pain was immediate and wrong — not the clean pain of a cut or a blow but the crushing pressure of jaws built to hold and shake. He shouted, not from fear but because the body made the sound on its own.

He tore backward and swung the hatchet with his free arm. The angle was bad, the force worse, but the blade bit into the wolf’s shoulder. The animal released him and sprang back, limping, one foreleg suddenly wrong beneath it. It did not go down, but it no longer came straight at him. It circled with its head low, favoring the injured side and looking for another opening.

And while Hurkuzak fought those two, three others went for Hakiia.

They came almost together. One from the front, straight over the open ground. One from the right, skirting the rocks. One from the left, using the dead ground Hurkuzak could not cover without turning his back.

Hakiia struck the first before it could settle its weight. His knife flashed upward and into the wolf’s throat, but the kill was not clean enough to stop momentum. The animal slammed into him full-on and drove him back against the stone. The impact hit his left shoulder. Hurkuzak heard the sound Hakiia made — not a cry, not even a word, just air forced through a body that had been damaged in too many places already.

The second wolf seized Hakiia’s arm.

Not the good one. The bad shoulder. Its jaws closed high, where shoulder became upper arm, and it pulled. Hakiia stabbed down into its neck and ribs with his right hand, but the wolf had hold and did not let go. The torn shoulder shifted under the strain. Something in the joint gave further. Hakiia’s knife arm faltered.

The third wolf went for his hand.

It caught his right hand just as he struck again, and the bite landed across the hand rather than the wrist. The wolf clamped and jerked sideways with all the force of its body behind it. Flesh tore. Hakiia’s grip broke. The knife dropped into the snow.

Then the wolves pulled him apart.

One had the ruined shoulder. One had the hand. The third, bleeding from the throat but not dead yet, had taken hold of his coat and the meat beneath it near the ribs. They dragged in different directions, each trying to tear loose a piece it could take. Hakiia’s broken legs bounced and twisted uselessly beneath him. The travois shifted. One of the lashings snapped. His body slid half off the frame and into the churned snow.

For an instant it was almost impossible to understand what Hurkuzak was seeing. His brother was alive, but the wolves had him as dogs might worry a carcass — each one pulling, resetting its grip, pulling again. His left arm was stretched wrong behind him. His right forearm jerked each time the wolf on the hand shook its head. The wolf on the shoulder braced its forelegs and leaned back with the strength of its whole spine, dragging Hakiia across the snow inch by inch.

Hakiia did not scream.

He made a hard, chopped sound with each pull, the sound of breath being knocked out of him in pieces. His face was white under the blood. His teeth were bare. His eyes were still open.

Hurkuzak moved.

The limping wolf tried to intercept him, darting across his path. Hurkuzak swung without slowing. The hatchet struck it across the muzzle and side of the head, not a killing blow but enough to send it reeling away into the dark with a burst of yelping rage. Then he was past it and on the wolves around Hakiia.

He reached the one on the hand first.

He buried the hatchet into its ribs behind the shoulder. The wolf let go and twisted, snarling, but the blow had gone deep. Hurkuzak wrenched the blade free and struck again. This time the edge went into the neck, and the wolf collapsed against Hakiia’s legs and stayed there.

The other two released and sprang back.

Not far. Just far enough to avoid the next blow.

One of them — the one that had Hakiia’s shoulder — still had blood all down its chest and jaw. It feinted in, testing whether Hurkuzak would follow. The other circled with its head low and its ears flat, searching for another angle. Hurkuzak stepped over Hakiia’s body and raised the hatchet with both hands, though one arm screamed at him and the other barely trusted itself. He shouted at them, a raw sound with no shape to it. He kicked at the dead wolf to clear space. He took one step forward.

The wolves hesitated.

That was all he needed.

He charged the nearer one. It gave ground at once. The other backed off with it. He kept coming, swinging the hatchet in broad, ugly arcs meant less to land than to promise what would happen if they came close again. The wolves yielded a little more, then a little more, then turned. Not all at once. One first, then the other. They drifted back toward the tree line, looking over their shoulders, unwilling to abandon the kill entirely but no longer certain they wanted to pay for it.

The limping wolf had already gone.

The silence after was not peace. It was the pause after violence, when the world seemed to draw breath and decide whether it was finished.

Snow around the rocks was torn up and blackened with blood. One dead wolf lay half across Hakiia’s legs. Another lay farther out in the clearing where Hurkuzak had dropped it at the start. The tracks of the others marked the snow in loops and lunges and sudden directional changes. The broken travois had twisted under Hakiia’s body. One pole had split. The lashings hung loose.

Hurkuzak knelt.

Hakiia lay half on the travois and half in the snow. His left arm was out behind him at an angle that made no sense. The shoulder had been mauled open through coat and leather and padding; the fabric there was shredded and packed dark with blood. His right hand was almost gone.

Not ruined. Not mangled. Gone, for the most part.

The wolf had taken nearly everything forward of the base of the thumb. What remained was a crushed, torn mass of palm, thumb, splintered bone, and strips of skin. The fingers were absent or reduced to fragments. Blood sheeted out of it with each beat of his heart.

Hurkuzak stared for one second too long.

Hakiia opened his eyes and found him.

“Don’t look,” he said. The words were thin and cut short by pain. “Fix it.”

Hurkuzak swallowed. “The hand —”

“I know about the hand.” Hakiia’s breathing hitched. “Knife. Heat it. Then bind what’s left.”

“The shoulder first.”

“The hand first,” Hakiia said, and there was still command in him, buried under all the damage. “The shoulder bleeds less.”

Hurkuzak moved because there was nothing else to do. He tore strips from a fur wrapping and jammed them around the stump of the hand to slow the blood while he built up the fire. His own forearm was bleeding freely where the wolf had bitten through, but it no longer seemed important enough to count. He fed the flames until they rose hot and bright, then thrust the knife blade into the heart of them and waited while his brother bled into the snow.

When the metal glowed, he pulled it free.

Hakiia braced himself with the back of his head against the rock. His jaw locked. His eyes did not close.

Hurkuzak pressed the heated blade to the opened hand.

The smell was rendered flesh, sweet and sickening. Hakiia’s whole body arched despite the broken legs. A sound came out of him then, low and continuous and stripped of anything human except endurance. Hurkuzak pressed and lifted and pressed again, sealing one opened surface and then the next, until the worst of the blood stopped.

Then he turned to the shoulder.

The bite there had torn through muscle and whatever stability remained in the joint after the fall. The arm did not seem connected properly anymore. When Hurkuzak cut the coat away he could see teeth marks, torn flesh, and a contour at the joint that spoke less of dislocation than of something pulled half apart. Hakiia tried once to move the arm. Nothing useful happened. The effort only made his face whiten further.

“The arm?” Hurkuzak asked.

“Gone for now,” Hakiia said. “Wrap it.”

So he wrapped it. Fur first, then cloth, then another strip pulled tight across the chest and around the back to hold the arm from moving. By the time he finished, both of them were trembling from cold and blood loss and the aftermath of exertion.

The remaining wolves watched from a distance. Three of them, perhaps four counting the limping one somewhere out in the dark. They stood beyond the clearing and studied the bodies of the dead and the men who had made them dead, and they recalculated.

“They’ll go,” Hakiia said. His voice was scraped down to almost nothing. “They lost too much.”

The wolves went. Not in panic. Not in fear. In the measured withdrawal of animals deciding that easier food still existed elsewhere. One faded first into the trees. Then another. Then the last visible shape turned and was absorbed by the dark.

The silence after was colder than before.

Hurkuzak sat back on his heels. His forearm throbbed where the teeth had gone in. One wolf dead by his hand. Another crippled badly enough that it might not hunt well again, if it lived. But Hakiia had paid for the rest.

The knife lay beside the fire. Hakiia’s right hand could not hold it now and perhaps never would again. His left arm lay strapped against him, useless. His shoulder was swollen under the wrappings. Blood had frozen in a dark crust over his chest, his neck, and the side of his face. His breathing was too shallow, too fast.

Hurkuzak wrapped his own forearm with a strip torn from his undershirt, pulling it tight with his teeth until the cloth went red.

“Your arm,” Hakiia said without opening his eyes.

“It’s wrapped.”

“How deep?”

“Deep enough.”

A pause.

“Can you pull?”

Hurkuzak looked at his hands. The left was cut and stiff. The right was bitten and weak. Between them he still had more use than Hakiia did. He looked at the broken travois, the twisted lashings, the dark valley ahead.

“I can pull,” he said.

Hakiia opened his eyes. He looked at Hurkuzak for a moment, measuring the lie or the truth of it, and then closed them again. The blood on his face had already begun to freeze.

“Then pull,” Hakiia said.
