# Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

The first wolf came at dusk.

Hurkuzak had been pulling the travois for what felt like hours but may have been less. The basin had narrowed into a shallow valley between rocky slopes, the trees thin and bent with altitude, the snow deep enough that each step was a labor of lifting and sinking and lifting again. His legs burned. His ribs sent a flare of pain with every breath that went deeper than shallow. His cut palm had opened again under the friction of the drag poles. The blood made the wood slick and warm.

Behind him, Hakiia lay on the travois with his eyes open and his broken legs pointed at the sky. The sky was darkening from gray toward black -- the kind of black that meant real cold.

The wolves had been following since the basin. Hurkuzak had seen them at intervals -- gray shapes at the edge of sight, appearing between trees and disappearing, never close enough to count reliably but never far enough to forget. They moved in the same direction he moved. They maintained the same pace.

Then one of them was closer.

It came from the left, angling in from behind a stand of dead timber, and it stopped twenty paces away and stood there. It was large -- larger than the dogs had been, with a thick winter coat and a wide head and eyes that caught the failing light and gave none of it back.

Hurkuzak stopped pulling. He turned to face the wolf and his hand went to the hatchet on his belt and the wolf watched his hand move and did not retreat.

"It's testing," Hakiia said from the travois. His voice was thin but steady. "It wants to see if you respond. Don't move toward it. Don't move away. Stand."

Hurkuzak stood. The wolf stood.

After a time the wolf turned and walked back toward the trees. Not running. Walking. Unhurried.

"Two more," Hakiia said. "Behind us, right side."

Hurkuzak turned. Two wolves stood at the tree line to the right, thirty paces back. They were smaller than the first but their positioning was different -- angled, spread apart, covering different lines of approach. They were not watching Hurkuzak. They were watching the travois.

"They can see I'm down," Hakiia said. "They know what that means." He pushed himself up on his elbows. His face tightened. His breathing shortened. The muscles in his arms trembled with the effort of holding himself partially upright. "Pull me closer to those rocks. The ones at the base of the slope."

Hurkuzak dragged the travois to the rocks Hakiia had indicated -- a low cluster of boulders at the foot of the valley wall, large enough to block approach from one direction and create a corner where the wolves could not come from behind. He set the travois down with Hakiia's back to the stone and his face toward the open ground.

"Give me the knife," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak gave him the knife. Hakiia held it the way he held all weapons -- low, angled, the blade an extension of his forearm rather than something gripped in his fist. Even lying down. Even broken. The knife in his hand looked like a threat.

"You'll take the hatchet," Hakiia said. "Stand in front of me. When they come -- and they will come -- they'll come from the sides first, testing. Then from the front. The first one through is not the attack. The first one through is the question. The attack comes from the one you're not looking at."

"How do you know -- "

"Because that is how anything that hunts in groups kills something bigger than itself. They separate attention. They stretch the defense. Then they come through the gap." He looked at Hurkuzak with eyes that were bright with pain and clear with a quality of focus that burned off everything unnecessary.

"I'll be the bait," Hakiia said. "I'm already down. They can see it. They'll come for me because I'm the easy kill. When they commit to me, you hit whatever is closest and you hit it hard enough that it does not get up."

"You can't fight from the ground."

"Watch me."

The wait was worse than the attack would be. Hurkuzak stood in front of the travois with the hatchet in his right hand and his body angled the way Hakiia had told him -- weight forward, weapon high, feet wide. The wolves moved at the edge of sight. They circled. They came closer and pulled back and came closer again, and each approach was a little nearer than the last, and each retreat was a little shorter.

Five of them visible now. The three from before and two more that had joined from the basin, drawn by the commotion or the blood or the simple gravity of opportunity. They spread themselves around the clearing in a loose formation that covered every approach, and they watched -- spread wide, none of them looking at each other, and yet the spacing was exact.

The wolves came.

They came the way Hakiia said they would. One from the left, trotting forward in a half-circle that drew Hurkuzak's eyes and his body to that side, its head low and its steps deliberate. Then one from the right, faster, cutting closer, angling toward the travois where Hakiia lay with the knife low and his breathing controlled. Hurkuzak turned to face the right-side wolf and the one on the left darted forward.

Hakiia's voice cut through the cold. "Left. Now."

Hurkuzak swung. The hatchet caught air. The wolf had already veered, pulling back with the liquid speed of something that had calculated the reach of the weapon and stayed one stride outside it. Hurkuzak stumbled forward and his bad knee buckled and he caught himself and the wolf on the right was closer now, much closer, and he could see its teeth and the breath coming from its mouth in short controlled bursts.

He swung again. The blade glanced off the wolf's shoulder and the impact jolted up through his arm and the wolf yelped and scrambled sideways and Hurkuzak followed it, swinging a third time, and the third swing went wide because his footing was wrong and his weight was forward and the hatchet buried itself in the snow instead of the animal.

Something hit him from the left. Weight and heat and the smell of breath and the scrape of claws through his coat and then teeth, teeth in the meat of his forearm, and the pain was immediate and enormous and deeply wrong -- not the clean pain of a cut or a blow but the grinding pressure of jaws designed to hold and not let go. He screamed. He did not decide to scream. His body screamed because it had been seized by something that would not release it.

He swung the hatchet with his free arm. The angle was bad and the force was worse but the blade struck bone -- skull or jaw, he couldn't tell -- and the wolf released and stumbled and Hurkuzak hit it again while it was still staggering and this time the hatchet sank into something that gave way and the wolf went down and did not get up.

Blood was running down his forearm and into his hand and the world was loud with his own breathing and behind him Hakiia was making a sound that was not a word but a command -- a compressed bark of direction that meant move, turn, look.

A wolf had reached the travois.

It had come while Hurkuzak fought, using the distraction exactly as Hakiia had predicted, slipping past the defense to reach the body on the ground. It was on the travois, its front paws on the furs, its head low and its weight forward and it was going for Hakiia's legs because the legs were broken and wolves knew where damage lived -- not through thought but through something older and more reliable.

Hakiia moved.

Hakiia's upper body rose from the travois in a motion that should not have been possible for a man with two shattered legs and nothing left to burn. His left hand reached for the wolf -- the arm grinding in the shoulder, the fingers slow to close, the joint protesting with a sound Hurkuzak could hear from five paces away -- and caught the animal by the scruff of the neck. The grip was imperfect, the damaged shoulder shuddering under the weight, but it held. His right hand drove the knife into the animal's throat and pulled it sideways and the wolf's blood came in a sheet that covered Hakiia's chest and face and arms and the wolf convulsed and Hakiia held it -- held the dying animal against his own body while it thrashed and kicked and the kicks struck his broken legs and the sound Hakiia made was not a sound that came from a place where words existed.

He held it until it stopped.

Then the stopped thing moved again. Not the wolf -- the wolf was dead. The jaw. The massive jaw that had been clamped around nothing during the dying convulsed one final time, a mechanical spasm that had nothing to do with life, and the jaw found Hakiia's right hand -- the hand still pressed against the wolf's throat, the hand still holding the knife -- and closed on it with the blind grinding force of a dead thing that had not finished killing.

Hakiia made a sound. Not the compressed sound from before. Something involuntary and worse.

Hurkuzak was already moving. He grabbed the wolf's muzzle and pried and the jaw resisted with the locked strength of a dead thing's last contraction and he pried harder and the jaw opened and Hakiia's hand came free and the hand was wrong.

The thumb was still there -- a stump of it, the joint intact but the flesh torn and the bone visible. The index and middle fingers were gone, sheared through at the knuckle. The ring and little fingers were still attached but broken, bent at angles that fingers do not bend, the skin split where the bone had pushed through.

The blood would not stop. It came from every opened surface, and the snow around Hakiia's hand turned dark in a spreading circle that grew too fast. He could not stop it -- too many openings, each one pouring freely.

Hakiia looked at his hand. The look was brief and flat, the look of a man taking stock rather than grieving. "Heat the knife," he said. His voice was the voice from the leg-setting. "In the fire. Until it glows. Then press it against what is open."

Hurkuzak built the fire back up with hands that were shaking and fed it until the flames were high enough and held the knife blade in the center of the fire and watched the metal change color while his brother bled into the snow. When the blade glowed he pulled it free and knelt beside Hakiia and pressed the flat of the heated metal against the open wounds of his brother's hand.

The smell was rendered flesh, the sweetness of burning tissue. Hakiia's body locked rigid. The compressed sound came again, shorter and harder, forced through a jaw clenched so tight that the muscles stood out like cords. Hurkuzak pressed and held and pressed again, moving the blade to each open surface, sealing what the wolf's jaw had torn open, and the blood stopped.

The left arm had stopped working. The exertion of the wolf kill -- the shoulder forced past what the cliff damage had left intact, the grinding Hurkuzak had heard, the weight of the wolf held by a joint that was already failing -- had completed what the fall had started. The arm lay at Hakiia's side and did not move. Hurkuzak watched Hakiia try to lift it once, his face concentrated with the effort, and the arm did not respond. The fingers did not twitch.

Hakiia lay in the wolf's blood with his eyes closed. His breathing was shallow and wrong, each exhale shorter than the one before. His right hand was a cauterized ruin. His left arm was dead weight. The wolf kill had cost him both.

The remaining wolves watched from a distance. Two dead. One limping from the blow to its shoulder. The rest -- three, maybe four -- stood in a loose arc at the edge of the clearing and looked at what had just happened and recalculated.

Hurkuzak stood in the bloody snow with the hatchet shaking in his hand and his forearm bleeding and his body vibrating with something that was not shaking and not stillness and not anything he recognized.

"They'll go," Hakiia said. His voice was thin, scraped down to nothing. "They lost two. They don't have the numbers to absorb that and press the attack. They'll go."

The wolves went. Not all at once. The limping one first, turning and moving back through the trees with a care it had not shown before. Then the others, one by one, drifting back toward the basin where the dead dogs still lay and where the eating was easier.

The silence after was not peace. It was the temporary absence of violence. The snow around them was churned and bloody, trampled into a dark confusion of boot prints and paw prints and drag marks. Two dead wolves lay in the clearing -- the one Hurkuzak had killed with the hatchet and the one Hakiia had killed with the knife and what was left of his hands. The dead wolves looked smaller than they had alive. They looked like animals. The living ones had looked like something else.

Hurkuzak looked at his brother. Hakiia lay in the wolf's blood with his eyes closed, his chest rising and falling in a pattern that was wrong -- too shallow, too fast. There was a slackening in him that did not look like rest, a settling that did not look like ease.

The knife lay in the snow beside him. His right hand could not hold it. His right hand could not hold anything. The cauterized ruin sat against the furs, dark and swelling where the burning had not reached. His left arm lay at his side, motionless, the shoulder settled into a position that suggested something fundamental had disconnected inside the joint.

Hurkuzak knelt beside him. His forearm was bleeding freely -- the bite had gone deep, through coat and clothing and into the muscle underneath. He wrapped it with a strip torn from his undershirt, pulling the binding tight with his teeth, and the cloth turned red almost immediately.

"Your arm," Hakiia said without opening his eyes.

"It's wrapped."

"How deep?"

"Deep enough."

"Can you pull?"

Hurkuzak looked at his hands. The left was cut. The right was bitten. Between them they held one working grip. He did not look at Hakiia's hands. He could not look at Hakiia's hands again yet.

"I can pull," he said.

Hakiia opened his eyes. He looked at Hurkuzak. The look lasted a moment and then it was gone. Hakiia closed his eyes. His breathing kept its wrong rhythm. The blood on his face had already begun to freeze.

"Then pull," Hakiia said.
