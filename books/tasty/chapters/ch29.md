Chapter 29: What Hakiia Carried

The wreck was smaller than catastrophe should be. A few lengths of splintered wood. Metal fittings rusted the color of old blood. Debris compressed into the mud by months of snow and melt.

He stood at the edge of it. The mud was at his ankles. The smell was earth and mineral rot and the cold groundwater that pooled in the ravine's lowest places. The weather had not held. What should have been clean, biting cold had turned instead into wet snow and freezing wind, and the ravine carried the evidence of it — water moving where there should have been crust, mud where there should have been firmness, everything softened, loosened, made unstable. The spring had not come cleanly. It had come badly.

The shallow current pushed through the debris field and around the rocks and over the bones of the dogs scattered in the mud downstream, disarticulated by scavengers, bleached where the sun had reached them, dark where the earth had held them. He did not count the bones. He did not look for the skull of the one he had killed himself — the quiet one, the left rear, the one whose back had broken in the fall and whose eyes had been clear and calm when Hurkuzak put the knife in. He looked at the wreck and began.

The work was slower than it should have been. The mud resisted. The freezing winds had driven water deep into the ground before the thaw, and now the soil held it like a wound that would not close. Every step shifted under him. Every pull risked collapse. The wreck had not simply settled — it had been worked on by weather, pressed, loosened, shifted again.

Pulling pieces free required kneeling in cold water and digging with both hands until the wreckage surrendered each piece separately, like a mouth releasing a held thing.

He found the runner first — the broken one, not the intact one he had used for the travois. It was wedged between two rocks, the wood swollen and soft, the metal banding underneath still holding its shape. He pulled it free and laid it on the bank and looked at it. His hands stayed at his sides.

He found rope. The rope had rotted but the knots had held, and the knots were Hakiia's — the particular hitches and bends that Hakiia tied with the automatic precision of a man who considered every knot a small test of character and judged the tier accordingly. Hurkuzak held the rotted rope and traced the knots with his thumb. His brother's hands preserved in fiber.

He found a tool roll. Leather, Hakiia's stitching, the contents rusted but identifiable — a small saw, an awl, a set of needles for heavy repair, a whetstone in a fitted pocket. The leather had protected them from the worst of the water. The rust was surface. The tools were still essentially whole.

He spread them on the bank beside the runner and the rope. He looked at them for a long time. Then he went back into the mud.

The sled's storage compartments had been built into the frame — Hakiia's design. The main compartment had broken open in the fall and its contents had scattered across the ravine, destroyed or dispersed by weather and animals over the intervening months. But the sled had secondary compartments. Smaller. Built into the frame's cross-members, accessible only if you knew where to look, sealed against moisture with wax and treated leather.

Hurkuzak had not packed those compartments. Hakiia had packed them himself, the morning they left, while Hurkuzak loaded the dogs. The division had seemed ordinary — Hakiia always kept the important work for himself, so consistently that the keeping was invisible.

He found the first compartment by feel. His hands moved along the cross-member — swollen, mud-caked, the wood soft enough that his fingers left impressions — and found the seam where the compartment lid met the frame. The wax seal had held. The treated leather gasket underneath had held. He pried the lid open with his knife and the wood split along the grain and inside was a space the size of two fists and inside the space was a bundle wrapped in oiled cloth.

He pulled it out. The oiling had preserved the cloth, and when he unwrapped it his hands were holding something that belonged to the life on the other side of the cliff. The bundle contained a small wooden box, sealed with wax, and inside the box were three containers. Ceramic. Small — each the size of his thumb. Stopped with wax seals that bore no mark he recognized. He held one up to the light. The light did not penetrate the ceramic. The contents did not shift when he tilted it.

The cold made the ceramic slick. His grip tightened. The wind moved through the ravine and found every gap in his clothing, every wet seam. The containers felt heavier than their size should allow.

Something tightened behind his eyes. A pressure at the base of his skull. He stood in the cold water with one of them in his hand while the sensation moved through him the way sound moves through a wall.

He set the containers aside and went back to the wreck.

The second compartment was harder to find. It was built into a different cross-member, lower in the frame, and the frame had partially collapsed onto it. He had to lever a section of broken wood away with the hatchet, working carefully because the compartment underneath was intact.

This compartment was longer, narrower, built to hold something flat. He opened it and inside was an oiled leather tube, the kind used for carrying documents in wet conditions. The tube was sealed at both ends with wax. He broke one seal and slid the contents out.

Papers. Not the casual notes of a hunting trip — weather observations, game counts, rough maps. These were written in Hakiia's hand, but smaller, more careful, more deliberate. Hurkuzak held the papers and did not read them. The handwriting was Hakiia's, and the care in every letter was Hakiia's — the same refusal to do anything carelessly.

He began to read.

The first page was a diagram. Concentric circles bisected by straight lines, notations at the intersections in a shorthand that was not language but something between words and reckoning — marks that carried instruction without spelling it. Hurkuzak knew neither the system nor its purpose. He turned the page.

The second page was a list. Items, quantities, preparation methods. Dried materials — some named, some described by what they did rather than what they were. Powders drawn from flesh and organ, described by their source with the care of a man who had opened bodies and looked. Preserved substances in sealed containers, how long each would hold noted beside it, what virtue each carried at full strength, what it became when it went stale. A kit assembled for a specific working, each piece weighed against what the rite required, alternatives noted where the preferred substance could not be had.

The physician's jars in Merrath. The notation on the parcels. The grading language, the preparation methods, the specificity of which tissues produced which effects. The list in his hands spoke the same tongue. Not the same words, not quite the same terms — but the same attention to source and preparation, the same knowledge of what flesh could carry and what it could not. He held both of them in his mind without being able to close the distance between them.

The pressure behind his eyes had moved into his chest. He heard his own breathing, sharper now, visible in the cold air.

He turned another page. More diagrams. These were anatomical — demon anatomy, rendered with a precision that required direct observation or detailed source materials. Tissues, organs, connections between systems. Arrows indicated transfer pathways. Notations described what was absorbed and what was lost. The diagrams studied how one body's properties entered another.

He turned another page.

The handwriting was the same. The care was the same. But the subject had changed — he could feel it in the density of the notation before he understood what he was reading, the way the lines pressed closer together and the margins filled in. He read slowly. The clinical distance from the previous pages was gone. These diagrams were not general. They were specific to two bodies — shared lineage written in the margins, physiological signatures rendered side by side. The word that appeared again and again in the notation was not the word for absorption or transfer.

He read the word. He read it again. It was written in Hakiia's careful hand and it said inheritance.

Hurkuzak's hands were shaking. He noticed because the papers trembled and there was no wind strong enough to account for it.

He found the correspondence at the bottom of the tube. Three fragments — not complete letters but portions, torn or cut from larger documents, preserved as a man preserves evidence or instructions. The handwriting was not Hakiia's. It was a different hand — precise, educated, the writing of someone accustomed to giving instructions and expecting them followed. The tone was the tone of men conducting business — arrangements, schedules, the movement of materials. Nothing warm. Nothing wasted. Materials supplied. A window of opportunity tied to season and location. And references to a subject that were clearly references to a person, though the word used was vessel.

He put the papers down.

He looked at the ravine below him. The mud. The current moving around the bones. The dark shape of the wreck half-swallowed by a thaw that had come wrong.

He picked the papers up.

He gathered the materials — the ceramic containers, the document tube, the loose pages — and held them against his chest. He climbed out of the ravine. The mud gave way under his feet. His hands found holds in the eroded earth and he pulled himself upward, out of the place where the sled had gone over, carrying what had been waiting in it.

He reached the top. The late afternoon light was golden and indifferent, warming the mud where he had dug.

The wind followed him up. It did not ease with elevation. It cut cleaner here, pulling heat from his hands, drying the mud in patches and leaving others slick.

He sat on the ridge above the ravine. He spread the materials before him on the ground — the containers in a row, the document tube beside them, the pages weighted with stones against the breeze. The light gave him enough clarity to read. He was not ready to read. He was going to read anyway, because not-reading was no longer shelter.

The pressure inside him had gone still. Not absent — held. Present in the way a room is present after a door has been shut inside it.

The light faded. Hurkuzak sat with the evidence of his brother's care spread before him and the evidence of his brother's intent folded inside it. The same hand. The same attention. The same refusal to do anything carelessly.

He read.
