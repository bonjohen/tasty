# Chapter 7: The Weight of What Is Left

He did not pull far.

Fifty paces from the rocks, maybe less, the travois snagged on something buried in the snow and Hurkuzak's legs folded and he went down to his knees and could not immediately stand again. The cold pressed in against his face where it met the snow, and for a moment the snow seemed warm by comparison with the air above it, and that wrongness was how he knew he was in trouble.

He got up. He freed the travois. He pulled.

The wolves did not return. The landscape was quiet in the way that comes after predators leave — the silence of having already made their decision. Hurkuzak moved through it with the slow, grinding effort of a body borrowing from something deeper, something that would need to be repaid but not yet. The cabin was ahead, somewhere. His body had not yet decided it was finished with him.

Behind him, Hakiia was quiet.

That was the first thing Hurkuzak noticed and the thing he tried not to think about. Since the cliff, since the fall, since the wolves, Hakiia had been the voice that held the situation together. He had directed, corrected, assessed, and commanded from the ground with the same relentless authority he had exercised from the front of the line, and that authority had been the architecture of Hurkuzak's obedience. Without it, the march was just a man pulling weight through snow.

He stopped after another hundred paces and turned to look at his brother.

Hakiia's eyes were open. But the quality of them had changed. They were focused on something above him — the sky, the tree canopy, the darkness settling over the valley — and the focus was softer. Duller.

"Hakiia."

The eyes moved. Slowly. They found Hurkuzak and held him and something behind them was working, trying to assemble the information — the face, the snow, the direction of travel — into something coherent.

"The cabin," Hakiia said.

"I'm heading toward it."

"Good." A pause. "Check the splints."

Hurkuzak set down the drag poles and knelt beside the travois. The splints on the left leg had shifted during the fight. The binding had loosened where the wolf's thrashing had knocked against the crossbar pieces, and the leg underneath had moved in a way that sent a deep, sick feeling through Hurkuzak's stomach. He retightened the binding. Hakiia's face did not change.

The right leg was worse. The ankle splint had held, but the skin above it had darkened further, almost black, and the swelling had spread upward into the calf. The flesh was hot to the touch — a wrong heat. Hurkuzak pressed gently against the swelling and felt the skin resist and then yield in a way that skin should not yield, and he pulled his hand back.

"How does it look?" Hakiia asked.

Hurkuzak could see what was happening inside the leg even if he could not name it. The color, the heat, the softness — these were the signs of a body losing. Blood was pooling where it should not pool.

"The splint is holding."

"That's not what I asked."

"It's swollen. The color is bad."

Hakiia looked at the sky. He said nothing for a while. Then he said, "Check the splints," and Hurkuzak realized that his brother had already said that, less than a minute ago, and had forgotten, and that the forgetting was new.

He cleaned what he could reach. The gash on Hakiia's forehead had stopped bleeding and frozen over in a dark crust that made the left side of his face look like it belonged to a different man. The right hand was worse than when he had cauterized it. The sealed tissue had darkened further, the skin around the burns hot and swollen in the way that meant infection was already working inside the damage. The thumb stump was discolored. The two remaining fingers, broken and fixed at their wrong angles, had swelled against the cauterized edges. The hand smelled wrong — thick and sweet, the smell of flesh beginning to rot.

The left arm had not moved since the fight. He lifted it to check the shoulder and the arm came up dead weight, the joints loose, the fingers hanging without tension. He let it go. It fell against the furs. It did not twitch. Whatever the cliff had started in the shoulder, the wolf fight had finished. Between the destroyed right hand and the dead left arm, Hakiia could not hold food. Could not hold water. Could not hold the knife that lay in the snow beside the travois.

He checked Hakiia's breathing. It was faster than it should have been and shallower than it should have been and every few breaths there was a pause — a gap where the rhythm skipped, as though the body had briefly considered stopping and then changed its mind. The pauses were short. They would get longer.

He tried to stop the bleeding from his own forearm. The wrap had soaked through and the wound underneath was still seeping, and the cold had slowed it but not stopped it. He tore another strip from his shirt and bound it over the first, pulling it tight enough that the pressure sent a wave of pain from wrist to elbow. His fingers were stiffening. The bite had done something to the tendons or the muscle that affected his grip, and when he flexed his hand the response came late and only partway, as though the fingers were deciding independently whether to answer.

He looked at his brother again. Hakiia's eyes had closed. His face, half-crusted with dried blood, half-shadowed by the failing light, had gone slack. The jaw had loosened. The hard line of his mouth, the measuring set of his brow — gone. What remained was simply a man, exhausted past any exhaustion Hurkuzak had witnessed, holding onto consciousness because he had nothing else left to hold.

They were in a shallow valley in deep winter with no shelter, no fire, minimal food, and a body that was dying. The wolves were gone but the cold was not. The cabin was ahead, somewhere in the dark that was now falling in earnest, and the distance between here and there was made of snow and pain and every step cost more than the step before.

For the first time since the cliff, the decision was his. Not because Hakiia had yielded authority. Because Hakiia's authority had always required his attention, and his attention was no longer continuous. He was conscious. He was present. But the commands were coming slower and repeating, and the gaps between them were filling with silence.

Hurkuzak built a fire. It took him a long time — his hands were bad, the wood was wet, and the cold killed the spark twice before the third caught. But the fire caught, eventually, because he kept trying past the point where trying felt useful, and the warmth was small and it was all they had.

He moved the travois closer to the flames. He arranged the furs over Hakiia's body and tucked them around the legs and the feet and the places where the cold entered first. The furs were stiff with blood — Hakiia's, the wolf's, his own, the distinctions lost in the freezing dark. He heated snow in his cupped hands and held the water to Hakiia's mouth. Hakiia drank some. Most of it ran down his chin and froze in the stubble there.

He sat beside his brother in the firelight and ate a strip of dried meat that tasted of nothing and chewed it until his jaw ached and swallowed it. He tried to give Hakiia a strip. Hakiia took it, held it, and did not eat it. After a while Hurkuzak took it back.

"You fought well," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak turned. Hakiia was looking at him. The eyes were clearer now — the firelight had brought them back from wherever they had drifted, or the warmth had, or the pain that the warmth made sharper by giving the body enough energy to feel it again.

"I fought badly," Hurkuzak said. "I missed twice. I got bitten."

"You killed it."

"You killed the one that mattered."

Hakiia made a sound that might have been a laugh if his body had contained enough air for laughter. "I killed it from the ground with two broken legs and the killing took the last of what my hands had to give. That is not a model for a second engagement." He paused. His breathing hitched and steadied. "You stood. When it came, you stood. That is not nothing."

Then Hakiia's right hand moved. What was left of it. The ruined thing came off the furs and moved toward Hurkuzak's arm — the unbitten one, the left — and the movement was slow, effortful. The thumb stump found the skin of Hurkuzak's forearm and pressed. The broken fingers did not close. Could not close, would never close again. What touched Hurkuzak was not a grip but a pressure, and the pressure held everything the hand had left. The touch lasted for a breath, maybe two. Then the hand fell back to the furs, trembling as it withdrew.

He sat with it. A broken hand on his arm, too damaged to grip and too stubborn to stop reaching.

Before dawn, Hurkuzak repacked the travois and checked the splints and checked Hakiia's breathing, which was worse, and checked his eyes, which were slower, and picked up the drag poles and began to pull.

Hakiia did not speak. Not to direct. Not to correct. Not to tell Hurkuzak that his angle was wrong or his pace was too slow or his grip on the poles was the grip of a man who was afraid of what he was holding.

He pulled through the valley. The snow was deep and his body had found a rhythm that was not strength but the absence of any alternative to strength — a mechanical repetition that demanded everything of his back, his legs, his ruined hands. The dawn came late and gray, and when it arrived it illuminated a landscape that looked exactly like the landscape he had pulled through in the dark, except now he could see how much of it remained. The valley went on. The trees went on. The horizon never moved.

His world contracted to the poles in his hands, the drag of weight behind him, and the next place his boot would fall. Step and sink and lift and step and sink and lift, and each cycle cost warmth he would not recover. The cabin was somewhere ahead, and every step toward it was a step away from what he had been that morning.

Behind him, Hakiia breathed. The pauses between breaths were longer now. The returns were shallower. Hurkuzak did not need to look back to hear what was happening.

He did not look back.

The snow had already half-covered the dead dogs. By morning, the valley would look clean.

He pulled. The poles wore grooves in his hands. The snow was deep and the cabin was far and behind him, the space where Hakiia's voice had been.
