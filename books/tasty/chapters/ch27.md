Chapter 27: What the Hunter Eats

Vark ate her morning meal in the gray light before dawn, crouching beside a cold camp with her back to a tree and her crossbow within reach. The meal was a strip of dried demon flesh from her kit — a thigh cut, aged three weeks, preserved in salt and rendered fat and wrapped in treated cloth. She chewed it slowly. Thorough chewing extracted more from the tissue. The flesh was fuel.

The taste was clean. Faintly gamey, carrying the mineral quality demon muscle held regardless of the kill. She had eaten enough of it to know the variations: young demons tasted leaner, sharper, with a metallic bite she could feel on her teeth. Older ones were richer, denser, with a depth that came only with age. The piece she was eating now was from a borderland demon she had killed two months prior — middling age, moderate quality, adequate for maintenance.

She was maintaining. Without regular intake the benefits degraded — strength first, then the cold tolerance that let her work the mountain ranges. She maintained the way a physician maintained a tonic regimen: the same measure at the same interval, the same attention to whether the body still answered.

She finished the strip and packed her kit and checked the trail.

Hurkuzak's track was two days old now. He had passed through a town called Merrath — a dry-goods vendor confirmed his presence, and a physician had noticed a man loitering outside his shop with the stare of someone seeing the trade for the first time. The physician had not been alarmed. People discovered the flesh economy at their own pace, and the discovery was usually followed by either a purchase or a departure. The man had departed.

She followed. The track led east from Merrath along a secondary road, then north into the forest, the same pattern: road to tree line, exposure to concealment, the practiced oscillation of someone who understood that roads were useful and dangerous. The fieldcraft was still better than it should have been, still carrying the quality she had identified from the beginning: learned rather than born into, shaped by another hand, not native to the one who used it.

She thought about the quarry as she tracked. The physical profile was established — strength, senses, healing, cold tolerance, and a combat capability that emerged under stress like a second self overriding the first. What interested her now was his avoidance giving way to something more deliberate. He was learning to use what he carried. And what he carried, by every measure she knew, was unprecedented.

She knew where she fit. At the bottom were the trappers, opportunistic and poorly equipped. Above them, hunters like herself, professional and augmented. Above the hunters, the merchants and physicians who never touched a body. And above them all, invisible, the patrons.

She had never met a patron. She had worked for them through so many intermediaries that the original source of a contract was untraceable by the time it reached her. But she knew they existed the way she knew the weather existed: by its effects. The premiums. The specific requirements. The contracts that paid multiples of the standard rate for product only a patron would specify. Evidence of an appetite that believed flesh, properly consumed, could produce not merely strength but transformation.

She had sent word to Koss. A courier, a coded message, enough to open the door. Koss would move it upward. Above Koss was a name she had not been given, which meant the name carried weight.

The morning warmed. She moved through the forest — efficient, low, staying off the ridge lines where her silhouette would carry.

She thought about her kills. Over fifteen years she had hunted and processed more demons than she could count precisely, though her tally — marks in the back of her field journal, one per kill — put the number somewhere between sixty and seventy. Most had been unremarkable — baseline specimens, standard properties, standard prices. Some had been notable: unusually resilient, unusually fast, carrying a sensory gift or a healing factor that placed them into the premium range.

Three had been dangerous. She remembered those clearly because the dangerous ones taught you where your augmentations ended. One had nearly killed her. The scar on her left shoulder blade was the reminder. She had consumed that one with particular thoroughness afterward, partly for the enhanced properties the tissues carried and partly for the completion of a calculation she had started when it drew blood.

She was a manufactured predator, built from the materials of her kills. Every augmentation she carried — the strength, the speed, the enhanced smell, the healing — was the product of systematic consumption sustained over years.

Hurkuzak was not supply. Not yet. He was proof of a deeper mechanism, one her own method could not match. She maintained. He had been transformed. The difference mattered economically. Her augmentations could be replicated by any hunter willing to do the work and accept the risk. What Hurkuzak represented could not be replicated through the standard channels, which did not include killing your brother, consuming him over weeks of isolation, and surviving the integration intact.

She had eaten demon flesh for years, and it had never produced anything like what the witnesses described — a second presence, a second will wearing his movements, a voice that shaped action. Her consumption was a transaction. What the quarry carried was something she did not yet know how to price. She looked east along the trail.

She considered the capture logistics. She would need a contained space and a means to drop him — rope and a soporific, something from the buyer's apothecary or prepared by herself — and ideally the support of the buyer's own personnel, who would have the resources to manage a specimen this valuable. The buyer would want to be involved. A specimen of this significance was not something you trusted to a courier. The buyer would send people, or come himself, and she would learn something about the resources behind the contract.

She paused at midday and examined the trail more closely. His pace had changed. He was slowing — not from injury, she could see no evidence of distress in the track. The stride was shorter, the placement more deliberate, as though his attention had turned inward. She had seen this before in quarry: the moment where the animal stops fleeing and starts processing. It could mean he was planning. It could mean he was despairing. Either way, his pattern was shifting.

The afternoon passed. She moved east, closing the gap by the small increments that professional patience allowed. She was positioning, and she was willing to wait.

The forest was quiet. She checked her restraint kit, measured the remaining cord, and settled in to watch the trail ahead darken into evening.
