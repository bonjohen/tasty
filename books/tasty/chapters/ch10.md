Chapter 10: The Carrying

He did not know how long he sat. The fire died and the cold replaced it and the cold was absolute. The snow fell on Hakiia's face and on his own hands and on the furs between them. It was the same snow it had always been. It did not know what had happened here.

At some point his body began to shiver. The shivering was not a decision. The body had its own idea, and the mind was somewhere else.

Hurkuzak stood. The standing took a long time — hands on the ground, knees braced, a push that failed and a second push that succeeded. The world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out and waited, and the balance came. He looked down at what was on the travois.

His brother lay with his eyes closed and his face still and the blood on the furs frozen. The body was no longer dying, or in pain, or commanding, or arguing, or saying please. What remained was mass without will, shaped like a man.

The first problem was the travois. The wolf fight and the dragging had wrecked it. The lashings hung loose, one pole cracked near the crosspiece, and the furs beneath were stiff with frozen blood and no longer slid clean. Hurkuzak knelt and retied what he could and reinforced the cracked pole with a strip of leather. He repositioned the furs and tested the frame, lifting the drag poles, pulling. It moved. Barely. The resistance was different now — heavier, dead-weighted — and he adjusted his grip and leaned and the frame pulled back at him in a way it had not pulled before. He pulled through it. The poles bit into the grooves they had already worn in his palms. The pain was not new, but the context was — yesterday these hands had pulled a living man. The poles did not know the difference.

The snow was deep and the trees were sparse and the dawn was coming in the slow, reluctant way winter dawns arrive — not as light but as a lessening of dark, a flat gray that showed the landscape without warming it. He pulled through the half-light. The travois scraped and caught and scraped again. His arms burned, his ribs burned, his bitten forearm pulsed with every pull — so regular it replaced the hours.

He pulled for what might have been an hour. The travois caught on roots and rocks under the snow. Each time it caught he stopped and worked it free, and each jolt traveled the frame, and the body shifted with the slack movement of a thing not choosing to move.

He stopped and rested and checked the travois. One of Hakiia's arms had fallen off the side and was dragging in the snow. Leaving it to drag was the kind of thing Hakiia would have hated. He lifted the arm — heavy, stiff, the joints already cold-set — and laid it across the chest and the hand rested there the way it had rested after the pressure on his wrist had eased. The thumb stump, the missing fingers, the broken ones fixed at their wrong angles, the dark sealed places where the burning had done its work. He could not look at that hand for long.

He apologized.

The word came out of him without thought. “Sorry,” he said. The silence after it was the silence of a word spoken to no one.

He pulled again. The landscape became a single texture — white, cold, featureless. The trees were bare poles. The sky was a ceiling. The rhythm of the march was the only thing between him and what was waiting to arrive.

A voice, or the memory of one: Lower your shoulder. You are pulling from the arms. Pull from the legs and the back.

He heard it. He almost turned around. Then there was only the wind and the scraping of the travois and the sound of his own breathing — loud, labored, like the wrong breathing Hakiia had made in the hours before the end.

He looked ahead and saw, through the trees, a shape that was the cabin — a wall face catching the light, the dark slot of a door, the horizontal of a snow-held roof. He walked toward it for ten minutes before the shape resolved into a rock face and a shelf of ice that had mimicked walls and windows. The cabin was not there. The cabin was still ahead, in the same indeterminate distance it had always occupied, receding as he approached.

A warmth on his left side, the side closest to the travois, as though the body behind him were still radiating heat. It was not.

More came after that. A tree that had not been there and then was. A sound like a voice carried on the wind that resolved, when he listened, into nothing. A moment where the ground seemed to tilt and the snow slid sideways and he braced himself against a fall that was not happening. He felt Hakiia's hand on his shoulder — the rough, corrective grip that had steered him a thousand times — and he turned, and there was only the travois and the body and the cold, and the grip dissolved into the wind's pressure on his coat.

He stopped in the early afternoon, if it was the afternoon — the light had not changed, the gray was the same gray since dawn, and only his body, which was more tired than before, told him time had passed. He sat beside the travois and ate the last strip of dried meat and chewed it mechanically and swallowed it without tasting it.

He looked at Hakiia's face.

He adjusted the body on the travois. The adjustment was efficient, mechanical — a repositioning of mass for better balance, a straightening of the limbs so they did not catch on the frame. He noticed that his hands moved differently now. Earlier, he had touched Hakiia's body with the care of a man handling something sacred. Now his hands were solving a problem.

He pulled.

The afternoon wore into something that might have been evening. Winter light did not change — it simply was, and then it was not — but the cold deepened, and the sun, wherever it hid, was leaving.

He stopped again. He did not build a fire. A fire needed what he no longer had — hands that worked, a mind that could hold a thought, wood that was not buried under snow. Searching for any of it meant stopping the march, and stopping was something he could not afford.

He spoke to Hakiia.

“You were right about the leg,” he said.

The silence listened. The wind moved through the trees and the snow fell and the travois sat behind him with its burden and the burden did not respond.

He picked up the drag poles. His hands closed around them and the wood was cold and the grooves in his palms had deepened, the dried blood holding the wood in place. He pulled. The valley went on. The trees went on. The snow went on. Behind him, the body of his brother slid through the world on a broken travois. The cabin was ahead. The cold did not stop.
