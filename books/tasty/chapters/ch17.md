# Chapter 17: The Descent

He left the cabin on a morning when the meltwater was loud enough to hear from the sleeping platform. The sound had been building for days — a trickle beneath the snow, then a murmur, then a steady rush that said the mountain was releasing what it had been holding. He gathered what was worth carrying: the knife, the hatchet, the skinning tools, the coil of rope, one blanket, and the jar of fat he had been using for cooking, scraped clean and refilled with snowmelt. He wrapped them in the blanket, tied the bundle to his back, and walked to the door. He opened it. The light hit his face, and the light was warm.

He looked back once. The cabin was small. Smaller than he remembered. A hearth, a platform, a table, an alcove with a closed door and nothing behind it. The marks on the wall above the platform were the only evidence that a person had lived there for months.

He walked away. The snow was patchy on the slope below the cabin — compacted in the shade, gone entirely where the sun reached the south-facing rock — and his boots found bare ground for the first time in months. The ground felt foreign under his feet, soft where he expected resistance. Birdsong. He had not heard birdsong since before the cliff. The sound was small and ordinary and it hurt in a way he could not name, because the world had continued without him.

He descended. The trail was not a trail — there was no marked path from the cabin to the lowlands — but the terrain offered a route. Down the drainage, along the stream bed where the ice had broken, across the boulder field where the snow was shallow enough to navigate. His feet chose their path with a competence that belonged to someone who had walked these mountains before.

He moved too well. He was not tired when he should have been. The altitude that had thinned his breath on the ascent in autumn barely touched him now. His lungs worked easily. His legs worked easily. His body descended the mountain the way water descends.

The voice offered observations. That slope is unstable below the outcrop; cross higher. The stream narrows ahead; the banks will be passable on the left side but not the right. Smell that — smoke, old, from the southeast; someone has a fire within a day's walk. The observations arrived in the same register as the cabin corrections, the way a guide speaks to a companion whose patience is running thin.

He did not argue. The slope was unstable. The stream banks were passable only on the left. The smoke was there, faint but real, carrying the information that people were nearby. He used the knowledge because refusing it was performance, and he was too tired for performance.

The forest changed as he descended. The twisted, wind-bent trees of the high country gave way to taller stands of pine and spruce, then to mixed forest where the underbrush was thick and the canopy blocked the sky. The air warmed. Not warm, but warmer, and his body registered the change as a loosening — something held tight for months beginning to release.

He smelled the settlement before he saw it. Smoke, yes, but also animals, and waste, and the thick smell of people gathered close — cooking, sweat, leather, dung. The smell hit him with an intensity that staggered him. Not its strength — its complexity. He could pull the threads apart the way he had pulled sounds from silence near the cabin, each one distinct, arriving in a flood. Horses, from the smell. Something smaller — goats or sheep. Woodsmoke from multiple sources, which meant a settlement. Meat cooking. The meat smell arrived and his body responded with a hunger that was not desperation. It was confusion. His body had been eating one thing for months and it wanted that thing, not this.

He reached the road in the late afternoon. It was a dirt track, rutted and muddy with snowmelt, running east-west through a valley that opened below the mountain. There were tracks — wagon wheels, hooves, boots. The first stirrings of trade after a winter that had closed the passes.

He followed the road east. Within an hour he reached the settlement: a cluster of low buildings arranged along the track, built from timber and stone, roofed with thatch or shake. A trading post, or a way station — food, shelter, supply, farrier.

He walked among them.

After months of solitude, the presence of other bodies was overwhelming. They moved in patterns he could not predict. They made sounds — conversation, laughter, argument, the clatter of tools and the bark of dogs — that arrived from multiple directions at once, turning the ordinary noise of a small settlement into a wall he had to push through.

He flinched. A man passed him carrying firewood and the man's proximity triggered a response that was not fear but readiness — his weight shifted, his hands came up, his body oriented toward the man. The man looked at him. Hurkuzak looked at the ground. Too late.

He made himself walk slowly. His body wanted to run and he had to hold it back the way a rider holds back a horse. He could feel the effort in his shoulders, in the stiffness of his gait, in the way his eyes tracked everything that moved.

A child ran past him. He startled. His hand moved to the knife at his belt — a flash, too fast. The child did not see. A man leaning against a building wall did, and his eyes followed Hurkuzak for several steps before returning to whatever he had been watching before.

He found the trading post. A long, low building with a board across trestles and the smell of old trade — leather, grain, rendered fat, dried meat. A woman behind the board watched him enter.

"What do you need?" she said.

"Food. Dried meat if you have it. Grain."

She studied him. He was aware of what she saw — gaunt in the face but not in the body, wearing clothes the mountains had ruined, carrying a bundle that contained weapons and not much else. He did not look like a trader. He did not look like a traveler. He looked like what he was: something the winter had made and released.

She sold him dried meat and a sack of grain. He paid with a coin he had found in the cabin — old, worn, of uncertain value, but she took it with a trader's sense — a coin in hand was better than none. He sat in the corner of the post and ate the dried meat.

It tasted like nothing.

The meat was salted and dried and it was the kind of food he had eaten his entire life. He chewed and swallowed and the motions meant nothing.

He heard a conversation at the counter. Two men, speaking to the woman, their voices low but not low enough for his ears. One of them used a word he did not recognize. But the silence that followed it — he recognized that. The woman's face changed. She did not respond. She spoke of other things then, and the brightness in her voice was the kind that closes a door without touching it.

The word was not repeated. The silence was not explained.

He sat in the corner of the trading post and felt a new kind of enclosure close around him.

He finished the tasteless meat. He bought what he could afford. He left the trading post and walked east along the road, and the road went east into the noise of the world.

The voice was quiet in the crowd. It had not spoken during the transaction or the eating or the overhearing of the conversation. But it was present. He could feel it the way he felt his own heartbeat — a presence that rode behind his eyes and looked out through them.

He was among people and entirely alone. The cabin's solitude had been simple. This was not.

The road went east. Hurkuzak walked. Behind him, the mountain held the cabin and the marks on the wall and the empty alcove. Ahead of him was a world that had no interest in what he carried.
