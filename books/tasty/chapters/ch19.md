Chapter 19: What Does Not Break

Six days on the road, and Hurkuzak had learned the rhythms of travel — where to stop, where to sleep, how to move through settlements without drawing the kind of attention that fastened on strangers. He kept his pace moderate, even when his body wanted to run. He carried his bundle the way any traveler carried a bundle — shifting, adjusting, making the weight look ordinary. He ate at roadside cook fires when they were available — ordinary food, flavorless. A man who ate without pleasure drew no questions.

Every person he passed registered: armed, unarmed, the way a man stood who carried something hidden, the gait of someone who had recently used a weapon. The road was exposed. Anyone behind you could see three hundred paces. Take the tree line. Parallel the road. Do not walk on it. The advice was sound. The advice was always sound.

The road narrowed between a river and a rocky hillside, and three men waited in the narrows.

Not soldiers. Not hunters. They watched the road for travelers moving alone and knew that most people, when violence came for them, surrendered. It had worked before. It would work again, on someone.

Hurkuzak saw them before they showed themselves. His eyes — his improved, unwanted, borrowed eyes — picked out the positions: one on the road ahead, standing in the open, meant to be looked at. Two more in the rocks to the right, concealed, but not well enough. He heard the weight shift on loose stone.

The one on the road first. Close the distance before he can swing. Take the club. The two in the rocks — the scrape of boot on stone, the controlled breathing of men who thought they were quiet — would move when they saw him go down. One left, one right, converging behind. Use the club on the one from the right. The one from the left will hesitate when the first two go down. That hesitation is your window. The third one: something metal, the faint smell of rust from the right, position uncertain.

He wanted to be invisible. The narrows had no tree line, and the men were already moving.

"Stop there," the man said. "Set down the bundle."

Hurkuzak stopped. His body was doing something his mind had not chosen — settling into a stance, weight forward, center low. Hakiia's stance. The one his brother had assumed a hundred times before converting stillness into violence.

"I don't want trouble," Hurkuzak said.

"Then set down the bundle."

He moved fast. The man on the road was still raising the club when Hurkuzak closed the distance and his hand found the man's wrist and turned it and the club came free. The man's arm made a sound that was not quite breaking. The man went down. Hurkuzak was already turning.

The one from the right came faster than he had predicted, knife out, the blade driving for Hurkuzak's side. Hurkuzak caught it. Not on the handle. On the blade itself. His hand closed around the metal. The metal cut into his palm. His other hand brought the club around in an arc that broke against the man's ribs. The man folded and dropped. The knife came free, Hurkuzak's blood on the blade, the blade in his hand now.

The third man had stopped. The hesitation lasted two heartbeats. In them, he took in his companions on the ground and Hurkuzak in the road, bloody knife in one hand, club in the other. The third man made the only intelligent decision. He ran.

Hurkuzak stood in the road. His hand was bleeding — blood running between his fingers, dripping onto the packed earth. His breathing was fast but controlled. The control was not his.

The one with the twisted arm made sounds that were not words. The other lay curled on his side, breath shallow.

Hurkuzak looked at them. The voice's judgment was immediate: they would live, they would not follow, it was done.

A wagon had stopped on the road behind him. Two people sat on the seat — a man and a woman, traders — staring at him.

He stood in the road. The readiness drained from him. The stance loosened without his choosing. The knife, the club, the blood — none of it had been a decision.

He picked up his bundle and walked past the wagon without looking at them. He walked fast.

Too much. You left witnesses.

Hurkuzak walked faster. His hand bled, and the bleeding was wrong — too slow. The cut was deep. It should have run freely. Instead the blood thickened, the edges drawing together at a speed his body had never managed before. Within an hour the bleeding would stop. By evening the wound would be closed. By tomorrow there would be a scar where there should have been an open cut.

The voice offered its judgment on what had happened. The disarm was right. Your feet were wrong when you reached for the second one — you stayed too far; that is why the blade found you. You have always done that. Step in or step back. You have never understood there is no middle ground.

He did not want a judgment. He wanted the voice to stop. He wanted his hands to stop shaking and his mind to stop returning to the fight — every step, every choice that had not been his. He wanted to be a man walking a road, not a man running from one because his body had done something his mind could not undo.

He left the road. He took the tree line, finally, the way the voice had been telling him to take it for six days. He moved through the forest parallel to the trade route, fast and silent. His body handled the terrain without effort. It was his now, and under pressure he could not control it.

The story of what happened in the narrows would travel faster than he could. Stories always did. And somewhere behind the stories, people listened for exactly this kind of report.

The forest received him. He moved through it easily, and the ease was the problem.

He could only delay the discovery, and each delay shortened the distance between himself and whatever was coming. The world had a use for bodies like his, and it sent people to find them.
