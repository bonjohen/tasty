Chapter 25: Two Sets of Eyes

He woke from a dream that had belonged to someone else.

The details were precise and wrong. He was standing in a place he had never been — a high meadow with the tree line far below and the sky wide and clear and the wind carrying the smell of ice and stone. His body was larger than his body. His hands, when he looked at them, were broader, the knuckles thicker, the palms scarred from rope and blade-hilt in the way long days of trapping leave their marks. He was looking at the landscape the way a man looks at a problem he intends to solve — and the whole of his attention was organized around a single question: how do I get there from here?

He lay in the cold camp and waited for the presence to settle. The waiting had become a practice. Each morning the border between his thoughts and the other thoughts took longer to reestablish, and held less.

The voice had not spoken during the dream. But it had been present — as the quality of attention, as the certainty that permeated every detail. The voice was the dream.

Not peace. Proximity — a closeness to the presence inside him that he had stopped fighting and had not yet accepted. He did not know what to do with the space between those two states except to watch.

The voice was more continuous now. Not louder — more frequent, and more various. It reacted to the world with emotions that were not his. A well-built stone wall along the road produced a flush of approval that settled in him like warmth — someone else's standards, someone else's eye. A poorly maintained road produced irritation out of proportion to the inconvenience, a tightening in his jaw that had nothing to do with him and everything to do with a man who had never forgiven laziness in any form. A bird of prey circling above the road produced a warmth in his chest — admiration, focused and uncomplicated, the way Hakiia had always watched things that did what they were made to do.

The privacy of his own mind was no longer private.

When the voice directed him to take a higher path along a ridge, he took it. Hakiia corrected. Hurkuzak followed. It had survived death and consumption and arrived unchanged on the far side of both.

He did not say the name aloud. Not yet. The saying aloud would make it irreversible — public, even if the only audience was the trees and the road and the spring air. He was not ready for irreversible.

He thought about the dream's purpose — the fixed point it had been moving toward. The certainty of it was Hakiia's. The high meadow, the wide sky, the cold smell of stone. The sense of planning a particular journey. He turned it in his mind and something in the turning produced a physical sensation rather than a thought — a low cold, a stillness, the particular quality of confined air. He let that sensation sit without reaching for it. It was not ready to be named.

He passed through a camp midday — not a settlement but a temporary gathering of travelers sharing a fire. He bought bread from a woman who baked it in the coals. The bread was warm and tasted of grain and ash — the first food in weeks that tasted of anything at all. He ate slowly. The flavor surprised him, and the surprise itself was a recovery, small and specific, of something the cabin had taken.

He handled the interaction competently. The competence was not entirely his. He saw the tension in the woman's shoulders ease when he kept his distance. Her hand moved from the knife at her belt to the cloth over the bread. He chose the right words — measured, ordinary, the kind of words that have no hooks, that drop through memory before he had cleared the far edge of the camp.

He noticed himself doing this, and the noticing was the strangeness: his hands had already made the right choice before he finished deciding. His mouth had already shaped the right distance. He watched himself act and was not entirely sure he had told himself to.

He noticed, as he left the camp, that the woman watched him go with an expression he had seen before — the slightly unsettled look of someone who has spoken with a thing that passed for normal but did not quite fit the passing. She did not look afraid. She looked uncertain.

The afternoon was quiet. He walked through woodland that was coming fully into leaf, the canopy closing overhead, the light filtering green and gold. He stopped in a clearing where the sun warmed the ground, sat with his back against a tree. Sun on his face. Wind in the new leaves. The presence inside him, steady, permanent.

"Hakiia," he said.

He said the name once. Quietly, without inflection. He did not expect a response. He did not receive one in words. But something shifted. A focusing of the presence, as though the name had been a call that turned something's head. The presence did not speak. But it listened.

He sat with the name in the air, the presence in his mind, the spring around him — and the grief that was not only guilt anymore. It was no longer the sealed air of the cabin. It was open now, cold and moving, carrying the killing and the eating and the love and the damage together, not trying to separate them.

He was carrying his brother with open eyes. Love without clearing the debt. Love without making it smaller than it was.

He thought about the dream again. The thought circled closer this time and almost touched. The feeling was a seed.

He stood. He walked east. The name stayed in the air behind him and the presence walked with him. The spring did not care about either of them. It went on, and they went with it.
