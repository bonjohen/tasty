Chapter 14: What Repetition Builds

He ate again the next day. And the day after. And the day after that.

The first time had been desperation — a body at the end of its reserves, a decision that was not a decision but a collapse. The second time he was not collapsing. He was hungry but not dying. He did not wait. He went to the alcove and he cut and he cooked and he ate. The second time was a choice. The third time was easier. The fourth time was almost routine.

Not the act itself — the act was something his hands had already learned, and his mind arrived after them. By the fifth time, the argument was gone. The thought had worn smooth: the body needed food and the food was there.

He developed methods. He learned which cuts preserved best in the alcove cold — the larger muscle groups held longest; the organs needed to be consumed within a day or two of being taken. He learned to portion — small amounts, often — stretching the supply the way he had stretched the grain. He learned that cooking slowly over low heat produced something his stomach accepted more easily than anything seared fast over high flame.

He learned his brother's body the way Hakiia had learned the elk — by necessity, by repetition, by the knife. Where the muscle was dense and where it was thin. Where the bone required the saw and where the knife alone was enough.

He used the rendered fat from the jar to cook with. He discovered the iron pan held its heat more steadily than the open hearth. The candles he had hoarded could be melted and mixed with the fat to extend it. He discovered that bone could be boiled into a broth that sustained him on days when cutting felt like too much. It was warm. It was filling. It tasted fine.

Weeks passed. The marks on the wall multiplied until they covered the space above the platform and began to creep along the adjacent wall. He stopped counting days and started counting weeks. The weeks told him nothing about when the time would end, but they were a shape he could hold.

He knew which stone in the floor rocked when he stepped on it. He knew the cold came through the gap between door frame and wall. Cloth torn from the blankets muffled it but never sealed it. He knew the fire burned best with the wood stacked in a specific pattern — small pieces at the base, larger pieces above, angled so the air could move between them. He had always known this. He could not remember learning it.

He had a day when the wind stopped. It had blown so long it had become the cabin's background noise, the sound underneath all other sounds. When it stopped, the silence felt like a pressure change. He sat in the silence and listened to nothing. The nothing was louder than the wind had ever been. It was the sound of a room with one person in it, and whatever that person had been before the winter, he was not that thing now, and the silence had no name for what he was instead.

He had a night when he woke and forgot. For a fraction of a breath between sleep and waking, he did not know where he was. The cabin was a room. The cold was cold. Then memory returned, all of it, and he was sitting upright on the platform with his hands gripping the edge of the wood and his heart doing something fast and wrong. His breath came in short, quick pulls, each one deliberate. He recognized the rhythm. He could not have said from where.

He had a morning when he caught himself humming. He was working at the hearth — tending the fire, preparing the day's meal — and the sound came from his chest without his permission. A low drone that was not a tune but the shape of one. He heard himself and stopped. The silence afterward was worse than the humming.

He wished Hakiia would stop looking at him. The eyes were covered. The door was closed. He had arranged the furs so the face would not show when he opened the alcove. And still the looking continued. Not from the body — from somewhere else, from the corners of the room, from the dark spaces between the stones, from the place behind his own eyes where Hakiia had always lived as critic and corrector and judge.

Sometimes the looking was the body. He would open the alcove door and the furs would seem to have shifted — not much, just enough to suggest the face underneath was almost visible, the eyes beneath the fabric turned toward the door. He would fix the furs. He would close the door. He knew the explanations. They did not help.

The fire was good and the wind was quiet. The meal had been more substantial than usual — a cut from the thigh cooked slowly in rendered fat, unseasoned, eaten with a thoroughness that left the pan clean and his stomach full for the first time in days.

He sat beside the fire afterward with the pan in his lap. His belly was full. The fire was warm. The pan was clean.

Demon meat is very tasty.

He was laughing alone in a cabin in the mountains with the taste of his brother in his mouth, and he could not stop.

The laughing turned into something else. Not crying. A vibration in his chest and throat that meant he was still alive.

He used every part. Waste was the only sin left to him that he could avoid. If the body was going to be consumed, then the consuming would be complete. Bone for broth. Marrow for fat. Organ for density. Sinew and tendon boiled soft for sustenance on days when the cuts were gone and the between-times needed filling. He used what Hakiia had given him the way Hakiia had used the elk.

The contradiction did not resolve.

His body improved. The wolf bite healed in days rather than weeks, the flesh closing over itself, the new skin dense and unfamiliar. His ribs stopped hurting. His knee stopped grinding. His palm, which had scarred thick and tight, softened. The scar thinned. His grip strengthened until the hand worked better than it had before the cliff.

He was stronger than he should have been. The changes did not arrive all at once. One morning it was his arms — the wood pile that had needed two trips now needed one. Then his legs, carrying him through snow that had been impassable a week before. Then his hands, closing around the axe handle with a grip that felt borrowed, as though his hands had woken knowing something years of practice had never given them.

The cold that had been killing him no longer reached as deep. He noticed a bird at the tree line two hundred paces out. His hearing had sharpened. He went outside in cold that should have driven him back to the fire within minutes. He stayed for an hour. The cold was still there. It no longer reached him. He told himself it was the food and the rest. He did not think past that.

The weeks continued. The marks on the wall spread. The alcove emptied as the cabin filled — filled with warmth, with strength, with the slow accumulation of physical capacity that grew each day as the body in the other room diminished. Hakiia was disappearing from the alcove. Hakiia was not disappearing from Hurkuzak. Flesh to fuel to function. The person moving through the cabin was not the person who had entered it.

Winter went on. The days lengthened by increments too small to see but large enough to feel. He did not know if this meant spring was approaching. Spring felt like something from another life — a life where seasons changed and time moved forward and people left cabins and returned to a world that contained other people. That life existed somewhere. It did not exist here.

He settled into a terrible equilibrium. He had food. He had warmth. He had shelter. The grief had not diminished. It had settled into him like the cold — not sharp anymore, just part of the air he moved through. He grieved the way the cabin held heat: imperfectly, with losses at every edge, but enough.
