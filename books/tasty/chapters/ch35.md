Chapter 35: What the Net Holds

The compound looked like refuge and that was the first lie.

Hurkuzak saw it from the road in the late afternoon — stone walls, a timber gate standing open, the smoke of a cooking fire rising from within. A trading compound of the kind that sat along every major road in the eastern territories. He had passed similar compounds since the cabin, avoided some, used others without incident.

The air was wrong. Damp, holding scent low to the ground. The smoke did not rise cleanly — it flattened, spread, lingered. Sound carried poorly. The place absorbed it.

The voice assessed the compound before Hurkuzak had crossed the field between the road and the gate. Terrain, cover, exits. The compound had stone walls high enough to discourage climbing, a single gated entrance wide enough for a wagon, and interior buildings that blocked sight lines from the gate to the far wall. The voice filed the details and offered no objection.

He entered through the open gate into a visible courtyard. A well, a water trough, hitching posts, and the quiet evidence of recent occupation. Packs against a wall. A cart with its tongue resting on the ground. The cooking fire was in a pit near the center, tended by no one, burning with the low steady heat of a fire that had been maintained for hours.

The ground inside was firm. Drained. Controlled.

The men were in the buildings. He did not see them when he entered. He saw them later, after the gate closed, and the seeing was already too late.

The gate mechanism was quiet — the hinges oiled, the crossbar seated in iron brackets worn smooth from use, dropping into position with a single push. A low, heavy thud. The sound did not carry. It settled into the damp air and disappeared.

The options he thought he carried had been closed days ago, shaped by someone who understood how hunted men move and had designed the ground to funnel this particular hunted man to this particular place.

The voice screamed.

Hurkuzak turned toward the gate.

The first man was already moving.

He came from the building on the left — fast, professional, carrying a small stoppered vial in his right hand. A second man emerged from the building on the right. A third dropped from the wall itself, landing in a crouch near the well, blocking the courtyard's center.

The voice took over — the way a rider seizes the reins when the hand that holds them goes slack. Hurkuzak's body moved with Hakiia's precision, Hakiia's economy of violence.

He hit the first man before the vial could be opened. The strike was to the wrist — fast, accurate, the kind of targeted violence that disarms without killing because killing costs time he did not have. The vial flew from the man's hand and shattered against the courtyard stones. The liquid spread in a dark stain and the smell that rose from it was sharp and sweet and wrong: a sedation draught, brewed strong enough to bring down something harder to kill than a man.

He turned the second man's own weight toward the stone wall with a sound that was half impact and half breath leaving a body. The second vial broke in the man's pocket and the sedation soaked through his clothes and the man's legs buckled as his own weapon did to him what it had been meant to do to Hurkuzak.

Two down. The third was different. Patient, positioned, waiting with the stillness of someone who had been told to wait. He held his vial in his left hand and a short blade in his right. The blade was for cutting, for opening skin so the sedation could enter the blood directly.

The fight with the third man was longer. The man was trained — not at the level the voice brought to combat, but trained enough to survive the first exchange and the second and to keep the blade viable. Hurkuzak — Hakiia — drove him back across the courtyard with strikes that were efficient and frightening and that left bruises and cuts on the man's arms and torso without finishing him. Every time Hurkuzak committed to the killing stroke the blade came up, angled toward exposed skin, the vial ready. He could not afford the opening.

The crossbow bolt hit him from above.

A line of fire across his right shoulder — a placement shot, delivered from the wall. The damp air carried the twang poorly; the impact arrived before the sound.

The pain did not stop him. But it pulled his attention upward for a fraction of a second, and the fraction was enough.

The third man closed the distance. The blade opened a cut across Hurkuzak's forearm — shallow, precise — and the vial followed, the liquid poured into the wound, and what entered his blood was wrong.

The sedation hit him like a tide. Not a wall — a rising. His legs did not buckle at once. His vision held. The first dose was in his blood. The second was in the courtyard stones, wasted. The third was on the man he had driven into the wall. But the first was enough to begin it — a distance opening between his intention and his body's response.

He struck the third man. The blow landed slower than it should have. He struck again and the man went down. Hurkuzak turned toward the wall where the bolt had come from. The turning took too long. The draught was spreading through his blood with the patience of something brewed for exactly this.

A figure on the wall. A woman. The crossbow reloaded, aimed, lowered — not fired again, because the firing was no longer necessary. She watched from the wall, evaluating the progress of her work. Below her, Hurkuzak's body was no longer answering his mind.

Vark.

He knew her the way prey knows its predator — not by name but by the shape of the threat. The bolt in his shoulder. The blood she had drawn from him.

He went to his knees. The sedation had reached his legs. What waited at the end was not death — containment, transport, delivery. The chain was pulling him in.

The voice fought. Inside the fog closing around his mind, the voice raged with a fury that was entirely Hakiia's — the fury of a man who had never submitted to a force he did not choose. The rage was useless against the draught. The voice could not burn off what was already in the blood. The voice could not override the body's mechanical failure. But the voice could refuse to stop, and the refusal was a pressure against the darkness. Hakiia had never surrendered. Not even when the dying was his own choice.

Hands on him. The treated cord around his wrists — something passed from it into his skin and the strength in his arms left him until his grip was strong, then weak, then nothing. More cord around his ankles. The restraint was methodical — the work of people who had done this before, who knew the order of it: down first, bound while the draught held, then doubled before the body began to burn it away.

The damp air pressed close. The sky above contracted to a receding point of light. The woman on the wall was still watching. She had not descended. She had not needed to. Her trap was closing the way she had built it to close.

The voice spoke.

Not as instinct. As words. As Hakiia's — the clipped authority, the tone of a man addressing another man in a room they both occupied.

Hold on.

Two words.

Hold on.

Hurkuzak held. The darkness came in and the holding continued and the two words were the last thing he heard before the sedation won. Hakiia was still there. Still fighting. The trap had been built for a body. What it held was a body and a brother, and the brother was not going to stop.
