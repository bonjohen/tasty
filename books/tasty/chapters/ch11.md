# Chapter 11: The Cabin at the End

The march became a country he lived in. It had its own geography — the same white ground, the same gray sky, the same trees repeating themselves. It had its own time, measured in the distance between one collapse and the next, the interval narrowing each time.

He fell. The first time was a stumble that turned into a knee-drop that turned into a slow sideways descent into the snow, and the snow was soft against his face, and the softness was dangerous. He got up. He pulled the travois straight. He pulled.

He fell again. This time he went all the way down, flat on his stomach, and the drag poles slid from his hands and the travois behind him stopped and the silence that followed had the quality of an ending. He lay in the snow and breathed, and the breathing was the hardest work his body had done all day.

He got up. He pulled.

The terrain changed without improving. The valley floor had been rising for what felt like hours, a gradual climb that added resistance to every step and made the travois heavier with each degree of incline. The trees grew denser, blocking the wind and the light both, and Hurkuzak moved through them as though the forest were closing around him, the way a corridor narrows when the walls lean in.

His hands had stopped hurting. This was not recovery. The grooves in his palms were deep enough now that the poles sat in them like tracks, and his fingers had curled around the wood into a position that might have been grip or might have been the beginning of rigor, and the distinction between the two was smaller than it should have been.

He fell a third time. A fourth. Each fall was longer on the ground and shorter on his feet, and each time he went down he had to retrieve the travois and get the body back into position because the body was stiff now, the cold and the hours having locked it into something that could no longer be repositioned with care. Hurkuzak repositioned it with force, pulling limbs and turning the torso the way a man moves furniture.

The fifth time he fell, he did not immediately get up. He lay beside the travois and looked at Hakiia's face — sideways, from the snow, at an angle that made the face look younger, less severe — and thought: I could leave him here.

The thought was not a decision. It was a fact that arrived without permission. He would move faster alone. The travois was wreckage. The body was weight. Without it, the cabin might be close enough to reach.

He could leave Hakiia's body in the forest and walk to the cabin and survive.

The thought stayed for several breaths, and the reckoning was correct, and Hurkuzak lay in the snow beside his brother's body and did not move.

He could not leave Hakiia here.

He was not yet the person who left his brother in the snow. He was still the person who pulled. Perhaps tomorrow, if tomorrow came, he would set the body down and walk away, and the walking would be one more destruction added to the list of destructions this trip had produced.

But not yet.

He got up. He pulled.

He lost time. Not consciousness — he was aware of pulling, of the sound of the travois, of the cold — but the thread between moments went slack and the moments arrived isolated, unlinked. He was pulling. Then he was resting. Then he was pulling again and the resting was gone from his memory as though it had happened to someone else. The trees moved past him in the same repeating pattern and he began to suspect that the valley was a loop, that he had been through this stretch before, that the same rock and the same bent pine and the same drift of snow against the same fallen trunk had already been passed and were coming around again, and he did not have the capacity to determine which was true.

The light changed. He noticed it the way a man in dark water knows which way is up. The trees were thinning. The valley wall on his right was rising, steepening, turning from slope into cliff face. The terrain was changing in ways that suggested boundary, edge, arrival.

The smell of smoke came first.

He did not trust it. But the smell persisted. Not fire — not current fire — but the old, embedded smell of smoke that lives in wood and stone after years of burning. The smell of habitation.

Then the trees opened.

The cabin stood at the end of a small clearing where the valley wall rose into a granite face. It was small. Stone walls, low and thick, built by someone who understood what winter did to anything that was not as heavy as the landscape it sat in. A timber roof pitched steep enough to shed snow, though the snow had accumulated anyway and the roof sagged under its weight. A door, closed, the wood dark with age and weather. One window, shuttered, the shutters hanging slightly askew. The whole structure looked like something the mountain had grown rather than something a person had built — patient, and indifferent to whether anyone came to it or not.

He stood in the clearing. He stood in the cold.

He pulled the travois across the clearing. The snow was shallower here, in the lee of the rock face, and the travois moved more easily. He reached the door. He tried the latch. It moved, stiff with cold and disuse, and the door swung inward into a dark space that smelled of old smoke and cold stone and the particular emptiness of a place that had not been lived in for a long time.

The cabin was a single room. A stone hearth against the far wall, black with soot, the firebox empty. A raised sleeping platform along one side, bare wood, no bedding. A shelf built into the wall above the platform, holding nothing. A table, small and rough, with one leg shorter than the others. The floor was stone, uneven, with gaps between the slabs where dirt and old leaves had collected. The walls were thick enough to block the wind but not the cold, and the cold inside the cabin was different from outside — still, enclosed, the cold of a room that had given up on warmth.

No food. No fuel beyond what he might scavenge from the forest. No supplies. No sign of recent human presence — no ashes in the hearth, no marks on the door frame, no smell except the old ghost of smoke that lived in the stone itself. A second door at the back, smaller, lower, latched from inside. The walls were a foot thick at the base, tapering slightly, the stones fitted without mortar and held by their own weight and the weight of the years that had pressed them together.

The cabin was shelter. That was all. A room where the problem changed shape — from exposure to enclosure, from open hostility to the patient hostility of a space with nothing in it.

He stood in the doorway and looked at the room that would be everything now — hearth and bed and prison. The room looked back with the indifference of a space that had sheltered people before and would shelter people again.

He dragged Hakiia's body inside because leaving it in the snow was still unthinkable. The travois would not fit through the door, so he lifted the body — heavy, stiff, resisting with the dull resistance of dead weight — and carried it pressed against his chest, staggering, and lowered it onto the stone floor with a care his muscles could barely execute and his mind demanded.

Hakiia lay on the floor of the cabin. Hurkuzak stood over him and looked down at the face he had closed the eyes of and the hands he had positioned on the chest.

He should close the door. He should build a fire. He should think about what came next.

His legs folded. His knees hit the stone floor and the flare through his damaged knee was distant, happening to a body he had stopped living inside. He slumped forward, catching himself on his hands — the ruined hands that had gripped the drag poles and the knife and his brother's body — and they held him for a moment and then they did not hold him, and he went down.

He lay on the stone floor of the cabin beside his brother's body and the cold was on both of them equally and the dark was filling the room because the door was open and the light outside was failing and neither the cold nor the dark knew the difference between the man who was dead and the man beside him.

The last thing he heard was the wind in the trees outside and the silence inside.

Then nothing.
