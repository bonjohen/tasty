# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 2: What the Captain Keeps

Reviewed against `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`. The book's deliberate stylistic choices (weighted sentences, restrained horror, pre-industrial register, spare interiority) are not flagged. Only prose that fails by the rule's own criteria is listed.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "She hung it from the gambrel by its ankles, waited for the last of the blood to drain into the trough, and watched the body sway once and settle."
**Problem:** Three-verb compound list with the weakest verb ("watched") carrying the most resonant image.
**Why it weakens:** Equal weighting flattens what should land. "Watched" is a soft verb where the body's motion deserves the focus.
**Suggested revision:** Drop "watched" and let the body's motion be the sentence's own action: "...the last of the blood drained into the trough, and the body swayed once and settled."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "She scored along the inside of both legs, then down the midline from pelvis to sternum, working the blade shallow enough to preserve the fascia underneath."
**Problem:** Slightly wordy. "Underneath" is redundant — fascia is, by definition, beneath the skin she is scoring.
**Why it weakens:** A precise procedural sentence carries an unnecessary word.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "underneath."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "One careless nick through the underlayer and the hide would begin to spoil within a day, the leather gone soft and useless, and she had seen that mistake cost a hunter a season's wages."
**Problem:** Run-on built of three clauses joined by commas and "and." The "she had seen" tail is a separate thought that deflates the technical detail in front of it.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence loses its center. Technical danger and personal anecdote should be separate beats.
**Suggested revision:** Break into two: "One careless nick through the underlayer and the hide would begin to spoil within a day, the leather gone soft and useless. She had seen that mistake cost a hunter a season's wages."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "She peeled the skin in sections, pulling with her left hand and cutting with her right, and the hide separated with a wet, tearing sound."
**Problem:** "Wet, tearing sound" tells the reader what the sound is rather than letting the verbs render it. "With a... sound" is a soft construction.
**Why it weakens:** Naming a sound is weaker than letting the action carry it. The verbs already do the work.
**Suggested revision:** "She peeled the skin in sections, pulling with her left hand and cutting with her right. The hide came away wet."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "The workspace was a rented stone room behind a tanner's yard in Cassavon — cold floor, drain in the center, iron hooks in the beams above."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing opening. "The workspace was" is the weakest possible sentence frame — a label followed by "was."
**Why it weakens:** Linking-verb openings stall the paragraph. The room itself can do the work.
**Suggested revision:** "The room was rented from a tanner in Cassavon — stone floor, drain in the center, iron hooks in the beams."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "She had used it for the last four jobs and the tanner had stopped asking questions after the second payment."
**Problem:** Wordy two-clause sentence; the second clause is the interesting one and is buried.
**Why it weakens:** Monotone joining; the punchline is buried.
**Suggested revision:** "Four jobs in, the tanner had stopped asking questions."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "Vark paid in advance, cleaned thoroughly, and kept her work to the hours between midnight and dawn when the streets were empty."
**Problem:** "Cleaned thoroughly" is vague-adverbial in a sentence whose other clauses are concrete.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter later shows her scrubbing the floor; telling here is unearned.
**Suggested revision:** "Vark paid in advance, scrubbed the room down after, and worked between midnight and dawn."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "She had seen what the final buyers paid for the same liver after it passed through two more hands, and the number could make a person angry. Anger was not useful. She was the hands."
**Problem:** "The number could make a person angry" is generic and abstract — "could make a person" is hedged distance.
**Why it weakens:** Abstract framing of an emotion the next sentence dismisses anyway. The dismissal lands harder if the anger is concrete first.
**Suggested revision:** "She had seen what the final buyers paid for the same liver after it passed through two more hands. The number was obscene. Anger was not useful. She was the hands."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "The heart carried less value in isolation, but certain physicians in the southern cities prescribed it paired with the liver — something about how the two worked together when eaten."
**Problem:** "Something about how the two worked together when eaten" is vague filler. The sentence ends with hand-waving.
**Why it weakens:** A POV who knows her trade should not narrate her own ignorance with "something about." It also softens what should be matter-of-fact.
**Suggested revision:** "The heart carried less value in isolation, but certain physicians in the southern cities prescribed it paired with the liver. The pair commanded a better price than the sum of its parts."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "She knew the price was better when the pair arrived in the same box from the same corpse."
**Problem:** Repeats the previous sentence's content. Also "She knew" is throat-clearing.
**Why it weakens:** Redundancy and a hedging verb. (Folded into Issue 9's rewrite.)
**Suggested revision:** Delete the sentence as part of the Issue 9 rewrite.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "Demon hands carried a value she had always found absurd, but the market set the price and the price was the price."
**Problem:** "She had always found" is past-perfect throat-clearing. "Always" is a soft qualifier the rule flags.
**Why it weakens:** Hedged distance from a wry observation.
**Suggested revision:** "Demon hands carried a value she found absurd, but the market set the price and the price was the price."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "She cut the major muscle groups from the thighs and packed them in salted cloth. She worked the shoulders next, then stripped the long muscles along the spine and packed them in the same way."
**Problem:** Two sentences, both opening "She [verb]," both ending in packing. Monotone rhythm and "in the same way" is filler that points at its own repetition.
**Why it weakens:** Identical openings and a self-referential closer.
**Suggested revision:** "She cut the major muscle groups from the thighs and packed them in salted cloth. The shoulders next, then the long muscles along the spine."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "It still had a face. The face had been unremarkable alive and was more so now."
**Problem:** "Was more so now" is a soft, telling construction. "More so" is vague.
**Why it weakens:** A line that should be cold and exact instead reaches for "more so."
**Suggested revision:** "It still had a face. The face had been unremarkable alive. Death had not improved it."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "She had tracked him for a day and a half, taken him at dawn while he slept beside a dead fire, and the kill had been clean enough that the product was undamaged. Clean kill, clean product."
**Problem:** The third clause summarizes ("clean enough that the product was undamaged"), and that summary is then immediately repeated by the next sentence ("Clean kill, clean product"), making the long form redundant.
**Why it weakens:** The summary clause says what the next sentence already says better.
**Suggested revision:** "She had tracked him for a day and a half and taken him at dawn while he slept beside a dead fire. Clean kill, clean product."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "By the time she heard the knock she was nearly finished."
**Problem:** "Was nearly finished" is a soft frame and a throat-clearing transition.
**Why it weakens:** The knock is the event. The state of her work is filler.
**Suggested revision:** "The knock came as she was finishing."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "Koss stood outside with his hands deep in his coat and his breath coming fast. His eyes found the carcass on the gambrel. His eyes left the carcass."
**Problem:** "His eyes found... His eyes left" repeats the noun and uses the soft "found/left" pair.
**Why it weakens:** Repetition without rhythmic purpose; could be tighter.
**Suggested revision:** "His eyes found the carcass on the gambrel and slid off it."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "Koss moved through the room with the careful steps of a man who did not want to touch anything."
**Problem:** Telling. "The careful steps of a man who did not want to touch anything" is the explanation rather than the image.
**Why it weakens:** Tells the reader the inference rather than letting body language do it.
**Suggested revision:** "Koss moved through the room without touching anything."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "He inspected the lined boxes, lifting each lid and leaning in to assess what was inside without quite bringing his face close enough to smell."
**Problem:** Long, qualifier-heavy ("without quite bringing his face close enough to"). "Quite" is one of the rule's named empty qualifiers.
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness and a banned hedge.
**Suggested revision:** "He inspected the lined boxes, lifting each lid and leaning in — never close enough to smell."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "Vark doubted Koss could tell a good liver from a bad one without help, but he paid reliably, never went back on a price once set, and did not ask where the corpses came from. That last quality was worth more than the other two combined."
**Problem:** "That last quality was worth more than the other two combined" explains the joke the list already makes.
**Why it weakens:** Tagging the punch line.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second sentence.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "Koss almost nodded."
**Problem:** "Almost" is the kind of soft hedge the chapter does not need. Either he nodded or he didn't.
**Why it weakens:** Vague gesture.
**Suggested revision:** Cut, or replace with a definite gesture.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "He looked at her as though the next part required something from her."
**Problem:** Telling-through-narration. The narrator interprets his look for the reader.
**Why it weakens:** Removes work the dialogue can do.
**Suggested revision:** "He looked at her. The next part required something from her." Or cut entirely.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "The hide was heavy — denser than human skin, tougher at the edges where the demon's shoulders had thickened in the deep ranges. She folded it with one hand, gripping and turning the stiff leather without effort. Koss looked away."
**Problem:** "Without effort" tells the reader the point of the gesture (her unnatural strength) instead of trusting the image. Koss looking away does the work — the tag deflates it.
**Why it weakens:** Explained payoff.
**Suggested revision:** "She folded it with one hand, gripping and turning the stiff leather. Koss looked away."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "Koss did not argue the point. He gathered the boxes and the folded hides and let himself out into the cold."
**Problem:** Functional. "Let himself out into the cold" is a touch generic but acceptable.
**Why it weakens:** Marginal.
**Suggested revision:** Leave.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 24
**Quote:** "Vark barred the door behind him and stood alone in the stone room with what remained — waste and inventory. She cleaned her tools. She scrubbed the stone floor until the water ran clean into the drain. She took the carcass down from the gambrel and wrapped it in limed canvas."
**Problem:** Three consecutive "She [verb]" sentence openings.
**Why it weakens:** The rule names "too many similar sentence openings" directly.
**Suggested revision:** Vary one or two: "She cleaned her tools. The floor she scrubbed until the water ran clean into the drain. The carcass she took down from the gambrel and wrapped in limed canvas."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 25
**Quote:** "The tanner would find nothing in the morning but the smell of lye and salt — his own smells. He would not look further."
**Problem:** "He would not look further" is a flat declarative that explains the implication of "his own smells."
**Why it weakens:** Saying the obvious thing the previous beat already implies.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "He would not look further."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 26
**Quote:** "The coins sat heavy and warm in Vark's coat as she walked the dark streets toward her room."
**Problem:** "Heavy and warm" leans cliché-adjacent for coins-in-pocket. "Dark streets" is generic.
**Why it weakens:** Two soft modifiers in one sentence.
**Suggested revision:** Trim modifiers or trade for one specific image. Acceptable as-is.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 27
**Quote:** "The number Koss had named was wrong. She filed it away. She would ask around. Carefully."
**Problem:** "Filed it away" is a faint cliché. Otherwise the staccato is voice-deliberate.
**Why it weakens:** Mild cliché only.
**Suggested revision:** "She kept it." Optional.
**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 2 is a controlled, professionally voiced procedural scene that mostly earns its weight. The prose is concrete, the procedural detail exact, and the voice matches the book's literary-dark register. The weaknesses are small but consistent: a handful of telling-after-showing tags, several "She [verb]" openings clustered together, hedging qualifiers ("quite," "almost," "could make a person," "always," "something about"), and three or four sentences that explain the inference instead of letting the image do the work. Tightening these will sharpen what is already a strong chapter without changing its character.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. (Issue 22) "without effort" tells the reader the point of the one-handed fold instead of trusting Koss's look-away.
2. (Issue 5) "The workspace was a rented stone room" — throat-clearing label opening for an important paragraph.
3. (Issue 18) "without quite bringing his face close enough to smell" — wordy hedge using a banned qualifier.
4. (Issue 9) "something about how the two worked together when eaten" — vague filler from a POV who knows her trade.
5. (Issue 14) Three-clause kill summary that immediately gets repeated in "Clean kill, clean product."
6. (Issue 24) Three consecutive "She [verb]" openings in the closing procedural beat.
7. (Issue 17) "the careful steps of a man who did not want to touch anything" — explains the body language instead of staging it.
8. (Issue 8) "the number could make a person angry" — abstract emotional gesture; should be concrete.
9. (Issue 13) "was more so now" — vague comparative where the cold tone wants exactness.
10. (Issue 3) Three-clause run-on that fuses technical danger with personal anecdote.

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses dominate:

1. **Tagged inference.** Several sharp images get an explanatory tail attached ("without effort," "He would not look further," "That last quality was worth more than the other two combined," "the careful steps of a man who did not want to touch anything"). The chapter's voice is strong enough that the tags consistently weaken the lines they follow.
2. **Hedging qualifiers.** "Quite," "almost," "could make a person," "always," "something about," "more so" appear in a chapter whose register is otherwise hard and exact. Each instance is small; the pattern is the problem.
3. **Procedural rhythm slipping into "She [verb]" repetition.** The butchering and cleanup paragraphs cluster identical sentence openings. A single varied opening per cluster fixes the monotone.

A fourth, lower-frequency pattern: minor wordiness in transitional sentences ("By the time she heard the knock she was nearly finished," "She had used it for the last four jobs and the tanner...").

## Note on Cause

The chapter's weaknesses are isolated wording problems, not structural failure. The scene works; the voice is right; the procedural logic is exact. What needs work is line-level discipline — cutting hedges, dropping tags, varying repeated sentence openings.
