# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 3: The Distance Between Them

Reviewed against `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`. This book is literary dark fantasy with deliberately weighted, atmospheric prose; weighted polysyndeton, long sentences, and restrained narration are intentional and not flagged. Only genuinely weak prose by the rule's criteria appears below.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "He fumbled with the dogs' harnesses, pulling straps tight and then tighter when Hakiia glanced over, and the dogs stood patient under his attention."
**Problem:** Weak verb / hedging adverbial. "Glanced over" is generic; the sentence's emotional weight depends on what the look does to Hurkuzak, but "glanced over" is the most neutral phrasing available.
**Why it weakens:** It buries the chapter's central dynamic — Hurkuzak's hands answering to Hakiia's attention — under a movie-camera verb.
**Suggested revision:** "...pulling straps tight and then tighter when he felt Hakiia's eyes on him..."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "The terrain changed within the first hour. The forest thickened, then gave way to a long slope of exposed rock and ice where the snow had been scoured thin by wind and the ground underneath was slick and treacherous."
**Problem:** "Slick and treacherous" is a doublet where the second word is a cliche and adds no information. "Treacherous terrain" is genre-default fantasy.
**Why it weakens:** Generic adjective drains the precision the rest of the paragraph earns.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "and treacherous." "Slick" carries the weight.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "the runners scraped with a sound that set Hurkuzak's teeth on edge"
**Problem:** Cliche.
**Why it weakens:** "Set his teeth on edge" is dead language. The book otherwise renders sensation precisely; this sentence flinches into a phrase.
**Suggested revision:** "...the runners scraped on a note that crawled up the back of his jaw." Or simply: "...scraped a sound he felt in his teeth."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "He said nothing for a while. Then he said it."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing. The second sentence labels its own arrival.
**Why it weakens:** Tells the reader the line is coming instead of letting it land. The first sentence already does the work.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "Then he said it." Let the dialogue arrive on its own line.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "Hakiia turned his head. Not all the way. Just enough to show that the comment had registered, which was more than most of Hurkuzak's observations received."
**Problem:** Mild explanation of subtext. "...which was more than most of Hurkuzak's observations received" interprets the gesture instead of trusting the reader to.
**Why it weakens:** The first two beats already render the partial turn vividly. The relative clause is the narrator stepping in.
**Suggested revision:** Trim or relocate. "Just enough to show the comment had registered. It was more than most of his brother's observations received." (Slightly stronger as a separate sentence; or cut entirely.)
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "If she was hurting, it was because the dog was weak, or the terrain was unlucky, or the pad had been poorly wrapped in the first place."
**Problem:** Passive construction "had been poorly wrapped" where active voice carries the accusation Hurkuzak is internalizing.
**Why it weakens:** The whole sentence is about who gets blamed; passive voice softens the blow at exactly the wrong moment.
**Suggested revision:** "...or Hurkuzak had wrapped the pad poorly in the first place."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "felt the old confusion settle over him like weather"
**Problem:** Underwritten / abstract emotion-naming. "Old confusion" is vague; the simile leans on "weather" doing all the work.
**Why it weakens:** "Old confusion" is the writer telling us what to feel; it summarizes instead of rendering. The chapter has been doing better than this.
**Suggested revision:** "...and watched his brother's back and felt the weather of being near him settle on him again." Or cut "old confusion" and let "settle over him like weather" do the work in a more grounded sentence.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "the altitude had thinned the air enough that Hurkuzak's lungs worked harder than his legs"
**Problem:** Slightly clinical phrasing — "the altitude had thinned the air" reads like exposition.
**Why it weakens:** Pre-industrial register; a man on a sled trail wouldn't think in "altitude." Mild voice slip.
**Suggested revision:** "...the air had grown thin enough that..." Drops the modern noun.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "Hakiia ate without pleasure, the way a man oils a blade because neglect is stupid, not because the blade deserves care."
**Problem:** Slight overwriting — the simile is good but the second clause explains it.
**Why it weakens:** "Not because the blade deserves care" closes a door the reader was happy to leave open. The first clause is enough.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia ate without pleasure, the way a man oils a blade because neglect is stupid." Trust the reader.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak said nothing. He felt something tighten in his chest at the word and did not look up from the fire."
**Problem:** "Felt something tighten in his chest" — vague body-cue cliche; "explaining emotion instead of letting the scene carry it."
**Why it weakens:** This is one of the chapter's few moments of genre-default emotion-writing. Two sentences earlier, Hakiia has invoked the draught and "becoming a burden" — the moment hits hard without underlining.
**Suggested revision:** "Hurkuzak said nothing. He did not look up from the fire." Cut the body-cue entirely.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "He paused. The fire cracked."
**Problem:** Generic atmospheric beat. "The fire cracked" is a stock dialogue spacer.
**Why it weakens:** It's a place-holder action that doesn't carry weight; the chapter has earned more specific filmic beats elsewhere.
**Suggested revision:** Either cut, or replace with something Hurkuzak observes about Hakiia in that pause. "He paused. Hakiia did not move."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "Then Hakiia looked away. He looked at the fire, and whatever he decided stayed inside him, and what he said was:"
**Problem:** Wordy / awkward construction. "What he said was:" is an unusually flat dialogue tag for this prose.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence is long and ends on a colon-tag the rest of the chapter doesn't use. It throat-clears the line that follows.
**Suggested revision:** "Then Hakiia looked away. He looked at the fire, and whatever he decided stayed inside him." (Then the dialogue lands without tag.)
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "The cabin. Still distant — a day's travel, maybe more if the snow deepened — but real. A destination that until now had been only a word Hakiia used to justify another hour of walking."
**Problem:** "A destination that until now had been only a word..." is the narrator stepping in to interpret. Mild telling.
**Why it weakens:** The reveal already does the work — the cabin appearing is the meaning. The interpretive sentence dilutes it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second sentence, or compress to: "Until now it had been only a word Hakiia used to justify another hour of walking."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "But tonight there was something underneath the certainty, a quality Hurkuzak could not name. Not rest. Not the easy blankness of a day completed. Something that did not match the day they had just had."
**Problem:** "A quality Hurkuzak could not name" is filler — the next two negations already do the un-naming work. Mild redundancy.
**Why it weakens:** The construction repeats itself. The "could not name" line is a label; the "Not rest. Not the easy blankness..." sentences are the actual rendering.
**Suggested revision:** "But tonight there was something underneath the certainty. Not rest. Not the easy blankness of a day completed. Something that did not match the day they had just had."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "He looked, in sleep, like someone who might have been easy to love if the world had not made him the way it did."
**Problem:** Slightly overwritten, slightly tells the emotion. "Easy to love" + "if the world had not made him the way it did" is two notions stacked, and the second is the kind of summary the book usually avoids.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter's whole work is showing this; the line then states it. Borderline — the line is beautiful — but it tilts toward summary.
**Suggested revision:** Keep, or trim: "He looked, in sleep, like someone who might have been easy to love." Cut the conditional and let the chapter's accumulated weight provide the rest.
**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 3 is mostly very strong. The McCarthy register is sustained, the dialogue is loaded, the dog-and-route conflict carries the brothers' larger argument with admirable economy. The weaknesses are local and isolated: a handful of clichés ("set his teeth on edge," "treacherous," "felt something tighten in his chest"), two or three throat-clearing sentences ("Then he said it," "what he said was:"), and a few interpretive intrusions where the narrator names an emotion the scene was already rendering. The chapter does not have structural weakness, monotone rhythm, or vague POV. It has small, fixable line-level slips.

## Top 10 Problems by Impact

1. **"set Hurkuzak's teeth on edge"** — cliche in a book that otherwise renders sensation precisely (High)
2. **"felt something tighten in his chest"** — generic body-cue at one of the chapter's emotionally heaviest moments (High)
3. **"Then he said it."** — throat-clearing sentence that telegraphs the dialogue (Medium)
4. **"what he said was:" + the long lead-in** — flat dialogue construction inconsistent with the rest of the prose (Medium)
5. **"slick and treacherous"** — dead doublet adjective (Medium)
6. **"old confusion"** + "like weather"** — abstract emotion-naming (Medium)
7. **"the pad had been poorly wrapped in the first place"** — passive at the moment of self-blame (Medium)
8. **"the altitude had thinned the air"** — modern noun in a pre-industrial register (Medium)
9. **"a quality Hurkuzak could not name. Not rest. Not the easy blankness..."** — labeling redundancy before the rendering (Medium)
10. **"glanced over"** — weak verb at a load-bearing observation point (Medium)

## Pattern Summary

The recurring weakness is **interpretive overlay** — moments where the narrator names what the scene is already showing, usually in a single trailing clause or short follow-up sentence. The chapter does not have a "show don't tell" problem at the scene level; it has it at the sentence level. A second smaller pattern is **stock body-cues and stock atmosphere** ("teeth on edge," "fire cracked," "tighten in chest") slipped into a chapter whose surrounding prose otherwise refuses such phrasing. A third is **throat-clearing dialogue lead-ins** ("Then he said it," "what he said was:") in what is otherwise a tightly handled dialogue scene. None of these are voice failures — they are isolated lapses inside an otherwise disciplined register.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **High priority (do first):** Replace "set Hurkuzak's teeth on edge" with a specific bodily sensation. Cut "felt something tighten in his chest" outright and let the silence and the firelight carry the moment.
2. **Medium priority (do next):** Cut "Then he said it." Cut "and treacherous." Replace passive "had been poorly wrapped" with active. Replace "altitude had thinned the air" with "the air had grown thin." Trim "old confusion" and let the simile stand. Cut "what he said was:" lead-in. Cut "a quality Hurkuzak could not name" and let the negations render directly. Sharpen "glanced over" to something closer to the felt experience.
3. **Low priority (optional polish):** Trim "which was more than most of Hurkuzak's observations received." Trim "not because the blade deserves care." Replace "The fire cracked" with a more specific beat or cut. Compress the cabin paragraph. Optionally trim "if the world had not made him the way it did."

The chapter does not need restructuring. It needs surgical line edits at roughly fifteen points.
