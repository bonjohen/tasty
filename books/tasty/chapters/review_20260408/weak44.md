# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 44: What the Ashes Remember

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1 — Redundant phrasing / wordiness
**Quote:** "His body demanded it — limbs heavy past their reserves, done with the business of carrying him."
**Problem:** "done with the business of carrying him" is filler dressed as voice; "the business of" is throat-clearing.
**Revision:** "His body demanded it — limbs heavy past their reserves, done carrying him."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 2 — Telling instead of showing / interpretive narration
**Quote:** "The prince had added page numbers in his own hand, small numbers in the corners, marking the order he believed was correct. His annotations crowded the margins like commentary on a sacred text — the scholar's utility, not the believer's reverence."
**Problem:** The final clause explains the image the simile already carried. The explanation flattens the moment.
**Revision:** Cut "— the scholar's utility, not the believer's reverence."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis
The chapter is a quiet, contemplative info-dump done with literary control. The voice is steady and the pre-industrial register holds. Its main weakness is a recurring habit of stating an idea, then restating it in a softer summary clause — the prose occasionally explains its own metaphors and triples its triplets. Trimming those tail clauses sharpens the chapter without altering its rhythm or argument.

## Pattern Summary
Recurring weakness: the tail-clause restatement. A strong image or claim is followed by a softer paraphrase that explains what the first clause already accomplished. Secondary pattern: triplets where the third element repeats rather than escalates.

## Revision Plan
1. Cut tail-clause restatements (Issues 2, 5, 6, 7, 11, 15).
2. Tighten the triplet in Issue 12.
3. Remove the throat-clearing transition in Issue 10.
4. Trim "the business of" filler in Issue 1.
5. Leave all earned literary closers and parallel structures intact.
