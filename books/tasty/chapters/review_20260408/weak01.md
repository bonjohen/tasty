# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 1: The Older Brother's Country

Reviewed: 2026-04-08
Rule: `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`
Voice notes: Literary dark fantasy, McCarthy/Morrison register. Weighted sentences and dense imagery are deliberate; only flags below are genuinely weak by the rule's criteria.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak tried to remember where his hand had been. It had been wrong, apparently. Where it was now was also wrong."
**Problem:** Filler word ("apparently") softens what should be a hard observation. The throat-clearing qualifier blunts the rhythm.
**Why it weakens:** "Apparently" is a hedge. The whole point of the moment is that Hurkuzak cannot win — the qualifier dilutes that with vague distance.
**Suggested revision:** Drop "apparently." "It had been wrong. Where it was now was also wrong."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "The correction had taken less than a minute. The feeling of having failed a test he had not known he was taking would last the rest of the day."
**Problem:** Telling instead of showing. The second sentence explains the emotion the scene has already rendered.
**Why it weakens:** The reader has just watched the failure. Spelling out its duration flattens the moment into commentary.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second sentence, or trim to a single clause attached to the first.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "They walked for another hour. The terrain climbed. The dogs worked harder and Hurkuzak worked harder and Hakiia simply walked, his pace unchanged, his breathing even."
**Problem:** Wordy ending with redundant clauses ("his pace unchanged, his breathing even") that restate "simply walked."
**Why it weakens:** The repetition explains the contrast the verb already carries.
**Suggested revision:** "The dogs worked harder and Hurkuzak worked harder and Hakiia simply walked."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak's face went numb until his cheeks felt like something he was wearing rather than something he was."
**Problem:** None — strong, kept as control sample.
**Priority:** N/A

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "Hakiia was watching the tree line a hundred paces uphill. His head was tilted in the way that meant he was listening with more than his ears — a stillness that tightened the air around him."
**Problem:** Two passive/stative constructions in a row ("was watching," "was tilted"). Weak verbs where active would tighten.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter elsewhere uses sharp active verbs; this paragraph drops into passive description at exactly the moment the prose should sharpen.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia watched the tree line a hundred paces uphill. His head tilted in the way that meant he was listening with more than his ears."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "It was still bedded when he first caught sight of it through a gap in the scrub, a mound of gray-brown against the white, and its head was up and its ears were working."
**Problem:** Run-on with weak "was" verbs and a stacked clause that loses the visual focus.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence does too much work. The image of the elk should land hard; instead it dribbles out across three "was" clauses.
**Suggested revision:** "It was still bedded when he caught sight of it through a gap in the scrub — a mound of gray-brown against the white, head up, ears working."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "He drew. The string pressed into his fingers and the bow bent and the arrow waited and the elk turned its head."
**Problem:** None — deliberate polysyndeton, strong rhythm. Kept as control.
**Priority:** N/A

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "The elk stood. It was fast, bigger standing than it had been lying down, and it was going to run and he was going to miss and he released."
**Problem:** "It was fast" is vague telling, and the chained "was going to" constructions are weak verbs in a moment that demands urgency.
**Why it weakens:** The paragraph's job is to compress panic into a heartbeat. "It was fast" is editorial labeling; "was going to run" is anticipated rather than felt.
**Suggested revision:** "The elk stood — bigger upright than it had been lying down — and it was going to run and he was going to miss and he released." (Drop "It was fast.")
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "Hakiia came from the right side of the tree line like something that had always been there and had simply decided to move."
**Problem:** None — strong characterizing simile. Control.
**Priority:** N/A

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "He covered the ground without visible effort, without noise, without the gracelessness that physical exertion cost ordinary bodies."
**Problem:** The third "without" clause overexplains. "Gracelessness that physical exertion cost ordinary bodies" is abstract where the prior two clauses were concrete.
**Why it weakens:** Two strong concrete negations are followed by an abstract one, breaking the rhythm and softening the image.
**Suggested revision:** "He covered the ground without visible effort, without noise, without the strain ordinary bodies pay for speed."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "His hands kept working while he spoke, separating and sorting with the efficiency of long practice."
**Problem:** "With the efficiency of long practice" is a tell-don't-show abstract phrase.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter has already shown the hands working with precision. This adds editorial label.
**Suggested revision:** "His hands kept working while he spoke, separating and sorting without looking."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "It was warm on one side, and frozen on the other. It was heavier than it should have been, as though organs carried a different weight than the bodies that housed them."
**Problem:** Two consecutive "It was" sentence openings; monotone rhythm.
**Why it weakens:** Same opener twice in a row drops a strong observation into flat reporting.
**Suggested revision:** "Warm on one side, frozen on the other. It was heavier than it should have been, as though organs carried a different weight than the bodies that housed them."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "He carried the haunches himself, not because Hurkuzak could not lift them, but because he would not trust the placement to someone who held a hatchet like it frightened him."
**Problem:** Wordy double-negative construction ("not because... but because") that restates the morning's correction.
**Why it weakens:** The reader already knows about the hatchet grip. Calling it back here works, but the "not because... but because" frame is bulky.
**Suggested revision:** "He carried the haunches himself. Not because Hurkuzak could not lift them, but because he would not trust their placement to a man who held a hatchet like it frightened him."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "Hakiia built the fire and began cutting and stacking blocks of hacked snow."
**Problem:** "Began cutting" is a weak verb construction. "Hacked snow" is also slightly redundant — the cutting is the hacking.
**Why it weakens:** "Began to" verbs delay action. The whole point of Hakiia is direct competence.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia built the fire and cut blocks of snow into a low wall."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak drove the stakes into the frozen ground, pounding them in with a heavy rock where the frozen soil resisted."
**Problem:** "Frozen" repeated within one sentence; "pounding them in" is redundant with "drove."
**Why it weakens:** Same word twice in one sentence is a pure tightening loss.
**Suggested revision:** "Hurkuzak drove the stakes into the ground, hammering them with a rock where the frozen soil resisted."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "The smell was rich and dark and ancient. Hurkuzak's stomach pulled tight."
**Problem:** None — strong. Control.
**Priority:** N/A

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "He sat. Hakiia handed him a piece on the point of his knife and Hurkuzak took it and ate it and it was good. Hakiia's cooking was always good. That never helped."
**Problem:** "It was good. Hakiia's cooking was always good." Repetition of "good" within consecutive sentences, and "was good" is the weakest possible verb choice.
**Why it weakens:** The moment wants a precise sensory note, not the word "good" twice. The repetition reads like first-draft placeholder.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia handed him a piece on the point of his knife and Hurkuzak took it and ate it. Hakiia's cooking was always good. That never helped."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "In the silence Hakiia's face softened, or the firelight made it seem so, and for a few minutes there was nothing between them but warmth and meat and the sound of the wood breaking down into heat."
**Problem:** None — strong, retained. Control.
**Priority:** N/A

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "He looked at the fire and felt the familiar collapse — the moment where the warmth between them turned into something instructional, and the instruction carried inside it the unshakable belief that Hurkuzak was not enough. Not strong enough. Not hard enough. Not willing enough to do what the world required."
**Problem:** "Felt the familiar collapse" is telling — naming the emotion before rendering it. The fragments after work; the lead-in is editorial.
**Why it weakens:** "Familiar collapse" labels what the next sentences should embody. The fragments alone do the work better.
**Suggested revision:** "He looked at the fire. The warmth between them had turned instructional again, and the instruction carried inside it the unshakable belief that Hurkuzak was not enough. Not strong enough. Not hard enough. Not willing enough to do what the world required."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "And the worst of it — the part he could never say and could never stop knowing — was that Hakiia was not wrong. Hurkuzak was softer. Slower. Less willing to do what cold and hunger and violence demanded. He knew this about himself the way he knew his own hands. Beneath the resentment and the envy and the fear, there was something worse: the knowledge that without Hakiia the world would be a place he did not know how to survive."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing opener ("And the worst of it") plus the parenthetical hedge plus "there was something worse" — three layers of editorial framing on one insight.
**Why it weakens:** The insight is strong enough to land without three signposts pointing at it. The dashed parenthetical and the second "worse" are filler scaffolding.
**Suggested revision:** "And Hakiia was not wrong. Hurkuzak was softer. Slower. Less willing to do what cold and hunger and violence demanded. He knew it the way he knew his own hands. Beneath the resentment and the envy and the fear lay the knowledge that without Hakiia the world would be a place he did not know how to survive."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "They slept with an oil lamp on, all wrapped in furs, and close enough that Hurkuzak could hear his brother's breathing."
**Problem:** "All wrapped in furs" is a vague aside that breaks the sentence's spine. The "all" is filler.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence wants to land on the breathing. The middle clause floats.
**Suggested revision:** "They slept wrapped in furs with an oil lamp burning, close enough that Hurkuzak could hear his brother's breathing."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "Above him the sky was enormous and black and the stars were scattered across it like spilled salt on a dark hide."
**Problem:** "The stars were scattered" — passive. Weakens an otherwise strong image.
**Why it weakens:** The simile is good; the passive construction blunts it.
**Suggested revision:** "Above him the sky was enormous and black, the stars scattered across it like spilled salt on a dark hide."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "He thought about the elk. The dark eye. The held breath. He thought about Hakiia's knife, the speed and the ease and the absolute absence of hesitation. He thought about the liver cooking on the stone and the warmth of it in his mouth."
**Problem:** None — controlled anaphora, deliberate. Control.
**Priority:** N/A

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 1 is strong, deliberate, and largely free of cosmetic weak-writing problems. The McCarthy/Morrison register is intact and most "weighted" sentences earn their weight. The recurring weakness is a mild over-reliance on labeling emotions or contrasts before letting the prose carry them ("the familiar collapse," "the worst of it," "with the efficiency of long practice"), and a small cluster of "was" verbs that flatten moments where active verbs would sharpen the image (the elk sighting, the stars). Word repetition ("good" / "good," "frozen" / "frozen") is the only outright first-draft slippage.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. (Issue 19) "Felt the familiar collapse" labels the emotion the scene was about to render.
2. (Issue 20) "And the worst of it... there was something worse" stacks three editorial framings on one insight.
3. (Issue 17) "It was good. Hakiia's cooking was always good." Repeated "good" weakens a key sensory beat.
4. (Issue 6) The elk first-sighting sentence loses focus to three "was" clauses.
5. (Issue 8) "It was fast" and "was going to" weaken the kill-shot moment.
6. (Issue 5) Two stative verbs back-to-back blunt the moment Hakiia senses the elk.
7. (Issue 22) Passive "stars were scattered" weakens a strong sky image.
8. (Issue 14) "Began cutting and stacking blocks of hacked snow" — weak-verb construction, redundant noun.
9. (Issue 15) "Frozen" repeated within one sentence.
10. (Issue 11) "With the efficiency of long practice" is editorial label.

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring habits in this chapter:

1. **Pre-naming emotion before rendering it.** Phrases like "the familiar collapse," "the feeling of having failed a test," "the worst of it" announce a feeling that the scene is already producing. The fragments and images that follow these labels are usually strong enough on their own.
2. **"Was"-verb softening at high-tension beats.** The chapter is mostly active, but key moments (the elk sighting, the kill-shot, the night sky) drop into "was" constructions that flatten precisely where the prose should harden.
3. **Local word repetition.** "Good"/"good," "frozen"/"frozen," paired "It was" openers — the only true first-draft residue in an otherwise polished chapter.
