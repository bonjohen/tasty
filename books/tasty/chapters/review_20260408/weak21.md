# Weak Writing Review — Chapter 21: What the Body Knows

Reviewer pass: weak-writing only. Voice/genre/horror restraint preserved per `books/tasty/CLAUDE.md`. Issues below isolate prose-level weakness, not stylistic choices.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "He unwrapped the cloth from his thigh and the wound was a raised line of new skin, pink and tight, where yesterday there had been a hole with a crossbow bolt in it."
**Problem:** Wordy, with a clumsy tail clause ("where yesterday there had been a hole with a crossbow bolt in it") that explains what the previous sentence and the next one already make clear.
**Why it weakens:** Saying obvious things the reader already understands; the prior sentence ("The bolt wound had closed by morning") has already established the contrast.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the trailing clause: "He unwrapped the cloth from his thigh. The wound was a raised line of new skin, pink and tight."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "He had pulled the bolt out himself — an act that should have produced a scream and instead produced a grunt and a quantity of blood that stopped flowing before he finished binding it."
**Problem:** Run-on; the sentence buries its strongest beat (blood that stopped on its own) under wordy, abstract phrasing ("an act that should have produced," "a quantity of blood").
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness, weak nominal phrasing, monotone rhythm.
**Suggested revision:** "He had pulled the bolt out himself. The act should have torn a scream from him. It had drawn a grunt and a brief flow of blood that stopped before he finished binding the wound."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "Now the blood was dried and the binding was unnecessary and the skin underneath was whole."
**Problem:** Triple "and" parallel reads as filler; two of the three clauses restate what we already know.
**Why it weakens:** Repetition; saying it more than once.
**Suggested revision:** "Now the blood was dried and the skin beneath the binding was whole."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "He needed supplies. He needed to move. The bolt had been aimed with professional precision. He needed to know whether the shooter was still following."
**Problem:** Three-out-of-four sentence openings repeat "He needed." Monotone rhythm and similar openings.
**Why it weakens:** Identical sentence openings flatten urgency; one of them is also passive ("had been aimed").
**Suggested revision:** Vary the openings and use active voice: "He needed supplies. He needed to move. The bolt had flown with professional aim. Whoever loosed it might still be tracking him."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "He was still following. Hurkuzak could feel it — not through hearing or sight, but a pressure at his back."
**Problem:** "Could feel" is a weak verb construction; the editorial "not through hearing or sight" is throat-clearing before the real image.
**Why it weakens:** Weak verb plus filler that delays the concrete image.
**Suggested revision:** "He was still following. Hurkuzak felt him as a pressure at his back."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "He moved through the crowd and tried to be small. The body wanted to scan, assess, position for advantage. Holding it back was like holding his breath."
**Problem:** "Tried to be small" is vague; the simile "like holding his breath" is inert and tells the strain rather than rendering it.
**Why it weakens:** Vague language; telling instead of showing.
**Suggested revision:** "He moved through the crowd with his shoulders pulled in. The body wanted to scan, assess, position for advantage. Refusing it took the same effort as not breathing."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "He bought food. He ate standing up beside a water trough, chewing the dried meat that tasted of salt and nothing else, watching the market with the sharp gaze he could not shut off."
**Problem:** "The sharp gaze he could not shut off" tells the reader what the next sentence then shows; it preempts the image.
**Why it weakens:** Telling, then showing the same point — redundancy.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the tell: "He ate standing up beside a water trough, chewing the dried meat that tasted of salt and nothing else, watching the market."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "His eyes kept pulling toward the wrong places: a man across the square whose coat hung wrong on one side; two figures by the farrier who were not watching a horse; a woman who had looked at him once and then very deliberately not again."
**Problem:** "Very deliberately" — empty intensifier on an already strong adverb.
**Why it weakens:** Filler ("very") that the rule explicitly flags.
**Suggested revision:** Drop "very": "a woman who had looked at him once and then deliberately not again."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "He told himself to stop. His eyes returned to each of the three without asking permission."
**Problem:** Two short sentences where one with stronger contrast would land harder; the second sentence is fine on its own — the first is throat-clearing.
**Why it weakens:** First sentence is reflexive filler before the real beat.
**Suggested revision:** "He told himself to stop looking. His eyes returned to each of the three without asking permission." (kept, but tightened — see below for harder cut option)
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "He was at a dry goods vendor, the dried meat in one hand and coins in the other, when he heard the voices rise at the neighboring stall."
**Problem:** "He was at" is throat-clearing; "heard the voices rise" is filtered through perception verbs that distance the reader.
**Why it weakens:** Weak opening verb plus a perception filter.
**Suggested revision:** "At a dry goods vendor, dried meat in one hand and coins in the other, he caught voices rising at the neighboring stall." Or stronger: "At the dry goods vendor, coins in one hand and meat in the other, voices rose at the neighboring stall."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak looked away. The dispute was not his concern. His concern was the transaction that would allow him to leave this settlement and continue moving east."
**Problem:** Word repetition ("concern" / "concern") used as a transition device, plus a wordy second sentence that explains motivation we already understand.
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness and unnecessary explanation.
**Suggested revision:** "Hurkuzak looked away. The dispute was not his. His business was the transaction and the eastern road."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak did not move. He stood at the neighboring stall with the meat in his hand. Moving had cost him twice already."
**Problem:** Slight redundancy — "did not move" then "stood" — that flattens what should be a held-still beat.
**Why it weakens:** Saying the same thing twice without gain.
**Suggested revision:** "Hurkuzak did not move. The meat was still in his hand. Moving had cost him twice already."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "Two more men materialized from the crowd. They were with the large man. The three of them surrounded the smaller man with practiced coordination — enforcers, collectors."
**Problem:** "They were with the large man" is a flat declarative explainer; the next sentence already makes the point. "Materialized" is a mild cliche for thugs appearing.
**Why it weakens:** Unnecessary explanation; one cliche verb.
**Suggested revision:** "Two more men stepped from the crowd. The three surrounded the smaller man with practiced coordination — enforcers, collectors."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "He could feel it happening before he understood that it was."
**Problem:** Abstract pronoun ("it ... it") and a weak perception verb construction.
**Why it weakens:** Vague; the reader has to back-fill what "it" refers to before the next sentence concretizes it.
**Suggested revision:** "He felt it happening before he understood what it was." (Minor tightening; the abstraction is partly intentional, so leave the structure but kill the modal "could.")
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "He tried to stop it. He tried, and met something that did not yield. Whatever it was — and he could not have said, in that moment, whether the refusal belonged to the voice or to his own body or to some composite thing that had no name yet — it had already decided."
**Problem:** Long parenthetical em-dash interruption that explains the ambiguity instead of rendering it. Sentence is overwritten and strains for effect; the prior two short sentences already created the beat.
**Why it weakens:** Overwritten; explains emotion rather than letting wording carry it; em-dash overuse.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the parenthetical and let the ambiguity sit: "He tried to stop it. He tried, and met something that did not yield. Whether the refusal came from the voice or his own body or some composite that had no name, it had already decided."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "The first enforcer saw him coming and did not process what he saw quickly enough."
**Problem:** "Process" is modern/clinical and weak as a verb. "Did not process ... quickly enough" is wordy.
**Why it weakens:** Weak verb; tonal drift toward modern register.
**Suggested revision:** "The first enforcer saw him coming and could not move in time."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "The second enforcer turned and swung and the swing was solid — the kind of swing that had always been enough — and it missed because Hurkuzak was not where the swing expected him to be."
**Problem:** Run-on; "swing" appears four times in one sentence; the closing clause "where the swing expected him to be" is awkward (a swing cannot expect).
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness, repetition, mild personification confusion.
**Suggested revision:** "The second enforcer turned and swung. The blow was solid — the kind that had always been enough — and it missed. Hurkuzak had moved inside the arc, inside the range where the fist had power."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "His elbow found the man's jaw. The connection was solid. The man dropped — instant, total, like a rope cut."
**Problem:** "The connection was solid" is a flat explainer between two strong concrete beats; "solid" was already used four lines earlier on the swing.
**Why it weakens:** Word repetition across close passages; flat middle sentence dilutes the punch.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the middle line: "His elbow found the man's jaw. The man dropped — instant, total, like a rope cut."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "The man dropped with the totality of a felled tree."
**Problem:** "Totality of a felled tree" strains for effect and is abstract where a verb-driven image would land harder. Also "felled tree" is close to cliche.
**Why it weakens:** Overwritten; abstract noun where a verb belongs.
**Suggested revision:** "The man dropped like a felled tree." Or stronger: "The man went down all at once, the way a tree goes down."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak stood among three fallen men and the voice was quiet. Just quiet. The outer silence and the inner silence arrived at the same moment. He did not know which was worse."
**Problem:** "Just quiet" is a fragment that adds nothing the previous clause didn't already say; the "outer silence / inner silence" framing is tidy and a little explanatory.
**Why it weakens:** Repetition for rhythm rather than meaning; mild explaining.
**Suggested revision:** Either cut "Just quiet." or replace it with something concrete: "Hurkuzak stood among three fallen men and the voice was quiet. The silence outside and the silence inside arrived at the same moment. He did not know which was worse."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "He looked at his hands. They were steady. After the wolves, they had shaken. Now — nothing."
**Problem:** Mostly clean, but "Now — nothing" is throat-clearing punctuation that the previous "They were steady" already conveys.
**Why it weakens:** Saying it twice.
**Suggested revision:** "He looked at his hands. They were steady. After the wolves, they had shaken." (cut the dash fragment) — Low because the rhythm has some value.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "Every face held the same expression. The faces were not grateful. They were afraid."
**Problem:** "Every face held the same expression" is a setup line the next two sentences make redundant; "faces" appears three times across two adjacent paragraphs (this one and the next: "He saw himself in their faces").
**Why it weakens:** Saying obvious things; word repetition.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the setup: "The faces were not grateful. They were afraid."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "He saw himself in their faces. They did not see a traveler who had intervened in a beating. They saw something that had emerged from the shape of a person and done things that persons do not do."
**Problem:** The line is good, but "things that persons do not do" is slightly soft / abstract for the chapter's strongest moment.
**Why it weakens:** Vague language at a beat that needs precision.
**Suggested revision:** "They saw something that had emerged from the shape of a person and done what persons cannot do." (Tighter; sharper.) Optional — Low priority.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 24
**Quote:** "He did not run — running would draw more attention. He walked fast, through the market, past the garrison, toward the eastern road."
**Problem:** "Running would draw more attention" explains the choice; the reader can infer it from context.
**Why it weakens:** Unnecessary explanation.
**Suggested revision:** "He did not run. He walked fast, through the market, past the garrison, toward the eastern road."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 25
**Quote:** "The eyes would follow him as stories. The stories would travel faster than he could — a man who moved like something inhuman, healed like something that did not obey the rules."
**Problem:** "Did not obey the rules" is bland and abstract; "the rules" is generic where a more specific image would land harder.
**Why it weakens:** Vague phrasing at a closing image.
**Suggested revision:** "...a man who moved like something inhuman, healed like something flesh was not supposed to heal."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 26
**Quote:** "He thought about Hakiia. Not the dead brother in the alcove, not the voice that corrected and commanded and had just used his body as a weapon. The Hakiia who had killed the elk with effortless violence and then mocked Hurkuzak for hesitating. The Hakiia who had killed the wolf from the ground with a fury that should not have been possible. The Hakiia who broke a man's arm for touching Hurkuzak's shoulder and called it education."
**Problem:** Anaphora is intentional and earns its place; flag only "killed" repeating in two consecutive sentences. Otherwise this is the chapter's strongest paragraph.
**Why it weakens:** Minor word repetition.
**Suggested revision:** Vary one verb: "The Hakiia who had taken the elk with effortless violence..." — Low.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 27
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak walked east. The world, he was learning, pressed constantly."
**Problem:** "Was learning" is a weak progressive; "pressed constantly" is abstract closing language for a final line that should land harder.
**Why it weakens:** Weak verb construction; abstract closing image.
**Suggested revision:** "Hurkuzak walked east. The world, he had begun to learn, pressed without rest."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 21 is mostly strong — the prose carries weight, the action beats are concrete, the closing meditation on Hakiia is the chapter's high point. The weaknesses are localized: a recurring pattern of explaining a beat just before or just after rendering it, several sentences that strain for effect with em-dash interruptions, a handful of weak perception verbs ("could feel," "process"), and some word-level repetition (swing / solid / faces / concern). None of the issues are voice violations; they are line-level tightening opportunities. Cutting the half-dozen explanatory clauses and the one overwritten parenthetical will sharpen the chapter without touching its restraint.

---

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. **Issue 17** — Run-on / four-times "swing" in the second-enforcer sentence. Most damaging because it lands inside the action climax.
2. **Issue 15** — Overwritten parenthetical interruption ("and he could not have said, in that moment...") in the moment Hurkuzak loses control.
3. **Issue 16** — "Did not process what he saw quickly enough" — weak verb plus modern register drift in the action beat.
4. **Issue 8** — "Very deliberately" — empty intensifier the rule explicitly flags.
5. **Issue 4** — Three "He needed" sentence openings in a row, monotone rhythm.
6. **Issue 22** — "Every face held the same expression" sets up what the next two sentences already say.
7. **Issue 6** — "Tried to be small" is vague; "like holding his breath" tells the strain.
8. **Issue 7** — "Sharp gaze he could not shut off" tells what the next sentence then shows.
9. **Issue 11** — "Concern / concern" repetition plus over-explained motivation.
10. **Issue 13** — "They were with the large man" flat explainer plus "materialized" cliche.

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses run through the chapter:

1. **Explain-then-show.** The prose frequently introduces a beat with a summary sentence ("Every face held the same expression," "the sharp gaze he could not shut off") and then shows the same thing in the following sentences. Cutting the summary line consistently improves the passage.
2. **Wordy nominalizations and weak perception verbs.** "Could feel," "did not process," "the connection was solid," "an act that should have produced." Replacing these with strong verbs tightens the prose.
3. **Repeated words and identical sentence openings inside short windows.** "Swing" four times in one sentence, "solid" twice in two paragraphs, "faces" three times in two paragraphs, "concern" twice in one paragraph, three "He needed" openings. None are stylistic anaphora — they are accidental repeats.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **High-priority fixes first** (Issues 8, 15, 16, 17): cut "very," collapse the long parenthetical, replace "process," and break up the four-"swing" sentence.
2. **Medium-priority cleanups** (Issues 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 11, 13, 18, 19, 22, 24, 25, 27): tighten wordy openings, kill explain-then-show pairs, vary the "He needed" openings, cut "concern / concern," sharpen the closing image.
3. **Low-priority polish** (Issues 3, 9, 12, 14, 20, 21, 23, 26): leave most of these unless time permits; many are rhythmic choices that have some value as written.
4. Preserve the Hakiia paragraph's anaphora — it is intentional and load-bearing.
5. Preserve all unmarked Bleed lines ("Stay. Hold. This is yours now.") — those are voice, not weakness.
6. Preserve every restrained-horror choice. The chapter's strength is what it does not say; do not add description.
