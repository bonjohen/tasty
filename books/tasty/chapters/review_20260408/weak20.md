# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 20: Rarity

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "She interviewed the traders. The man was eager to talk. The woman was cautious. The woman had seen display fights between flesh-fed soldiers in the eastern cities. This was faster than she expected from a man who looked the way this one looked: thin-faced, poorly dressed, carrying a blanket bundle on his back like a refugee."
**Problem:** "looked the way this one looked" is wordy and awkward. The phrasing draws attention to itself without adding meaning.
**Why it weakens:** Clumsy circumlocution where a clean construction would land harder. The sentence loops on itself.
**Suggested revision:** "This was faster than she expected from a man so plainly drawn — thin-faced, poorly dressed, carrying a blanket bundle on his back like a refugee."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "He was crouching at the water's edge."
**Problem:** Weak progressive verb. "Was crouching" softens the image where the simple past is sharper.
**Why it weakens:** The progressive form bleeds the picture; the chapter elsewhere uses crisp simple past.
**Suggested revision:** "He crouched at the water's edge."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "His left hand was in the water. His right hand — the one that had caught the knife — was wrapped in cloth and he was unwrapping it and she saw the cut and she saw what the cut was doing."
**Problem:** Run-on built on stacked "and" clauses. Two "she saw" clauses are repetitive in a flattening way (not the rhetorical doubling the chapter uses elsewhere).
**Why it weakens:** The rhythm becomes rambling rather than building. Three "and"s in a row dilute the reveal that is supposed to land in the next isolated line.
**Suggested revision:** Split into beats. "His left hand rested in the water. His right — the one that had caught the knife — was wrapped in cloth. He unwound the cloth. She saw the cut, and she saw what the cut was doing."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "The edges of the wound were visibly closer together than a two-day-old palm laceration should have allowed."
**Problem:** "Visibly" is throat-clearing — a hedge that tells the reader to notice rather than letting the image speak. "Should have allowed" is wordy.
**Why it weakens:** Filler weakens a key reveal sentence. Vark is a precise observer; the prose should be precise too.
**Suggested revision:** "The edges of the wound sat closer together than a two-day-old palm laceration permitted."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "She did not pursue immediately."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing transition. The next sentence does the actual work; this one only announces it.
**Why it weakens:** A line that explains the absence of an action rather than rendering the action she does take.
**Suggested revision:** Cut. Begin the paragraph with "She descended to the stream and examined the place where he had been crouching."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "She reckoned it. At the current rate, the bolt wound would be functionally closed within a day."
**Problem:** "Reckoned it" is vague throat-clearing — a hand-wave at calculation that the next sentence already performs. "Functionally" is hedging modern abstraction.
**Why it weakens:** Two filler words muffle a precise estimate. The next sentence is the reckoning; the announcement is dead weight.
**Suggested revision:** "At the current rate, the bolt wound would be closed within a day."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "She watched him disappear into the trees. Her sight carried further than it should have. She tracked the diminishing figure — the limp evening out, the gait adjusting, the body managing the wound as she watched."
**Problem:** "As she watched" is redundant — the paragraph opens with "She watched." The phrase repeats what the reader already knows.
**Why it weakens:** Tail-end filler that flattens an otherwise sharp catalogue.
**Suggested revision:** "She tracked the diminishing figure — the limp evening out, the gait adjusting, the body managing the wound."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "A live kill of this quality was worth more than a dead one — how much more, she couldn't yet say."
**Problem:** "Yet" is filler. The hedge is implicit in "couldn't say."
**Why it weakens:** Small word, small drag. Vark's voice is built on reckoning, not on tentative future tense.
**Suggested revision:** "A live kill of this quality was worth more than a dead one — how much more, she could not say."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "She would need to be more careful. He knew he was being hunted now. The bolt had told him that."
**Problem:** "The bolt had told him that" is unnecessary explanation. The previous sentence already conveys it.
**Why it weakens:** Says the obvious thing twice. Trust the reader.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "The bolt had told him that." Let "He knew he was being hunted now" stand.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "She sat beside the stream and thought about markets. The flesh trade had its grades, and at the top — where only the kind of wealth that does not name a price could reach — sat what could not be named because it had no name yet."
**Problem:** "Could not be named because it had no name yet" is a tautology. The clause restates itself.
**Why it weakens:** Self-canceling phrasing — circular instead of incantatory.
**Suggested revision:** "She sat beside the stream and thought about markets. The flesh trade had its grades, and at the top — where only wealth that does not name a price could reach — sat the things still without a name."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "Whatever this man carried in his flesh had no category in her experience. No established price. No precedent she could put a number to."
**Problem:** Three sentences that say the same thing. The repetition is anaphoric in shape but redundant in content.
**Why it weakens:** The structural rhythm is a strength of the chapter, but here the three beats all carry the same information rather than escalating.
**Suggested revision:** Compress to two beats with distinct content: "Whatever this man carried in his flesh had no category in her experience. No established price, no precedent she could put a number to."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "The buyers who wanted that kind of proof — that a thing they had theorized about for generations was real, was stable, walked and bled and healed — would pay whatever it cost to obtain it."
**Problem:** "Whatever it cost to obtain it" is wordy and the trailing "it" is ambiguous (proof? the man?). "To obtain it" is throat-clearing.
**Why it weakens:** A long sentence loses its punch in the final phrase.
**Suggested revision:** "The buyers who wanted that kind of proof — that a thing they had theorized about for generations was real, was stable, walked and bled and healed — would pay whatever it cost."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "She cleaned her crossbow. She packed the blood vial. She noted the direction of his flight and the diminishing blood trail that confirmed her reckoning."
**Problem:** The third sentence is heavier and breaks the spare rhythm of the first two. "That confirmed her reckoning" repeats what the previous paragraph already established.
**Why it weakens:** The list cadence stalls under the qualifier.
**Suggested revision:** "She cleaned her crossbow. She packed the blood vial. She noted the direction of his flight, and the blood trail confirmed the reckoning."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "Vark started walking."
**Problem:** Weak verb construction. "Started walking" softens an action that should land cleanly.
**Why it weakens:** "Started" is a filler verb that delays the real verb. The closing beats need precision.
**Suggested revision:** "Vark walked."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "She was behind, patient and certain, and the distance between them was a number that would eventually reach zero."
**Problem:** "Eventually" is filler. The certainty is the point; "eventually" softens it.
**Why it weakens:** A closing line should not hedge.
**Suggested revision:** "She was behind, patient and certain, and the distance between them was a number that would reach zero."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "the relaxed travel mode of the earlier track"
**Problem:** "Travel mode" is faintly modern/mechanical and tonally flat next to the chapter's other diction.
**Why it weakens:** A small register slip in an otherwise disciplined paragraph.
**Suggested revision:** "the easy travel pace of the earlier track"
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "And the goodness had the same quality she had noticed in the initial track assessment: learned rather than inherent."
**Problem:** "Track assessment" is bureaucratic. "And the goodness" repeats "good" awkwardly.
**Why it weakens:** Slightly clinical phrasing in a paragraph that should feel like Vark's mind closing on a verdict.
**Suggested revision:** "And the skill carried the same quality she had noticed at the first track: learned rather than inherent."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 20 is mostly disciplined, observational, and tonally controlled — Vark's procedural intelligence is a strong vehicle for the deliberate prose. The weaknesses are not structural; they are line-level. Recurring soft spots: filler adverbs ("visibly," "eventually," "yet," "functionally"), tautological hedges ("could not be named because it had no name yet"), throat-clearing verbs ("started walking," "She did not pursue immediately," "She reckoned it"), and one run-on stacked on "and" that mutes the chapter's central physical reveal. Several places double or triple a beat that would land harder once. The chapter's signature anaphora and short-sentence cadence are working well; the fix is trimming away the words that diffuse them.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. Run-on stack of "and" clauses dilutes the wound-healing reveal (Issue 3)
2. "Eventually" softens the closing line of the chapter (Issue 15)
3. "Visibly" hedges the first wound-closure observation (Issue 4)
4. "Vark started walking" — weak verb at the closing beat (Issue 14)
5. Tautology: "could not be named because it had no name yet" (Issue 10)
6. Throat-clearing: "She did not pursue immediately" (Issue 5)
7. Throat-clearing: "She reckoned it" + "functionally" (Issue 6)
8. Redundant explanation: "The bolt had told him that" (Issue 9)
9. "Looked the way this one looked" — clumsy circumlocution (Issue 1)
10. "As she watched" tail repetition (Issue 7)

## Pattern Summary

The recurring weakness is filler that softens precision. Vark's voice is built on reckoning, measurement, and unhedged statement. The chapter slips when it adds adverbs ("visibly," "eventually," "functionally," "yet") that hedge the very precision the prose claims. A second pattern is the announcing sentence — "She did not pursue immediately," "She reckoned it" — where a beat is named instead of rendered. A third is occasional doubling: a triple-beat anaphora where the three beats carry the same information, or a tail clause that repeats what the sentence already said. None of these are voice failures; they are passes that still need a final trim.

## Revision Plan in Priority Order

1. **High priority — fix first.** Break the run-on at Issue 3 so the wound-healing reveal lands. Drop "eventually" from the closing line (Issue 15).
2. **Medium priority — line trims.** Cut "visibly," "functionally," "yet," "as she watched," and "started." Remove the announcing sentences "She did not pursue immediately" and "She reckoned it." Cut the redundant "The bolt had told him that." Replace "looked the way this one looked" and "could not be named because it had no name yet." Fix "track assessment" / "and the goodness." Tighten the long sentence ending in "to obtain it."
3. **Low priority — cosmetic.** Compress the three-beat repetition about category/price/precedent. Smooth the closing list cadence. Replace "travel mode."
