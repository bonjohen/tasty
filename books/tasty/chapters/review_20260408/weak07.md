# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 7: The Weight of What Is Left

This review applies the weak-writing rule (`.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`) only. Consistency, genre/blunders, and structure are handled by separate passes. The book's deliberate voice — McCarthy/Morrison-leaning, weighted sentences, restrained horror, unmarked Bleed, pre-industrial register — is treated as load-bearing and not flagged as weakness.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "Fifty paces from the rocks, maybe less, the travois snagged on something buried in the snow and Hurkuzak's legs folded and he went down to his knees and could not immediately stand again."
**Problem:** Run-on / over-coordinated sentence. Five clauses chained with "and" flatten what should be a hard physical collapse into a polite list.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence rhythm telegraphs nothing. The fall and the failure to rise should land as separate beats.
**Suggested revision:** Break into two or three sentences. Let the collapse have its own period.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "The cold pressed in against his face where it met the snow, and for a moment the snow seemed warm by comparison with the air above it, and that wrongness was how he knew he was in trouble."
**Problem:** "and that wrongness was how he knew he was in trouble" explains the previous image. Telling-after-showing.
**Why it weakens:** The contrast between snow and air already says it. The interpretive tag deflates the moment.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the explanatory clause. Trust the image.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak moved through it with the slow, grinding effort of a body borrowing from something deeper, something that would need to be repaid but not yet."
**Problem:** Slightly abstract / wordy. "Something deeper, something that would need to be repaid but not yet" gestures rather than naming.
**Why it weakens:** The metaphor is already present in "borrowing"; the trailing clause repeats it without adding heat.
**Suggested revision:** Trim to "borrowing from something that would need to be repaid." One image, not two.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "He had directed, corrected, assessed, and commanded from the ground with the same relentless authority he had exercised from the front of the line, and that authority had been the architecture of Hurkuzak's obedience."
**Problem:** Four near-synonymous verbs piled together, then an abstract noun ("architecture of obedience") that explains rather than shows.
**Why it weakens:** Listing verbs is a form of throat-clearing. The abstract second half tells the reader what the first half meant.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the verb list to two. Sharpen the second clause or cut it.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "The eyes moved. Slowly. They found Hurkuzak and held him and something behind them was working, trying to assemble the information — the face, the snow, the direction of travel — into something coherent."
**Problem:** "something … something" (vague repetition) and the parenthetical list weakens the line. Also the verb "was working" is weak progressive.
**Why it weakens:** Two "somethings" in one sentence read as authorial groping.
**Suggested revision:** Replace one "something" with concrete content; tighten "was working" to "worked."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "the leg underneath had moved in a way that sent a deep, sick feeling through Hurkuzak's stomach"
**Problem:** Telling. "Sent a deep, sick feeling through Hurkuzak's stomach" is a stock interior reaction.
**Why it weakens:** The leg moving wrong is the horror; naming the stomach feeling is interpretive padding.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the stomach line or replace with a physical reaction (a held breath, a withdrawn hand).
**Priority:** High

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "The flesh was hot to the touch — a wrong heat."
**Problem:** "Hot to the touch" is a cliché; "a wrong heat" then re-explains it.
**Why it weakens:** Cliché plus restatement.
**Suggested revision:** "The flesh was hot. A wrong heat." or simply "The flesh ran a wrong heat."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak could see what was happening inside the leg even if he could not name it. The color, the heat, the softness — these were the signs of a body losing. Blood was pooling where it should not pool."
**Problem:** Telling and explaining. "These were the signs of a body losing" is the narrator interpreting for the reader. "Blood was pooling" is passive progressive.
**Why it weakens:** Three sentences performing the same job — labeling the leg as bad — when the previous paragraph already showed it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut to one line. Let the previous paragraph stand.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "Hakiia looked at the sky. He said nothing for a while."
**Problem:** "For a while" is filler / vague.
**Why it weakens:** Imprecise duration in a chapter where every other beat is exact.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "for a while" or replace with a concrete beat (a breath, a count).
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "the skin around the burns hot and swollen in the way that meant infection was already working inside the damage"
**Problem:** Explanatory tag: "in the way that meant infection was already working." The narrator interprets the symptom.
**Why it weakens:** The reader can read swelling and heat. Naming infection here is medical-clinical, which the book avoids.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the interpretive clause; let the senses do it.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "It was faster than it should have been and shallower than it should have been and every few breaths there was a pause — a gap where the rhythm skipped, as though the body had briefly considered stopping and then changed its mind."
**Problem:** Run-on coordinated structure plus a tail clause that personifies the body in a way that softens the horror.
**Why it weakens:** "Briefly considered stopping and then changed its mind" is cute. The chapter is not cute.
**Suggested revision:** Break into two sentences. Cut the personification or harden it.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "the wound underneath was still seeping, and the cold had slowed it but not stopped it"
**Problem:** Mild wordiness — "slowed it but not stopped it" is the kind of clause Strunk-and-White would call balanced filler.
**Why it weakens:** Two negatives doing the work of one observation.
**Suggested revision:** "the cold had slowed it, not stopped it." Saves a syllable, sharpens the rhythm.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "as though the fingers were deciding independently whether to answer"
**Problem:** Mild personification cliché ("fingers deciding") and the adverb "independently."
**Why it weakens:** Adverb weakens; the image is borderline whimsical for the register.
**Suggested revision:** "as though the fingers answered on their own time" or cut.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "exhausted past any exhaustion Hurkuzak had witnessed, holding onto consciousness because he had nothing else left to hold."
**Problem:** "Past any exhaustion Hurkuzak had witnessed" is the kind of comparative reach that explains how tired. "Holding onto consciousness … nothing else left to hold" is sentimental wordplay.
**Why it weakens:** Both halves talk about exhaustion instead of showing it. The slack jaw and loosened mouth above already did the work.
**Suggested revision:** Cut both halves; let the physical description carry it. Or trim to one short clause.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "They were in a shallow valley in deep winter with no shelter, no fire, minimal food, and a body that was dying."
**Problem:** Stage-management / inventory paragraph. Catalogues conditions the reader already knows.
**Why it weakens:** This is a recap sentence in a chapter that does not need recap.
**Suggested revision:** Cut entirely, or compress to a single concrete image.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "The wolves were gone but the cold was not. The cabin was ahead, somewhere in the dark that was now falling in earnest, and the distance between here and there was made of snow and pain and every step cost more than the step before."
**Problem:** Continued recap. "Falling in earnest" is a mild cliché. "The distance … was made of snow and pain" is an aphoristic flourish that explains the upcoming march before it happens.
**Why it weakens:** Two sentences telling the reader what the next paragraphs will show.
**Suggested revision:** Cut or compress severely.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "For the first time since the cliff, the decision was his. Not because Hakiia had yielded authority. Because Hakiia's authority had always required his attention, and his attention was no longer continuous. He was conscious. He was present. But the commands were coming slower and repeating, and the gaps between them were filling with silence."
**Problem:** Over-explanation of an inferred shift. The reader already saw the repeated "Check the splints" and the slack jaw. This paragraph names what the previous beats demonstrated.
**Why it weakens:** Explains an emotional/structural turn instead of trusting the reader.
**Suggested revision:** Cut to one or two sentences. The first sentence and the last sentence can carry it.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "It took him a long time — his hands were bad, the wood was wet, and the cold killed the spark twice before the third caught. But the fire caught, eventually, because he kept trying past the point where trying felt useful, and the warmth was small and it was all they had."
**Problem:** "Eventually" is filler. "Past the point where trying felt useful" is interpretive. "The warmth was small and it was all they had" is sentimental capping.
**Why it weakens:** Three minor weaknesses in one breath that flatten an otherwise good beat.
**Suggested revision:** Tighten to one sentence. The third spark catching is the image; trust it.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "the firelight had brought them back from wherever they had drifted, or the warmth had, or the pain that the warmth made sharper by giving the body enough energy to feel it again."
**Problem:** Three nested "or" clauses with an abstract physiological tag. The chain "or … or … by giving the body enough energy to feel it again" is wordy and clinical.
**Why it weakens:** The image dissipates across alternatives. The narrator is hedging.
**Suggested revision:** Pick one cause or compress: "the firelight, or the pain the firelight let him feel."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "He sat with it. A broken hand on his arm, too damaged to grip and too stubborn to stop reaching."
**Problem:** "Too damaged to grip and too stubborn to stop reaching" is on the edge of an aphorism. It is also borderline sentimental in a chapter that has earned the right to restraint.
**Why it weakens:** A clean physical beat is followed by a tagline that interprets it.
**Suggested revision:** Either cut the second sentence or strip it to "A broken hand on his arm." Less is the gesture.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "Hakiia did not speak. Not to direct. Not to correct. Not to tell Hurkuzak that his angle was wrong or his pace was too slow or his grip on the poles was the grip of a man who was afraid of what he was holding."
**Problem:** The four "not to" anaphora is effective but the closing clause ("the grip of a man who was afraid of what he was holding") is overwrought and explains Hurkuzak's interior state.
**Why it weakens:** The structural rhythm is good; the final flourish strains for poetry.
**Suggested revision:** Trim the last item or cut "of a man who was afraid of what he was holding."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "a mechanical repetition that demanded everything of his back, his legs, his ruined hands"
**Problem:** "Mechanical" is anachronistic for the pre-industrial register and weak as a metaphor here. The book's CLAUDE.md explicitly avoids modern register slips, but this also reads as weak word choice (vague abstraction).
**Why it weakens:** "Mechanical" performs the work of "rhythmic" or "thoughtless" without earning it.
**Suggested revision:** Replace "mechanical repetition" with a physical word ("dumb repetition," "blind repetition," "the rhythm of nothing left").
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "Step and sink and lift and step and sink and lift, and each cycle cost warmth he would not recover."
**Problem:** The triple-verb cadence is good. "Each cycle cost warmth he would not recover" then explains the cadence.
**Why it weakens:** Telling after showing.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second clause. The cadence is the cost.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 24
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak did not need to look back to hear what was happening."
**Problem:** Slightly wordy interpretive line.
**Why it weakens:** "He did not look back" already follows. The interpretive line is redundant scaffolding.
**Suggested revision:** Cut or compress to "He could hear what was happening behind him."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 25
**Quote:** "He pulled. The poles wore grooves in his hands. The snow was deep and the cabin was far and behind him, the space where Hakiia's voice had been."
**Problem:** "The snow was deep and the cabin was far" is an inventory clause we have already heard twice. The closing image is good.
**Why it weakens:** The closing line is the chapter's last beat; the inventory in front of it dilutes the landing.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the inventory clause. End on the closing image.
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

The chapter is largely strong. The voice is controlled, the physicality is precise, and the dying-brother beats land. The recurring weakness is a tic of *telling-after-showing*: the narrator builds a strong image, then adds an interpretive tag explaining what it meant. A second tic is *inventory recap* — short paragraphs that catalogue the situation (no shelter, no fire, the cold, the cabin) when the chapter has already established all of it. Both tics are local and removable. None of them require restructuring or rewriting scenes; they want surgical trims.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. Issue 17 — over-explaining the shift in authority from Hakiia to Hurkuzak (entire paragraph)
2. Issue 15 — recap inventory paragraph ("They were in a shallow valley…")
3. Issue 16 — continued recap and aphoristic flourish about the distance to the cabin
4. Issue 14 — sentimental gloss on Hakiia's exhaustion
5. Issue 8 — three sentences naming what the leg description already showed
6. Issue 2 — explanatory "that wrongness was how he knew" tag
7. Issue 6 — stock "deep, sick feeling through his stomach" interior reaction
8. Issue 10 — clinical "infection was already working" interpretive tag
9. Issue 25 — inventory clause dulling the chapter's final landing
10. Issue 11 — coordinated run-on plus personified-body conceit on the breathing

## Pattern Summary

- **Telling-after-showing.** The author repeatedly writes a strong sensory beat and then adds a sentence naming what it meant (Issues 2, 6, 8, 10, 14, 17, 23).
- **Inventory recap paragraphs.** Short paragraphs that re-list the situation (no fire, no food, cold, cabin somewhere) appear three times in the back half (Issues 15, 16, 25). They are leftover scaffolding.
- **Coordinated run-ons with "and … and … and."** Used effectively in places, but two or three sentences are over-coordinated (Issues 1, 11).
- **Vague filler.** "For a while," "eventually," "something … something," "in the way that meant" (Issues 5, 9, 10, 18).
- **Aphoristic taglines.** Closing flourishes that strain for poetry (Issues 14, 16, 20, 21). The chapter's restraint is its strength; flourishes weaken it.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut the two recap paragraphs.** Issue 15 and Issue 16 are the largest deletions. Tighten or remove.
2. **Trim the authority-shift paragraph.** Issue 17 — keep the first sentence, cut or compress the rest.
3. **Strip telling-after-showing tags.** Issues 2, 6, 8, 10, 14, 23 — surgical sentence-end deletions.
4. **Tighten the run-ons.** Issues 1 and 11 — split into shorter sentences.
5. **Replace filler and aphoristic flourishes.** Issues 5, 9, 18, 19, 20, 21, 25 — small line edits.
6. **Replace "mechanical repetition."** Issue 22 — single-word swap for register.
7. **Leave the chapter's structure, dialogue, and image inventory untouched.** All revisions are line-level.

The chapter's bones are good. The work is removal, not addition.
