# Chapter 13 — Weak-Writing Review

Reviewer pass: weak-writing only. Voice-deliberate features (weighted sentences, restrained horror, unmarked Bleed, anaphora, stark short sentences) are preserved. Items below are flagged because they fail the rule's criteria — vague verbs, filler, unnecessary explanation, monotone runs, or stating the obvious.

---

## Per-Issue Findings

### 1. Throat-clearing opening / vague verb
**Quote:** "More days passed. Or he believed they did."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing opener; "More days passed" is the limpest possible day-marker, and the corrective ("Or he believed they did") explains what the next sentence will already show through the lost-edge wakings.
**Why it weakens:** Wastes the chapter's first beat on a generic frame instead of dropping the reader into the deteriorated time-sense.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "More days passed. Or he believed they did." Open on the marks themselves, which already carry the same idea concretely.
**Priority:** High

### 2. Telling sentence summarizing the next sentence
**Quote:** "He was weaker. It lived in the way standing up from the platform now required a sequence of movements that a month ago would have been a single act."
**Problem:** "He was weaker" is a told summary of the very next sentence, which shows it. The pronoun "It" is also fuzzy.
**Why it weakens:** Telling immediately followed by showing dilutes the show.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "He was weaker." Let the standing-up sentence carry the weight: "Standing from the platform now required a sequence of movements that a month ago would have been a single act."
**Priority:** High

### 3. Repetition restating the obvious
**Quote:** "He was aware of his body consuming itself — fat first, now muscle."
**Problem:** "He was aware of" is filler; the chapter is already deep POV. The fact also sits adjacent to the standing-up image and explains the same idea twice.
**Why it weakens:** Filler verb plus explanatory tag where the image already tells us.
**Suggested revision:** Drop "He was aware of" — "His body was consuming itself — fat first, now muscle."
**Priority:** Medium

### 4. Wordy connective; redundancy
**Quote:** "The fire continued because he continued to feed it, and the feeding of the fire was the thread that connected each day to the next, and the thread was thin but it held."
**Problem:** Three "and" clauses repeating the same image; "the feeding of the fire" is a wordy nominalization of the verb just used.
**Why it weakens:** Repetition without escalation; the sentence loops on itself.
**Suggested revision:** Tighten to one sentence with one repetition for rhythm: "The fire continued because he fed it. The feeding was the thread connecting one day to the next, thin but holding."
**Priority:** Medium

### 5. Empty intensifier / vague qualifier
**Quote:** "Afterward the swelling subsided slightly"
**Problem:** "Slightly" is a hedge that adds nothing. The pain-change clause already delivers the qualified improvement.
**Why it weakens:** Vague qualifier where omission is stronger.
**Suggested revision:** "Afterward the swelling subsided" or "the swelling went down."
**Priority:** Low

### 6. Fragment that explains itself
**Quote:** "He stopped talking to Hakiia. Not because the impulse had faded. Because the talking required energy he did not have."
**Problem:** Fine in cadence, but "the impulse had faded" is abstract. The point would land harder without the throat-clearing negation.
**Why it weakens:** Explanation rather than image.
**Suggested revision:** "He stopped talking to Hakiia. The talking required energy he did not have." (Drop the middle fragment.)
**Priority:** Medium

### 7. Two-sentence stall before the real beat
**Quote:** "The rationalizations came.\n\nThey came slowly, the way weather comes."
**Problem:** Two sentences delivering one idea; the second restates "came" three times in a sentence about weather. The simile is fine but the buildup is throat-clearing.
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness and word-echo in service of a transitional aside.
**Suggested revision:** Compress: "The rationalizations came slowly, the way weather comes."
**Priority:** Medium

### 8. Repetition of a phrase across two consecutive paragraphs
**Quote:** "Animals eat their dead. Wolves, ravens, insects, fish — the dead fed the living. The dead did not need their bodies. The living did.\n\nThe world did not care what he ate. The world had dropped him and his brother off a cliff and sent wolves and cold and starvation. Weakness drew predation. The dead fed the living."
**Problem:** "The dead fed the living" closes both paragraphs back-to-back. Once is incantatory; twice is loop.
**Why it weakens:** Diminishes the line by making it a refrain too quickly.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second instance. End the second paragraph on "Weakness drew predation."
**Priority:** Medium

### 9. Adjective-stack that explains rather than evokes
**Quote:** "the preserved cold of a space that had not been warmed"
**Problem:** The explanatory tail ("of a space that had not been warmed") tells the reader what "preserved cold" already implies.
**Why it weakens:** Unnecessary clarification of an image that worked on its own.
**Suggested revision:** "the preserved cold of a closed space" or simply "the preserved cold."
**Priority:** Low

### 10. Repetition / wordy parallel
**Quote:** "He had been avoiding this. He had been avoiding the eyes especially, because the eyes were Hakiia's and Hakiia's eyes had always seen him too clearly."
**Problem:** "He had been avoiding" twice, then "eyes" three times in one sentence. The point is clear after "He had been avoiding the eyes."
**Why it weakens:** Echo without escalation.
**Suggested revision:** "He had been avoiding the eyes especially. Hakiia's eyes had always seen him too clearly."
**Priority:** Medium

### 11. Filler "did not" stack / saying the obvious
**Quote:** "He sat on the platform. He did not eat anything because there was nothing to eat."
**Problem:** "There was nothing to eat" is the only thing the chapter has been telling us for paragraphs. The clause restates the premise.
**Why it weakens:** Saying what the reader already knows.
**Suggested revision:** "He sat on the platform. There was nothing to eat." (Or cut the second sentence outright.)
**Priority:** Medium

### 12. Wordy chain; weak verbs
**Quote:** "He thought about the shape in the alcove. The shape thought about nothing."
**Problem:** "Thought about" twice, the second as a flat ironic inversion. Cute but soft.
**Why it weakens:** Weak verb, telegraphed irony.
**Suggested revision:** "He thought about the shape in the alcove. The shape was past thinking." (Or cut the second sentence.)
**Priority:** Low

### 13. Monotone "He" openings — one paragraph, six in a row
**Quote:** "He closed the alcove door. He returned to the platform. He lay down. He did not eat that day, and the not-eating was the last thing left that felt like his own."
**Problem:** Combined with the surrounding paragraphs ("He went back…", "He sat…", "He did not eat the next day. He searched…"), the rhythm becomes machine-stamped. The book uses anaphora intentionally, but a stretch this long flattens the cadence into list-prose.
**Why it weakens:** Monotone sentence rhythm; too many similar sentence openings clustered.
**Suggested revision:** Break one or two openings. E.g., "The platform took him back. He lay down."
**Priority:** Medium

### 14. Restating the obvious / empty intensifier
**Quote:** "He went outside, into the chest-deep snow, and dug toward the tree line and looked for anything — bark, roots, frozen berries, the corpse of a small animal that the snow had buried — and found only snow and more snow and a cold that would kill him if he stayed."
**Problem:** Long but earned; the only flab is "the corpse of a small animal that the snow had buried" — the buried-by-snow tail is implied by the chest-deep snow already established.
**Why it weakens:** Minor wordiness inside an otherwise muscular sentence.
**Suggested revision:** "the corpse of some small buried animal."
**Priority:** Low

### 15. Filler tag / told emotion
**Quote:** "Then the stomach, which had stopped signaling hunger and started signaling something worse."
**Problem:** Acceptable, but the pair "stopped signaling… started signaling" leans clinical and the "something worse" hedges. Not flagged.
**Priority:** (no fix — within voice tolerance)

### 16. Wordy explanation undercutting the image
**Quote:** "He chose the shoulder because the shoulder was already gone. The tissue there had been dying since the cliff, the nerves severed, the flesh cooled and discolored days before the man it belonged to had stopped breathing."
**Problem:** Strong sentences, but "He chose the shoulder because the shoulder was already gone" preempts the explanation that follows. The next sentence does the work; the topic line is a told version.
**Why it weakens:** Telling-then-showing; both can't survive.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "He chose the shoulder because the shoulder was already gone." Begin: "The shoulder was already gone. The tissue there had been dying since the cliff…"
**Priority:** Medium

### 17. Throat-clearing dash interruption
**Quote:** "Cutting from the part that was already lost felt — not easier. Less impossible."
**Problem:** Works as a stutter but the dash + correction is a tic the chapter uses several times. Fine.
**Priority:** (no fix)

### 18. Weak verb / filler
**Quote:** "He knelt there for a long time."
**Problem:** "For a long time" is generic. The chapter uses it three times. Once is fine; twice is a tic.
**Why it weakens:** Filler that doesn't measure anything.
**Suggested revision:** Replace one instance with concrete sensory time-marking. Here: "He knelt until the cold from the stone moved through his knees." (Then trim the next sentence's now-redundant clause.)
**Priority:** Medium

### 19. Telling line that names what the scene already does
**Quote:** "He had not known survival could be so cruel."
**Problem:** Stated emotion / abstract editorial. The cabin, the screaming-not-a-sound, the cutting all carry this. The line tells the reader how to feel.
**Why it weakens:** Telling instead of showing; throat-clearing summary inside a scene that doesn't need it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut.
**Priority:** High

### 20. Wordy philosophical aside in a physical moment
**Quote:** "Eating it raw was a line he could not cross today, though the line that mattered was already behind him."
**Problem:** "Today" + the clarifying clause turns a sharp interior beat into editorializing. The point is true but the prose explains it.
**Why it weakens:** Stating subtext directly.
**Suggested revision:** "Eating it raw was a line he could not cross. The line that mattered was already behind him." (or cut the second clause entirely.)
**Priority:** Medium

### 21. Empty intensifier / saying what we just read
**Quote:** "He chewed and swallowed and was done before the mind had finished refusing."
**Problem:** Strong sentence. Keep.
**Priority:** (no fix)

### 22. Restating the obvious
**Quote:** "He ate the small piece and then he put the pan down."
**Problem:** "The small piece" was named two sentences ago. "He ate" already happened. The sentence restates a beat just to add "put the pan down."
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness; saying what the reader already knows.
**Suggested revision:** "He put the pan down."
**Priority:** Medium

### 23. Filler / weak verb
**Quote:** "He was sick. He was on his knees on the stone floor, retching."
**Problem:** "He was sick" is told; the next sentence shows. Two flat copular openings ("He was… He was…").
**Why it weakens:** Telling-then-showing; weak verbs.
**Suggested revision:** "He made it to the stone floor before the retching took him." Or just: "He was on his knees on the stone floor, retching."
**Priority:** High

### 24. Empty intensifier
**Quote:** "Not from cold."
**Problem:** Fine.
**Priority:** (no fix)

### 25. Long monotone "and… and… and" parallel
**Quote:** "long enough that the fire burned lower and the shadows lengthened and the cold crept back in from the edges of the room where the warmth no longer reached."
**Problem:** Acceptable but the closing relative clause ("where the warmth no longer reached") is the kind of explanatory tail the chapter has too many of — "cold crept back in from the edges of the room" already says it.
**Why it weakens:** Unnecessary explanation.
**Suggested revision:** "…the cold crept back in from the edges of the room."
**Priority:** Low

### 26. Telling sentence
**Quote:** "He slept. He did not mean to sleep. Now there was energy. The body took what it needed and pulled him under."
**Problem:** "Now there was energy" is a told beat that the next sentence shows. Also a comma-fragment in voice but functional.
**Why it weakens:** Telling immediately followed by showing.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "Now there was energy." The body sentence carries it.
**Priority:** Medium

### 27. Empty intensifier
**Quote:** "The cabin was very quiet."
**Problem:** "Very" is the rule's textbook empty intensifier. The next sentence carries the weight; "very" weakens the beat.
**Why it weakens:** Empty intensifier.
**Suggested revision:** "The cabin was quiet." (Or "The quiet in the cabin was complete.")
**Priority:** High

### 28. Mid-sentence repetition
**Quote:** "He was a man who had killed his brother and eaten his brother and was sitting beside a fire that burned on wood while the rest of him burned on something else entirely."
**Problem:** Strong sentence. The "burned on wood / burned on something else" parallel earns its place. Keep — this is the chapter's title-line moment.
**Priority:** (no fix)

### 29. Throat-clearing emphasis line
**Quote:** "He was alive. That was the fact."
**Problem:** "That was the fact" is editorial — the sentence before it is the fact. The tag explains.
**Why it weakens:** Restating; throat-clearing at the close of the chapter.
**Suggested revision:** "He was alive. That was all." (Or simply: "He was alive.") Choose the punchier of the two.
**Priority:** Medium

### 30. Final two beats — fine, leave alone
**Quote:** "He sat with it.\n\nHe would eat again tomorrow."
**Problem:** Strong close. Keep.
**Priority:** (no fix)

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 13 is doing its hardest work in the right register — the cabin scene is patient, physical, and restrained. The weakness is editorial padding inside a strong frame: throat-clearing openers ("More days passed", "He was weaker", "He was sick", "He had not known survival could be so cruel"), small intensifiers and qualifiers ("very quiet", "subsided slightly"), and a few places where one sentence tells what the next sentence shows. There is also a stretch in the middle where "He…" sentence openings cluster densely enough to flatten the rhythm the chapter normally varies. None of this is structural; it is line-level fat the rule asks the editor to skim off.

## Top 10 Problems (Ranked by Impact)

1. "He was sick. He was on his knees on the stone floor, retching." — told beat immediately followed by the show. (#23, High)
2. "He had not known survival could be so cruel." — narrator-editorial telling at the worst possible place. (#19, High)
3. "He was weaker. It lived in the way standing up…" — told summary swallowed by the next sentence. (#2, High)
4. "More days passed. Or he believed they did." — throat-clearing chapter opener. (#1, High)
5. "The cabin was very quiet." — textbook empty intensifier. (#27, High)
6. Six-in-a-row "He …" openings around the searching/sitting paragraphs — monotone rhythm flattens the cadence. (#13, Medium)
7. "Animals eat their dead… The dead fed the living. … Weakness drew predation. The dead fed the living." — refrain-too-soon repetition. (#8, Medium)
8. "He chose the shoulder because the shoulder was already gone." — topic-line that preempts the showing sentence following it. (#16, Medium)
9. "He had been avoiding this. He had been avoiding the eyes especially, because the eyes were Hakiia's and Hakiia's eyes…" — echo-without-escalation. (#10, Medium)
10. "He thought about the shape in the alcove. The shape thought about nothing." — soft inversion/weak verb. (#12, Low/Medium)

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses:

1. **Tell-then-show pairs.** Several sentences are followed by the very image that would have done the same work alone. The chapter's deep-POV frame doesn't need the topic sentence first.
2. **Editorial throat-clearing at openings and closings.** The chapter starts with "More days passed" and uses small editorial tags ("That was the fact", "He had not known survival could be so cruel") at moments that should be silent or imagistic.
3. **Echo-paragraphs.** A line repeats inside the same paragraph or in the next ("the dead fed the living", "He had been avoiding… the eyes… the eyes were Hakiia's… Hakiia's eyes"). Used sparingly the chapter's anaphora is its strength; used three times in a paragraph it stalls.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. Cut the four told/throat-clearing offenders: #1, #2, #4 (the second "the dead fed the living"), #19, #23. Each is a simple deletion or single-sentence trim.
2. Kill the empty intensifier "very" in "The cabin was very quiet."
3. Tighten the eyes/avoiding cluster (#10) to two sentences.
4. Trim the topic-line preempting the shoulder description (#16).
5. Break the long "He…" run (#13) by varying one or two sentence openings.
6. Compress the rationalizations stall (#7) into one sentence; drop "He was aware of" (#3) and "He thought about the shape… The shape thought about nothing" (#12).
7. Drop "Now there was energy" (#26) before the body-took-him sentence.
8. Trim residual hedges: "subsided slightly" (#5), the explanatory tail on "preserved cold" (#9) or the relative clause on "where the warmth no longer reached" (#25), and "He ate the small piece and then he put the pan down" (#22).
9. Decide between "He was alive. That was the fact." and a tighter alternative (#29).

The chapter's spine — voice, scene order, image patterning, the title-line burning-on-something-else moment — should not be touched.
