# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 5: What the Wolves Smell

## Per-Issue Findings

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak gathered what the fall had left him."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing opening / underwritten lead.
**Explanation:** The opening sentence is a generic summary that primes the reader but does no work. It tells us a thing happened without committing to image, body, or pressure. The chapter has a far stronger sentence in line two ("The work took time he did not have"), and the actual physical detail begins after that. The first line is a runway the chapter doesn't need.
**Suggested revision:** Cut it. Open on "The work took time he did not have," or fold the gather idea into the second paragraph's action.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "Every piece he found felt like a negotiation with the disaster — what it had taken, what it had allowed, what it had scattered beyond reach."
**Problem:** Telling instead of showing; abstract framing where the next paragraphs already show it concretely.
**Explanation:** The line tells us the gathering "felt like a negotiation" and then the rest of the section dramatizes exactly that. The abstract sentence steals the punch from the concrete paragraphs that follow.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the sentence and let the inventory carry the meaning, or compress to a single concrete beat tied to one object.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "He moved through the debris field in a widening circuit, crouching and digging and pulling things from the wreckage with his cut hand and his good hand."
**Problem:** Wordiness; weak verbs ("moved," "pulling things"); the parallel "with his cut hand and his good hand" is doing literal work that the next paragraphs already do better.
**Explanation:** "Moved" is weak. "Things" is vague when the chapter goes on to name specific objects. The hand detail is delivered more powerfully later when the cut palm starts costing him.
**Suggested revision:** Tighten to a shorter sentence with one active verb and trust the inventory paragraphs to do the rest. E.g., "He worked the debris field in a widening circuit, crouching and digging with his cut hand and his good one."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "The food was mostly gone."
**Problem:** Vague qualifier ("mostly"); telling.
**Explanation:** "Mostly" is the kind of soft hedge the chapter otherwise avoids. The next two sentences specify exactly what survived; the topic sentence isn't needed.
**Suggested revision:** Drop the topic sentence and start the paragraph with the burst meat bundles.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "He gathered what he could see and stuffed it into his coat."
**Problem:** Weak verb pair; the action is generic.
**Explanation:** "Gathered what he could see" is filler — of course he gathered what he could see. The line is doing nothing the previous sentence didn't already imply.
**Suggested revision:** Compress: "He stuffed what he could find into his coat."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "The skinning tools he found in a leather roll that had landed in a crevice between two rocks."
**Problem:** Awkward inversion; the next two sentences begin "He found... He found... He found..." creating monotone openings.
**Explanation:** The inverted construction reads stilted, and four consecutive "He found" beats flatten the rhythm. One of them needs a different shape.
**Suggested revision:** "He found the skinning tools in a leather roll wedged between two rocks." Then break the "He found" rhythm in the hatchet/runner/rope paragraph by varying at least one verb.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "All of Hakiia's instruments, packed with the same precision their owner brought to everything."
**Problem:** Telling; mild authorial intrusion.
**Explanation:** The list above already shows the precision. The summary line names the abstraction the reader just inferred, and the phrase "their owner" is an awkward dodge for Hakiia's name.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the line, or replace with a single concrete observation. The two-word "Hakiia's stitching" already does the work.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "He found the hatchet ten paces from the sled, half-buried in loose earth. He found the surviving sled runner — cracked but not broken — and dragged it free from the wreckage. He found a coil of rope, frayed at one end but usable. The bow had snapped at the grip."
**Problem:** Too many similar sentence openings ("He found... He found... He found..."); monotone rhythm.
**Explanation:** Three "He found" anaphora is borderline; pushing into a fourth "He found" earlier in the section makes the inventory feel mechanical instead of weighted.
**Suggested revision:** Break the pattern in at least one beat. E.g., "The hatchet lay ten paces from the sled, half-buried in loose earth. He pried the surviving sled runner — cracked but not broken — free of the wreckage. A coil of rope, frayed at one end but usable. The bow had snapped at the grip."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "He carried what he had back to Hakiia and laid it out in the snow and Hakiia looked at it the way a general looks at the remains of an army after a defeat."
**Problem:** Run-on with three "and" clauses; the simile is a near-cliche reach for grandeur in a chapter whose voice is otherwise restrained.
**Explanation:** The polysyndeton has been used effectively elsewhere in the chapter, but the closing simile is the kind of grand metaphor the book otherwise avoids — and "the way a general looks at the remains of an army" is a slightly hand-me-down image.
**Suggested revision:** Break the sentence and replace or trim the simile. E.g., "He carried what he had back to Hakiia and laid it out in the snow. Hakiia looked at it a long time."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "He shifted against the rock and his face went white and came back."
**Problem:** Slightly awkward phrasing — "came back" is ambiguous (color came back? consciousness?).
**Explanation:** The reader has to pause to parse. In a chapter this physically precise, the elision reads imprecise rather than economical.
**Suggested revision:** "He shifted against the rock and the color went out of his face and returned."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak looked at the left leg. The break was below the knee, and the lower segment had rotated in a way that made the anatomy difficult to read."
**Problem:** Clinical / abstract phrasing ("the lower segment," "anatomy difficult to read") in a chapter that elsewhere uses concrete physical language.
**Explanation:** "Lower segment" is the wrong register — it sounds like a manual. "Anatomy difficult to read" is an evasion from a writer's vocabulary, not Hurkuzak's. The next sentence ("the shape of the leg was wrong") is exactly the right register; the earlier line is the wrong one.
**Suggested revision:** "The break was below the knee, and the shin had rotated in a way his eye refused to follow."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "The flesh above the boot was swollen and discolored and the shape of the leg was wrong in a way that his hands did not want to approach."
**Problem:** Run-on; soft verbs ("was swollen and discolored").
**Explanation:** The sentence stacks two limp adjectival clauses with "and... and..." before reaching the strong final image. The strong image is buried.
**Suggested revision:** "The flesh above the boot bulged dark and tight. The shape of the leg was wrong in a way his hands did not want to approach."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "as though his body were a wall and he was pressing himself against it from the inside to keep it from falling."
**Problem:** Overwritten simile straining for effect; the literal image is hard to visualize.
**Explanation:** The metaphor is structurally confused — pressing against a wall from the inside to keep the wall from falling is a mixed image. The intent (Hakiia bracing internally against pain) is clear but the figure muddles it.
**Suggested revision:** "as though his body were a wall he was holding up from the inside."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "and he had never done this before, and Hakiia directed him through each step with patient irritability, pausing between instructions for breath that took longer than it should have."
**Problem:** Run-on tail; "patient irritability" is an oxymoron that asks the reader to do work the scene already does.
**Explanation:** "Patient irritability" explains a tone the dialogue is already showing. The "and... and..." drag flattens what should be a tight, exhausted exchange.
**Suggested revision:** Break into two sentences and trust the dialogue to carry the tone. "He had never done this before. Hakiia directed him through each step, pausing between instructions for breath that took longer than it should have."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "Even now. Even broken and gray-faced and unable to move without becoming something worse. Even now, Hakiia corrected. Hurkuzak felt the familiar anger rise. Then it turned."
**Problem:** "Hurkuzak felt the familiar anger rise" is naming the emotion instead of rendering it; "Then it turned" is vague.
**Explanation:** The fragment trio is doing strong rhythmic work, then the line collapses into emotional summary ("felt the familiar anger rise") followed by an abstract pivot ("Then it turned") that the reader can't picture.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the explicit emotion-naming. The fragments and the dialogue beat that follows already carry the shift. "Even now. Even broken and gray-faced and unable to move without becoming something worse. Even now, Hakiia corrected. The anger came up in him and then went sideways into something he had no name for."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "He set it as well as he could, using the last of the crossbar pieces and more harness leather, and Hakiia endured it with the same compressed silence, and when it was done they sat together in the bloody snow and breathed."
**Problem:** Run-on; the second leg is summarized too lightly after the first leg was rendered in detail.
**Explanation:** The polysyndeton works in places, but here it lumps three discrete beats (setting, enduring, resting) into one breathless line. After the careful rendering of the first leg, the second deserves at least sentence-level separation.
**Suggested revision:** Break at "and Hakiia endured" and at "and when it was done." Three sentences.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "the wolves found it the way they found everything — by following the smell of blood downhill."
**Problem:** Mild over-explanation; the dash clause flattens an otherwise good line.
**Explanation:** "The way they found everything" is doing work, and then the explanatory clause finishes the joke for the reader. The image is sharper without the explanation.
**Suggested revision:** "the wolves found it the way they found everything. They followed the smell of blood downhill." Or just: "the wolves found it by following the smell of blood downhill."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "with the patience of something that has never needed to hurry."
**Problem:** Borderline cliche; tense slip ("has never needed" in a past-tense narration).
**Explanation:** The phrase reads as a familiar fantasy beat, and the present perfect "has never" mid-paragraph creates a small grammatical wobble against the surrounding past tense.
**Suggested revision:** "with the patience of something that had never needed to hurry." Or sharper: "still, with the patience of a thing that did not hurry."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "The first wolf started down the slope in a careful, sideways descent, testing the footing, pausing every few steps to look at the two living things at the center of the basin and judge the cost of approaching them against the certainty of the meal that was already dead and waiting."
**Problem:** Long run-on; the final clause over-explains the wolf's calculus.
**Explanation:** This is the longest sentence in the chapter and it carries too much. The wolf's calculation is interesting but the prose explains it in a writer's vocabulary instead of leaving it implicit in the wolf's behavior.
**Suggested revision:** Split into two sentences. End on the image of the wolf weighing them, not on a mini-essay about cost-benefit.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "It ate with the steady focus of an animal that wasted nothing."
**Problem:** Telling; cliche-adjacent.
**Explanation:** "Wasted nothing" is a phrase the reader has met many times. The previous sentence already shows the wolf eating with focus.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the line, or replace with a smaller, harder physical detail.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "The nearest one had blood on its muzzle that was not its own and its gaze held the steady appraisal of something deciding whether another thing was food."
**Problem:** Wordy second clause; "another thing was food" is abstract where concrete would hit harder.
**Explanation:** The first half of the sentence is excellent. The second half names what the look means, and naming it is weaker than the look itself.
**Suggested revision:** "The nearest one had blood on its muzzle that was not its own. It looked at him the way it had looked at the dogs."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "The dead dogs would not last. When the dogs were gone there would be nothing in this basin but Hakiia on the ground and Hurkuzak standing over him, and the cliff at their backs, and the wolves already knowing where they were."
**Problem:** Run-on; the explanatory "when the dogs were gone" sentence walks the reader through math the prior line already implied.
**Explanation:** The first short sentence is doing all the work. The second is the writer making sure we got it.
**Suggested revision:** Trim the second sentence or cut the "when the dogs were gone" preamble. "The dead dogs would not last. After that there would be only Hakiia on the ground and Hurkuzak above him and the cliff at their backs."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "and Hakiia's broken legs dragged across the snow and the sound he made was longer this time, drawn out, and his hands found Hurkuzak's shoulders and gripped and his fingers dug into the muscle and left something that would be there tomorrow."
**Problem:** Long run-on with five "and" joins; the rhythm goes from weighted to breathless and overshoots.
**Explanation:** The polysyndeton is a deliberate stylistic device, but at five conjunctions in a single sentence the chain stops compressing and starts spilling. The reader loses the discrete beats.
**Suggested revision:** Break after "drawn out." Two sentences. Keep the polysyndeton in one of them, not both.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 24
**Quote:** "He lay on his back with his legs splinted and his face the color of the snow beneath him and his breathing wrong — too fast, too shallow, past what the body could absorb."
**Problem:** "Past what the body could absorb" is abstract and slightly explanatory.
**Explanation:** The two concrete details ("too fast, too shallow") are sharp. The summary clause that follows softens them into commentary.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "past what the body could absorb."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 25
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak picked up the drag poles. The travois was heavy. He leaned into the poles and pulled and the travois moved, slowly, grudgingly."
**Problem:** "The travois was heavy" is a flat telling sentence sandwiched between two concrete ones.
**Explanation:** The weight is shown by "leaned into the poles" and "moved, slowly, grudgingly." The middle sentence states what the surrounding sentences enact.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the middle sentence.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 26
**Quote:** "and Hurkuzak could feel their attention on his back like the weight of something deciding how much longer it was willing to wait."
**Problem:** Slightly overwritten simile; "the weight of something deciding how much longer it was willing to wait" is the kind of explanatory metaphor the chapter has already used (Issue 19, Issue 21).
**Explanation:** The pattern of "X looked at him with the appraisal of something deciding whether Y" appears multiple times in this chapter. By the third occurrence the device wears out.
**Suggested revision:** Vary or trim. "and Hurkuzak could feel their attention on his back, patient and unhurried."
**Priority:** Medium

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 5 is doing strong work — the physical render of the leg-setting and travois construction is among the chapter's best material, and the dialogue is clean and pressured. The weakness is consistent and narrow: the chapter reaches for explanatory similes and emotion-naming summary lines in moments where the surrounding concrete prose has already done the work. It also slips into a few long polysyndetic run-ons that overshoot the device, and a brief stretch of monotone "He found / He found / He found" openings flattens the inventory rhythm. The wolves arrive with three near-identical metaphors of patient calculation, and one or two should be varied. None of this is a voice problem; it is a "trust the rendering" problem.

## Top 10 Problems by Impact

1. (Issue 19) Long run-on describing the first wolf's descent — the chapter's longest sentence carries the most explanatory freight.
2. (Issue 11) Clinical "lower segment / anatomy difficult to read" register break in the leg description.
3. (Issue 15) "Felt the familiar anger rise" — emotion-naming where the fragments already carry the shift.
4. (Issue 8) Four-beat "He found... He found..." monotone in the inventory paragraph.
5. (Issue 26) Repeated "appraisal of something deciding" simile pattern (third use is the breaking one).
6. (Issue 23) Five-conjunction run-on when Hurkuzak loads Hakiia onto the travois.
7. (Issue 9) Polysyndeton + general-after-defeat simile reaches for grandeur in a restrained chapter.
8. (Issue 14) Run-on tail with "patient irritability" oxymoron explaining a tone the dialogue shows.
9. (Issue 13) Mixed/confused "wall pressed from the inside" simile.
10. (Issue 25) Flat "The travois was heavy" sentence sandwiched between two that enact the weight.

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses:

1. **Explain-after-render.** A strong concrete beat is followed by an abstract or simile sentence that names what the beat already showed (anger rising, wolves calculating, body absorbing strain, general after defeat). The rendering is doing the work; the explanation is taking it back.
2. **Polysyndeton overshoot.** The "and... and... and..." rhythm is a real device in this voice, but a handful of sentences chain four or five conjunctions where two or three would land harder. The cadence tips from weighted to spilled.
3. **Monotone sentence openings in inventory beats.** When the prose is cataloguing recovered objects or wolf movements, it falls into "He found / He found / The first / Then a second / Then a third" patterns that need at least one variation each to keep the rhythm alive.

A smaller fourth pattern: a clinical/writerly word ("lower segment," "anatomy," "wasted nothing") slipping into a chapter whose default register is harder and more bodily.

## Revision Plan in Priority Order

1. Cut or rewrite the four explanatory/emotion-naming lines (Issues 11, 15, 19, 25). These return the most ground per cut.
2. Break the inventory monotone (Issue 8) by varying one of the "He found" openings.
3. Vary the third "appraisal of something deciding" simile (Issue 26) and trim the second similar one (Issue 21).
4. Break the two longest polysyndeton run-ons (Issues 23, 16) into two sentences each.
5. Tighten or replace the "general after defeat" simile (Issue 9) and the mixed-image "wall from the inside" simile (Issue 13).
6. Trim the "patient irritability" tail (Issue 14) and the wolf-explanation tail (Issue 22).
7. Fix the present-perfect tense slip in the wolf line (Issue 18).
8. Sweep the lower-priority filler/topic-sentence cuts (Issues 1, 4, 5, 10, 17, 24) only if they don't disrupt rhythm.
