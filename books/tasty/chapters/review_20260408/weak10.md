# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 10: The Carrying

Reviewed against `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`. The book's deliberate stylistic choices (weighted McCarthy-leaning sentences, polysyndetic chains, restrained horror, unmarked Bleed) are not flagged as weakness — only prose that fails on its own terms is.

---

## Per-Issue Entries

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "The shivering was not a decision. It was the body announcing that it had a different idea about what happened next, and the body's idea was survival, and it did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely."
**Problem:** Unnecessary explanation / telling. The first sentence already lands the point ("not a decision"). The follow-up explains the metaphor at length and dilutes it.
**Why it weakens:** The second sentence narrates around the image instead of trusting it. "Different idea about what happened next" and "did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely" are both abstractions of something the first sentence already showed.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the second sentence, or compress it: "The shivering was not a decision. The body had its own idea, and the mind was somewhere else."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "The world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out and waited and the balance came, not through strength but through stubbornness."
**Problem:** The "not X but Y" tag explains the emotion the sentence already carries.
**Why it weakens:** The image of him holding his arms out and waiting is already stubbornness. Naming it flattens it.
**Suggested revision:** "The world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out and waited, and the balance came."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "The body was no longer the body of a man who was dying or a man who was in pain or a man who was commanding or arguing or saying please. What remained was mass without will, shaped like a man."
**Problem:** Slight overwriting / slight throat-clearing. The "no longer the body of a man who was…" construction lists past states the reader has just read in chapter 9. The second sentence is the line that lands.
**Why it weakens:** The catalogue delays the punch line. The cumulative effect would be sharper if shorter.
**Suggested revision:** Trim the catalogue: "The body was no longer dying, or in pain, or commanding, or arguing, or saying please. What remained was mass without will, shaped like a man."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "The wolf fight and the long dragging had wrecked it — the lashings had loosened, one of the poles had cracked near the crosspiece, and the furs underneath were stiff with frozen blood and no longer slid cleanly."
**Problem:** Passive/possessive past-perfect chain ("had wrecked," "had loosened," "had cracked") deadens what should be concrete physical detail.
**Why it weakens:** Past-perfect verbs in series sound like inventory rather than damage felt by a man kneeling beside the thing.
**Suggested revision:** Active present-of-the-scene: "The wolf fight and the dragging had wrecked it. The lashings hung loose, one pole cracked near the crosspiece, and the furs beneath were stiff with frozen blood and no longer slid clean."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak knelt and retied what he could retie and reinforced the cracked pole with a strip of leather and repositioned the furs and tested the frame by lifting the drag poles and pulling."
**Problem:** Polysyndetic chain that runs past its rhythmic purpose. The book uses "and…and…and" deliberately, but here it becomes a checklist.
**Why it weakens:** Six clauses linked by "and" in one sentence loses the McCarthy-cadence and becomes process narration.
**Suggested revision:** Break in two: "Hurkuzak knelt and retied what he could and reinforced the cracked pole with a strip of leather. He repositioned the furs and tested the frame, lifting the drag poles, pulling."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "It moved. Barely. The resistance was different now, wrong in a way he could not account for, and he adjusted his grip and leaned and the frame still pulled back at him with a stubbornness that had not been there before."
**Problem:** "Stubbornness that had not been there before" is abstract and explains the resistance instead of rendering it. Also a near-repeat of the "stubbornness" word from earlier in the same paragraph block.
**Why it weakens:** Vague language ("different," "wrong in a way he could not account for") followed by another abstraction ("stubbornness"). The reader needs the felt weight of a corpse versus a man.
**Suggested revision:** "It moved. Barely. The resistance was different now — heavier, dead-weighted — and he adjusted his grip and leaned and the frame pulled back at him in a way it had not pulled before."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "His mind did, and his mind was the least of what he needed."
**Problem:** Telling / on-the-nose summary of the preceding image.
**Why it weakens:** The previous line already sells the contrast (the poles do not know yesterday these hands pulled a living man). This line interprets it for the reader.
**Suggested revision:** Cut. Let "The poles did not know the difference" close the paragraph.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "The valley continued."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing opening. "Continued" is a weak transitional verb that does no work.
**Why it weakens:** It announces a paragraph rather than starting one. The next sentence is the actual opening.
**Suggested revision:** Cut, or replace with the concrete image that follows: "The snow was deep, the trees sparse, and the dawn came in the slow, reluctant way winter dawns arrive…"
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "He pulled through this half-light and the travois scraped and caught and scraped again and his arms burned and his ribs burned and his bitten forearm pulsed with every pull, so regular it replaced the hours."
**Problem:** Run-on / monotone "and"-chain. Six clauses chained without a break.
**Why it weakens:** The rhythm flattens. "His arms burned and his ribs burned" is also a parallel structure that begs to be tightened.
**Suggested revision:** "He pulled through the half-light. The travois scraped and caught and scraped again. His arms burned, his ribs burned, his bitten forearm pulsed with every pull — so regular it replaced the hours."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "He pulled for what might have been an hour. The travois caught on roots buried under the snow and on rocks hidden beneath the white surface and each time it caught he had to stop and pull it free and each time he pulled it free the jolt traveled through the frame and the body shifted and the shifting was the movement of something that was not choosing to move."
**Problem:** Long run-on; redundant phrasing ("roots buried under the snow and rocks hidden beneath the white surface" — same concept said twice with synonyms); repeated "each time… each time."
**Why it weakens:** Wordiness, near-repetition, and a closing abstraction ("the movement of something that was not choosing to move") that explains the image instead of trusting it.
**Suggested revision:** "He pulled for what might have been an hour. The travois caught on roots and rocks under the snow. Each time it caught he stopped and worked it free, and each jolt traveled the frame, and the body shifted with the slack movement of a thing not choosing to move."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "Leaving it to drag felt like something Hakiia would have criticized."
**Problem:** Telling. "Felt like something X would have criticized" is summary, not scene.
**Why it weakens:** The line names a feeling rather than rendering it. The Bleed is supposed to enter unmarked; this is the narrator stepping in to label it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut, or convert to direct image: "Leaving it to drag was the kind of thing Hakiia would have hated."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "He lifted the arm — heavy, stiff, the cold had begun its work on the joints — and laid it across the chest…"
**Problem:** Em-dash insertion that uses a full independent clause ("the cold had begun its work on the joints") inside the dash break, creating a small grammatical lurch.
**Why it weakens:** The dash structure works for fragments ("heavy, stiff") but the inserted clause weakens it. "Had begun its work on the joints" is also slightly cliched.
**Suggested revision:** "He lifted the arm — heavy, stiff, the joints already cold-set — and laid it across the chest…"
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "The word came out of him without thought. 'Sorry,' he said, and the silence that followed was the hollow that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear."
**Problem:** Slight overwriting / explanation. "The hollow that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear" is a sentence-length gloss on the silence after "sorry."
**Why it weakens:** The reader already knows Hakiia is dead. The image labels its own meaning.
**Suggested revision:** "The word came out of him without thought. 'Sorry,' he said. The silence after it was the silence of a word spoken to no one."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "A voice — or the memory of one, which had been part of his life so long its absence was a presence:"
**Problem:** Abstract over-explanation of the Bleed at the moment it should enter unmarked. The construction ("which had been part of his life so long its absence was a presence") is precisely the kind of narrator interpretation the book's voice rules warn against.
**Why it weakens:** Tells the reader what to feel about the voice instead of letting the voice arrive. Also breaks the stated Bleed convention (enter unmarked).
**Suggested revision:** "A voice, or the memory of one:" — let the line that follows do the work.
**Priority:** High

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "Then the wind and the scraping of the travois and the sound of his own breathing, which was loud and labored and not unlike the wrong breathing Hakiia had made in the hours before the end."
**Problem:** Sentence fragment that begins with "Then" and never completes a verb; the "not unlike" double-negative is weak.
**Why it weakens:** "Not unlike" is hedged language where direct comparison would be sharper. The fragment also leaves the action floating.
**Suggested revision:** "Then there was only the wind and the scraping of the travois and the sound of his own breathing — loud, labored, like the wrong breathing Hakiia had made in the hours before the end."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "He looked ahead and saw, through the trees, a shape that was the cabin — a wall face catching the light, the dark slot of a door, the horizontal of a snow-held roof. He walked toward it for ten minutes before the shape resolved into a rock face with a shelf of ice along its top edge that had caught the light in a way that mimicked walls and windows."
**Problem:** Wordiness in the second sentence; "in a way that mimicked walls and windows" explains the illusion the first sentence already set up.
**Why it weakens:** The reveal works best if the resolution is curt — the cabin was not there.
**Suggested revision:** "He looked ahead and saw, through the trees, a shape that was the cabin — a wall face catching the light, the dark slot of a door, the horizontal of a snow-held roof. He walked toward it for ten minutes before the shape resolved into a rock face and a shelf of ice that had mimicked walls and windows."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "A warmth on his left side, the side closest to the travois, as though the body behind him were still radiating heat. It was not. The body was cold."
**Problem:** Telling. The two-sentence correction ("It was not. The body was cold.") explains the hallucination after rendering it. Trust the reader.
**Why it weakens:** The first sentence already sets the wrongness with "as though." The follow-up turns the moment into commentary.
**Suggested revision:** "A warmth on his left side, the side closest to the travois, as though the body behind him were still radiating heat. It was not."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "More came after that. A tree that had not been there and then was. A sound like a voice carried on the wind that resolved, when he listened, into nothing. A moment where the ground seemed to tilt and the snow slid sideways and he braced himself against a fall that was not happening because the ground was level and his mind was not."
**Problem:** "Because the ground was level and his mind was not" is on-the-nose telling — explains the hallucination instead of letting it stand.
**Why it weakens:** The list of three hallucinations is doing strong work; the explanatory tag flattens the third.
**Suggested revision:** "…a moment where the ground seemed to tilt and the snow slid sideways and he braced himself against a fall that was not happening."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "He felt Hakiia's hand on his shoulder — the rough, corrective grip that had steered him a thousand times, the grip that belonged to a hand that no longer existed — and he turned and there was only the travois and the body and the cold, and the phantom grip dissolved into the wind's pressure on his coat."
**Problem:** Long sentence with two parallel em-dash glosses on "grip." The second gloss ("the grip that belonged to a hand that no longer existed") is the kind of explanatory restatement the rules flag.
**Why it weakens:** The reader already knows the hand is gone (it was burned and broken in earlier chapters and the body is on the travois). The line names what it could trust.
**Suggested revision:** "He felt Hakiia's hand on his shoulder — the rough, corrective grip that had steered him a thousand times — and he turned, and there was only the travois and the body and the cold, and the grip dissolved into the wind's pressure on his coat."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "He stopped in the early afternoon, if it was the afternoon — the light had not changed, the gray was the same gray it had been since dawn, and the only reason he believed it was later was that his body was more exhausted than it had been."
**Problem:** Wordy. "The only reason he believed it was later was that his body was more exhausted than it had been" uses fourteen words to say "his body was more tired."
**Why it weakens:** The sentence loses momentum at the end. "More exhausted than it had been" is also weak comparative.
**Suggested revision:** "He stopped in the early afternoon, if it was the afternoon — the light had not changed, the gray was the same gray since dawn, and only his body, which was more tired than before, told him time had passed."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 21
**Quote:** "The afternoon wore into something that might have been evening. The light did not change — winter light did not change, it simply was and then it was not — but the temperature dropped in the small, measurable increments that meant the sun, wherever it hid behind the gray, was leaving."
**Problem:** Repetition / over-explanation. "The light did not change — winter light did not change" repeats itself; "in the small, measurable increments that meant the sun…was leaving" is wordy explanation.
**Why it weakens:** The image of light that "simply was and then it was not" is strong; the explanation around it is filler.
**Suggested revision:** "The afternoon wore into something that might have been evening. Winter light did not change — it simply was, and then it was not — but the cold deepened, and the sun, wherever it hid, was leaving."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 22
**Quote:** "He did not build a fire. A fire needed what he no longer had well enough — hands that worked, a mind that could hold a thought, wood that was not buried under snow."
**Problem:** "What he no longer had well enough" is awkward and slightly imprecise — "well enough" hangs.
**Why it weakens:** Reads like a phrase the writer talked themselves into. The list that follows is strong; the tag-line is weak.
**Suggested revision:** "He did not build a fire. A fire needed what he no longer had — hands that worked, a mind that could hold a thought, wood that was not buried under snow."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 23
**Quote:** "The silence listened."
**Problem:** Cliche-adjacent. "The silence listened" is a borderline overwrought figure.
**Why it weakens:** It is also slightly mismatched with the book's restraint — the surrounding sentences earn their weight; this one strains.
**Suggested revision:** Either delete, or replace with the felt detail that follows. Possibly: "The silence held."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 24
**Quote:** "He pulled. The valley went on. The trees went on. The snow went on. Behind him, the body of his brother slid through the world on a broken travois, and Hurkuzak pulled because the cabin was ahead, and because the cold did not stop."
**Problem:** "Went on" three times in three sentences is intentional but the closing "because the cabin was ahead, and because the cold did not stop" tells the reason instead of letting the rhythm carry it.
**Why it weakens:** The triple "went on" lands. The because-clause then explains the chapter's whole motor in a single tag, which the chapter has already shown.
**Suggested revision:** "He pulled. The valley went on. The trees went on. The snow went on. Behind him, the body of his brother slid through the world on a broken travois. The cabin was ahead. The cold did not stop."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 10 is doing strong work — the corpse-as-mass conceit, the failing travois, the hallucinated cabin, the unmarked Bleed line, and the closing image are all earning their place. Where the chapter weakens itself is at the seam between image and explanation. The writer keeps adding a small interpretive tag after a strong image — "and his mind was the least of what he needed," "because the ground was level and his mind was not," "the hollow that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear." These tags treat the reader as if the image alone won't carry. The other recurring problem is rhythmic: "and"-chains that have run past their McCarthy purpose into checklist-narration, and a few wordy comparative constructions ("more exhausted than it had been," "what he no longer had well enough") that slacken otherwise tight paragraphs. Cutting the explanatory tags and tightening two or three run-ons would lift the chapter substantially.

## Top 10 Problems by Impact

1. Issue 14 — Over-explanation of the Bleed's first entry breaks the unmarked-voice rule.
2. Issue 7 — "And his mind was the least of what he needed" interprets the chapter's strongest image.
3. Issue 18 — "Because the ground was level and his mind was not" flattens the hallucination list.
4. Issue 10 — Long run-on with synonym repetition and an explanatory closing abstraction.
5. Issue 9 — Six-clause "and"-chain that loses its rhythm.
6. Issue 6 — Vague resistance ("different," "stubbornness") where felt weight is needed.
7. Issue 15 — "Not unlike" hedged comparison and an unfinished fragment.
8. Issue 17 — Two-sentence correction ("It was not. The body was cold.") explains the hallucination.
9. Issue 1 — Second sentence narrates around the metaphor of involuntary shivering.
10. Issue 21 — Repetition ("light did not change — winter light did not change") and wordy "small, measurable increments" tag.

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses:

1. **Explanatory tags after strong images.** The writer trusts the image, lands it, then adds one more clause that names what the image already meant. This is the dominant weakness in the chapter and the cleanest fix.
2. **Run-on "and"-chains pushed past their cadence.** The book's polysyndeton works in pairs and triples; in this chapter several sentences carry five and six "and"s and become process narration rather than rhythm.
3. **Wordy comparative and hedge constructions.** "More exhausted than it had been," "what he no longer had well enough," "not unlike," "in a way he could not account for." These slacken otherwise taut sentences and read as half-formed phrasing the writer didn't return to.

A minor fourth pattern: a few near-cliche figures ("had begun its work on the joints," "the silence listened") that are out of register with the book's restraint.

## Revision Plan in Priority Order

1. **First pass — cut the explanatory tags.** Remove the closing interpretive clauses on Issues 7, 14, 17, 18, 11, and 24. These are surgical deletions that immediately strengthen the prose.
2. **Second pass — break the run-on chains.** Tighten Issues 9, 10, and 5 by inserting periods where the rhythm has flattened. Keep the polysyndeton in pairs and triples; cut where it exceeds.
3. **Third pass — replace vague/hedge constructions with concrete weight.** Issues 6, 15, 20, 22 — substitute felt physical detail for "different," "not unlike," "more exhausted than it had been," "what he no longer had well enough."
4. **Fourth pass — minor compressions.** Issues 1, 2, 3, 16, 19, 21 — tighten the few remaining sentences that say a thing twice.
5. **Optional polish.** Issues 12, 23 — replace the borderline cliches.
