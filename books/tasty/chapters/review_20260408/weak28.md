# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 28: The Ground Remembers

## Per-Issue Entries

### 1. Redundant restatement of "nothing" (Medium)
- Quote: "The nothing was what he wanted. Nothing did not have merchants or physicians or stalls..."
- Problem: Echo-repetition padding. The first sentence already establishes the want; the second rephrases for emphasis but then stacks another long clause.
- Revision: Cut "The nothing was what he wanted." Let the list do the work: "Nothing had no merchants, no physicians, no stalls..."
- Priority: Medium

### 2. Over-explained metaphor (Medium)
- Quote: "the comfort was the comfort of a man sharing his skull with something that had taken up residence with the authority of someone who had always taken whatever room he wanted."
- Problem: The simile runs long and double-dips ("taken up residence" + "taken whatever room"). The cleverness flattens the menace.
- Revision: Trim to "the comfort of a man sharing his skull with a tenant who had always taken whatever room he wanted."
- Priority: Medium

### 3. Throat-clearing qualifier (Low)
- Quote: "His breathing shortened by a degree so small that he would not have noticed it except that the voice noticed it first"
- Problem: Explains the mechanism of noticing. The insight is strong; the scaffolding is not.
- Revision: "His breathing shortened. The voice noticed before he did."
- Priority: Medium

### 4. Telling the reader the theme (High)
- Quote: "His body knew this place. His mind was still catching up."
- Problem: States outright what the preceding paragraph already rendered behaviorally. Flattens the dread into summary.
- Revision: Delete both sentences. The bone-response paragraph ends stronger on "refusing to revisit."
- Priority: High

### 5. Overwritten sentence straining for effect (Medium)
- Quote: "Recognition required effort or accident or the particular cruelty of a landscape that waits until you are standing in it before it tells you where you are."
- Problem: Triplet escalation with a second-person shift that breaks POV briefly. The final clause aestheticizes a moment that should be plain.
- Revision: "Recognition required effort, or accident, or a landscape cruel enough to wait until he was standing in it."
- Priority: Medium

### 6. Hedged, over-qualified description (Medium)
- Quote: "though he had not remembered them at all until this moment and the remembering arrived not as an image but as a spatial relationship"
- Problem: Long parenthetical that interrupts the sentence's momentum and explains the nature of memory rather than rendering it.
- Revision: Break the sentence. "He had not remembered them until now. The memory came as a spatial relation — the distance between this tree and that rock..."
- Priority: Medium

### 7. Repeated sentence opener / weak rhythm (Medium)
- Quote: "He stopped. He was twenty paces from the edge and he stopped because his body stopped"
- Problem: "Stopped" three times in fifteen words. The repetition is trying to be incantatory but reads as stumble.
- Revision: "He stopped twenty paces from the edge. His body had stopped first."
- Priority: Medium

### 8. Over-explained memory-vs-reality beat (High)
- Quote: "It was not a cliff the way he remembered it. Memory had preserved the catastrophe — the sudden break, the vertical drop, the sound of the sled splintering on the rocks below. What remained was a long muddy scar..."
- Problem: The paragraph explains the comparison it's making. The image of the scar and the weathered fracture would land harder without the preamble about memory.
- Revision: Cut the memory gloss. Open on the physical: "The cliff had weathered. What memory held as a sudden break was now a long muddy scar..."
- Priority: High

### 9. Telling instead of showing (High)
- Quote: "He was not brave. He was not deliberate. He was pulled."
- Problem: Three flat declaratives summarizing what the previous simile (mourner to grave) already showed. Explicit interpretation of a moment the reader has already received.
- Revision: Delete. End the paragraph on "a survivor back to the ruins of a house."
- Priority: High

### 10. Over-explained skill attribution (Medium)
- Quote: "and he adjusted with a precision that was the voice's contribution — the borrowed skill that read terrain the way a mason reads stone."
- Problem: Attributes the skill explicitly, then similes it. The Bleed works best unmarked (per CLAUDE.md); labeling it "the voice's contribution" punctures that.
- Revision: "and he adjusted with a precision that did not feel like his own."
- Priority: High

### 11. Repetition of "body/carried" loop (Low)
- Quote: "He had carried Hakiia's body out of a place like this... He had carried the body to the cabin. He had consumed the body in the cabin. He carried the body still."
- Problem: Intentional incantation — likely a stylistic choice echoing McCarthy. Leaving as-is.
- Priority: Low (no change)

### 12. Cliche risk (Low)
- Quote: "the path he had walked since the cliff had always been a circle, and the circle was closing."
- Problem: Slightly aphoristic; the "circle closing" image is close to cliche but the preceding concrete work earns it. Leaving as-is.
- Priority: Low (no change)

### 13. Redundant final clause (Medium)
- Quote: "The voice inside him had gone still with the stillness of something that already knew what he was about to find."
- Problem: "Still with the stillness of" is tautological filler before the real clause.
- Revision: "The voice had gone still the way something goes still when it already knows what he is about to find."
- Priority: Medium

### 14. Weak intensifier / explanatory aside (Medium)
- Quote: "the mud and silt that a winter's melt had carried into the ravine's lowest point"
- Problem: Explains the mud's provenance after the image has already landed. Slows the step.
- Revision: Cut the clause entirely.
- Priority: Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

The chapter is doing strong work and the voice is the right voice. Its weakness is a recurring instinct to gloss its own images: after rendering a physical moment well, the prose adds a summarizing sentence or an interpretive pair that tells the reader what the image meant. The second habit is over-long similes — triple-clause constructions where a two-clause version would hit harder. The chapter does not have a diction problem or a structural problem; it has a trust problem.

## Pattern Summary

- **Gloss-after-image**: a concrete rendering followed by a short abstract summary of what it meant (#4, #8, #9).
- **Triple-clause similes**: similes that keep extending past their landing (#2, #5).
- **Explanatory asides** inside long sentences (#3, #6, #14).
- **Tautology for rhythm** (#13, #7) — repetition used as incantation sometimes lands (paragraph about carrying the body) and sometimes trips (stopped/stopped/stopped).

## Revision Plan (priority order)

1. Delete the gloss summaries (#4, #9).
2. Rewrite the cliff/memory paragraph to lead with the physical (#8).
3. Unmark the voice's skill contribution (#10).
4. Trim the two triple-clause similes (#2, #5).
5. Fix the "stopped" stumble (#7).
6. Remove tautology and explanatory asides (#13, #6, #3, #14, #1).
