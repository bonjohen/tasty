# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 4: The Road Gives Way

Reviewed against `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`. Voice exemptions per `books/tasty/CLAUDE.md`: weighted McCarthy/Morrison-leaning prose, restrained horror, and unmarked Bleed are intentional and not flagged.

---

## Per-Issue Entries

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "and Hurkuzak could see shapes that did not belong to the landscape."
**Problem:** Abstract / vague language. "Shapes that did not belong" is a generic horror placeholder where concrete debris is already named in the next paragraph. The line softens what should land hard.
**Why it weakens:** Withholds what is in fact about to be specified, so it reads as throat-clearing rather than dread.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the line, or replace with a single concrete image (a runner, a flank, a twisted harness) so the eye lands on a thing, not a category.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "with its neck at an angle that did not allow for life"
**Problem:** Wordy circumlocution. "Did not allow for life" is six words doing what one word does.
**Why it weakens:** Flattens the image into abstraction at the exact moment a concrete picture would hit harder.
**Suggested revision:** "with its neck wrong" or "with its neck broken" — let the angle speak in fewer beats.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "and his brother was somewhere inside it or beneath it or beyond it."
**Problem:** Triple-prepositional hedge. The three options pad the line and dilute urgency.
**Why it weakens:** Reads as the narrator listing possibilities instead of the brother searching. Information without pressure.
**Suggested revision:** "and his brother was somewhere inside it." The single preposition is enough; the search itself in the next sentences carries the rest.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "the kind of noise a body makes when breathing requires a decision"
**Problem:** Overwritten / straining for effect. The construction calls attention to itself.
**Why it weakens:** Pulls the reader out of the moment to admire phrasing rather than feel the cough.
**Suggested revision:** Tighten — "the noise of a body deciding whether to breathe" — or cut the formulation and let the cough/groan stand.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "He waited. He did not breathe."
**Problem:** Telling instead of showing — and the second clause contradicts the texture of the first. Two flat statements where one image would do.
**Why it weakens:** "Did not breathe" is a stock gesture; the chapter has earned more.
**Suggested revision:** "He waited." Cut the second sentence, or replace with a held physical detail (jaw set, mouth open).
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "the angles were not angles that legs were meant to make"
**Problem:** Wordy and self-explaining. The "angles… angles… meant to make" repetition is the writer hedging.
**Why it weakens:** Tells the reader what to think about the angle instead of trusting the next sentence (which lands the concrete image).
**Suggested revision:** "the angles were wrong." Trust the specific lines that follow.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "his breath came in short controlled bursts that sounded like someone counting"
**Problem:** Soft simile that explains rather than shows. "Someone counting" is generic and reaches outside the scene.
**Why it weakens:** Drops in a comparison the chapter doesn't need; the controlled breath is already vivid.
**Suggested revision:** "his breath came in short controlled bursts" — drop the simile, or replace with a number ("four counts in, four out") if the counting matters.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "The traces he cut free from the wreckage and bundled. The harnesses he left."
**Problem:** Inverted syntax used twice in a row creates a mannered rhythm that reads as pastiche rather than voice.
**Why it weakens:** Two consecutive object-fronted clauses call attention to the construction.
**Suggested revision:** Vary one — "He cut the traces free and bundled them. The harnesses he left." Restores rhythm without losing register.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "The climb down was ugly."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing topic sentence. Tells the reader the verdict before the evidence arrives.
**Why it weakens:** The next four sentences prove the climb was ugly. The label is redundant.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the line and open the paragraph on the action: "He moved on his hands and his seat…"
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "He kept moving. He did not look up."
**Problem:** Two flat declaratives doing the work of one image; "did not look up" is a stock survival beat.
**Why it weakens:** After the braided howls — a strong image — the chapter coasts on convention.
**Suggested revision:** Cut to one line: "He kept moving." Or: "He kept his eyes on his hands."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "He reached the bottom bruised, bleeding, and shaking."
**Problem:** Three-adjective summary list — telling rather than showing. The previous paragraph already rendered each item concretely (split palm, struck ribs, struck knee).
**Why it weakens:** Repeats earned information in flat summary form. Wastes the climb's specificity.
**Suggested revision:** "He reached the bottom." The reader already has the bruises.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "The basin was a mess of broken earth and scattered snow and wreckage."
**Problem:** Vague summary repeated from earlier. The basin has been described already; "mess of" is filler.
**Why it weakens:** A generic recap instead of forward motion to Hakiia.
**Suggested revision:** Cut, or compress to "The basin was wreckage." Move directly to finding Hakiia.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "He pulled stone and snow and pieces of the sled away from Hakiia's body, working around the broken legs, trying not to move anything that would shift the weight onto what was already shattered."
**Problem:** Long compound sentence with three trailing participles. The final clause ("what was already shattered") is abstract restatement of "broken legs."
**Why it weakens:** The participle stack flattens the rhythm of a tense moment, and the abstract closer dilutes the broken-leg image.
**Suggested revision:** Split: "He pulled stone and snow and pieces of the sled away from Hakiia's body. He worked around the broken legs, careful not to shift them." Tighter and more present.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "Their bodies lay in the open, and the smell rose into the cold air like a signal."
**Problem:** "Like a signal" is a soft, explanatory simile that names the meaning instead of letting it sit.
**Why it weakens:** Tells the reader what the smell does; the wolves in the next paragraph already do that work.
**Suggested revision:** "Their bodies lay in the open. The smell rose into the cold air." Let the howl that follows interpret the smell.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "Hakiia heard it too. His eyes went to the cliff edge, then the dead dogs, then Hurkuzak."
**Problem:** "Heard it too" is throat-clearing — narrating that he registers what we just registered. The eye-tracking line carries the meaning alone.
**Why it weakens:** The throat-clear blunts a sharp three-beat image.
**Suggested revision:** Cut "Hakiia heard it too." Open on the eyes.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "from somewhere below and to the right, muffled by stone and snow and distance"
**Problem:** Wordy scene-setting parenthetical. Three "and"s in a list of obstructions slow the line at the moment of first contact.
**Why it weakens:** Pads the breath before the cough. The cough wants to land sooner.
**Suggested revision:** "from somewhere below, muffled" — drop to a single direction and a single obstruction.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "the blood was bright and hot against the cold stone"
**Problem:** Hot/cold contrast is a cliché in injury writing. "Bright and hot" is fine, "against the cold stone" is the stock pairing.
**Why it weakens:** A reach for sensory contrast that has been worn smooth by use.
**Suggested revision:** "the blood was bright and hot." Drop the contrast.
**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

The chapter is structurally strong and the voice holds. Sentence rhythm is mostly good, the action is concrete, and the dialogue between brothers is exactly the right pressure. The weaknesses are local: a recurring habit of writing one beat too many (a flat label sentence followed by the concrete one that earns it), occasional abstract circumlocutions where a simple word would hit harder ("did not allow for life," "angles… meant to make," "what was already shattered"), and a few stock survival gestures ("did not breathe," "did not look up," hot blood / cold stone) that the rest of the prose has earned the right to skip.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. **Issue 9** — "The climb down was ugly." Throat-clearing topic sentence. (Medium)
2. **Issue 11** — "bruised, bleeding, and shaking." Repeats just-rendered detail in flat summary. (Medium)
3. **Issue 6** — "angles were not angles that legs were meant to make." Wordy hedge before the concrete image. (Medium)
4. **Issue 13** — Participle-stacked compound sentence in the digging beat. (Medium)
5. **Issue 2** — "neck at an angle that did not allow for life." Wordy circumlocution. (Medium)
6. **Issue 15** — "Hakiia heard it too." Throat-clear before the eye-tracking image. (Medium)
7. **Issue 14** — "like a signal." Soft explanatory simile. (Medium)
8. **Issue 1** — "shapes that did not belong to the landscape." Vague placeholder. (Medium)
9. **Issue 3** — "inside it or beneath it or beyond it." Triple-preposition hedge. (Medium)
10. **Issue 4** — "the kind of noise a body makes when breathing requires a decision." Overwritten. (Medium)

## Pattern Summary

Three recurring weaknesses:

1. **Label-then-prove:** Flat verdict sentences ("The climb down was ugly," "bruised, bleeding, and shaking," "Hakiia heard it too") set up images the prose then renders concretely. The label is always weaker than the proof.
2. **Abstract circumlocution at stress points:** When a body or an angle is the subject, the prose reaches for "did not allow for life," "angles legs were meant to make," "what was already shattered." The chapter's concrete images do this work better; the abstractions soften them.
3. **Soft explanatory similes/closures:** "like a signal," "like someone counting," "shapes that did not belong" — moments where the prose interprets instead of trusting the next image.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut throat-clearing topic and recap sentences.** Remove "The climb down was ugly," "He reached the bottom bruised, bleeding, and shaking" → "He reached the bottom," "Hakiia heard it too," and the basin recap "The basin was a mess of broken earth and scattered snow and wreckage."
2. **Tighten abstract circumlocutions.** Replace "did not allow for life" with "broken." Replace "angles were not angles that legs were meant to make" with "the angles were wrong." Fold "what was already shattered" into a tighter split sentence.
3. **Drop soft similes.** Cut "like a signal." Cut "shapes that did not belong to the landscape" or replace with one concrete image. Cut "the kind of noise a body makes when breathing requires a decision" or compress.
4. **Reduce hedge constructions.** "Inside it or beneath it or beyond it" → "inside it." "Below and to the right, muffled by stone and snow and distance" → "below, muffled."
5. **Trim stock survival gestures (Low).** "He did not breathe," "He did not look up," "bright and hot against the cold stone" — leave or trim only if they don't disrupt rhythm.

## Failure Mode Note

This chapter is not failing structurally or tonally. It is a strong scene with a handful of local soft spots — primarily the writer giving the reader a verdict sentence one beat before the image that proves it. Cutting those, and tightening three or four abstract closures, will lift the chapter without altering its shape.
