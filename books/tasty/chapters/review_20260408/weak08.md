# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 8: What Hakiia Asks

Reviewed against `.claude/rules/weak-writing.md`. The book's deliberate stylistic choices (weighted sentences, unmarked Bleed, restrained horror, pre-industrial register, McCarthy/Morrison-leaning prose) are not flagged. Only genuine weakness is flagged.

---

## Per-Issue Entries

### Issue 1
**Quote:** "His body moved the way a wheel turns after the hand that spun it is gone, and the travois dragged behind him through the snow."
**Problem:** Mixed/strained metaphor. A wheel that keeps turning after the hand is gone is a momentum image, but Hurkuzak's body is laboring under load (poles, dragged sled) — the opposite of free spin. The simile fights the literal action.
**Suggested revision:** Replace the wheel image with one that fits forced labor under exhaustion, or trim it: "His body moved on its own. The travois dragged behind him through the snow."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 2
**Quote:** "He had to believe it because the alternative was not something he could carry in addition to everything else."
**Problem:** Wordy, abstract, slightly explanatory. "Not something he could carry in addition to everything else" is a workaround for a simpler, more concrete idea.
**Suggested revision:** "He had to believe it. He could not carry the alternative."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 3
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak stopped. He set down the drag poles and his shoulders screamed with the release of tension and his knees tried to fold and he locked them and stood."
**Problem:** Run-on built on a chain of "and"s that flattens into monotony. Four independent actions strung together with the same conjunction. Loses rhythm.
**Suggested revision:** Break into shorter beats: "Hurkuzak stopped. He set down the drag poles. His shoulders screamed with the release. His knees tried to fold. He locked them and stood."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 4
**Quote:** "Not the drifting, dulled eyes of the last several hours but the sharp, certain eyes that Hurkuzak had known his entire life — the eyes that assessed the world and found it legible."
**Problem:** Slightly overwritten and explanatory. "That assessed the world and found it legible" is the narrator interpreting rather than letting the image work.
**Suggested revision:** Trim the interpretive tail: "Not the drifting, dulled eyes of the last several hours but the sharp, certain eyes Hurkuzak had known his entire life."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 5
**Quote:** "Hakiia looked at the sky for a moment. Then at his legs. Then at Hurkuzak."
**Problem:** "For a moment" is filler. The fragment chain works, but the qualifier weakens the first beat.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia looked at the sky. Then at his legs. Then at Hurkuzak."
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 6
**Quote:** "He said this last part with a flatness that carried more weight than any emphasis could have."
**Problem:** Telling instead of showing, and explaining the emotional weight directly to the reader. The flatness is already on the page in the dialogue; the gloss undercuts it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the line entirely, or reduce to: "He said this last part flat."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 7
**Quote:** "He stood too fast. His bad knee buckled. He caught himself and did not sit back down. He could not sit beside this demand."
**Problem:** "He could not sit beside this demand" is an abstract gloss after concrete action. Mild telling — the staging already carries it.
**Suggested revision:** Cut the final sentence, or rephrase as concrete: "He stayed standing."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 8
**Quote:** "Hakiia watched him settle into the snow and then he spoke again with the same voice he had used for every correction, every hard lesson he had ever delivered."
**Problem:** Long compound sentence with a wordy interpretive tail. "Every correction, every hard lesson he had ever delivered" is the narrator labeling the voice instead of letting the dialogue prove it.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia watched him settle into the snow. Then he spoke in the voice he used for hard lessons."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 9
**Quote:** "I have read my body the way I read any ground, and the ground is against us."
**Problem:** Strong line, but in context the next sentence ("I am dying") repeats what the metaphor already delivered. Slight redundancy, not a kill.
**Suggested revision:** Leave the metaphor; trust it to carry the meaning without the immediate gloss.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 10
**Quote:** "Hakiia closed his eyes for a moment. When he opened them, the sharpness had shifted — not softer, but older, drawing on something deeper than argument."
**Problem:** "For a moment" is filler. "Drawing on something deeper than argument" is abstract and explanatory — narrator interpretation rather than rendered behavior.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia closed his eyes. When he opened them, the sharpness had shifted. Not softer. Older."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 11
**Quote:** "The words settled over Hurkuzak like snowfall."
**Problem:** Cliche-adjacent and a little soft, especially since literal snow is falling around them in this scene. Borrowed image rather than earned one.
**Suggested revision:** "The words settled in him and stayed."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 12
**Quote:** "He thought of the draught. The gentle version of this act. A dying person in a familiar room, surrounded by family, the cup brought with care and received with dignity. That was what the practice looked like when the world was being kind. This — a knife in the snow, a brother with no hands, the cold pressing in from every direction — this was what the practice looked like when it was not."
**Problem:** Strong rhetorical structure but slightly overlong. "Received with dignity" and "when the world was being kind" lean toward summary. The contrast can land harder if compressed.
**Suggested revision:** Trim the abstract phrases; keep the concrete contrast.
**Priority:** Low

### Issue 13
**Quote:** "Hakiia's voice softened, and the softening was worse than the blade because it carried inside it the thing Hurkuzak had felt at the fire — the almost-tenderness, the brief warmth that lived behind the correction."
**Problem:** Long sentence that loses force in a series of explanatory phrases. The narrator interprets the softening rather than letting it land.
**Suggested revision:** "Hakiia's voice softened. The softening was worse than the blade. It carried the almost-tenderness from the fire, the warmth that lived behind the correction."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 14
**Quote:** "The wind moved through the trees and the snow shifted on the branches above them and a small cascade of powder fell through the air and landed on Hakiia's chest and he did not brush it away."
**Problem:** Run-on built on chained "and"s — five clauses in one breath. Loses the silence the moment is trying to create. Monotone rhythm.
**Suggested revision:** "The wind moved through the trees. Snow shifted on the branches. A small cascade of powder fell and landed on Hakiia's chest. He did not brush it away."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 15
**Quote:** "The cold did not care that they were arguing about something that would change everything. The cold had its own work."
**Problem:** "Something that would change everything" is vague and slightly clichéd — the kind of phrase the rest of the chapter avoids. The second sentence is strong; the first weakens it.
**Suggested revision:** "The cold did not care what they were arguing about. The cold had its own work."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 16
**Quote:** "There was truth in it. Not all truth — it was not comfort he was protecting. It was something about who he would be on the other side of this act. But there was enough truth in Hakiia's words that they could not be dismissed. Hakiia knew it. He used that knowledge the way he used everything: precisely."
**Problem:** Paragraph drifts into abstract self-analysis. "Something about who he would be on the other side of this act" is the kind of vague philosophical gesture the chapter otherwise resists. Repeats "truth" three times. Telling.
**Suggested revision:** Compress and concretize: "There was truth in it. Not all of it — what he was protecting was not comfort. But there was enough that he could not dismiss it. Hakiia knew. He used the knowledge the way he used everything: precisely."
**Priority:** High

### Issue 17
**Quote:** "His voice had dropped. The anger had changed into something colder — anger at the situation, at the legs, at the cliff that had broken them and the wolves that had worsened them and the cold that was finishing the work."
**Problem:** Narrator is cataloguing the anger's targets — telling instead of letting the dialogue do it. The list also chains "and"s and slides toward over-explanation.
**Suggested revision:** "His voice had dropped. The anger had changed into something colder."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 18
**Quote:** "Hurkuzak opened his mouth and closed it. The plan did not exist. The cabin was shelter, not rescue. There was no one coming. There was no help within reach. There was only winter and cold and two brothers, one dying, the other being asked to make the dying faster and cleaner."
**Problem:** Anaphora ("There was no... There was no... There was only...") works once, but the passage piles on past the point. "Make the dying faster and cleaner" restates what the dialogue has already established. Saying it twice.
**Suggested revision:** Cut one of the "There was no" lines and trim the closing summary.
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 19
**Quote:** "He could not. The word was in his body like a bone, weight-bearing and immovable. He could not kill his brother."
**Problem:** Repeats "He could not" within three sentences. The middle image is strong; the bookending dilutes it.
**Suggested revision:** "The word was in his body like a bone, weight-bearing and immovable. He could not kill his brother."
**Priority:** Medium

### Issue 20
**Quote:** "Behind him, Hakiia breathed the wrong breathing and said nothing, and the silence between them was no longer the silence of a man who had stopped commanding."
**Problem:** "Breathed the wrong breathing" is an awkward repetition that calls attention to itself without earning it (the earlier "His breathing was wrong" worked better). The closing clause is abstract and elaborate where a sharper image would land harder.
**Suggested revision:** "Behind him, Hakiia's breathing was wrong, and the silence between them was no longer the silence of a man who had stopped commanding."
**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 8 is one of the book's most important scenes and the prose is largely earning its weight. The dialogue is taut, the staging is restrained, and the emotional pressure builds without melodrama. The weaknesses are concentrated in two places: (1) chained "and"-conjunction run-ons that flatten the rhythm of paragraphs that should breathe in shorter beats, and (2) narrator interpretation tacked onto strong concrete moments — small explanatory tails that tell the reader what the scene already shows. There is also a recurring impulse to philosophize at the edges of strong dialogue ("something about who he would be on the other side of this act," "would change everything") that drops the chapter's otherwise concrete register.

## Top 10 Problems Ranked by Impact

1. Run-on chained-"and" sentence in the snowfall paragraph (Issue 14) — flattens a moment that should be silent.
2. Run-on chained-"and" sentence when Hurkuzak first stops (Issue 3) — loses physical specificity at a key beat.
3. Narrator interpretation paragraph after Hakiia's challenge (Issue 16) — vague abstraction in an otherwise concrete chapter.
4. Telling-not-showing gloss "carried more weight than any emphasis could have" (Issue 6) — explains what the line already does.
5. Long interpretive tail on softening voice (Issue 13) — loses force across explanatory phrases.
6. Hakiia's anger catalogued by narrator (Issue 17) — over-explains.
7. "Settled over Hurkuzak like snowfall" cliche-adjacent image (Issue 11) — borrows where it should earn.
8. Wheel-after-hand metaphor fights the action (Issue 1) — strained simile.
9. "He could not sit beside this demand" abstract gloss (Issue 7) — narration after staging already carried the meaning.
10. "Breathed the wrong breathing" awkward repetition (Issue 20) — calls attention to itself.

## Pattern Summary

Two dominant patterns:

- **Chained "and"-conjunction run-ons.** When the writer wants to pile sensation, the default is to attach clauses with "and" until the rhythm flattens. Most of the chapter avoids this, but it surfaces at three or four important beats and weakens them.
- **Interpretive tails on strong sentences.** The chapter has a habit of writing one excellent concrete sentence and then attaching a phrase that tells the reader what it meant. These tails lean abstract ("found it legible," "changed everything," "carried more weight than any emphasis could have," "drawing on something deeper than argument"). The fix is almost always to cut the tail.

A minor third pattern: filler qualifiers ("for a moment") and repeated key words ("truth," "could not," "the dying") in tight passages.

## Revision Plan in Priority Order

1. **High priority — fix the run-ons.** Break the snowfall paragraph (Issue 14) and the "stopped" paragraph (Issue 3) into shorter beats. Trust the period.
2. **High priority — cut the interpretive tails.** Remove the gloss on flatness (Issue 6), trim the softening sentence (Issue 13), and compress the "truth in it" paragraph (Issue 16).
3. **Medium priority — concrete over abstract.** Replace the wheel simile (Issue 1), the "alternative he could not carry" (Issue 2), the "could not sit beside this demand" (Issue 7), the snowfall cliche (Issue 11), and the cold-doesn't-care line (Issue 15).
4. **Medium priority — trim filler and repetition.** "For a moment" cuts (Issue 10), the cataloguing of anger (Issue 17), the duplicated "could not" (Issue 19), the "breathed the wrong breathing" (Issue 20), and the over-listed "There was no" anaphora (Issue 18). Trim Issue 4 and Issue 8 for the same reason.
5. **Low priority — polish.** Issues 5, 9, and 12 — small filler and minor redundancy that can be addressed in a final read but are not load-bearing.
