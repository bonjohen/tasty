# Weak-Writing Review — Chapter 34: The Professional

## Per-Issue Findings

### 1. Redundant explanation (High)
**Quote:** "He was walking into it. He did not know he was walking into it."
**Problem:** The second sentence restates the first with negation; the irony is already on the page.
**Revision:** Cut the second sentence, or compress to "He was walking into it, blind."
Priority: High

### 2. Throat-clearing/over-explanation (Medium)
**Quote:** "Vark crouched beside the dead fire and read the camp the way she read every camp — with her hands, her nose, her weight settling into the ground beside the tracks."
**Problem:** "the way she read every camp" is filler before the concrete list arrives. The list itself is the show.
**Revision:** "Vark crouched beside the dead fire and read it with her hands, her nose, her weight settling into the ground beside the tracks."
Priority: Medium

### 3. Telling after showing (High)
**Quote:** "the foot placement showed the confidence of experienced mountain hunters, confidence this man had not been born with and had acquired from the thing inside him."
**Problem:** The clause after the comma explains what the prior clause already implies; dilutes the menace with thesis statement.
**Revision:** End at "experienced mountain hunters." Trust the reader.
Priority: High

### 4. Wordy abstraction (Medium)
**Quote:** "She was not chasing. She was shaping."
**Problem:** Strong, keep — but the surrounding paragraph then re-explains it: "Let him walk where he wanted to walk. Ensure that where he wanted to walk led where she needed him to arrive." The chiasmus is clever but says the same thing twice.
**Revision:** Cut to one beat: "Let him walk where he wanted. Ensure it led where she needed."
Priority: Medium

### 5. Restated thesis (High)
**Quote:** "An ordinary buyer wanted meat. A sophisticated buyer wanted specific tissues, properly preserved. The principal wanted a living man, and what lived in him, and was willing to pay a sum that told her she did not need to understand why."
**Problem:** The prior paragraph already established the principal wants the quarry intact; this restates with rhetorical scaffolding.
**Revision:** Trim the first two sentences; keep the third.
Priority: High (paragraph-level cut)

### 6. Over-explained metaphor (Medium)
**Quote:** "the pressures accumulated the way snow accumulates on a slope — each addition imperceptible, until the weight makes sliding in one direction inevitable."
**Problem:** The simile is good; the gloss after the dash explains the simile.
**Revision:** "the pressures accumulated the way snow accumulates on a slope."
Priority: Medium

### 7. Authorial thesis intrusion (High)
**Quote:** "Not a cordon, which was too obvious for a quarry with transferred tactical instincts, but presence."
**Problem:** "transferred tactical instincts" is clinical/expository; reads like author note. Vark's earlier observation already conveyed it.
**Revision:** "Not a cordon — too obvious — but presence."
Priority: High

### 8. Redundant pair (Medium)
**Quote:** "She had built the architecture with redundancy, understanding that a sharp quarry finds and avoids the obvious snares. The real snare is the thing they choose instead."
**Problem:** "understanding that..." is exposition; the aphorism that follows does the work alone.
**Revision:** "She had built the architecture with redundancy. The real snare is the thing they choose instead."
Priority: Medium

### 9. Telling emotion / summary (High)
**Quote:** "She was not underestimating him. She had watched him fight — the bandit ambush, the public eruption. She had seen how fast the hands moved, the way the hesitation emptied from his face in the instant before contact. But dangerous was not uncatchable."
**Problem:** "She was not underestimating him" announces the paragraph's claim before showing it. Cut the topic sentence; let the evidence land first.
**Revision:** Drop the opening sentence.
Priority: High

### 10. Repetition of "He was no longer running. He was going somewhere." (Medium)
**Quote:** "Something had reorganized his direction and given him purpose. He was no longer running. He was going somewhere."
**Problem:** Three sentences for one observation. The first sentence is abstract ("reorganized his direction"); the next two restate.
**Revision:** "He was no longer running. He was going somewhere."
Priority: Medium

### 11. Filler triplet (Medium)
**Quote:** "She ate. The meal was demon flesh — a strip from her kit, unremarkable. She checked her crossbow. She checked her restraints. She checked the compound's gate mechanism twice."
**Problem:** Strong rhythm — keep. But "The meal was demon flesh — a strip from her kit, unremarkable" is a sentence whose payload is "unremarkable," which undercuts itself.
**Revision:** "She ate a strip of demon flesh from her kit." Keep the checks.
Priority: Low–Medium

### 12. Abstract gloss (Medium)
**Quote:** "She was there to ensure the system performed as designed and to make the corrections that all systems require when plan meets ground."
**Problem:** "the corrections that all systems require when plan meets ground" is generalized aphorism that flattens the specificity.
**Revision:** "She was there to make the corrections plan and ground always require."
Priority: Medium

### 13. Closing redundancy (High)
**Quote:** "He would come tomorrow. The trail said so. His pace said so. She had built the ground to receive him. The ground was ready. She was ready."
**Problem:** "The ground was ready. She was ready." is a verbal flourish that restates "She had built the ground to receive him."
**Revision:** Cut "The ground was ready."
Priority: Medium

### 14. Final-line ambiguous pronoun (Medium)
**Quote:** "The walking was the last free thing he would do for a long time. Neither troubled her sleep."
**Problem:** "Neither" has no clear plural antecedent — the reader has to reverse-engineer "his loss of freedom" + "her role in it." Strong intent, weak grammar.
**Revision:** "The walking was the last free thing he would do for a long time. It did not trouble her sleep. Neither did the rest."
Priority: Medium

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 34 is structurally tight and tonally on-register for Vark — cold, geometric, professional. The dominant weakness is **explanatory tailing**: strong concrete observations are followed by clauses that translate them into thesis. The chapter trusts its imagery less than it should. Cut the explanatory tails and the prose sharpens immediately. No voice problems, no continuity issues, no register slips.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is the **explanatory tail**: a concrete image, then a comma or new sentence that explains what the image means. The voice is otherwise disciplined; the imagery is precise. The fix is consistent — cut the gloss, trust the image.
