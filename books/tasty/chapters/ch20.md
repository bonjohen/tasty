Chapter 20: Rarity

Two men on the road, both injured, both mobile but diminished. One with a wrist injury consistent with a joint lock that wrenched the tendons without completing the break — the strength to break, and the choice not to. One with cracked ribs, curled on the roadside, breathing in the careful pattern of a man who had discovered his thorax had new limits. A club on the ground, dropped. A knife with the man's blood on it — he had caught the blade. Metal through the palm as an acceptable trade for control of the weapon.

She interviewed the traders. The man was eager to talk. The woman was cautious. The woman had seen display fights between flesh-fed soldiers in the eastern cities. This was faster than she expected from a man so plainly drawn — thin-faced, poorly dressed, carrying a blanket bundle on his back like a refugee.

"His hands," the woman said. "When he moved, his hands knew what they were doing before the rest of him did. Like the hands belonged to someone else."

She examined the blood on the knife. Fresh, several hours old, already darkening at the edges in the spring air. She touched it, brought her finger to her nose. The scent was wrong — not the standard iron-and-salt smell of human blood, and not the slightly denser, slightly sharper smell of demon blood as she normally encountered it.

She spoke to the two injured men. The one with the wrist was cooperative in the way that injured people who want sympathy are cooperative — eager to explain that they had been wronged by a creature that was not natural, that moved in ways that no ordinary man moved, and that the three of them had been simply defending the road. Vark let the narrative play itself out. The facts were in the ground, not in the telling.

The third man — the one who had run — she found at a campsite a mile west, nursing his courage with alcohol stolen from a cache he had hidden in the rocks. He was more honest, or more afraid. He had seen the traveler's face. Young, but worn in ways that did not match — lines around the eyes, a hollowness in the cheeks that belonged to longer years. The eyes had assessed the three of them the way a wolf assesses prey, the calculation complete before any of them realized it had begun.

Everyone who had seen the man described the same thing: the body did not match what the body did.

She followed the trail east from the scene of the fight. The quarry had taken the road for a quarter mile, moving fast — the stride length extended, the pace aggressive. Then the trail left the road and entered the tree line, and the change was immediate: the pace slowed, the movement became deliberate and tactical, the path chose cover and concealment over speed. Professional.

She tracked him for two days.

The tracking was the most informative thing she had done since beginning the pursuit. The quarry moved through the forest with the skill set she had identified at the first campsite, now operating at full capacity rather than the relaxed travel mode of the earlier track. He changed direction twice to test for pursuit, doubling back on his own trail in a pattern that would have confused an ordinary tracker and that cost Vark an hour the first time and twenty minutes the second. He crossed water three times — not at fords but at points where the current was strongest and the bottom was rocky, choosing difficulty over detection. He camped without fire, ate without cooking, and slept — if he slept at all — in positions that left no compression marks she could find.

He was good. Better than standard quarry. Better than most professional fugitives she had tracked. And the skill carried the same quality she had noticed at the first track: learned rather than inherent. The movements were correct, but the corrections were recent, as though the knowledge had been acquired, not grown. The hands knew what they were doing before the rest of him did. The trader's wife had seen it clearly. The capabilities had been grafted on. They were running in a body that had not grown them.

On the second afternoon, she closed the distance.

She had been maintaining a gap of four hours — close enough to track reliably, far enough to avoid detection by senses she now knew were keener than a man's should be. But the quarry had made a mistake. He had stopped to drink at a stream and stayed too long — injury, exhaustion, or a lapse in the discipline that had kept him hidden. She used the lapse. She moved to a position above and behind him, on a ridge that offered a clear line of sight to the stream below, and she assessed.

He crouched at the water's edge. She could see his face in half-view — the thin face, the strong frame, the posture too settled and balanced for that frame, the stillness of a man who was listening to the forest the way she listened to the forest. His left hand rested in the water. His right — the one that had caught the knife — was wrapped in cloth. He unwound the cloth. She saw the cut, and she saw what the cut was doing.

It was healing.

The edges of the wound sat closer together than a two-day-old palm laceration permitted. The tissue was pink and healthy where it should have been inflamed and seeping. The rate of closure exceeded any demon capability she had observed in fifteen years of fieldwork.

She loaded the crossbow.

She chose the outer thigh. She sighted. She exhaled. She released.

The bolt struck exactly where she intended. He dropped from the crouch to a roll faster than the pain should have allowed — reflexes ahead of the signals. He came up facing her direction. Not her position, but the correct quarter.

He ran. The bolt was in his thigh, and he ran with it, and the gait adjustment he made for the wound was minimal. The body treated the injury as a variable, not a crisis.

She descended to the stream and examined the place where he had been crouching. Blood. She touched it, smelled it. The same wrong-right scent from the knife. She collected a small amount in a vial from her kit — standard practice for high-value kills — and sealed it.

She examined the tracks of his departure. The stride was shorter than normal — the wound was affecting his gait — but the pace was high and the route selection was still tactical. He was heading for dense cover, for terrain that would make crossbow follow-up shots difficult.

He was bleeding less than the wound should have produced. Not bandaged — she could see from the tracks he had not stopped. The body was closing the wound on its own, and she could measure the rate from the diminishing drops.

At the current rate, the bolt wound would be closed within a day. A standard demon would require three to five. An unaugmented human, weeks.

She watched him disappear into the trees. Her sight carried further than it should have. She tracked the diminishing figure — the limp evening out, the gait adjusting, the body managing the wound. She noted the time. She would check the stream site again in an hour, count the blood drops, measure the trail, and have a reckoning of his rate by nightfall.

She let him go.

A live kill of this quality was worth more than a dead one — how much more, she could not yet say. Killing him now would yield organs and tissue that would command impressive prices. The blood alone, with its mixed scent, would interest the kind of buyers who paid for what had never been seen before. But alive, studied, and eventually delivered to the right buyer, he was worth more than her last ten kills combined.

She would need to be more careful. He knew he was being hunted now. His response — the immediate tactical flight, the terrain selection, the controlled management of the wound — told her that running him down would require her full professional attention.

She sat beside the stream and thought about markets. The flesh trade had its grades, and at the top — where only wealth that does not name a price could reach — sat the things still without a name. Whatever this man carried in his flesh had no category in her experience. No established price. No precedent she could put a number to. The buyers who wanted that kind of proof — that a thing they had theorized about for generations was real, was stable, walked and bled and healed — would pay whatever it cost.

She cleaned her crossbow. She packed the blood vial. She noted the direction of his flight and the diminishing blood trail that confirmed her reckoning. She would follow at distance, test again, build a full accounting of what he was. When it was complete she would contact Koss, and the price she named would reflect rarity.

Vark walked. The forest was quiet. The quarry was ahead, wounded and running. She was behind, patient and certain, and the distance between them was a number that would reach zero.
