Chapter 3: The Distance Between Them

They broke camp before dawn. Hakiia had the fire killed and the sled repacked while the sky was still black, moving through the work with the unhurried certainty of a man who had known since the night before what the morning would require. Hurkuzak's hands were clumsy with cold. He fumbled with the dogs' harnesses, pulling straps tight and then tighter when he felt Hakiia's eyes on him, and the dogs stood patient under his attention.

The terrain changed within the first hour. The forest thickened, then gave way to a long slope of exposed rock and ice where the snow had been scoured thin by wind and the ground underneath was slick. The dogs struggled for footing. The sled fishtailed on the patches of bare stone and the runners scraped a sound Hurkuzak felt in his teeth. Hakiia walked ahead, picking the line, and Hurkuzak followed with one hand on the sled rail and the other braced against whatever the ground offered — a rock, a frozen stump, the bark of a tree bent sideways by wind.

The slope steepened. The dogs dropped their heads and hauled, and Hurkuzak leaned into the sled from behind and pushed until the muscles in his legs burned and his breathing became something he could hear over the wind. Ahead of him, Hakiia's pace did not change.

At the crest the wind hit them full. It came from the north with nothing between its origin and their faces but open sky, and the cold inside it was a different thing from the cold they had been carrying. This was cold that moved. It found the gaps in Hurkuzak's wrappings and pressed through. The dogs curled their tails under and tucked their ears and kept pulling.

Hurkuzak looked at the terrain ahead and saw that the trail Hakiia was following ran along the ridgeline, exposed and bare, for what looked like half a league before descending back into tree cover. Below them, to the left, the forest continued in a sheltered hollow where the wind would break against the trunks and the snow would be deeper but softer. The dogs would move more easily there. The sled would ride better.

He said nothing for a while.

“The trees would be easier on the dogs.”

Hakiia did not stop walking. “The ridgeline is faster.”

“The dogs are working harder up here. The surface is wrong for the runners.”

“The dogs will manage.”

“The left rear is still favoring the pad I wrapped last night. If she splits it again up here she'll bleed through the binding and we'll have to stop.”

Hakiia turned his head. Not all the way. Just enough to show that the comment had registered, which was more than most of Hurkuzak's observations received. He looked at the ridgeline ahead, then at the low ground below, and then forward again. He did not change course.

They took the ridge. The wind battered them for the full distance. The dogs worked twice as hard as they would have in the trees, and by the time the trail dropped back into cover the left rear was limping badly and Hurkuzak had to stop and rewrap the pad with numb fingers while the dog stood trembling and Hakiia stood watching, saying nothing.

Hurkuzak did not say anything about it either. There was no gain in being right about the dog. If she was hurting, it was because the dog was weak, or the terrain was unlucky, or Hurkuzak had wrapped the pad poorly in the first place. That was how Hakiia arranged things in his head. The route itself would never be the error.

They descended into the valley and the trees closed around them again and the wind died to something bearable. The dogs settled into their pace. The sled found its rhythm. Hurkuzak walked behind and watched his brother's back and felt the weather of being near him settle on him again.

It was past midday when Hakiia stopped again. They had been climbing steadily since the valley floor, the trail switching back and forth through heavy timber, and the air had grown thin enough that Hurkuzak's lungs worked harder than his legs. Hakiia built a small fire — more for the dogs than for themselves, though he would not have said so — and they sat across from each other on fallen logs and ate dried meat and drank snowmelt that tasted of nothing.

Hakiia ate without pleasure, the way a man oils a blade because neglect is stupid, not because the blade deserves care. Hurkuzak ate slowly. The meat was tough and salted and his jaw ached from the cold.

“Father would have taken the trees,” Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak looked up. Hakiia was staring into the fire. His face was angular, composed, harder than the wood it was lit by. But something in his voice had shifted.

“He would have taken the trees,” Hakiia said again. “And he would have been right about the dogs and wrong about the rest.”

“Wrong about what?”

“About what the extra hour costs. You save the dogs, you lose the daylight, you camp in a worse position, you eat into the supplies. Father always thought the gentle choice was free. It never is.” He looked at the fire. “He was a kind man. Kind men can afford kindness when the world is being kind back. When the world stops, they break.”

Hurkuzak knew this ground. Hakiia returned to it the way a man returns to a wound, pressing the bruise to see if it still hurts.

“He didn't break,” Hurkuzak said. “He died.”

“He died because he broke. Because when the hard thing was in front of him he looked for the gentle way around it and the gentle way was not there and he did not have a second answer.” Hakiia's voice was flat. Not cruel, exactly. Certain. The way a man is certain about weather or stone. “He should have asked for the draught. Any man would have asked. Mother would have given it — that is what it is for. Instead he held on and made them watch him go by pieces.” He broke a stick and fed half of it to the fire. “Never live so long that you become a burden. That is what the old ones said. Father knew the words. He could not do the thing.”

Hurkuzak said nothing. He did not look up from the fire.

“I loved him,” Hakiia said. “But he was wrong about what the world costs.”

“And the lesson was what?”

Hakiia looked at him across the fire. The look had weight.

“The lesson,” Hakiia said, “is that you are exactly like him. You have his hands. His hesitation. His way of looking at a dying animal and feeling something for it before you finish the work. And if you stay this way — if nobody teaches you what the world does to people who flinch — you will die the way he died. Not old. Not ready. Just surprised.”

Hurkuzak sat with the heat of the fire on his face and the cold at his back. Hakiia stated. He corrected. He declared what the world was and then treated disagreement as evidence of the weakness he had just diagnosed.

“You think strength is one thing,” Hurkuzak said. He said it quietly. “You think it looks like you. Sounds like you. Moves like you. And anything that looks different is just weakness waiting to get someone killed.”

Hakiia's expression did not change.

“The dog,” Hurkuzak said. “I told you about the trees and the dog. You took the ridge. The dog is bleeding through the wrap. You were wrong about the route and you'll never know it because you don't track that kind of cost. You track speed and distance and position and those are the things that matter to you and so those are the only things that are real.” He paused. The fire cracked. “Father noticed things too. Maybe they weren't the things that would have saved him. But pretending they're nothing — pretending that seeing is the same as flinching — that's not strength. That's a man who has walled off everything he cannot use.”

Hakiia looked at him.

Then Hakiia looked away. He looked at the fire, and whatever he decided stayed inside him.

“We'll reach the cabin by tomorrow if the weather holds.”

That was all. He stood and kicked snow onto the fire and began repacking the sled.

The weather did not hold.

They walked through the afternoon under a sky that never opened but seemed to lower by degrees, gray sinking toward them until the peaks disappeared and the valley lost its far edges. The trail narrowed and climbed and the trees grew smaller and more twisted with altitude. The snow was deep here, undisturbed at first, and the dogs worked through it. Then the flakes changed.

The first snow was light and dry enough to drift off the shoulders of their coats. The second came fatter, wetter, sticking where it landed. Before long the air was full of it — wet snow blown sideways by a wind that had found them again. It clung to fur, to lashes, to the dogs' backs. It gathered in the folds of Hurkuzak's wrappings and melted there under the trapped heat of his body, then turned cold again as the wind pressed through.

The change was worse than the earlier cold. Cold could be fought with movement. Dry snow could be brushed away. This soaked. It crept in. The gloves grew heavy. The leather ties swelled. Water ran down the sled lashings and glazed over again. Hurkuzak could feel the dampness collecting between his shoulder blades and under his arms and around his waist where his layers were pressed tight by the work.

The dogs felt it too. Their coats darkened and clumped. The left rear dog, already favoring the wrapped pad, began to lag each time the trail steepened. Twice Hurkuzak had to put both hands on the sled and take more of its weight because the team had lost its rhythm on the slickening ground. Once the runners skidded sideways into a half-buried stone and nearly rolled the load.

Hakiia noticed the stumble. He corrected the line, shortened the dogs with a word, and kept moving.

Near evening, when the snow thickened enough that distance became guesswork, Hakiia pointed. Hurkuzak followed the gesture and, through the shifting veil of wet white, saw where the valley narrowed and the tree line bent against a long slope of exposed granite.

“The cabin is on the other side of that.”

The cabin. Still distant — a day's travel, maybe more now, if the snow kept falling like this and the wind continued to drive it into drifts — but real. A destination that until now had been only a word Hakiia used to justify another hour of walking.

“There,” Hakiia said. Nothing else. He did not describe it.

By the time they chose a place to camp, both men were wet beneath their outer layers. The hollow they found was good by the standards the mountain allowed — dense trees, some break from the wind, enough cover to keep the sled from icing over completely — but it was not good enough. The snow still found them in gusts. The wind still came through the branches in sudden knives.

Hakiia built the fire. That took longer than it should have. The gathered wood was snow-packed and damp under the bark, and even the deadfall that looked dry had taken on hidden moisture from the storm. Hurkuzak split what he could while Hakiia worked the tinder with the stubborn patience he gave everything, and when flame finally caught it did so reluctantly, a weak orange life that needed feeding every few breaths before it would stand on its own.

The dogs huddled close to the heat. Steam lifted from their coats in faint threads. Hurkuzak knelt by them and began the work of making them survivable for the night.

He checked the left rear dog last. The pad had bled through the wrapping, as he had known it would, and now the wet had made everything worse. Blood, melted snow, and slush had frozen together in the binding so that the hide sat stiff around the paw like a shell. He melted snow in his cupped hands and held the warmth against the dog's foot until the ice softened and released. Then he cleaned the wound and wrapped it again with fresh hide, tighter this time, with a layer of rendered fat beneath the binding to resist the wet. He worked more carefully than he had that morning. Wet cold found mistakes faster than dry cold did.

He went over the others after that, though they needed less from him. He rubbed them down with handfuls of dry grass pulled from under the lee side of a fallen trunk, trying to wick moisture from their fur before it could freeze deeper into them. He adjusted the harnesses. He checked feet. He ran his hands along their backs and flanks and legs looking for heat that meant swelling or the flinch that meant strain. He spent longer with them than necessary because the weather made necessary a larger thing.

When he returned to the fire, Hakiia was already wrapped in his furs and looking at the sky through the gap in the canopy where the smoke rose and then flattened under the low clouds instead of disappearing. His brother's face in firelight had always been the same — hard, certain. But tonight there was something underneath the certainty. Not rest. Not the easy blankness of a day completed. Calculation, perhaps. Or the knowledge that his promise about tomorrow had become less true with every wet gust.

They ate quickly because the food was stiff with cold and there was nothing to be gained by lingering. Hurkuzak could feel the damp in his sleeves even this close to the fire. Every time the wind shifted it found the wet places in his clothing and pressed them into his skin. His feet had gone beyond cold into the numb heaviness that warned of trouble if it lasted too long.

He thought of their father. A kind man. Hakiia had said it without irony. Hakiia looked at their father and saw a warning. Hurkuzak looked at their father and saw what was worth the cost.

He did not know what to do with the space between those two things.

Hakiia slept first, as he always did. One breath he was there. The next he was somewhere else, breathing slowly, his body relaxing into the ground with the trust of a man who had never been attacked in his sleep and could not imagine why anyone would be.

Hurkuzak lay awake and watched his brother in the firelight. Hakiia's face was different in sleep — not softer, exactly, but unclenched. The line between his brows was gone. His jaw had loosened. He looked, in sleep, like someone who might have been easy to love if the world had not made him the way it did.

Outside the ring of firelight, the storm kept working. Wet snow hissed into the branches and slid from them in heavy little falls. The wind never settled. It worried at the shelter, reached through seams in cloth and fur, and turned every patch of dampness into a place of concentrated misery. Hurkuzak fed the fire twice in the night, not because it was low but because he was afraid to let it become low. He shifted the drying things closer and then farther back. Nothing truly dried. It only became less wet for a little while.

Shield and wound. Walking ahead through the snow.

Hurkuzak closed his eyes.

The fire burned low. The dogs breathed. The wet cold continued.
