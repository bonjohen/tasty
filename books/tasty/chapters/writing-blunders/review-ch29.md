## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> The voice stirred. Not as a word. As a dense attention behind his eyes, the way a sound barely audible becomes loud when the listener recognizes what it is. The presence knew these objects. Not as ideas. As things it had touched.

**Problem:** Narration interprets before the reader can experience.

**Why it weakens:** The phrase "The presence knew these objects" is direct authorial declaration. The Bleed's rules in `book/CLAUDE.md` require the voice to enter Hurkuzak's narration unmarked — the reader must feel the boundary dissolve, not be told it has. Here the narrator steps outside Hurkuzak's perspective to deliver a fact about the Bleed's interiority. It names the phenomenon and explains it ("Not as ideas. As things it had touched") before the reader has had a chance to experience unease. The simile ("the way a sound barely audible becomes loud") is also too clarifying — it domesticates something that should feel wrong.

**Revision direction:** Cut the explanation. Render the reaction somatically through Hurkuzak's body without labeling it as the Bleed's response. Let the reader feel that something besides Hurkuzak is reacting, without the narrator confirming it.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> The voice was no longer stirring. It was pressing. A weight behind his eyes, in his chest, at the base of his skull. A hand pushing against the back of his eyes. Not words. The presence was a man watching his own diary read aloud in a room full of strangers. Not commentary. Panic.

**Problem:** The Bleed's internal state is named and interpreted — premature revelation of what Hakiia's presence knows and feels.

**Why it weakens:** This passage explains Hakiia's emotional experience to the reader. "The presence was a man watching his own diary read aloud in a room full of strangers" is an interpretive simile that identifies Hakiia as an aware, reactive subject who has something to hide. It confirms that the Bleed understands what the materials mean and is experiencing something like shame or alarm. This is information the reader should not receive this cleanly at this moment. The outline calls for Hurkuzak's comprehension to lag behind the reader's, not for the narration to catch up by labeling the Bleed's inner state. The single word "Panic" is the worst offender — it is a direct emotional declaration from a narrator who should be withholding.

**Revision direction:** Replace the interpretive simile and "Panic" with physical sensation only. The Bleed should not be legible here — it should be pressure that has no name. The reader will do the interpretive work if the sensation is right.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> He turned another page and the page was about siblings.

**Problem:** Premature and telegraphed reveal — the sentence announces its own importance.

**Why it weakens:** This sentence functions as a chapter-title announcement. It tells the reader "the important thing is coming now." The section outline's instruction is to let the reader reach the implication before Hurkuzak does, creating dread. Instead, this sentence creates dramatic fanfare. Hurkuzak would not experience the page as "about siblings" in a single glance — he would read it and feel something before he could frame what it meant. The bluntness also collapses the gathering dread of the previous pages. Everything before has been accumulation; this sentence cashes it in too directly.

**Revision direction:** Remove the heading-style announcement. Let Hurkuzak notice the shift in the writing's register — the compression, the urgency — before he understands what the content means. The subject of siblings should emerge from the notation itself, not from a framing sentence.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> The notation changed. The clinical distance compressed into something urgent. The diagrams became specific to a pairing — two related bodies, shared lineage, overlapping physiological signatures. The notes described what happened when the consumption occurred between siblings: not merely physical properties but reflexive memory, instinctual patterns, fragments of identity. The word used was not transfer. The word was inheritance.

**Problem:** The narrator explains what the documents say rather than letting Hurkuzak read them.

**Why it weakens:** This paragraph is a summary delivered by an omniscient narrator, not a POV experience. Hurkuzak is ostensibly reading documents, but we get a clean, organized précis of their contents: here is the shift in register, here is what the diagrams show, here is the key terminological distinction. The outline asks for the reader to reach the implication before Hurkuzak does. Instead, the narrator delivers the implication fully formed and labeled. "The word used was not transfer. The word was inheritance" is the reveal arriving in the form of a thesis statement, not a discovered fact. The reader has no work to do.

**Revision direction:** Show Hurkuzak reading — specific details he encounters on the page, physical reactions he cannot explain, fragments of notation he only partially grasps. Let "inheritance" appear in the text as a word he reads and sets aside, not as a word the narrator selects and highlights for the reader.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> The word vessel had a meaning in this context that Hurkuzak's mind was approaching the way a man approaches a cliff edge, knowing the drop is there.

**Problem:** Subtext stated directly — the approaching-a-cliff simile explains the moment instead of delivering it.

**Why it weakens:** This is one of the chapter's most important beats: Hurkuzak is on the verge of understanding that he was the intended subject of Hakiia's ritual. The simile is precise and evocative, but it interprets his psychological state rather than rendering it. By telling us he is "approaching" the knowledge and "knowing the drop is there," the narration confirms that the reader's inference is correct and that Hurkuzak is nearly there. It explains the emotional experience of proximity to a terrible truth instead of immersing the reader in that experience. The cliff simile also echoes the literal cliff, which is meaningful but here feels applied rather than felt.

**Revision direction:** Let the weight of "vessel" fall without commentary. Show what Hurkuzak does after reading it — puts the paper down, picks it up — which the next sentence actually does, but the simile robs that physical beat of its force by pre-explaining it.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> He put the papers down. He picked them up.

**Problem:** Strong physical beat that is too brief and therefore underdeveloped given its structural importance.

**Why it weakens:** This is the closest the chapter comes to showing Hurkuzak's inner state through action rather than narration — the compulsive return to what is unbearable. But following the over-explained "approaching a cliff edge" simile, it reads as a brief physical notation rather than a fully realized beat. The outline calls for the reader to feel Hurkuzak's body knowing before his mind does. Two sentences capture the gesture but do not give it room to accumulate.

**Revision direction:** Expand slightly. Let the putting-down and picking-up be physically specific — where does he look when the papers are down, what does the pause feel like, what does he see in the ravine below or the sky above. The gesture deserves space.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> He gathered the materials — the ceramic containers, the document tube, the loose pages — and held them against his chest the way he had held Hakiia against the travois, with the same grim obligation. He climbed out of the ravine. The mud gave way under his feet. His hands found holds in the eroded earth and he pulled himself upward with strength that belonged to the brother who had sealed these things against wet and cold and carried them into the mountains, and the pulling felt like the sled going over the edge — the same sick lurch of ground becoming air.

**Problem:** Over-explained parallel — the structural echo of carrying Hakiia is interpreted for the reader instead of rendered.

**Why it weakens:** The parallel between carrying Hakiia and carrying the materials is thematically right. But "with the same grim obligation" names the emotional register directly, and "the pulling felt like the sled going over the edge — the same sick lurch of ground becoming air" goes further: it delivers the theme (this is a second fall) as narration, not experience. The reader should feel the echo without being told that the echo is there and what it means. The phrase "strength that belonged to the brother" also crosses the Bleed boundary in a labeled way — the narrator explicitly attributes Hurkuzak's physical capability to Hakiia's inheritance.

**Revision direction:** Trust the physical action to carry the parallel. Cut or compress "the same sick lurch of ground becoming air" — the detail of climbing out of the place where the sled fell, carrying things the dead man hid, is enough.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> The explanation was a door, and the door was opening, and behind the door was not darkness but clarity. The clarity was worse.

**Problem:** The ending over-literalizes its own metaphor.

**Why it weakens:** "The explanation was a door, and the door was opening" is a functional image. "Behind the door was not darkness but clarity. The clarity was worse." delivers an epigram — a neatly inverted paradox that sounds like a conclusion. The section outline calls for an ending that feels like a door opening onto something terrible, with Hurkuzak about to understand what Hakiia brought on this trip. The epigram closes the moment instead of leaving it open. The reader leaves the chapter with a packaged thought, not a felt dread. It is also slightly too self-aware — "The clarity was worse" is a line that knows it is a good last line.

**Revision direction:** End on image or sensation, not paradox. Let the reader sit with Hurkuzak and the spreading materials and the late light without a closing epigram. The last image in the outline — Hurkuzak holding evidence without understanding it fully, a door opening — is the right structure; it just needs to stop before the narrator caps it.

**Priority: High**

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> The physician's jars in Merrath. The notation on the parcels. The grading language, the preparation methods, the specificity of which tissues produced which effects. The list in his hands spoke the same tongue. Not the same words — Hakiia's notes came before the merchants had formalized their language, before the trade had settled into its current terms — but close enough to belong to the same knowledge. Cousins, not strangers.

**Problem:** Lore clarification inserted where uncertainty would be stronger — the narrator explains the historical/linguistic relationship between Hakiia's notes and the flesh trade.

**Why it weakens:** The information that Hakiia's notes predate the merchants' formalized language is historical context that Hurkuzak has no way to know. This is the narrator providing lore to confirm the connection between Hakiia and the flesh trade, where Hurkuzak should be forming a disturbing association he cannot fully explain. "Cousins, not strangers" is a tidy epigrammatic capper. The passage explains too much about the flesh economy's history in order to make the connection explicit. The connection should be felt — something in the vocabulary, the method, the precision — not explained as a matter of chronology.

**Revision direction:** Remove the historical clarification ("Hakiia's notes came before the merchants had formalized their language"). Keep the recognition as a felt similarity, not a genealogy. Trust the accumulated details — the preparation methods, the specificity — to create the association.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> The voice inside him had gone still — not absent but held, the way breath is held before something breaks. Whether it was grief or the desperate energy of something caught and unable to flee, he could not tell. Hakiia had never been readable, alive or dead or discovered.

**Problem:** The Bleed's absence is interpreted and then named with alternatives; the final sentence explains the central ambiguity instead of inhabiting it.

**Why it weakens:** "Whether it was grief or the desperate energy of something caught and unable to flee" presents two labeled options for what the Bleed's stillness might mean. Offering named alternatives ("grief" vs. "something caught and unable to flee") is a form of explanation — it presents the ambiguity as a menu rather than allowing the reader to experience genuine uncertainty. "Hakiia had never been readable, alive or dead or discovered" then explains why the ambiguity exists. This is the narrator stepping back and delivering a thematic statement about Hakiia's character, at a moment when the prose should be doing everything except standing back.

**Revision direction:** Cut the two-option structure. Cut the thematic capper. Let the stillness be just stillness — held, pressured, present. Let Hurkuzak not interpret it.

**Priority: Medium**

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 29 is doing most of its structural work correctly. The pacing of discovery is sound, the physical detail of the wreck is strong, and the accumulation from personal effects to the cache to the documents follows the outline's logic faithfully. The prose at the sentence level is frequently precise and atmospheric.

The chapter's primary failure is at the boundary between experience and interpretation. Again and again — in the Bleed passages, in the document-reading passages, in the closing paragraphs — the narrator moves from rendering to explaining. It names what the reader should feel (panic, grief, dread), it delivers thematic conclusions as epigrams, and it identifies the structural parallels (the cliff, the travois) rather than trusting the reader to feel them. The result is a chapter that performs its emotional beats rather than landing them.

The second failure is Bleed discipline. The story rules require the Bleed to enter Hurkuzak's narration unmarked. Several passages instead report on the Bleed as an observed phenomenon — "the presence knew these objects," "the presence was a man watching his own diary" — breaking the POV in a way that clarifies what should remain disturbing.

The reveals arrive in clean, legible form where they should arrive as accumulated sensation.

## Pattern Summary

The writer's recurring weakness in this chapter is **interpretive impatience**: the prose builds sensation and detail correctly, then cannot hold the tension without labeling it. This manifests as:

- Epigrams deployed at moments of maximum pressure ("Panic," "The clarity was worse," "Cousins, not strangers")
- Similes that explain emotional states rather than render them
- The Bleed voice described from outside rather than experienced from within
- Summary narration replacing POV experience at the moment of discovery
- Structural parallels flagged for the reader rather than felt

The writer understands the chapter's emotional architecture clearly. The problem is trust — the prose does not trust the reader to arrive at the right feeling without guidance.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Fix the Bleed passages** (Issues 1 and 2). Remove all narration that names the Bleed's inner state or identifies it as a distinct aware presence. Replace with somatic sensation only — pressure, heat, constriction, spatial distortion — without label or interpretation.

2. **Restructure the sibling-pages discovery** (Issues 3 and 4). Remove "the page was about siblings" as a heading sentence. Replace the summary paragraph with Hurkuzak reading specific fragments — notation, a word, a diagram that stops him — without the narrator organizing the content for him.

3. **Cut "The clarity was worse"** (Issue 8). End the chapter before the epigram. Let the image of Hurkuzak sitting with the materials in the fading light carry the closing weight.

4. **Remove the "approaching a cliff edge" simile** (Issue 5) and let "He put the papers down. He picked them up." carry more weight by expanding it slightly (Issue 6).

5. **Remove historical lore and "Cousins, not strangers"** (Issue 9). Keep the felt recognition; cut the explanation.

6. **Cut the two-option grief/panic reading and the Hakiia-never-readable capper** (Issue 10). Let the stillness be still.

7. **Trim the carrying-out-of-ravine passage** (Issue 7) to remove the named parallel.

---

## Primary Problem Classification

**Premature revelation + narration that interprets before the reader can experience.**

The chapter's tension mechanism is correct, but the narrator repeatedly rescues the reader from uncertainty at the moments when uncertainty is the entire point. Every High-priority fix involves removing an interpretation, a label, or an epigram that the prose has not earned the right to deliver yet.
