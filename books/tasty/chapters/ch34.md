Chapter 34: The Professional

She found his camp on the sixth morning.

Not the camp itself — he had broken his fire and scattered the ash and moved on before dawn, the way he always moved now, gone before first light, his body already thinking ahead of his mind. But the camp's remains were legible to someone who had been reading this particular trail for weeks. The depression in the grass where he had slept. The warmth still held in the earth where the fire had been, diminished but not gone because the ground held water now and released heat slowly. The direction he had taken, east and slightly north, toward the trade road that ran along the river valley — the same river valley where the principal's people had established the collection point.

The weather had not held. The ground recorded everything differently now. Tracks did not preserve cleanly; they softened, spread, collapsed at the edges. But disturbance still spoke. Compressed soil, bent growth, the way water pooled differently where weight had passed. Vark read it without needing clarity. Precision was not required. Pattern was.

He was walking into it, blind.

Vark crouched beside the dead fire and read it with her hands, her nose, her weight settling into the ground beside the tracks. His track was strong. Heavier than it had been a month ago, the musculature still increasing, the transfer still depositing physical mass in ways that exceeded normal growth. The stride had lengthened. The foot placement showed the confidence of experienced mountain hunters. He was moving like something the market would pay a fortune for — and the fortune had already been named, the highest figure Vark had ever been offered for anything, living or dead.

She packed her kit and followed. The urgency had passed weeks ago. Now the work was geometry. She was not chasing. She was shaping. He moved east because something had changed in his pattern — she did not know its specifics, only that the aimless avoidance had become directed travel. His direction aligned with where she needed him to go. Let him walk where he wanted. Ensure it led where she needed.

The intermediary had been low enough in the network that he moved goods and asked no questions above his station. The meeting had confirmed what the premium suggested: whoever sat above him wanted this badly enough to mobilize resources that ordinary contracts did not justify. He had provided sedation compounds calibrated for augmented demons, restraint cord treated to hold without tearing what they were buying. The principal wanted the quarry intact.

The principal wanted a living man, and what lived in him, and was willing to pay a sum that told her she did not need to understand why.

She had spent three weeks shaping the ground. She had spoken to people ahead of his track — innkeepers, trappers, riders working the trade roads. Small conversations, small coins, small misdirections. A road mentioned as dangerous because of bandits. A ford described as washed out — and with the wet cycles, that lie held better than it should have. A camp known to be hostile to strangers. Each piece of false information created a tiny pressure on his route, and the pressures accumulated the way wet snow accumulates — heavier, less visible, more certain in its effect.

She had also positioned three men along the convergence corridor — professionals in a secondary way, experienced enough to be useful and expendable enough to be placed without concern for their survival. Not a cordon — too obvious — but presence. A hunter's camp here. A patrol there. The smell of an occupied fire at a crossroads. Each presence closed an option he might otherwise have taken, and the closing was invisible because each presence appeared to be what it claimed to be.

His inherited instincts were good. Better than she had expected. He had read two of her planted warnings and gone around them, choosing paths his borrowed sense told him were cleaner. But those paths were also part of the funnel. She had built the architecture with redundancy. The real snare is the thing they choose instead.

She had watched him fight — the bandit ambush, the public eruption. She had seen how fast the hands moved, the way the hesitation emptied from his face in the instant before contact. But dangerous was not uncatchable. The system had been built to contain exactly this level of strength.

His movement had changed again in recent weeks. He no longer drifted between road and tree line. He chose routes with the decisiveness of someone traveling toward a destination rather than away from a threat. He was no longer running. He was going somewhere.

She did not need to know where. She only needed his direction and hers to overlap, and they did.

The collection point was a trading compound in the river valley — a walled enclosure used by merchant caravans as a staging area, with stone buildings, a central courtyard, and a single gated entrance that could be sealed. A space he would approach as an ordinary waypoint on his eastward route. A space that could become containment in the time it took to close a gate.

She thought about the taxonomy of what she was delivering as she walked. In fifteen years of hunting she had brought in demons across the full range of what the market knew — common quarry, choice ones, rarities worth enough to fund a season from a single carcass. She had never delivered something like this. Two selves sharing one skull, and the second one still growing instead of eating the first alive. She had heard of cases that failed — the whisper network carried enough of those, broken things, mad things, men who had taken a sibling and lost themselves in the taking — but never a case that held. This was the first.

She would deliver it. She would name her price. The price would change her life permanently, and that was why she had spent three weeks building this trap instead of risking a direct fight.

She reached the compound at midday and walked its perimeter. The walls were sound. The gate was a mechanism one person could operate. The courtyard was open enough that a man approaching from the eastern road would see a waypoint, not a snare — until the gate closed behind him.

The ground inside the walls was firmer — packed, drained, maintained. Outside, the approach roads still held moisture, tracks softening as they aged. Inside, nothing softened. Containment required stability.

She spoke with the three positioned men. Professional conversations, brief, specific. Their roles were defined. Their timing was established. The sedation compounds were distributed — one to each, with instructions on delivery method and the absolute requirement that the quarry not be killed or critically injured. The principal's instructions had been clear: he is to arrive alive, conscious, and intact. Damage the body, damage the asset.

Vark understood. She had been delivering living quarry for years. A living delivery was harder than a dead one — a dead body could be preserved, transported, processed at the buyer's convenience. A living body fought, fled, deteriorated, required maintenance. A living body with an active transferred self was something she had never delivered before.

The afternoon passed. She ate. The meal was demon flesh — a strip from her kit, unremarkable. She checked her crossbow. She checked her restraints. She checked the compound's gate mechanism twice.

She would be on the wall when it happened. Above, watching, able to redirect the men with a gesture they knew. She would not engage directly unless the situation required it — his combat capabilities made direct engagement a risk she did not need to take when the architecture did the work. She was not there to fight him. She was there to ensure the system performed as designed and to make the corrections plan and ground always require.

The sun lowered. The compound settled into the quiet of a place that knows what it is waiting for. Vark sat on the wall with her back to the stone and her crossbow across her knees and watched the eastern road where it emerged from the tree line half a mile out. The air remained damp. Sound carried differently — softened, absorbed. Nothing approached.

She waited with the patience that had built her career — the kind that paid because it was professional, not passive.

He would come tomorrow. The trail said so. His pace said so. She had built the ground to receive him. She was ready.

She closed her eyes and let the compound's quiet settle around her. When she opened them the road was still empty, patient, leading east to where he walked toward her without knowing it.

The walking was the last free thing he would do for a long time. It did not trouble her sleep. Neither did the rest.
