Chapter 26: The Market of Parts

The town was called Merrath. It sat at the junction of three trade roads, large enough that one more face was not a face but a number.

He entered from the north road in midmorning and the market was already working. Stalls lined the central street, awnings of stretched hide and cloth shading tables of goods. Haggling, hammering, the clatter of wheels, the complaints of animals.

He moved the way the voice had taught him: peripheral, unhurried, eyes forward, attention wide, reading the crowd for gaps. The voice offered commentary — that man is armed, the alley on the left has two exits, the three soldiers near the grain stall are watching the east entrance, not the crowd — and Hurkuzak received it and kept walking.

The smell reached him before the stall did. Not bad — wrong.

Salted, dried, rendered flesh, and his stomach clenched. The cabin. The pan over the hearth. The specific scent that demon flesh produced when it was cooked.

He stopped. He looked at the parcels. They were neatly wrapped and labeled with a notation system he did not recognize — symbols and numbers, a grading language, the shorthand of a trade that had developed its own vocabulary. The merchant behind the stall was a small man with careful hands who watched Hurkuzak look and said nothing.

Hurkuzak did not buy anything. He walked on. The stall multiplied as he walked. A physician's shop with jars on shelves, the jars containing materials he did not need to see to name. The labels on the jars used terms that arrived in his mind already named, already arranged, in a register that was not quite his.

Liver draw, hot-rendered. Marrow, pressed and settled. Sinew braid, dry-cured.

The terms belonged to a trade in bodies like his, broken down with the same care he had used in the cabin. He knew the terms. He was not certain he had known them yesterday. The question of where they had come from rose and he did not let it finish rising — not yet, not here, not with the smell still in his nose and the physician's window still in front of him.

He walked further. The market's structure became visible once he knew what he was looking at. Most stalls sold ordinary goods — grain and cloth and tools. But woven through the ordinary commerce was a second economy, visible to those who knew the signs and invisible to those who did not. The signs were the smell, first. The notation, second. A quality of discretion that was not secrecy but tact.

A butcher's stall sat at the market's edge, slightly removed from the main traffic, shaded by a canvas awning that was cleaner than its neighbors. The butcher cleaned his blade between cuts with a thoroughness that suggested the residue mattered. He weighed each portion on a scale finer than any mutton seller would own. Each cut was wrapped in treated cloth and marked with the same notation system the preserved-meat merchant had used.

A woman was purchasing from the butcher. She discussed her order the way a person discusses a prescription. This cut was from the upper limb, good for general vitality. That preparation was concentrated marrow, recommended for injuries that would not close on their own. The rendering was done within twelve hours of harvest, which preserved the active properties. She paid without negotiating. The price was established.

Two men stood beside a wagon discussing shipment in the tone that grain merchants used to discuss grain. The numbers were quantities. The descriptors were tissue types: long muscle, dense bone, organ suite. One mentioned freshness and the other mentioned price and the discussion carried the same flat certainty that men who sell any goods daily carry — weight, freshness, the expected margin.

A man at a nearby stall complained about the cost of moving organs across the mountain routes. The complaint sounded the same whether the goods were wool or wheat or the liver of someone who had walked and spoken and carried a name a week ago.

Children ran past the wagon. A woman at the adjacent stall called to them — something about supper, something ordinary. No one in this marketplace was horrified. No one was performing a secret.

Something moved in the space behind his thoughts. Not a word — a quality of attention, a particular stillness, the way a predator goes still when it recognizes something it has seen before.

He had already done what these people paid for. He had consumed his brother completely — every tissue, every organ, every part. The effects they purchased in careful doses, he had inherited in total. He stood at the butcher's scale. The feeling belonged to more than one person, and the two of them did not agree on what it was.

The physician's notation. The grading language. The terms that had arrived in his mind already named.

He knew those terms. Knew them the way he knew things he had not learned. He put it away with the other things he was not ready to carry, and walked on.

A woman at a fabric stall discussed the relative merits of two suppliers for treated wrapping cloth — which held better under winter transport, which had the cleaner certification marks. She might have been discussing vintners. She might have been discussing anything.

Hurkuzak watched her conclude the transaction and fold her purchase under her arm and walk away satisfied. An ordinary woman at the end of an ordinary errand, the afternoon light coming down flat and cold across the market roofs, everything ordinary, nothing wrong.

He left Merrath in the late afternoon carrying provisions. He walked east. The voice was quiet, but the quiet was not empty.

He thought about the notation on the physician's jars. The way it had arrived in him already legible, already sorted.

Not yet.
