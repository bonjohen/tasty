# Chapter 23: The Familiar Tongue

He was three days east of the market town and still running from what his hands had done.

Not running physically. His pace was measured, deliberate — tree-line travel the voice had demanded since the bandit fight. The market had settled that argument. His body could not be trusted in public. That was established now. It contained a violence that rose under pressure with the sureness of a drawn bow — total, precise, and it did not wait for him before it moved.

The market was behind him but the market was not behind him. The faces of the crowd. The sound the enforcer's jaw had made. The way his own hands had moved with a sureness that was not learned but inherited — and the crowd had seen it.

He traveled through spring forest. The forest asked nothing of him, and the nothing was a relief — temporary, because solitude was a condition he was no longer sure he occupied. The cabin had been solitary. The road had been crowded. Inside him, regardless of what was outside, was the company of one — and the one's opinions were getting harder to separate from his own.

The corrections no longer sounded like survival instinct. They sounded like a person Hurkuzak knew, directed at specific failures he had been corrected for his entire life.

You are walking like something that wants to be caught. Feet are too heavy. Weight is wrong. Commit to the step or do not take it.

The criticism arrived and the old architecture of obedience activated — adjust before the next correction lands. He adjusted. His feet lightened. His weight shifted forward.

He caught himself mid-step. The phrase that formed in his mind — lazy ground — was not his phrase. It was Hakiia's, invented or inherited from their father's trade language, and it had lived exclusively in Hakiia's vocabulary for Hurkuzak's entire life.

The phrase had arrived already shaped — not retrieved, not recalled. It carried the weight of Hakiia's diction the way the corrections did, delivered by something he could not see.

He stopped walking. He stood in the forest and listened to the silence that followed the phrase and the silence was not empty. It was the silence of a room with someone else in it. A specific someone.

He did not name the someone. Not yet. The naming was a door, and he was not ready to open it.

He started walking again. The terrain required attention, and the attention was a relief.

The forest was spring forest now — wet underfoot, the canopy showing green at the margins where buds were opening, the air carrying the smell of turned earth and snowmelt. He moved through it with the borrowed fluency that had become his default — reading terrain, choosing footfall, moving by a judgment his mind issued without being consulted. That judgment was not his.

He reached a river crossing by midafternoon. The river was swollen with melt — fast, brown, carrying debris — and the crossing options were limited. A ford downstream, visible from the road, exposed. A narrowing upstream where the current was faster but the distance was shorter and the banks were screened by trees. A fallen tree further up that spanned the gap but sat high and was slick with spray.

The voice had an opinion. The log. Cross high, stay dry, keep moving. The ford is visible from three directions and anyone on the road sees you standing in the water.

Hurkuzak looked at the log. It was high and the surface was wet and the water beneath it was fast enough that a fall would mean being carried downstream, battered against rocks, into the open water where the road ran close.

The ford is safer for the crossing and more dangerous for the exposure. The log is dangerous for the crossing and safer for everything after. Choose.

He chose the log. He crossed it with a balance his body provided and his mind watched — feet placing themselves on the slick bark with the weight distribution of someone who had crossed a thousand such logs. He reached the far side without slipping.

Hurkuzak had followed the way he had always followed. He had not decided to follow. That was established now too.

That evening he found a sheltered camp between two rock faces and built a fire the way the corrections had taught him — draft at the base, wood angled the way Hakiia had drilled into him, the lay that held heat longest. He ate from his dwindling supplies. The food was still wrong — flat, textureless, his body taking it in without acknowledgment the way a sick animal eats: joylessly, by obligation, because stopping was worse.

A memory arrived. Not his.

It was brief — a flash of sensory information that belonged to a different body and a different time. A ridge seen from above, the perspective of someone taller — the snow bright, the wind strong, the dogs visible far below laboring through the draw — while the watcher assessed the terrain ahead and chose the route that offered the best combination of speed and visibility.

The ridge. The route Hakiia had chosen the day Hurkuzak suggested the trees. Seen from Hakiia's perspective. Seen through eyes that were assessing sightlines and wolf approaches while Hurkuzak below was worrying about the dog's pad.

The memory dissolved as quickly as it had arrived, leaving behind sensation — cold mountain wind on a face that was not his, the weight of a larger body. And underneath both: certainty.

Hurkuzak sat beside the fire. The space the memory had occupied was warm, the way a recently vacated seat is warm.

He told himself it was exhaustion. The mind starved of rest made things. The specificity — the angle, the height — was reconstruction, his own mind filling gaps with whatever it could reach. The explanation was plausible. Grief invented what it needed.

He told himself this. The telling held — but the holding required more effort than it had the day before.

The night deepened. The fire settled. The forest made its sounds — branches, water, the small lives of small creatures.

The voice was quiet.

For the first time, the quiet did not feel like absence. It felt like company. Difficult, impossible company — but company.

He did not speak. He did not name what sat beside the fire with him. He was not ready — but the pretending was costing him more each day than the naming would cost all at once.

He lay beside the fire and closed his eyes. The closing was easier than it had been in weeks. The darkness behind his eyelids was not empty, and the not-empty was terrible and comforting in equal measure — the way love and cruelty had always lived side by side in the man whose name he would not yet say.
