# Chapter 40: What the Hunter Sees

The stronghold operated the way a good loom operates — with the quiet of parts that fit.

Vark had been inside it for two days and the two days had taught her more about the upper reaches of the flesh economy than fifteen years of fieldwork. The processing rooms were clean. Not clean the way a butcher's floor was clean — scrubbed, adequate for the next carcass. Clean the way a physician's instruments were clean. The stone floors were smooth and graded toward drainage channels. The cutting surfaces were dense hardwood, oiled and maintained. The storage vaults held a cold that did not leave — packed earth and straw and something else, something that held the air cold past reason. The personnel moved through the corridors with the economy of people who no longer thought about what they were doing — a handler transferring a specimen container, a guard checking a door, each motion stripped to its minimum.

She had expected competence. She had not expected elegance.

The principal — Tessivane, who had introduced himself with the courtesy of a man who did not need to impress — had provided her with quarters. A room in the outer wing, near the gate, with a bed that was better than anything she had slept on in years and a window that faced the courtyard. The lock worked from the inside. A professional courtesy. The delivery's quality had been verified by people whose instruments and methods she had never seen applied to a living subject, and their assessment confirmed what she already knew: the subject was extraordinary.

She had been paid. Not in full — the final portion held against a consultation period the principal described as brief and standard. The initial deposit had been confirmed by courier, and the amount was the largest single payment she had received in her career by a factor of six.

She ate breakfast in the staff dining area — demon flesh prepared with a sophistication that was new to her. Cuts trimmed with anatomical precision, cooked in rendered fat, seasoned with something that drove the effect deeper and cleaner than she had known it to go. She could feel the difference. The meal entered her system with a clarity she had not encountered in the field.

She thought about the subject while she ate.

She had tracked him for months. Wounded him twice. She knew his speed, the strength that exceeded his visible musculature, the combat reflexes of a fighter more dangerous than any she had personally engaged — reflexes that were not his, that had come into him from the dead brother the way a fire takes when the conditions are right. The capabilities were real and she had priced them correctly. The delivery had demonstrated that.

What the examination would show was another matter. The principal would want to understand what he had purchased. That was not her concern.

The morning was quiet. The principal would conduct his examination in the days ahead. She was available, as the contract specified. Present. Ready.

She noticed the first irregularity at midday.

It was small. A guard who should have been at the junction of the inner and outer corridors was not there. The post was unoccupied. She saw it as she walked from the dining area to the courtyard — a corridor that should have held a guard standing in the practiced boredom of a man on watch, and instead held empty stone.

She noted it. She did not act on it. Guards shifted for many reasons — a latrine visit, a reassignment, a brief task. The absence was a data point, not a conclusion.

She walked the courtyard. Two men worked in the loading area near the gate, shifting containers. A third stood near the gate mechanism. The air carried the smell of new growth from beyond the walls — the season had moved while she had been tracking the subject through the eastern hill country. Everything was ordinary.

She went back inside. The guard was still not at the junction.

She continued to her room and checked her equipment — crossbow assembled, string waxed, three broadheads in the rack. Knife clean. Restraint kit packed. She did not expect to need any of it. The subject was restrained, examined, and contained under guard and the prince's suppression compounds. He was not her responsibility.

The second irregularity came an hour later. A sound from the interior — not an alarm, not a shout, but a percussive impact that carried through the stone the way a heavy object carries through a floor. A thud. One. Then nothing. The kind of sound a body makes when it hits stone from standing height without attempting to arrest its fall.

She listened. The stronghold resumed its quiet. She waited for what should have followed — voices, movement, the sound of someone responding to a body falling. Nothing came.

She buckled her knife belt and took her crossbow from the rack and loaded a bolt.

The corridor outside her room was empty. The torchlight was steady. She moved toward the inner wing where the sound had originated — controlled pace, weight on the balls of her feet, crossbow up and indexed along the corridor's left wall. She had cleared corridors before.

She reached the junction. The guard was not at the junction because the guard was on the floor of the junction.

He was alive. Breathing. The breathing was shallow and irregular but present. No visible wound. No blood. He lay where he had fallen — not sprawled, not thrown, but lowered. As though the connection between will and body had simply been cut, and the body folded. His weapon — a short, heavy blade — was still in its sheath. He had not drawn it. Whatever had reached him had reached him before the drawing was possible.

Vark crouched beside him. She checked his pulse. Strong. She checked his pupils. Equal, reactive. She smelled his breath. No sleeping draught — she knew the smell of the prince's formulations, and this was not that. She pressed her thumb into the hollow below his ear and the guard's body contracted once and then relaxed and did not wake.

She looked at the corridor beyond the junction. It led deeper into the stronghold — toward the storage vaults, the treatment rooms, the inner wing where the principal's study was located.

A door at the far end of the corridor was open. It should not have been open. A heavy door — reinforced timber, iron-banded, with a locking bar that required deliberate effort to move. It led to the storage wing. The door was always locked. It was open now. The lock bar had been lifted and set aside with the care of someone who understood the mechanism and did not need to damage it.

She moved down the corridor toward the open door with the crossbow aligned and her breathing regulated.

She reached the door and looked through it and saw the second guard.

This one was not breathing. He was on his back in the storage corridor and his head was turned to the side and the angle of the turn was wrong — not dramatically, not grotesquely, but wrong by the few degrees that separated a man looking to his left from a man whose neck had been rotated past its structural limit. His weapon was half-drawn.

Dead guard. Neck broken. Weapon partially drawn — response time perhaps one second from alert to incapacitation. No weapon marks. No blood. His hand was still on the grip.

The subject in the study. His capabilities — the speed, the strength, the dead brother's combat instincts operating in him like a second set of hands.

She moved deeper.

The storage corridor branched. Left toward the preservation vaults. Right toward the treatment rooms and the inner wing. She paused at the branch and read the space the way she read a forest trail: not what was visible but what was absent. The left branch was untouched — dust, stillness, the cold air of sealed vaults. The right branch showed passage. A scuff on the stone where a foot had pivoted. A torch that had been knocked from its bracket and lay on the floor, still burning, the flame flat against the stone and painting the wall in moving light.

She took the right branch.

Two more guards. These were alive — one unconscious against the wall, slumped in the boneless posture of total incapacitation, the other crawling toward a door with the slow mechanical persistence of a man whose body was obeying commands his mind could not fully form. His leg was broken. The break was clean — the kind a stonemason might produce with a chisel, one precise impact mid-shin. He was not screaming. He was not making any sound at all. His face was blank — the kind of blank that comes after the mind stops trying to understand.

Vark stepped past him. She did not help him. She did not stop. She processed the evidence — the trajectory of the violence, the direction of travel — and recognized what it described. Each guard reached before he could respond. No excess. No wasted motion.

Something had changed. The instability was gone.

She reached the corridor that led to the principal's study. The corridor was long and lit and at the far end was a door that was open, and through the open door she could see the study, and in the study she could see that the restraint chair was empty and the restraints had been opened — not broken, not torn, but unbuckled, the treated lining of the restraints peeled back and laid across the chair's arm with the deliberateness of a person who had studied the mechanism and understood it and removed it without urgency because the urgency was not in the removal but in what came after.

The study was empty. The subject was somewhere in the stronghold, moving through it with the integrated purpose of a being who had read the facility the way a hunter reads terrain — the way she read terrain — and had converted that reading into action.

The stronghold had gone quiet — not silence, but the absence of routine. No footsteps in distant corridors. No sounds of processing from the lower level. No voices. The quiet of a machine that has stopped.

Vark stood in the corridor outside the empty study and adjusted her grip on the crossbow and felt something she had not felt in a very long time. Her hand had tightened on the stock without her telling it to. Her breathing had shortened. The skin along her forearms had drawn tight, the fine hairs raised, the body already afraid before she had decided to be.

She breathed. She checked the bolt in her crossbow and positioned her off-hand for a fast reload. She had always known where the prey was. What it would do. That knowing had been absolute. She did not know where he was now.

She moved toward the deeper chambers.

She entered the deeper corridor. The torchlight shifted. The stone walls narrowed. Ahead, a junction opened into the space between the storage wing and the ritual chambers — a wide, low-ceilinged room used for transfers and staging, with pillars and poor sight lines.

She went in. She had eaten the flesh of demons and it had made her more than what she had been born to be, and the more had always been enough.

The room was quiet and the quiet was waiting.
