# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 21: What the Body Knows

**Source chapter:** `book/chapters/ch21.md`
**Section:** 10 (Public Eruption)
**Reviewer role:** Developmental and line editor, setting consistency
**Review rules applied:** `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`
**Setting baseline:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "He had pulled the bolt out himself — an act that should have produced a scream and instead produced a grunt and a quantity of blood that stopped flowing before he finished binding it."

**Problem:** "a quantity of blood" is clinical, near-medical measurement language. It reads like a physician's chart notation — the sort of abstract quantification that belongs to a world with documented clinical practice and standardized medical discourse, not to a pre-industrial demon traveler describing his own wound in a pre-scientific register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The narrator is rendering Hurkuzak's experience from inside his own consciousness. A person without clinical training would not mentally portion blood into "a quantity." He would feel the heat, the volume, the way it moved — not count it as a measured unit.

**Better direction:** "He had pulled the bolt out himself — an act that should have produced a scream and instead produced a grunt and blood that came fast and then, before he finished binding it, stopped." Or simply: "...and blood that stopped, faster than blood should stop."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "The bolt had been aimed with professional precision."

**Problem:** "Professional precision" is a modern compound that smuggles in bureaucratic-vocational framing. The notion of a profession as a recognized occupational category with associated standards of practice is a relatively modern social construction. In a pre-industrial world, the more natural framing would be around skill, training, the hand of someone who knows their work — not the abstraction of "professionalism."

**Why it breaks immersion:** The word "professional" appears several more times in the chapter (in reference to Vark, in reference to the enforcer's blow). Each use compounds the problem. The chapter begins to sound like an HR assessment, not a dark fantasy novel.

**Better direction:** "The bolt had been aimed by someone who knew their work." Or: "The bolt had been aimed well — too well for coincidence."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "His hearing pulled the noise apart before he could stop it: conversation, hammering, vendors calling, children, dogs, the grinding of a mill west of the square, a smithy ringing."

**Problem:** This passage is largely fine and pre-industrial in its content (mill, smithy, vendors). No flag needed for the imagery itself. However, "pulled the noise apart" is a modern idiom that could be more precise. This is Low priority because the metaphor is functional and the imagery is grounded.

**Better direction:** "His hearing sorted the noise before he chose to — conversation, hammering..." The distinction is small but sharpens the sense that his perception is acting on him, not cooperating with him.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "The voice cataloged every face: that man is carrying a weapon under his coat, left side; that woman is watching you — merchant wariness, not interest; those two by the farrier are not waiting for a horse, they are waiting for something else, their attention is wrong."

**Problem:** "Cataloged" is the problem word here. It is a classification and record-keeping term from a world with formal archives, taxonomies, and institutional organization. In pre-industrial settings, cataloging implies libraries, inventories, bureaucratic systems — it is a word that belongs to systems of record.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The voice inside Hurkuzak reads as though it is running a bureaucratic assessment. The activity being described — reading a crowd, noting threats — is perfectly in-world, but the verb that frames it carries an institutional register that belongs elsewhere.

**Better direction:** "The voice read every face..." or "The voice moved across the crowd like a finger down a list..." or simply "The voice named them one by one..."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "It was a dominance blow — open-handed, across the face."

**Problem:** "Dominance blow" is psychological-analytical language. It names the social mechanism behind the strike using a modern behavioral-science frame. A pre-industrial narrator — or a character processing violence from inside a pre-industrial world — would not reach for "dominance" as the operative concept. They would describe what they saw: a blow meant to show who was in charge, a blow to teach a lesson, a blow for the watching crowd.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Dominance" as a behavioral category belongs to 20th-century ethology and social psychology. Using it here makes the narration sound like it is analyzing the scene from outside the world rather than living inside it.

**Better direction:** "It was an instructional blow — open-handed, across the face. The kind meant to be witnessed." Or: "It was a teaching blow — the kind men use when they want a lesson remembered by everyone watching."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "Two more men materialized from the crowd. They were with the large man. The three of them surrounded the smaller man with practiced coordination — enforcers, collectors."

**Problem:** "Practiced coordination" combined with "enforcers, collectors" imports modern organizational-crime vocabulary. "Enforcers" and "collectors" are specific roles within a rationalized criminal or debt-extraction apparatus — the language of organized crime syndicates with defined functional specializations. While debt collection and enforcement existed in pre-industrial societies, naming these roles with such clean taxonomic precision feels modern.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This passage reads like a crime-fiction writer noting character types for a contemporary thriller. The functional roles may be correct, but the clean labeling gives it the flavor of genre categorization rather than in-world perception.

**Better direction:** "Two more men appeared from the crowd — his men, clearly. The three of them closed around the smaller man like they had done this before." The reader can infer the function without the modern taxonomic labels.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "The voice was not speaking in words. It aligned his body toward the threat the way a compass aligns toward north."

**Problem:** This is the most significant single anachronism in the chapter. The compass metaphor depends on knowledge of a manufactured navigational instrument that operates on magnetic principles. Even if compasses exist in some form in this world, the simile presupposes that the reader and narrator both think of "compass pointing north" as an automatic, universally understood mechanism — which is a modern navigational assumption embedded in a pre-industrial fantasy setting with no stated basis for such an instrument.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The metaphor is vivid and effective, but it derives its clarity from a modern technological reference frame. A character from this world would navigate by landmarks, stars, rivers, and animal trails — not compass bearings. The simile quietly exports the narrator's modern frame of reference into the world.

**Better direction:** Something that conveys automatic, involuntary alignment without the instrument: "The way a dog's head turns toward a sound before it means to." Or: "The way water finds slope without deciding to fall." Or: "The way a hawk's head turns before the hawk knows there is movement below." These options preserve the "automatic, pre-conscious orientation" meaning while staying inside the world.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "His resistance met something stronger — not the voice, but the body itself, rebuilt by months of consumption, carrying reflexes calibrated by a lifetime that was not his."

**Problem:** "Calibrated" is precision-instrument language. Calibration is the process of tuning a measuring device or mechanism to a standard — it belongs to a world of technical manufacturing and standardized precision. It is the language of clocks, gauges, scales, and instruments.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Applying calibration to human reflexes subtly mechanizes Hurkuzak's body in a way that belongs to a more technological worldview. It is a small slip, but in a prose style this precise, small slips compound. The world is pre-industrial; the narration should not reach for instrument-shop vocabulary when describing the body.

**Better direction:** "...carrying reflexes shaped by a lifetime that was not his." Or: "...carrying reflexes honed by a lifetime that was not his." Both preserve the precision of the idea without importing a technical manufacturing register.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "The second enforcer turned and swung and the swing was competent and it missed because Hurkuzak was not where the swing expected him to be — he had moved inside the arc, inside the range where the fist had power."

**Problem:** "Competent" is another modern vocational-assessment term. In the context of evaluating someone's violence, "competent" belongs to the professional performance-review language identified in Issue 2. It implies a standard of practice being met — the register of a trainer assessing a student, not a fighter reading another fighter in real time.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Like "dominance blow" and "professional precision," "competent" keeps applying modern occupational categories to pre-industrial physical conflict. The cumulative effect is that violence in this chapter starts to sound like it is being assessed by a consultant rather than experienced by a survivor.

**Better direction:** "The second enforcer turned and swung and the swing was solid, the kind of swing that had always been enough — and it missed." This conveys the enforcer's confidence and experience without the vocational label.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "The blow landed where the neck met the jaw — a point the voice knew."

**Problem:** This is not strictly an anachronism, but "a point the voice knew" is slightly ambiguous in a way that could be clarified. The phrase implies anatomical precision — the voice is directing Hurkuzak to a specific structural point. This is fine for the setting, but the near-clinical "a point" construction is adjacent to the "quantity of blood" register flagged in Issue 1. The phrase is almost, but not quite, anatomical.

**Better direction:** This is borderline. One option: "The blow landed where the neck meets the jaw. The voice had known it before his hands did." This removes the quasi-clinical distance and makes the voice's knowledge active and preceding, which fits the arc better.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 11

**Passage:**
> "Hurkuzak stood among three fallen men and felt the voice settle into quiet satisfaction — the grim pleasure of a problem solved with minimum force."

**Problem:** "Minimum force" is doctrine language — specifically, the doctrine of proportional or minimum necessary force, which is a legal and military concept with a specific modern history. Police manuals, military rules of engagement, and legal frameworks use this phrase. A pre-industrial character experiencing a violent aftermath would not frame the voice's satisfaction in terms of "minimum force."

**Why it breaks immersion:** Even if the idea (efficient, targeted violence) is correct for Hakiia's pragmatic philosophy, the specific phrase "minimum force" brings in a legal-bureaucratic register that does not belong here. It makes the voice sound like a use-of-force policy rather than an inherited predator's satisfaction.

**Better direction:** "...the grim pleasure of a fight finished cleanly." Or: "...the grim pleasure of someone who has never needed more than exactly enough." Or: "...the satisfaction of waste avoided." These preserve the meaning while staying in-world.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 12

**Passage:**
> "Steady as a craftsman's."

**Problem:** This is not an anachronism — craftsmanship is pre-industrial. However, the simile is slightly generic. More a weak writing flag than a consistency issue. Noted at Low priority.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 13

**Passage:**
> "They did not see a traveler who had intervened in a beating. They saw something that had emerged from the shape of a person and done things that persons do not do."

**Problem:** "Emerged from the shape of a person" is an effective image, but "the shape of a person" implies a kind of ontological category-system thinking that borders on modern philosophical framing. This is marginal — the image works and is not clearly out-of-era. Noted at Low for consideration.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 14

**Passage:**
> "The inheritance was not a gift. It was a colonization — growing stronger with each eruption, each moment the world pressed hard enough for the man Hakiia had been to step forward."

**Problem:** "Colonization" is a modern political-historical term with a very specific meaning shaped by centuries of European imperial history and subsequent academic discourse. Using it as a metaphor for psychological or spiritual invasion imports a conceptual framework that does not exist in this world. It is the most tonally displaced word in the closing paragraph.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The concept being described — one self invading and occupying another — is valid and important. But "colonization" as a metaphor reaches for a modern political vocabulary that the world and the narrator do not have. It sounds like the author's voice intruding into Hurkuzak's consciousness, not Hurkuzak's own thought.

**Better direction:** "The inheritance was not a gift. It was an occupation." Or: "It was an overgrowth — something rooting deeper with each eruption." Or: "It was a taking — growing stronger with each moment the world pressed hard enough for Hakiia's hand to reach through." "Occupation" or "overgrowth" or "possession" all carry the meaning without the political-historical import.

**Priority:** High

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 21 is well-executed — the prose is controlled, the action is rendered with appropriate dread, and the voice's agency during the eruption is handled with the right restraint. The setting inconsistency problems here are not structural failures. They are vocabulary slippages: individual word choices that briefly export the author's modern frame of reference into a pre-industrial world.

The chapter's prose has an elevated, literary register that mostly serves the world well. But that register occasionally slips into adjacent vocabularies — clinical measurement, modern psychological analysis, use-of-force doctrine, imperial political theory, and precision-instrument language — each of which subtly undermines the immersive effect the writing otherwise builds.

No scene, beat, or image is broken by these issues. Every problem is a word or phrase that can be replaced without touching structure or meaning.

## Pattern Summary

The chapter has one consistent recurring weakness: the narrator's vocabulary periodically drifts into modern institutional registers — clinical, vocational, bureaucratic, political — when describing violence, perception, and power. The author's control of the pre-industrial setting is strong at the level of physical detail (smithy, farrier, garrison, dried meat, crossbow bolts). The drift happens at the conceptual level, in the words used to frame and analyze events: "cataloged," "calibrated," "professional," "competent," "minimum force," "dominance blow," "colonization," and "compass." These are all modern analytical categories being applied to pre-modern experience. The world is built right; the analytical vocabulary layered over it is not fully in-world.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. Replace "It was a colonization" — find a pre-industrial metaphor for gradual internal occupation or overgrowth. (High)
2. Replace the compass simile — find an animal-behavior or natural-world metaphor for automatic, pre-conscious alignment. (High)
3. Replace "a problem solved with minimum force" — reframe the voice's satisfaction in terms the world supports: clean work, no waste, exactly enough. (High)
4. Replace "It was a dominance blow" — describe what the blow communicated without importing the behavioral-science category. (High)
5. Replace "calibrated" with a word that conveys shaping or honing without precision-instrument connotation. (Medium)
6. Replace "The voice cataloged every face" with a verb that describes enumeration or reading without archival register. (Medium)
7. Replace "professional precision" and "competent" — describe skill and effectiveness without vocational-assessment framing. (Medium)
8. Replace "a quantity of blood" — render the blood through sensation and movement, not measured volume. (Medium)
9. Review "enforcers, collectors" — consider whether these taxonomic labels are needed or whether the function can be implied. (Low)
10. Review "pulled the noise apart" — consider whether the idiom serves well enough or could be sharpened. (Low)

---

## Root-Cause Note

The problems in this chapter are **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The world is coherent. The narrator's voice is consistent and serves the literary dark register well. The failures are local: specific words imported from modern institutional and analytical vocabularies. The fixes are correspondingly local — no structural revision is required, and no beats or scenes need rethinking. This is a line-edit pass problem, not a developmental problem.
