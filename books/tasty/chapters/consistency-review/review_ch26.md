# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 26: The Market of Parts

**Source file:** `book/chapters/ch26.md`
**Review rules:** `.claude/rules/poor-consistency.md`
**Setting baseline:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity. Literary dark register. (book/CLAUDE.md)
**Section context:** Section 12, beat 12 — Hurkuzak observes the flesh economy in a trade-road settlement.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Findings

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "The terms were the terms of a trade, and the trade was the trade of bodies like his, disassembled with the same care he had used in the cabin."

**Problem:** "Disassembled" is modern industrial-mechanical diction. It belongs to the vocabulary of machinery, manufacturing, and assembly lines — none of which exist in this setting. Even as metaphor it imports a factory-floor frame.

**Why it breaks immersion:** In a pre-industrial world the natural analogy for rendering a body into parts is butchery, harvest, or rendering — craft vocabularies, not industrial ones. "Disassembled" implies a reversible, engineered process built from components, which is exactly the register this world's setting rules prohibit.

**Better direction:** "broken down," "rendered," "taken apart the way a skilled butcher takes apart a carcass," or simply "divided with the same care."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "The information arrived without source attribution, as though a library had been opened inside his mind and the catalogue was being browsed by a reader who knew exactly what to look for."

**Problem:** "Source attribution," "library," and "catalogue being browsed" together read as modern information-retrieval metaphor. "Source attribution" is bureaucratic/academic language. "Browsed" applied to mental access is a contemporary digital metaphor (browse/browsing web, library database). A "catalogue being browsed" is especially anachronistic — catalogues as organized retrieval systems with that name and bureaucratic structure are a post-industrial institution.

**Why it breaks immersion:** These three terms cluster into a single extended metaphor drawn from print culture and library science at their most organized and modern. The sentence tries to convey information flooding into Hurkuzak's mind, but it renders that experience in the vocabulary of a card-catalogue room or a digital database. Neither belongs to this world.

**Better direction:** Retain the sense of involuntary knowing without the institutional framing. Options: "as though a cellar door had opened inside him and something down there already knew where everything was stored," or "the knowing arrived already sorted, already arranged, as if it had been waiting for him to ask." Avoid "source attribution" and "catalogue."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "Liver extract, rendered. Marrow suspension. Sinew concentrate, dried."

**Problem:** "Marrow suspension" and "Sinew concentrate" use the register of pharmaceutical chemistry. "Suspension" in the sense of a substance held in a liquid medium is a modern chemistry and pharmacy term. "Concentrate" as a processed, standardized product is similarly modern. Pre-industrial apothecaries and physicians did use preparations, but their vocabulary ran toward decoctions, tisanes, tinctures, infusions, salves, and powders — not suspensions and concentrates.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The labels are meant to signal that this trade has developed its own professional vocabulary — that is narratively correct and effective. But the specific words chosen belong to nineteenth-century pharmaceutical science at the earliest. In this setting, the labels should feel alien and specialized without sounding like they came off a medicine bottle manufactured in a laboratory.

**Better direction:** Keep the three-part rhythm — it works well. Replace with period-plausible analogues: "Liver, rendered. Marrow, settled in oil. Sinew, pressed and dried." Or invent trade-language that feels old rather than clinical: "Liver draw, hot-rendered. Marrow pressing. Sinew braid, dry-cured." The oddness of unfamiliar trade jargon is more immersive than recognizable pharmaceutical terminology.

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "He stood outside the physician's shop and felt the voice supply information in a continuous stream..."

**Problem:** "Supply information in a continuous stream" is modern data/communications diction. "Supply information" is neutral but leans technical-bureaucratic. "Continuous stream" strongly evokes digital data transfer — streaming, data streams, broadcast. Even in a metaphorical sense, the phrase belongs to a communications-technology frame.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The narration is describing the Bleed's transfer of knowledge into Hurkuzak's consciousness. The prose elsewhere uses more atmospheric, body-anchored language for this experience. This phrase steps out of that register into something that sounds like a technical description of a signal being transmitted.

**Better direction:** "the voice poured knowledge into the gap" / "the voice filled the space with what it knew" / "Hakiia's knowing arrived unbidden and continuous, one thing following the next without pause." Keep the relentlessness of the experience without the data-stream vocabulary.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "He cleaned his blade between cuts with a thoroughness that suggested the residue mattered."

**Problem:** "Residue" is a technical-scientific term for leftover material after a reaction or process. In a butcher context it is not egregiously wrong, but it sits slightly above the register of what a butcher or observer in this world would naturally reach for. It implies chemical or clinical precision rather than craft knowledge.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Minor but consistent with a pattern of clinical vocabulary (see Issues 1, 2, 3). In context — a butcher managing cross-contamination between flesh types of different market value — the observation is narratively correct. The word choice makes it feel like a lab technician's note rather than a moment of grim recognition.

**Better direction:** "He cleaned his blade between cuts with a care that said the leavings of one cut must not touch the next" / "the thoroughness of a man who knew the difference between cuts mattered to the buyer." Or simply: "He wiped his blade clean after every cut."

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "The complaint sounded the same whether the product was wool or wheat or the liver of a creature that had been alive and thinking a week ago."

**Problem:** No isolated vocabulary problem, but the phrasing "a creature that had been alive and thinking" has a faint modern animal-welfare or cognitive-science frame — "thinking" specifically. The broader issue is mild: the sentence works well emotionally. But "thinking" as a value-marker for moral concern is a modern philosophical distinction (sentience debates, animal cognition) that would not map onto this world's moral categories, which frame the taboo through kinship and taboo, not cognition.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The sentence implies Hurkuzak's moral frame is "they were conscious." The world's actual horror is "they were like us — family, kin, community." The source of the taboo is not sentience but identity and kinship. A character in this setting would register moral weight through those categories, not through a modern "thinking beings" frame.

**Better direction:** "the liver of someone who had walked and spoken and carried a name a week ago" / "the liver of a being whose kin were somewhere still breathing." This keeps the weight without importing a cognitive-rights frame.

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "One mentioned freshness and the other mentioned price and the discussion carried the same matter-of-fact specificity that any commodity price carries among people who trade in it daily."

**Problem:** "Commodity price" is modern economic terminology. A "commodity" in the modern financial sense — a standardized, tradeable, fungible good with a market price — is a concept belonging to the development of organized commodity markets (grain exchanges, futures trading) in post-medieval European economic history. Its casual use here imports a modern economic category into what should feel like an older, more embedded market structure.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The sentence is trying to describe the normalization of the flesh trade by comparing it to ordinary commerce — which is correct and effective. But the specific vocabulary of "commodity price" frames the market in modern financial terms rather than the guild and merchant structures that fit a pre-industrial world.

**Better direction:** "the discussion carried the same flat certainty that men who sell any goods daily carry — price, weight, freshness, the expected margin" / "spoke of it the way merchants speak of any goods they have handled long enough to stop seeing."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "A man at a nearby stall complained about import tariffs on preserved organ shipments from the eastern provinces."

**Problem:** "Import tariffs" is borderline. Tariffs exist in pre-modern economies and the word itself has medieval precedents. However, "import tariff" as a compound phrase has a distinctly modern administrative-policy ring, reinforced by "preserved organ shipments" — "shipments" is particularly modern (it belongs to organized logistics and shipping industries). Together the phrase sounds more like a trade policy briefing than a medieval merchant's complaint.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Mild individually, but in combination with the other clinical-commerce vocabulary in this chapter, the phrase adds to a pattern of modern economic diction colonizing what should be a pre-industrial market scene. The complaint itself is right — this is exactly the kind of detail that shows a normalized industry. The framing just needs to sound older.

**Better direction:** "complained about the road tolls on preserved organ goods coming from the east" / "cursed the guild levies on organ trade out of the eastern provinces." "Levies," "tolls," "duties," "guild fees" — all fit the register better than "import tariffs."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "He was not an anomaly. He was a proof of concept."

**Problem:** "Proof of concept" is modern product-development and engineering language. It belongs to the vocabulary of prototyping, product management, and technology development. As an internal thought or authorial observation it is out of register with the world.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The sentence captures an important insight — Hurkuzak understands that his transformation is what the market has been clumsily trying to replicate. That insight is correct and important for the chapter. But "proof of concept" imports start-up/laboratory vocabulary into a pre-industrial fantasy. It is the kind of phrase that a modern author reaches for without realizing it belongs to a specific contemporary register.

**Better direction:** "He was not a curiosity. He was the answer they had been paying to approach." / "He was not strange. He was the thing the market had been groping toward through crude work and careful money." / "The parts were merely meat. The integration was what they had been trying to buy."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "They would commission it. They would recruit participants. They would find the right demons, the right pairing, the right circumstances."

**Problem:** "Recruit participants" carries modern bureaucratic-organizational language. "Recruit" belongs to military and institutional vocabulary that in its modern form implies formal organizational process. "Participants" is clinical and institutional — it sounds like a research study or a program, not a conspiracy or a ritual arrangement.

**Why it breaks immersion:** These sentences are Hurkuzak's dawning realization that the flesh economy's upper tier would not wait for chance but would arrange the rite deliberately. That is a strong moment. But "recruit participants" drains the horror out of it by making it sound like project management rather than a predatory network arranging the death and consumption of specific people.

**Better direction:** "They would arrange it. They would find the right demons, the right brothers, the right moment." / "They would seek out the right pairing and put the right circumstances in place." Remove "commission," "recruit," and "participants" — replace with verbs and nouns that fit an older world of patronage, arrangement, and predation.

**Priority: Medium**

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 26 is structurally sound and tonally effective. It accomplishes its section goals: the flesh economy is revealed as normalized, systemic, and professionally organized, and Hurkuzak's layered recognition — that the market mirrors what he did in the cabin, that Hakiia knew more than he should have — lands with appropriate weight. The Bleed's reaction is correctly handled. The prose voice is generally literary and controlled.

The setting-consistency problems are predominantly vocabulary-level, not structural. The chapter has a recurring pattern: when describing the professional organization of the flesh trade, the narration reaches for modern economic, clinical, pharmaceutical, and technological vocabulary. "Suspension," "concentrate," "commodity price," "import tariffs," "proof of concept," "recruit participants," "disassembled," "source attribution," "catalogue being browsed" — these belong to a modern frame of organized commerce, laboratory science, and institutional administration. The pre-industrial setting should produce a different vocabulary: craft terms, guild and merchant language, older administrative terms (tolls, levies, duties), apothecary language (decoctions, tinctures, renders), and the grammar of trade by personal relationship rather than standardized product.

None of the problems damage the chapter's emotional architecture. All are correctable at the word and phrase level without structural revision.

## Pattern Summary

The chapter's recurring weakness is modern-commerce and modern-clinical vocabulary colonizing the description of organized trade. When the narration moves into "this is an industry" mode, it reflexively reaches for the language of modern industries: pharmaceutical labels, institutional vocabulary, economic theory, data transfer, project management. The author's pre-industrial world has its own economic and craft vocabulary available — older terms for taxation, older terms for pharmaceutical preparation, older analogies for how knowledge arrives unwanted in the mind — but those alternatives are not being reached for. The result is a chapter where the horror of normalization is correctly conceived but partially undercut by the modern bureaucratic language used to describe it, which makes the system sound like a contemporary supply chain rather than a pre-industrial atrocity.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace "proof of concept"** with period-appropriate phrasing that captures the same insight without product-development vocabulary.
2. **Revise the library/catalogue metaphor** for the Bleed's knowledge transfer — cut "source attribution" and "catalogue being browsed"; find a body- or cellar-anchored alternative.
3. **Replace "Marrow suspension / Sinew concentrate"** with pre-industrial apothecary or trade language: settled marrow, pressed sinew, oil-rendered preparations.
4. **Replace "disassembled"** with "rendered," "broken down," or "taken apart."
5. **Revise "supply information in a continuous stream"** — remove the broadcast/data register.
6. **Replace "a creature that had been alive and thinking"** with kinship/identity framing consistent with the world's moral categories.
7. **Replace "commodity price"** with older market vocabulary.
8. **Replace "import tariffs on preserved organ shipments"** with "road tolls," "guild levies," or "duties on organ trade."
9. **Replace "recruit participants"** — use arrangement and predation vocabulary instead.
10. **"Residue" (optional)** — revise only if tidying the full chapter; not urgent.

---

## Root-Cause Note

The problem is **isolated wording**, not worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The chapter's emotional logic is intact, the world's rules are being honored in structure and in dramatic terms, and the narrative voice is consistent. The failures are at the level of individual word choices where the author's modern vocabulary habits surface under the specific pressure of describing organized commerce and professional systems. These are exactly the conditions — industry, trade, classification, administration — where modern default vocabulary is hardest to override. The fix is targeted lexical revision, not structural or voice revision.
