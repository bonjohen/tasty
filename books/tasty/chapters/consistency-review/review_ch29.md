# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 29: What Hakiia Carried

**Reviewed against:** `poor-consistency.md` rules
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Chapter function:** Discovery of Hakiia's hidden ritual cache in the wrecked sled.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "The notations at the intersections in a shorthand that was not language but something between language and mathematics."

**Problem:** Modern framing of mathematics as a formal discipline distinct from language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Mathematics" as an abstract, named discipline is a product of literate, institutionalized intellectual culture. A pre-industrial demon from a tribal or feudal background would not frame an unknown notation system by reference to a named academic category. He might say the marks were "neither word nor number" or "between reckoning and speech" — describing function, not discipline.

**Suggested direction:** "shorthand that was not language but something between words and reckoning" or "not writing but closer to tally-marks, if tally-marks could carry instruction."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "Powders derived from biological sources, described by origin tissue with the specificity of someone who understood anatomy."

**Problem:** "Biological sources" and "anatomy" are clinical, scientific register terms that belong to modern or at minimum late-Enlightenment medical vocabulary.

**Why it breaks immersion:** In a pre-industrial world — even one with a structured flesh economy — "biological sources" is a phrase that would not occur to a narrator who lives inside that culture. The flesh economy uses professional vocabulary, but it would be trade vocabulary: the names of organs, the names of beasts, the names of preparations. "Biological sources" imposes a modern taxonomic frame. Similarly, "anatomy" as a named discipline (rather than body-knowledge or surgeon's understanding) carries Enlightenment overtones. Hurkuzak would not frame Hakiia's expertise in anatomical terms as a named field.

**Suggested direction:** "Powders drawn from flesh and organ, described by their source tissue with the care of a man who had opened bodies and looked." Or: "...described by origin as a butcher might describe cuts — specific, jargon-edged, unsentimental."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "Preserved substances in sealed containers, their shelf lives noted, their active properties catalogued."

**Problem:** "Shelf lives" and "active properties" are commercial and scientific register language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Shelf life" is a modern commercial concept tied to product distribution, retail inventory, and standardized food safety language. A pre-industrial character — even one familiar with preserved goods — would think in terms of "how long it keeps" or "how long before it loses its virtue," not "shelf life." Similarly, "active properties" is pharmacological language imported from clinical science. The flesh economy uses specialized vocabulary, but the narrator's framing of Hakiia's notes would borrow the trade's own register, not a modern scientific one.

**Suggested direction:** "...their keeping time marked, their virtues and uses catalogued." Or: "...how long each would hold, what it did at full strength, what it became when it went stale."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "A kit assembled for a specific procedure, each component checked against a requirement, substitutions noted where the primary material was unavailable."

**Problem:** "Procedure" and "primary material" are clinical and bureaucratic register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Procedure" in the clinical sense (a named medical or surgical action with defined steps) is a modern formulation. In a pre-industrial setting, the equivalent register would be "rite," "working," "preparation," or "operation" in the older sense of a labor carried out. "Primary material" is likewise modern procurement language, suggesting a supply chain and materials specification rather than a craft practitioner choosing the best tool available. These phrases come from a narrator who thinks in modern bureaucratic categories, not from inside the world.

**Suggested direction:** "A kit assembled for a specific working, each component weighed against what the rite required, alternatives noted where the preferred substance could not be had." Or simply: "...each piece checked against what was needed, with notes on what could serve if the true thing was not at hand."

**Priority: High**

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "The physician's jars in Merrath. The notation on the parcels. The grading language, the preparation methods, the specificity of which tissues produced which effects. The list in his hands used the same vocabulary. Not identical — Hakiia's notes predated the market by months — but adjacent."

**Problem:** "Predated the market" and "adjacent" (in the abstract relational sense) are modern analytical constructions.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Predated" as a temporal-priority construction is natural in modern analytical writing but sits oddly inside a character's experiencing consciousness. More significantly, "adjacent" in the sense of being conceptually related or near — without physical proximity — is a modern rhetorical habit. In ordinary pre-industrial speech, a person would not think "adjacent"; they would think "of the same family," "spoke the same tongue," "came from the same source," or "close enough to be kin." "Adjacent" imports a modern sense of proximity-without-contact from data visualization or mapping culture.

**Suggested direction:** "Not the same — Hakiia's notes came before the market, before the merchants formalized their language — but close enough to belong to the same knowledge. Cousins, not strangers."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "The tone was transactional — arrangements, schedules, logistics."

**Problem:** "Transactional" and "logistics" are modern commercial and military-supply-chain register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Transactional" as an adjective describing a communicative tone is modern business and psychology language. A pre-industrial narrator would not reach for "transactional" to describe correspondence; they might say "the writing of a man conducting business" or "the language of contracts" or "arrangements between men who trusted neither warmth nor waste." "Logistics" is specifically a term from military and commercial supply-chain management that became generalized in modern usage. In a pre-industrial register it would be "the arrangements" or "the planning of supply and movement" — described, not named.

**Suggested direction:** "The tone was the tone of men conducting business — arrangements, schedules, the movement of materials. Nothing warm. Nothing wasted."

**Priority: Medium**

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "The word vessel had a meaning in this context that Hurkuzak's mind was approaching the way a man approaches a cliff edge, knowing the drop is there."

**Problem:** No setting-consistency issue here — "vessel" is appropriate and the metaphor is native to the world. Flagging for confirmation: this is clear and strong.

**Priority: Not an issue** — included for completeness.

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "with strength that belonged to the brother who had packed these materials into hidden compartments and carried them into the mountains, and the pulling felt like the sled going over the edge — the same sick lurch of ground becoming air."

**Problem:** "Hidden compartments" appears twice in the chapter (also earlier). The term is not anachronistic, but "compartments" may be slightly modern-engineering in its precision when applied to a hand-built sled.

**Why it flags:** Sled construction in pre-industrial settings would be described in more material, craft terms: "spaces built into the frame," "sealed pockets," "the locked spaces behind the cross-members." "Compartments" is not impossible but carries a faint industrial-design register. More importantly, this is a stylistic note, not a hard anachronism.

**Suggested direction:** As used earlier ("secondary compartments"), the word is working within acceptable range. The repetition in the climb scene is redundant. Consider replacing with "the things he had brought up from the mud" or "what the wreck had given him."

**Priority: Low**

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "He spread them on the bank beside the runner and the rope. He looked at them for a long time."

**Problem:** None — this is clear, period-appropriate prose. Not an issue.

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "A hand pushing against the inside of his forehead."

**Problem:** Minor — the precision of "inside of his forehead" reads slightly clinical/anatomical in a way that is borderline.

**Why it flags:** The image is strong and primarily succeeds on visceral grounds. The slight clinical precision of "inside of his forehead" (rather than "behind the face" or "behind the brow") is a near-miss anachronism — forehead as an anatomical structure is fine, but the phrasing sounds slightly like a person who thinks of the skull as a container in a physiological sense. This is the weakest flag in the chapter.

**Suggested direction:** "A hand pushing against the back of his eyes" or "pressing from behind the brow" would feel more naturally bodily and less clinical.

**Priority: Low**

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 29 is a strong piece of prose. The setting consistency problems are not numerous and none of them shatter the chapter's atmosphere. They are concentrated in a specific zone: the passages where Hurkuzak processes the contents of Hakiia's cache. This makes structural sense — when the narrator reaches analytical or descriptive prose about specialized materials and documents, modern vocabulary bleeds in. The chapter's emotional and atmospheric language (the mud, the knots, the dog bones, the travois echo, the final image of the clarity being worse than darkness) is clean and well-calibrated to the world.

The problems are language-register problems, not worldbuilding contradictions. The world itself is consistent. The anachronisms are all in the analytical-descriptive layer: how the narrator frames Hakiia's knowledge and materials. This is a common drift pattern when describing technical or specialized content in historical or fantasy settings — the author reaches instinctively for modern vocabulary to convey precision and expertise.

## Pattern Summary

The chapter has one recurring weakness: when the narrator must describe Hakiia's specialized materials, notes, and knowledge, the prose reaches for modern clinical, scientific, and commercial vocabulary. Words like "biological sources," "anatomy," "shelf lives," "active properties," "transactional," "logistics," and "procedure" all come from the same drift: the author's modern analytical vocabulary bleeding in when describing expertise. The rest of the chapter — physical sensation, emotional interiority, the Bleed's behavior, the objects and their weight — is well inside the world's register.

This is the narrowest kind of setting inconsistency: not a character behaving anachronistically, not a modern social assumption, not a wrong metaphor, but a narrator who sounds briefly like a person who has studied biology, pharmacology, and business when describing what amounts to a pre-industrial ritual practitioner's kit.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace "biological sources" and "anatomy."** These are the most clearly anachronistic and the fix is simple — trade vocabulary or craft description replaces clinical framing.

2. **Replace "shelf lives" and "active properties."** Substitute keeping-time and virtue language native to craft-and-trade registers.

3. **Replace "procedure" and "primary material."** Substitute "working" or "rite" for procedure; "preferred substance" or "the true thing" for primary material.

4. **Revise "transactional — arrangements, schedules, logistics."** Keep the content; replace "transactional" and "logistics" with descriptive prose that conveys the same businesslike register without naming it in modern commercial terms.

5. **Revise "predated the market" and "adjacent."** Substitute temporal description and an organic proximity metaphor (cousins, same tongue, same family of knowledge).

6. **Revise "something between language and mathematics."** Replace "mathematics" with "reckoning" or a functional description of what the marks did.

7. **Low-priority:** Address "inside of his forehead" and the redundant "compartments" if the above revisions create space for a final polish pass.

---

## Root Cause Note

This chapter's issues are **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The narrative voice is consistent and strong throughout. The world itself is not being contradicted. The failures are local and clustered in a single functional zone: the analytical description of Hakiia's cache contents. A targeted pass through those passages — replacing scientific and commercial register with trade, craft, or plainly descriptive language — will resolve all High and Medium priority issues without touching the chapter's atmosphere or emotional architecture.
