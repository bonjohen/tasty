# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 35: What the Net Holds

**Reviewer:** Setting Consistency Editor
**Date:** 2026-04-06
**Chapter:** 35 (Section 16, "What the Net Holds")
**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "the hinges oiled, the crossbar mounted on a track that allows it to slide into position with a single motion"

**Problem:** Modern precision engineering language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Mounted on a track" implies machined or factory-toleranced hardware — a groove-and-runner system associated with industrial precision fitting. Pre-industrial gates use drop-bars, pivot-bars, or sliding timber beams set into iron or wood brackets. The word "track" specifically conjures modern rail or channel engineering and does not belong to the register of a medieval-equivalent setting.

**Better direction:** "the crossbar seated in iron brackets" or "the crossbar fitted to drop runners worn smooth from use" or simply "the crossbar settled into its fittings with a single push." The emphasis on smooth operation can remain; the word "track" should go.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "A small ceramic vial in his right hand"

**Problem:** "Ceramic" is technically possible but tonally clinical.

**Why it breaks immersion:** Ceramic is not wrong as a period material, but "ceramic vial" reads with a laboratory precision that is slightly out of register for this world's narration style. Elsewhere the prose uses "treated cord," "sedation draught," and "preservation compounds" — functional, concrete language. "Ceramic" sits slightly above that level of material precision. Pre-industrial settings would more naturally describe this as an earthenware vial, a clay stoppered vial, or simply a sealed vial. The issue is minor but cumulative with other clinical language in the chapter.

**Better direction:** "a small stoppered vial" or "a clay-sealed vial." Let the material function rather than be classified.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "the smell that rose from it was chemical and sweet and wrong: a sedation draught, concentrated, designed for a body stronger than a normal man's"

**Problem:** "Chemical" is a modern scientific register word.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is the most significant single-word problem in the chapter. Hurkuzak is a pre-industrial character in a pre-industrial world. He has no concept of "chemical" as a category — the word belongs to modern scientific classification. A person of this world and era smelling something unnatural would describe it through sensation, material analogy, or cultural reference: burnt metal, something like pine resin but wrong, the smell of a physician's cabinet, the smell of something that should not enter the body. "Chemical" is not a smell-word available to this narrator. Its use here is a clean anachronism.

The second phrase, "concentrated, designed for a body stronger than a normal man's," reinforces this: "concentrated" and "designed for" are pharmaceutical/industrial formulation terms. These slip the narration into a product-specification register that does not fit the world.

**Better direction:** Replace "chemical and sweet and wrong" with something sensory: "sharp and sweet and wrong, the kind of wrong that lives in the back of the throat." For "concentrated, designed for a body stronger," try "strong enough to bring down something larger than a man" or "prepared for larger prey than any physician would admit to treating."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "A sedation draught, concentrated, designed for a body stronger than a normal man's."

**Problem:** "Designed for" implies intentional pharmaceutical engineering.

**Why it breaks immersion:** As noted above, "designed for" is formulation language — the vocabulary of someone who manufactures products to specifications. In this world, a poison or sedation compound would be "prepared for," "made for," "mixed to handle," or "brewed strong enough for." The verb "designed" is too modern-clinical.

**Better direction:** "brewed for a body stronger than a normal man's" or "prepared to bring down something harder to kill than a man."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "using the man's forward momentum against him, redirecting the charge into the stone wall"

**Problem:** "Redirecting" as a fighting term carries modern martial arts vocabulary.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Redirecting" in the context of combat is language from modern martial arts instruction — judo, aikido, self-defense curricula. The prose is describing a physically plausible grapple, but the verb gives it a contemporary instructional flavor. The word is not impossible in period usage, but its fighting-technique application is distinctly modern. The surrounding prose ("not around but through, using the man's forward momentum against him") is already vivid and does not require the addition of "redirecting."

**Better direction:** "He moved through the second man the way water moves through a gap — not around but through, turning the man's own weight toward the wall." Or simply cut "redirecting" and use "driving the charge into the stone wall."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "The bolt in his thigh. The blood she had collected."

**Problem:** "The blood she had collected" implies clinical specimen collection.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is a reference back to an earlier chapter where Vark draws Hurkuzak's blood, but "collected" is the word of a laboratory technician or a modern specimen-handler. In a pre-industrial context, blood taken for study or purchase would be "drawn," "taken," "kept," or "bought." "Collected" in the modern sense of organized specimen acquisition is an anachronism.

**Better direction:** "The bolt in his thigh. The blood she had drawn from him." Or "The blood she had taken and studied."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "He was product entering the supply chain. He was meat with a consciousness, a specimen with a value"

**Problem:** "Supply chain" is modern logistics and business terminology.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Supply chain" is a twentieth-century business and logistics concept. It does not belong in pre-industrial vocabulary. The Tasty world has a structured flesh economy, and the narrative has elsewhere used "the system," "the trade," "the network," "delivery," and "supply" as separate terms — all of which are pre-industrial compatible. But "supply chain" as a compound noun is distinctly modern business language. It is a jarring anachronism in this otherwise well-controlled passage.

"Specimen" also reads as slightly clinical, though it is present earlier in the chapter and in the outline and carries some established usage in-world. The word is tolerable but worth noting.

**Better direction:** "He was product passing down the trade road. He was meat with a consciousness, a thing with a price." Or "He was cargo now, passing from hand to hand toward the man at the end of the chain." Keep the "chain" metaphor if desired, but not the compound noun.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "the treated cord around his wrists — he felt the chemical suppression enter through his skin, the muscular contraction in his arms reducing"

**Problem:** "Chemical suppression" and "muscular contraction... reducing" are clinical/physiological language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Chemical suppression" is clinical pharmaceutical terminology. A pre-industrial narrator would not categorize what is happening to his body as "chemical suppression." He would feel the cord as wrong, numbing, dead — something that steals the strength from his hands. Similarly, "muscular contraction in his arms reducing" is physiological description of the kind found in a medical textbook, not in a character's felt experience of being restrained. This is a place where the narrative voice slips from interiority into a detached anatomical narrator.

**Better direction:** "the treated cord at his wrists — something passed from it into his skin and the strength in his arms began to leave him, muscle by muscle, until his grip was only strong and then merely strong and then nothing." Or: "the cord at his wrists leaching warmth from his hands, the grip going out of him." Preserve the sequence of loss without clinical vocabulary.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "the sequence: sedate, then restrain while the sedation held, then reinforce before the body began to burn the draught away"

**Problem:** Clinical procedural sequence phrasing.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Sedate, then restrain... then reinforce" reads like a medical or pharmaceutical protocol — step-by-step clinical procedure language. While the Tasty world has a professionalized flesh economy, the narration is in Hurkuzak's fading POV, and the level of procedural clinical articulation does not match his perspective or the world's vocabulary. A more period-appropriate register would use plainer action language: "they knew the order of it — down first, then bound while the draught held, then doubled before the body could burn it away." The idea can survive; the clinical register should not.

**Better direction:** "They knew the order — down first, bound while the draught held, then doubled before the body began to fight it off." Keep the competence and the procedural knowledge, but shift the language from medical protocol to practical know-how.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "Her mechanism was performing as designed."

**Problem:** "Mechanism" and "performing as designed" are engineering/systems language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** These two words together create a machine-design register that belongs to modernity: mechanisms perform, and things perform as designed in engineering and quality-control contexts. The prose is attempting to show Vark's detachment and professional satisfaction, which is entirely correct tonally. But the vehicle for that detachment is modern systems language. A pre-industrial equivalent would speak of her work, her plan, her trap — it would use craft or hunting vocabulary, not engineering evaluation.

**Better direction:** "Her work was proceeding as she had built it to." Or: "Her trap was closing the way she had built it to close." Or lean into the hunting register: "The drive was running true."

**Priority:** Medium

---

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 35 is a well-executed chapter — the capture sequence is structured with discipline, the Bleed's escalation to direct speech lands correctly, and the prose maintains the atmospheric weight the novel requires. The voice transition ("Hold on") is earned and handled with appropriate restraint.

The setting consistency problems are concentrated in two areas. First, the chapter uses clinical and pharmaceutical vocabulary — "chemical," "chemical suppression," "designed for," "concentrated," "muscular contraction reducing," "sedate/restrain/reinforce" as procedural sequence — that pulls the narration out of the pre-industrial world and into a modern medical register. Second, "supply chain" is a clean modern business anachronism that breaks the world's language without providing any stylistic benefit.

These are not deep worldbuilding failures. They are localized vocabulary slippages — places where the author's modern frame of reference leaked through. The structure and emotional architecture of the chapter are sound. The fixes are targeted and do not require restructuring.

## Pattern Summary

The recurring weakness is **clinical and technical vocabulary leaking into a pre-industrial interiority.** The author reaches for precision and finds it in modern pharmacological, physiological, engineering, and logistics language — registers that do not belong to this world or to Hurkuzak's narrating consciousness. The problem is not the ideas. The ideas are correct: sedation compounds exist, restraint is procedural, Vark is systematic. The problem is the vocabulary used to render those ideas. "Chemical," "designed for," "supply chain," "mechanism performing as designed," and "muscular contraction reducing" all belong to modernity, not to this world.

A secondary pattern: the chapter occasionally narrates Hurkuzak's felt experience from outside rather than inside. The clinical physiological descriptions ("muscular contraction in his arms reducing") are the clearest examples. These read like a medical observer's report rather than a person feeling their strength leave them.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace all clinical pharmaceutical language:** "chemical" (twice), "chemical suppression," "designed for," "concentrated" as a pharmaceutical descriptor. Use sensory and experiential replacements throughout. This is the highest-leverage fix because it appears in the chapter's most emotionally important passage.

2. **Replace "supply chain"** with period-appropriate equivalents: "the trade road," "the chain of hands," "the network pulling him toward its center."

3. **Revise the cord/restraint passage** to remove "chemical suppression" and "muscular contraction reducing." Render the experience of loss sensorially, from inside Hurkuzak's failing body.

4. **Replace "Her mechanism was performing as designed"** with hunting/craft register equivalent. "Her trap was closing true" or "Her work was proceeding as she had built it."

5. **Replace "mounted on a track"** with a pre-industrial gate description. "Crossbar fitted to drop runners" or "crossbar seated in iron brackets."

6. **Replace "the blood she had collected"** with "drawn" or "taken."

7. **Revise "sedate, then restrain... then reinforce"** sequence to trade/practice language rather than clinical protocol.

8. **Replace "redirecting the charge"** — cut "redirecting" and use simpler action verb.

9. **Review "ceramic vial"** — consider "earthenware vial" or "stoppered vial."

---

## Root Cause Note

This chapter's problems are **isolated wording failures**, not deep worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift. The world is internally coherent; the emotional register of the narration is correct; the Bleed's progression is handled well. The author's modern vocabulary is the source of the slippage — specifically, modern medical, pharmaceutical, engineering, and logistics language that provides precise and efficient description but belongs to a different era. The fixes are local, targeted, and do not require any restructuring of the prose.
