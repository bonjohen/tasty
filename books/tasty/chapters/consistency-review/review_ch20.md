# Setting Consistency Review — Chapter 20: Rarity

**Chapter:** Ch20 — Rarity (Section 9, Vark POV)
**Setting:** Pre-industrial dark fantasy. Wagons, sleds, bladed weapons, bows, traps. No engines, firearms, or electricity.
**Review focus:** Anachronistic language, modern idioms, vocabulary mismatches, and immersion breaks.

---

## Issue-by-Issue Analysis

---

### Issue 1

**Passage:**
> "a joint lock that strained the ligaments without completing the break"

**Problem:** Clinical anatomical terminology — modern sports-medicine register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Ligaments" as a precise clinical category, combined with the framing of a "joint lock" (a martial arts term with a modern technical genealogy), produces phrasing that sounds like a physician's injury report or a forensic assessor's notes in a contemporary setting. A pre-industrial specialist — even a skilled and educated one — would have a different vocabulary for connective-tissue injury. She might note that the joint was wrenched, that it would swell and stiffen, that the tendons were strained but not severed, or simply that the man's wrist was wrong without being broken. The clinical precision of "ligaments" is the specific intrusion.

**Suggested direction:** Replace with sensory and practical observation: "a wrist wrenched badly enough to swell, not broken, but the joint would not bear weight without complaint." Keep the clinical detachment in her framing — she is a professional — but calibrate it to pre-modern anatomical vocabulary.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 2

**Passage:**
> "The subject used the..." / "the subject's blood on it" / "a live specimen of this quality" / "Killing this subject without understanding him" / "a complete profile"

**Problem:** Modern clinical/bureaucratic register — "subject," "specimen," "profile."

**Why it breaks immersion:** This cluster of terms belongs to the language of modern scientific research, forensic documentation, and institutional casework. "Subject" as a label for a tracked individual is a bureaucratic/clinical term with a specific modern institutional origin. "Specimen" appears in natural history and biology in ways that feel contemporary rather than pre-industrial. "Profile" as a dossier of behavioral and physical characteristics is a modern intelligence and law-enforcement concept. Individually, each is defensible; collectively, they layer a modern procedural idiom onto what is meant to feel like a different world's professional vocabulary.

The narrative notes in the section outline explicitly want Vark to think in the "grammar of her trade: value, risk, yield, patience." The actual grammar of a pre-industrial flesh trader would be mercantile and physical, not clinical and administrative.

**Suggested direction:** Vark can retain her cool professional detachment by using the language of trade and craft: "the man," "the quarry," "the body," "this one," "the kill," "a full reading of his worth." Reserve "specimen" for moments where she is deliberately treating him as a categorized object rather than as ambient narration. Drop "subject" in favor of "the man" or "the quarry" throughout. Replace "profile" with "a full accounting" or "a reckoning of his capabilities."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 3

**Passage:**
> "already oxidizing in the spring air"

**Problem:** "Oxidizing" is a term from chemistry — specifically from the science of chemical reactions involving oxygen, developed in the 18th and 19th centuries.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The concept behind the observation — that exposed blood darkens and crusts as it dries — is entirely observable by anyone in any era. The word "oxidizing" is not. It requires the conceptual framework of chemistry and a named understanding of oxygen as an element. No pre-industrial character would frame this mental observation in these terms. The author clearly means "drying and darkening," which is a perfectly valid and period-appropriate observation. The word choice is the problem.

**Suggested direction:** "already darkening in the spring air" or "already going brown at the edges" or "drying at the rim, the deeper color of blood that has met the cold air." Stay with the sensory observation; lose the chemical mechanism.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 4

**Passage:**
> "She had been maintaining a gap of four hours — close enough to track reliably, far enough to avoid detection by senses she now knew were augmented."

**Problem:** "Augmented" as a technical descriptor for enhanced physical capability.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Augmented" in the sense of senses or physical capabilities that have been boosted or enhanced beyond baseline carries a distinctly modern — and specifically technological — register. It belongs to the vocabulary of human enhancement, cybernetics, and biological engineering as understood in a modern or science-fiction frame. In a pre-industrial fantasy world, a tracker with Vark's experience would think about sharpened senses in the vocabulary of her own world: keener, expanded, strengthened, outside ordinary limits, wrong in the way that changed flesh is wrong. The word "augmented" imports a modern concept of deliberate technical enhancement.

**Suggested direction:** "senses she now knew were keener than a man's should be" or "senses she knew ran ahead of ordinary perception" or "senses that had been changed along with the rest of him."

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 5

**Passage:**
> "a broadhead designed for penetration testing"

**Problem:** "Penetration testing" is modern technical/industrial language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Penetration testing" as a formal designation belongs to the vocabulary of materials science, ballistics research, and product engineering. Even in a pre-industrial world where a skilled crossbow maker or hunter might test a bolt's penetration, the formalized noun phrase "penetration testing" — with its industrial-standardization register — sounds like a product specification sheet. Vark would think of the bolt by what it does, not by what category of test it is designed for.

**Suggested direction:** "a broadhead made for depth, not spread — the kind used to measure what a body would give." Or simply: "a broadhead bolt, standard for deep-wound assessment." Or more plainly: "a broadhead. Made to go deep, not to widen — she wanted to know what the wound would do, not end what the wound would do it to."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 6

**Passage:**
> "demonstration bouts between augmented fighters in the eastern cities"

**Problem:** Second instance of "augmented" as a technical descriptor; "demonstration bouts" has a modern sporting-event register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** See Issue 4 for "augmented." "Demonstration bouts" compounds the problem: "bout" is a legitimate period-appropriate word for a fight, but "demonstration bout" — a formal, staged exhibition match — carries the vocabulary of organized sport as a commercial spectacle in the modern sense. In a pre-industrial world, staged fights between flesh-enhanced warriors might exist, but the phrasing should feel like that world's professional parlance, not modern sporting culture's.

**Suggested direction:** Keep the witness's observation but reframe it. "She had seen display fights between flesh-fed soldiers in the eastern cities" or "She had watched paid men who carried demon flesh trade blows for a merchant's amusement." The key is that the referent (public display of enhanced fighters) is plausible in this world — only the vocabulary needs adjusting.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 7

**Passage:**
> "She ran the numbers."

**Problem:** Modern financial/quantitative idiom.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "She ran the numbers" is a contemporary idiom originating from commercial and accounting culture, specifically the act of quickly computing financial or statistical figures, often with the implication of a mental spreadsheet or rapid calculation. It is vivid and efficient in a modern context. In a pre-industrial world, even a commercially minded professional would frame mental calculation differently: she tallied, she weighed it, she reckoned the cost, she counted. "Ran the numbers" imports the idiom of financial modeling into a world that does not have the institutional vocabulary to support it.

**Suggested direction:** "She reckoned it." Or "She counted: at the current rate..." Or simply restructure so the calculation follows immediately without the framing phrase.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 8

**Passage:**
> "Through her augmented vision she tracked the diminishing figure"

**Problem:** Third instance of "augmented" as a descriptor for enhanced sense perception — now applied to Vark's own vision.

**Why it breaks immersion:** This is the most jarring instance of the three. The earlier uses described a body the narrator was assessing from outside. Here, Vark's own inner narrative labels her own perception with the same clinical term she might apply to a subject under study. It creates a register inconsistency: a pre-industrial professional would not experience her own enhanced senses using the language of technical specification. She would simply perceive with the sharpness she had, or note that her sight carried further than a standard hunter's, or that the flesh she had consumed gave her distance.

**Suggested direction:** "Through eyes that the years of consumption had sharpened, she tracked the diminishing figure" — or more economically: "Her sight carried further than it should have. She tracked him until the trees took him." Or simply: she watched him, and her sight held him longer than any ordinary watcher's would have.

**Priority:** High

---

### Issue 9

**Passage:**
> "He was crouching at the water's edge. She could see his profile"

**Problem:** "Profile" in the sense of a lateral view of a face is relatively neutral, but combined with later uses of "profile" as a dossier-document, it creates contamination. This specific use is lower priority but worth noting.

**Why it breaks immersion:** The word "profile" appears three times in the chapter. Two uses carry the modern investigative/market-research sense (building a "complete profile," delivering "rarity" to buyers). This third use — visual description of his face from the side — is the ordinary English meaning and would have been in use in pre-modern writing. It creates no standalone problem. However, the repetition allows the administrative sense to infect the visual one, creating a slightly clinical tone where Vark seems to be processing his appearance as data rather than as perception.

**Suggested direction:** "She could see his face from the side" or "She could see his face in half-view." Reserve "profile" for the one moment that best captures her market-research mentality; use the direct visual description here.

**Priority:** Low

---

### Issue 10

**Passage:**
> "a preliminary healing curve by nightfall"

**Problem:** "Healing curve" is modern clinical/quantitative language.

**Why it breaks immersion:** A "curve" in the sense of a plotted rate of change — as in a healing curve, a learning curve, a demand curve — is a concept borrowed from mathematics and its application to empirical measurement. While curves exist in pre-industrial geometry, the phrase "healing curve" as a descriptor for a rate-of-recovery measurement belongs to modern medicine, physiology, and the language of clinical outcome measurement. Vark is thinking quantitatively here, which fits her character; the vocabulary is the problem.

**Suggested direction:** "She would count the drops again at dusk and have a reckoning of how fast he closed by nightfall." Or: "by nightfall she would know his rate." Stay quantitative but use the merchant's or tracker's vocabulary rather than the physician's.

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 11

**Passage:**
> "The flesh economy had established tiers, and at the top, reserved for the kind of wealth that did not negotiate, was the category of unprecedented."

**Problem:** "Tiers" and "the category of unprecedented" carry modern economic/commercial register.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Tiers" as a formal classification of market strata is a modern economic concept. While pre-industrial commerce certainly had rankings, the formal conceptualization of a market into numbered or labeled "tiers" belongs to the vocabulary of modern business and organizational theory. "The category of unprecedented" compounds this with the language of market segmentation and product classification. Vark is a sophisticated professional, and she should think in terms of value and rarity — but through the idiom of the trade she actually operates in, not the corporate taxonomy of a modern market analyst.

**Suggested direction:** "The flesh trade had its grades, and at the top — where only the kind of wealth that does not name a price could reach — sat what could not be named because it had never existed before." Or more briefly: "The trade had its heights. She had never dealt at those heights. She was dealing there now." The key is to convey market stratification without the corporate register of "tiers" and "category."

**Priority:** Medium

---

### Issue 12

**Passage:**
> "a body carrying two demons' properties in integrated form"

**Problem:** "Properties" and "integrated form" are modern scientific/chemical descriptors.

**Why it breaks immersion:** "Properties" in the sense of the measurable characteristics of a substance or organism (as in chemical properties, material properties) and "integrated form" as a descriptor for a combined or merged state belong to the vocabulary of modern chemistry, biology, and materials science. A pre-industrial trader thinking about what makes this man valuable would think in different terms: what he carries, what he has taken in, what runs through him, what two natures have become one inside him.

**Suggested direction:** "a body where two demons had become one thing, woven through each other rather than merely layered." Or: "a man who carried both natures inside him, not one on top of the other but grown together." The concept is complex — preserve the complexity with the world's own vocabulary.

**Priority:** Medium

## Overall Diagnosis

Chapter 20 is a strong chapter. The concept is well executed: Vark's professional detachment reads as genuinely chilling, the tracking sequence earns its length, and the clinical wounding scene achieves exactly the disturbing effect the outline specifies. The prose is controlled and intentional throughout.

The setting consistency problem is concentrated and specific: the author has given Vark the vocabulary of a modern market analyst, clinical researcher, and forensic specialist rather than the vocabulary of a pre-industrial flesh trader who has absorbed the language of her profession organically. The individual intrusions are mostly localized — a word here, a phrase there — but they cluster around Vark's professional interior monologue, and they collectively give her a register that belongs to a contemporary world rather than this one.

The good news is that the underlying thinking is right. Vark should think in categories, rates, grades, and values. She should be quantitative and detached. The emotional content of her POV is cold and calculating. None of that needs to change. Only the vocabulary needs to be calibrated to a world without chemistry, clinical medicine, modern statistics, or corporate market segmentation.

---

## Top 10 Most Immersion-Breaking Issues (Ranked by Impact)

1. **"augmented" (three instances)** — The single most pervasive intrusion. Applies her own enhanced senses and Hurkuzak's enhanced body alike to a technical term belonging to modern human-enhancement discourse. Appears in witness dialogue, in Vark's professional assessment, and in her own perception. Highest impact because it runs through the chapter's core conceit.

2. **"subject" (multiple instances)** — Bureaucratic/clinical label for Hurkuzak. Belongs to the institutional vocabulary of research ethics, criminal justice, and clinical trials. Drains humanity from the POV in a way the text intends, but through the wrong idiom.

3. **"She ran the numbers"** — A vivid modern idiom that momentarily breaks the pre-industrial register precisely at a point where Vark's quantitative detachment should feel most alien and chilling.

4. **"already oxidizing in the spring air"** — Chemical-science vocabulary applied to blood drying. Specific and jarring because the observation is correct but the mechanism ("oxidizing") belongs to post-industrial chemistry.

5. **"Through her augmented vision"** — The most self-aware of the three "augmented" uses: Vark applying clinical enhancement terminology to her own perception rather than to the subject she is assessing.

6. **"a broadhead designed for penetration testing"** — Industrial product-specification phrasing. The concept is right; the register is contemporary manufacturing and ballistics research.

7. **"a preliminary healing curve by nightfall"** — Modern clinical outcome measurement language applied to field tracking.

8. **"The flesh economy had established tiers"** — Corporate market-stratification language where the trade's own vocabulary would serve better.

9. **"a body carrying two demons' properties in integrated form"** — Chemistry and biology register for a concept the world's own idiom could carry more effectively.

10. **"ligaments"** — Isolated clinical anatomical term in an opening passage otherwise written in good observational register.

---

## Pattern Summary

The recurring weakness is a single, consistent error: Vark's professional interiority has been written in the vocabulary of modern institutions — clinical medicine, market analysis, biological research, forensic documentation — rather than in the vocabulary of the pre-industrial trade she actually practices. The author correctly identifies that Vark should be calculating, categorical, and detached. The implementation assigns her the wrong vocabulary set for expressing those qualities.

The chapter's worldbuilding is not incoherent. The flesh economy, the tracking methodology, the bolt wound as diagnostic tool — all of these are internally consistent with the setting. The problem is that the language used to describe what Vark does and thinks keeps reaching for modern professional registers to convey professional competence and detachment, when pre-industrial equivalents are available and would serve better.

This is narrator-voice drift, not worldbuilding inconsistency. The story knows what world it is in. The narration periodically forgets.

---

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Replace all three instances of "augmented"** with sensory and experiential language appropriate to a world where enhanced capabilities are understood through flesh, consumption, and embodied change — not through technical specification.

2. **Replace "subject" throughout** with "the man," "the quarry," "this one," or trade-specific nominals. Reserve clinical distance for Vark's attitude, not her vocabulary.

3. **Replace "She ran the numbers"** with a period-appropriate reckoning idiom or cut the frame and begin directly with the calculation.

4. **Replace "oxidizing"** with a sensory description of blood drying and darkening.

5. **Replace "penetration testing"** with a functional description of what the bolt is made to do and why she chose it.

6. **Replace "augmented vision"** with language that describes Vark's enhanced sight from inside her experience of it.

7. **Replace "preliminary healing curve"** with a reckoning/counting phrase.

8. **Revise "tiers" and "category of unprecedented"** to use the flesh trade's own grade vocabulary rather than modern market segmentation.

9. **Revise "properties" and "integrated form"** to describe the two-demon composite in the world's own idiom.

10. **Replace "ligaments"** in the opening passage with a practical, observational anatomical description.

---

## Failure Mode Assessment

This chapter is failing because of **isolated wording problems**, not deeper worldbuilding inconsistency or narrator-voice drift in the structural sense. The setting, the characters, the scene logic, and the emotional architecture are all sound. The intrusions are specific vocabulary choices concentrated in Vark's professional interior monologue. A targeted word-level pass — without restructuring any scenes — will resolve the chapter's consistency problems entirely.
