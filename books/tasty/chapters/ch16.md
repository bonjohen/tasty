Chapter 16: What Winter Leaves Behind

The snow did not melt. It compacted. The ice did not thaw. It thinned at the edges. The sky, which had been a solid gray for weeks, began to show occasional breaks where a color that was not quite blue appeared and vanished. Winter was not ending. But it was loosening.

Hurkuzak felt it in the cabin. The walls held heat longer. The fire demanded less. The draft through the gap in the door frame was still cold but had lost its killing edge — something in it now carried the smell of wet stone, of earth not quite thawed but no longer frozen solid. He did not know when spring would come, or whether it would come in time.

The corrections had graduated from practical adjustments — the fire, the door, the sleeping position — to opinions about how he moved. He walked too carefully, it informed him. He placed his feet with the deliberation of a person who expected the ground to betray him, and the placement wasted energy and announced his presence to anything listening. A man in the mountains moved by committing to each step, not by testing each step. Hurkuzak moved like prey.

He did not argue with the voice. Arguing required acknowledging it as something separate from himself, and the acknowledgment was a door he was not ready to open.

He sat by the hearth on a day when the wind was quiet, thinking about the argument — the trees and the ridge, Hakiia's insistence on the exposed path. The memory was his own. It carried the texture of his own thinking: hurt, bewildered.

The ridge had given clear eyes in every direction — far enough to see wolves before they could see a man. The trees cut that to nothing. The dog was a cost, and it was the cheaper cost.

He had not thought about sightlines. He had thought about the dog.

The correction had the quality of someone else's certainty — not recalled, not reconstructed, but known from inside the decision itself, from the position of whoever had made it. He had not been in that position. He had been behind, watching, not understanding.

He looked at the fire. He checked the door. He did not name what he had heard.

The voice did not press the point. It waited. It had a particular way of waiting. Hurkuzak had been on the receiving end of that kind of patience before. He recognized it without wanting to. He looked away from the recognition the way he would look away from something seen in poor light — not because it disappeared, but because looking directly would make it real.

To name it would be to make it true. He did not name it.

The voice had other opinions. It disliked the way Hurkuzak ate — slowly, carefully, with the deliberation of a person still weighing each bite. There was impatience in it, the pressure of a person waiting for you to stop hesitating.

One evening he pressed his hand against the granite wall behind the hearth and found it warm — the rock holding the day's heat and giving it back slowly in the dark. The thought arrived that whoever built this cabin had known, had chosen the granite face for that reason.

He caught his reflection in the water pot one morning. The features were the same — the jaw, the cheekbones, the scar on his palm. But the softness Hakiia had tried to burn away was diminished. Not gone, but overlaid with something harder — a stillness that watched. He looked at it for a moment. Then he looked away.

He left the cabin.

Not permanently. Not as a departure. As a test. He walked past the tree line, down the slope toward the valley where he had dragged the travois. The walking was easy. The landscape was readable — the terrain speaking in a language he now understood, where the wind had carved the surface into shapes that told you which way it always came from.

He could track. He had never learned to. The skills were in his hands, in his feet, in the way his eyes moved across the surface of the snow before he understood what they were looking for. He stopped at a place where something had crossed in the night — the depression of the prints still faintly visible under a skin of new powder — and he knew what it was, roughly when it had passed, roughly which direction it was moving. He did not know how he knew. He stood in the snow and felt the knowledge sitting in him like something that had always been there, waiting for the right terrain.

He used it. He had already done too much to survive to start refusing now.

He walked for an hour. When he returned, he stood in the doorway and looked at the room that had been his world. The last of Hakiia's body was gone. He had used everything — bone for broth, marrow rendered, the final sinew boiled soft. The alcove was empty. He looked at the bare stone floor where his brother's body had lain. He had cleaned it himself — on his knees, methodical, final. Cold stone, empty air, the smell of nothing.

He closed the alcove door for the last time.

The light grew. The snow compacted. The first sounds of running water came from somewhere below the cabin — a stream or a seep released by the first loosening of ice.

The cabin's grip was loosening. Beyond the mountains was a world he would need to reenter.

He stood outside one morning as the light came over the ridge. The warmth on his face was thin, provisional, the warmth of a season that had not yet committed. He could feel his own stillness — not the stillness of cold or exhaustion, but the stillness of a body waiting, listening, aware that the other presence in the room was listening too. He had not asked for this. He had not consented to it. He had survived, and survival did not end when the act ended.

He would leave soon. He would walk out of these mountains carrying more than he had when he walked in, and not all of it was his.
