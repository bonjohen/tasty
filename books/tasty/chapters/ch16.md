# Chapter 16: What Winter Leaves Behind

The snow did not melt. It compacted. The ice did not thaw. It thinned at the edges. The sky, which had been a solid gray for weeks, began to show occasional breaks where a color that was not quite blue appeared and vanished. Winter was not ending. But it was loosening.

Hurkuzak felt it in the cabin. The walls held heat longer. The fire demanded less. The draft through the gap in the door frame was still cold but had lost its killing edge — something in it now carried the smell of wet stone, of earth not quite thawed but no longer frozen solid. He did not know when spring would come, or whether it would come in time.

The voice had continued.

It was more frequent now and harder to dismiss. The corrections had graduated from practical adjustments — the fire, the door, the sleeping position — to opinions about how he moved. He walked too carefully, it informed him. He placed his feet with the deliberation of a person who expected the ground to betray him, and the placement wasted energy and announced his presence to anything listening. A man in the mountains moved by committing to each step, not by testing each step. Hurkuzak moved like prey.

He did not argue with the voice. Arguing required acknowledging it as something separate from himself, and the acknowledgment was a door he was not ready to open.

But the voice had more to say.

He was sitting by the hearth on a day when the wind was quiet, and his mind drifted to the journey — the trail before the cliff, the path Hakiia had chosen along the ridgeline. He was thinking about the argument — the trees and the ridge, Hakiia's insistence on the exposed path. The memory was his own. It carried the texture of his own thinking: hurt, bewildered, the old confusion of a man trying to understand why his brother chose speed over mercy.

Then the correction came.

The ridge had given clear eyes in every direction — far enough to see wolves before they could see a man. The trees cut that to nothing. The dog was a cost, and it was the cheaper cost.

He had not thought about sightlines. He had thought about the dog.

The correction had the quality of someone else's certainty — not recalled, not reconstructed, but known from inside the decision itself, from the position of whoever had made it. He had not been in that position. He had been behind, watching, not understanding.

He looked at the fire. He checked the door. He did not name what he had heard.

The voice did not press the point. It waited. It had a particular way of waiting — the certainty of a person who does not need to argue because they already know which way the argument will resolve. Hurkuzak had been on the receiving end of that kind of patience before. He recognized it without wanting to. He looked away from the recognition the way he would look away from something seen in poor light — not because it disappeared, but because looking directly would make it real.

He did not name it. To name it would be to say his dead brother's mind was alive inside his own, and he did not want to think the thing he was starting to think — because if he thought it, there was no way to unthink it. Because it might be true.

The voice had other opinions. It disliked the way Hurkuzak ate — slowly, carefully, with the deliberation of a person who was still thinking about each bite. There was impatience in it, the pressure of a person waiting for you to stop hesitating. One evening he pressed his hand against the granite wall behind the hearth and found it warm — the rock holding the day's heat and giving it back slowly in the dark. The thought arrived, unbidden, that whoever built this cabin had known that. Had chosen the granite face for that reason. Had placed the hearth against it so the stone would work through the night after the fire burned low. Hurkuzak had never thought about a cabin this way.

He caught his reflection in the water pot one morning. The features were the same — the jaw, the cheekbones, the scar on his palm. But the softness Hakiia had tried to burn away was diminished. Not gone, but overlaid with something harder — a stillness that watched. He looked at it for a moment. Then he looked away.

He left the cabin.

Not permanently. Not as a departure. As a test. He walked past the tree line, down the slope toward the valley where he'd dragged the travois. The walking was easy. The landscape was readable — the terrain speaking in a language he now understood, where the wind had carved the surface into shapes that told you which way the wind always came from.

He could track. He had never learned to. The skills were in his hands, in his feet, in the way his eyes moved across the surface of the snow before he understood what they were looking for. He stopped at a place where something had crossed in the night — the depression of the prints still faintly visible under a skin of new powder — and he knew what it was, roughly when it had passed, roughly which direction it was moving. He did not know how he knew. He stood in the snow and felt the knowledge sitting in him like something that had always been there, waiting for the right terrain.

He used it. He had already done too much to survive to start refusing now.

He walked for an hour. When he returned, he stood in the doorway and looked at the room that had been his world. The last of Hakiia's body was gone. He had used everything — bone for broth, marrow for sustenance, the final sinew boiled soft. The alcove was empty. He looked at the bare stone floor where his brother's body had lain. He had cleaned it himself — on his knees, methodical, final. Cold stone, empty air, the smell of nothing.

He closed the alcove door for the last time.

The days continued. The light grew. The snow compacted further. The first sounds of running water came from somewhere below the cabin — a stream or a seep released by the earliest loosening of ice.

The season was turning. The cabin's grip was loosening. Somewhere beyond the mountains was a world he would need to reenter.

He stood outside one morning as the light came over the ridge. The warmth on his face was thin, provisional, the warmth of a season that had not yet committed. He could feel his own stillness — not the stillness of cold or exhaustion, but the stillness of a body that was waiting for something, listening for something, in the way a person listens when they know the other presence in the room is also listening. He had not asked for this. He had not consented to it. He had simply survived, and survival had its costs, and the costs did not stop accruing when the act was over.

He would leave soon. That was all he knew. He would walk out of these mountains carrying more than he had when he walked in, and not all of it was his.
