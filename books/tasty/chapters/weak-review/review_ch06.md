# Editorial Review: Chapter 6 — What Hunger Teaches

## Issue Log

### 1.
**Passage:** "the snow deep enough that each step required him to lift his knees above the surface and press forward and sink and lift again"
**Problem:** Triple-and chain / wordiness. Four verbs chained with "and" to describe a repetitive action. The mechanics of snow-walking are over-described.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence already has three strong clauses before this one (basin narrowing, trees thin and bent, snow deep). By the time we arrive at "lift...press...sink...lift" the reader is slogging through the prose the same way Hurkuzak is slogging through the snow -- but that effect can be achieved with less.
**Revision approach:** Compress. "Deep enough that each step was a labor of lifting, sinking, lifting again." Or cut the mechanics entirely and let "deep enough" do the work alongside the next sentence about burning legs.
**Priority:** Medium

### 2.
**Passage:** "His ribs sent a flare of pain with every breath that went deeper than shallow."
**Problem:** None. This is precise, physical, and earns its place. The construction "deeper than shallow" is good -- it defines the constraint without naming the injury.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 3.
**Passage:** "His cut palm had opened again under the friction of the drag poles and the blood made the wood slick and warm in a way that felt like the only warmth left in the world."
**Problem:** Overwriting. "In a way that felt like the only warmth left in the world" is a rhetorical reach. The blood on the wood is a strong, concrete image. The cosmic claim about warmth dilutes it.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence is doing two jobs: physical detail (blood on wood) and thematic commentary (warmth scarcity). The physical detail is better on its own.
**Revision approach:** "His cut palm had opened again under the friction of the drag poles. The blood made the wood slick and warm." Stop there. The cold is already established. The reader supplies the irony.
**Priority:** Medium

### 4.
**Passage:** "Behind him, Hakiia lay on the travois with his eyes open and his hands at his sides and his broken legs pointing at the sky that was darkening from gray toward the black that meant real cold, the kind that killed at its own pace and did not care whether you were ready."
**Problem:** Run-on / overloaded sentence. This sentence tries to carry: Hakiia's position, his eyes, his hands, his legs, the sky color, and a philosophical statement about the cold. Three "and" chains plus a relative clause plus a second relative clause.
**Why it weakens:** The image of Hakiia on the travois is strong. The sky-into-cold meditation crowds it. The reader loses the visual clarity in the sentence's length.
**Revision approach:** Split. "Behind him, Hakiia lay on the travois with his eyes open and his broken legs pointed at the sky. The sky was darkening from gray toward black -- the kind of black that meant real cold." Cut "the kind that killed at its own pace and did not care whether you were ready" entirely. It's a generic cold-is-deadly observation that the chapter has already earned through physical detail.
**Priority:** High

### 5.
**Passage:** "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying, the way a current accompanies a leaf, patient and steady and moving toward the same destination for different reasons."
**Problem:** Overwriting / stacked metaphor. The distinction between pursuing and accompanying is sharp. The current-and-leaf simile that follows weakens it -- the simile makes wolves passive (a current) and Hurkuzak natural (a leaf), which inverts the actual dynamic. Then "patient and steady and" is another triple-and.
**Why it weakens:** The first two sentences do the work. The simile is decorative, and the triple-and adjective chain further dilutes it.
**Revision approach:** "They were not pursuing. They were accompanying." Full stop. Let the word "accompanying" carry the menace. It already does.
**Priority:** High

### 6.

**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 7.
**Passage:** "with a thick winter coat and a wide head and eyes that held the flat reflective quality of something that did not need to understand what it was looking at in order to know what to do with it"
**Problem:** Overwriting. "The flat reflective quality of something that did not need to understand what it was looking at in order to know what to do with it" is a 26-word clause appended to a physical description. The philosophical content (instinct vs. understanding) is the writer editorializing about the wolf's gaze.
**Why it weakens:** "A thick winter coat and a wide head" is concrete and effective. The clause about understanding vs. knowing is an essay fragment, not a physical observation. It tells the reader what the eyes mean rather than what they look like.
**Revision approach:** Describe the eyes concretely. "Eyes that reflected the light without holding it" or "eyes that held nothing he could read." Keep it physical.
**Priority:** High

### 8.
**Passage:** "The cold stood between them and did not choose a side."
**Problem:** Overwriting / personification. The cold as a neutral bystander is a reach. The sentence is trying for a McCarthy-style tricolon (Hurkuzak stood / wolf stood / cold stood) but the third beat personifies the weather in a way that tips toward ornament.
**Why it weakens:** The standoff between man and wolf is tense. The personified cold breaks the tension with a literary flourish. The reader notices the writer.
**Revision approach:** Cut the sentence. "Hurkuzak stood. The wolf stood." The silence between them does what the cold-personification is trying to do, without the artifice.
**Priority:** Medium

### 9.

### 10.

**Why it weakens:** The physical details are strong. The introductory clause pre-announces them, reducing their impact.

**Why it weakens:** The reader already knows Hakiia is broken. The passage tells us it should look absurd, then tells us it doesn't, then tells us what it looks like instead. Three beats where one concrete image would be stronger.
**Revision approach:** "Even lying down. Even broken. The knife in his hand looked like a threat." Compress the middle.

### 23.

## Overall Diagnosis

This is a well-constructed action chapter with strong bones. The wolf fight is effectively paced, the tactical dialogue is the chapter's best asset, and several passages achieve the restrained precision the project aims for ("The hatchet caught air," "The dead wolves looked smaller than they had alive," "Between them they held one working grip"). Hakiia's voice is consistent and convincing throughout -- terse, tactical, never self-pitying.

The primary weakness is **over-explanation**. The chapter repeatedly shows something effectively and then follows it with a sentence or clause that names, summarizes, or philosophizes about what the reader just witnessed. The wolf kill costs Hakiia his hands -- the reader sees this -- and then the prose tells them "the man who had held weapons with unconscious authority his entire life had no hands left." The standoff with wolves creates menace -- and then the prose explains "the arithmetic of easy meat." The pattern is consistent: strong scene, followed by authorial commentary that trusts the reader less than the scene does.

The secondary weakness is **overwriting**. Similes and metaphors stack where one would land harder. Wolves are given philosophical relationships to knowledge. The cold personifies. Pain trajectories get forecast. The chapter's best moments are its simplest -- and there are many of them. The revision task is mostly subtraction.

The tertiary weakness is **triple-and chains**. The sentence structure "X and Y and Z and..." appears throughout, creating a rhythmic monotone that flattens emphasis. Breaking these chains will sharpen the prose and allow the strongest images to land with their full weight.

What works well: the dialogue, the pacing of the wolf approaches, the physical choreography of the fight, the sensory specificity of the bite and the cauterization, the emotional restraint of the final exchange ("I can pull"), and the repeated structural motif of Hakiia predicting exactly what will happen.

## Top 10 Problems, Ranked by Impact

1. **Hakiia summary after wolf kill** (issue #30) -- "the terrifying competence, the disproportionate violence, the refusal to be less than lethal" converts a powerful scene into a character essay. The scene already did the work.
2. **"The man who had held weapons" epigram** (issue #43) -- Summarizes the loss of Hakiia's hands when the reader just watched it happen. Trusts the reader least at the chapter's most powerful moment.
3. **Recursive blood sentence** (issue #23) -- "The fast was the problem because the fast meant..." becomes a syntactic exercise when the moment demands urgency.
4. **Violence/tenderness aphorism** (issue #13) -- "Understood violence the way Hurkuzak understood tenderness" is a thesis statement interrupting a survival scene.
5. **Wolf-accompanying simile** (issue #5) -- Current-and-leaf weakens the menace. "They were accompanying" is already terrifying.
6. **Cabin flash-forward** (issue #25) -- "The cabin that had not happened yet" breaks the temporal frame and telegraphs a future scene.
7. **Courage-fear cancellation** (issue #32) -- 30-word philosophical definition of a feeling that could be rendered in five.
8. **Breathing-as-abstraction** (issue #29) -- "The kind of breathing that comes after a person has done something that cost them everything they had left to spend" -- 22 words of abstraction where concrete description is needed.
9. **Wolf-eyes philosophy** (issue #7) -- 26-word clause editorializing about instinct vs. understanding when the reader needs to see the eyes.
10. **Tide-rhythm over-explanation** (issue #16) -- Three layers of saying the wolves are closing in when the parallel structure already shows it.

## Pattern Summary: Recurring Weaknesses

**1. Explaining after showing.** The chapter's most persistent habit. A strong image or scene beat is followed by a sentence that names the emotion, identifies the theme, or summarizes the significance. The wolf kill, the hand destruction, the standoff, the final look -- all are followed by commentary that the scenes have already earned. The chapter trusts its dialogue more than its narration. The revision should bring the narration's trust level up to match.

**2. Overwriting through stacked elaboration.** Single images are followed by explanatory clauses, similes, or philosophical asides that dilute them. "They were accompanying" becomes a current-and-leaf simile. "Careful steps" becomes a wolf learning a new fact about the world. "Cauterized stump" becomes "a dark, cauterized ruin where a hand had been." In each case, the first image is the strongest.

**3. Triple-and chains.** "And...and...and..." appears in at least six sentences, chaining independent clauses at equal syntactic weight. This flattens emphasis and creates rhythmic monotony. The chapter's best sentences are short and declarative ("The hatchet caught air." "The wolves came."). The and-chains work against that strength.

**4. Authorial aphorisms in character thought.** Several of Hurkuzak's observations sound like the writer making thematic points: violence-as-tenderness parallel, courage-and-fear cancellation, arithmetic of easy meat. These are polished beyond what the character would think in the moment.

**5. Vague "something" constructions.** "Something that did not need to understand," "something visible," "something else...something that lived behind," "something that was not courage and was not fear." The word "something" signals the prose reaching for meaning without committing to specificity.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut scene-summarizing sentences.** After every strong scene beat (wolf kill, hand destruction, standoff, final look), check: does the next sentence explain what just happened? If yes, cut it. Primary targets: issues #30, #43, #38, #13.

2. **Trim to single images.** Where a metaphor or description is followed by elaboration, keep the first image. Cut the simile, the philosophical aside, the explanatory clause. Primary targets: issues #5, #7, #16, #35, #36.

3. **Break triple-and chains.** Each chained sentence: identify the strongest clause, give it its own sentence. Subordinate or cut the rest. Primary targets: issues #4, #23, #47.

4. **Cut the cabin flash-forward** (issue #25). Let the echo work when the reader reaches that chapter. The cauterization scene is powerful enough on its own.

5. **Ground abstractions in the body.** Where the prose names feelings abstractly ("something that was not courage and was not fear," "the kind of breathing that comes after"), replace with physical rendering. Primary targets: issues #29, #32, #33.

6. **Cut redundant descriptions.** Where injuries or states are described twice (broken fingers in #42, hand loss summarized in #43), keep the first and cut the repeat.

7. **Preserve what works.** Do not touch the dialogue, the fight choreography, the sensory details of the bite and cauterization, the structural payoff of Hakiia's predictions, or the final exchange. These are the chapter's strengths and they are considerable.
