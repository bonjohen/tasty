# Editorial Review: Chapter 2 — What the Captain Keeps

---

## Issue Log

### 1.
**Passage:** "The demon had been dead since before dawn and the cold had done half of Vark's work for her."
**Problem:** None. Strong opening. Clean, efficient, establishes character, profession, tone, and setting in one sentence. The "half of Vark's work" is concrete and implies routine.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 2.
**Passage:** "and the hide separated with the sound of something consenting to come apart"
**Problem:** Vague simile. "Something consenting" is a "something [gerund]" construction that gestures at meaning without landing. What consents? The image is reaching for an unsettling personification but doesn't commit to a concrete sound.
**Why it weakens:** The paragraph is otherwise rigorously physical -- scoring, peeling, cutting. This simile breaks the tactile register for an abstraction.
**Revision approach:** Commit to the sound. What does separating hide actually sound like? A wet tearing. A slow peel. Or commit to the personification: "with the sound of flesh letting go." That's concrete and unsettling without hedging behind "something."
**Priority:** Medium

### 3.
**Passage:** "The first had been difficult -- not emotionally, she had seen worse by then, but technically."
**Problem:** Unnecessary parenthetical explanation. "Not emotionally, she had seen worse by then" is Vark explaining herself to the reader. It pre-empts a question the reader may not have asked, and the clarification breaks the sentence's momentum.
**Why it weakens:** The chapter is establishing Vark as unsentimental. Pausing to clarify that the difficulty was technical, not emotional, is itself a sentimental gesture -- it shows self-consciousness about what the reader might think. A truly unsentimental character (and narrator) would just say it was technically difficult.
**Revision approach:** Cut the parenthetical. "The first had been difficult. Demon anatomy ran close enough to human..." The reader will understand the difficulty is technical from the sentences that follow.
**Priority:** Medium

### 4.
**Passage:** "Demon anatomy ran close enough to human that the instinct was to cut where you would cut for a human carcass, and that instinct was wrong in several important places."
**Problem:** Wordiness. "That instinct was wrong in several important places" restates what "close enough to human" already implies. The next three sentences then enumerate exactly where the anatomy differs, making the summary redundant.
**Why it weakens:** The sentence announces what the paragraph is about to demonstrate. The demonstration is strong; the announcement is unnecessary.
**Revision approach:** Cut "and that instinct was wrong in several important places." Move directly from the instinct to where it fails: "Demon anatomy ran close enough to human that the instinct was to cut where you would cut for a human carcass. The organs sat differently."
**Priority:** Low

### 5.
**Passage:** "The organs sat differently. The muscle attachments anchored at slightly different points along the bone. The rib structure was wider and more dense than it should have been."
**Problem:** None. This is good. Three short declaratives, each specific. The rhythm is flat and professional -- it sounds like how Vark thinks. "Than it should have been" is the only phrase that carries any editorial weight, and it's the right kind: not emotional, just a practitioner noting deviation from the expected model.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 6.
**Passage:** "Learning the map had cost her three kills and the loss of a liver that would have covered two months of supplies."
**Problem:** None. Good. Concrete cost, specific detail. "Three kills and the loss of a liver" -- the liver gets its own mention because it's the highest-value organ, which the next paragraph will establish. Efficient worldbuilding.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 7.
**Passage:** "She knew the map now. She could have drawn the cuts with her eyes closed."
**Problem:** Continuing after the point has been made. "She could have drawn the cuts with her eyes closed" is a cliche (doing something with eyes closed = mastery) that adds nothing to "She knew the map now." The first sentence is sufficient and more original.
**Why it weakens:** "Eyes closed" is a stock phrase for expertise. The "map" metaphor is specific to Vark's learned anatomy; the eyes-closed line is generic.
**Revision approach:** Cut "She could have drawn the cuts with her eyes closed." "She knew the map now" carries the weight alone.
**Priority:** Medium

### 8.
**Passage:** "She had used it for the last four jobs and the tanner had stopped asking questions after the second payment."
**Problem:** None. Good detail. "After the second payment" is the right kind of specificity -- it tells the reader how quickly money buys silence in this world.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 9.
**Passage:** "Vark paid in advance, cleaned thoroughly, and kept her work to the hours between midnight and dawn when the streets were empty and curiosity was sleeping."
**Problem:** Personification. "Curiosity was sleeping" is a mild overreach. The rest of the sentence is concrete operational detail. The personification is a small poetic gesture that doesn't match Vark's voice.
**Why it weakens:** Vark thinks in logistics, not metaphor. This is the author briefly overriding the character's register.
**Revision approach:** "When the streets were empty" is sufficient. Cut "and curiosity was sleeping." Or replace with something concrete: "when the streets were empty and no one was looking."
**Priority:** Low

### 10.
**Passage:** "with the specific knowledge that not knowing was worth more than knowing, and that the woman who rented the room paid enough to make the distinction comfortable"
**Problem:** Overwriting. This is a clever formulation -- "not knowing was worth more than knowing" -- but it's doing double duty: it both explains the tanner's arrangement and editorializes about how willful ignorance works in the trade. The next clause ("paid enough to make the distinction comfortable") then explains the first clause.
**Why it weakens:** Two layers of explanation for a simple idea: the tanner doesn't ask because the money is good. The sentence before this one ("the way the rest of the trade town understood") already sets up the idea of collective avoidance.
**Revision approach:** Simplify. "The tanner understood what she did in his back room the way the rest of the trade town understood: by choosing not to." The money is already established (she pays in advance). The comfort of the arrangement is implied.
**Priority:** Medium

### 11.
**Passage:** "A fresh one from a mature demon -- properly drained, cleanly removed, no puncture to the bile sac -- could cover her costs for a season."
**Problem:** None. Good. The parenthetical conditions (drained, removed, no puncture) are concrete professional knowledge, not decoration. They earn their place.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 12.
**Passage:** "She had seen what the final buyers paid for the same liver after it passed through two more hands, and the number was enough to make a person angry if anger were useful. It was not."
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. "It was not" is a two-word sentence that tells the reader Vark does not get angry, which the reader already understands from everything about her character so far. The sentence before it -- "enough to make a person angry if anger were useful" -- is the stronger construction because it contains Vark's worldview in its conditional grammar ("if anger were useful"). "It was not" undercuts that by explaining the conditional.
**Why it weakens:** The conditional already implies that anger is not useful to her. The follow-up is a blunt restatement.
**Revision approach:** Cut "It was not." End on "if anger were useful." The reader gets it.
**Priority:** Medium

### 13.
**Passage:** "Vark understood the architecture of the trade the way she understood anatomy: as a system with fixed relationships between parts, where her position was defined by what she could reach and what she could not. She was the hands. The profit belonged to the people who owned the table."
**Problem:** Overwriting / stacked metaphors. Three metaphors in rapid succession: architecture, anatomy, and then the hands-and-table image. Each is individually competent, but stacked they feel like the prose is working too hard to be insightful. "She was the hands. The profit belonged to the people who owned the table" is strong and concrete. The preceding sentence is the scaffolding that should be removed to reveal it.
**Why it weakens:** The hands/table image does all the work. The anatomy comparison and the "system with fixed relationships" phrasing are abstract setup for a concrete payoff. Cut the setup.
**Revision approach:** After the anger sentence, go directly to the image: "She was the hands. The profit belonged to the people who owned the table." The reader understands the system from this alone.
**Priority:** High

### 14.
**Passage:** "The heart carried less value in isolation, but certain physicians in the southern cities prescribed it paired with the liver -- something about the interaction of properties when consumed together. Vark did not know the theory and did not need to. She knew the price, and the price was better when the pair arrived in the same box from the same body."
**Problem:** Wordiness / repetition. "Vark did not know the theory and did not need to" is a throat-clearing transition. The next sentence ("She knew the price") already implies she doesn't know the theory by demonstrating what she does know instead. Also, "the price" appears three times in two sentences.
**Why it weakens:** The paragraph is saying: she doesn't know, she doesn't need to know, but she knows the price. That's three beats for one idea. The middle beat is filler.
**Revision approach:** Cut "Vark did not know the theory and did not need to." Go directly from the physicians' practice to: "She knew the price was better when the pair arrived in the same box from the same body." One sentence. Same information.
**Priority:** Medium

### 15.
**Passage:** "Demon hands carried a value she had always found faintly absurd -- she suspected superstition more than pharmacology -- but the market set the price and the price was the price."
**Problem:** None. "The market set the price and the price was the price" is a strong Vark-voice line. The tautology conveys her pragmatism. "Superstition more than pharmacology" is the right amount of opinion for this character.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 16.
**Passage:** "She worked the shoulders next, then stripped the long muscles running parallel to the spine. These carried a modest value for their density and preserved well if salted within the first few hours."
**Problem:** Minor showing-too-much. The market details about muscle value and salting timeline are starting to accumulate. The chapter has now priced the liver, kidneys, heart, hands, bone, and muscle. The worldbuilding is becoming an inventory list.
**Why it weakens:** The reader understands the economy from the liver and heart paragraphs. Each additional pricing note yields diminishing returns on characterization and worldbuilding. By the sixth item, the reader knows the system.
**Revision approach:** Compress the muscle paragraph. Cut the value and salting details. "She worked the shoulders next, then stripped the long muscles along the spine and packed them in salted cloth." The salting is the only physical detail that matters for the scene.
**Priority:** Low

### 17.
**Passage:** "attention without ceremony, care without sentiment. A job. Her job. She was good at it."
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. The entire chapter to this point has demonstrated attention without ceremony and care without sentiment. These four sentences name what the reader has been watching for two pages. "A job. Her job. She was good at it" is three sentences for one idea, and the idea is already self-evident.
**Why it weakens:** This is the chapter's most explicit trust-failure. The prose has been superb at showing Vark's professionalism. These sentences stop showing and start labeling.
**Revision approach:** Cut from "attention without ceremony" through "She was good at it." End the paragraph on the rhythm of the work: "and she moved through it with the rhythm of long practice." That's the sentence that earns its place.
**Priority:** High

### 18.
**Passage:** "It still had a face. The face had been unremarkable alive and was less so now."
**Problem:** None. "It still had a face" is strong. The blunt observation followed by the understated "was less so now" is exactly Vark's voice -- clinical, dry, unbothered. Good.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 19.
**Passage:** "moving like someone who did not expect to be watched. He had been wrong about that."
**Problem:** None. Clean. "He had been wrong about that" is perfectly timed. No overwriting.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 20.
**Passage:** "That was the standard. Clean kill, clean product. Everything else was waste."
**Problem:** Telling / continuing after the point. "That was the standard" is a throat-clearing label. "Clean kill, clean product" is the standard -- it doesn't need to be announced first. "Everything else was waste" is a third sentence restating the same idea.
**Why it weakens:** "Clean kill, clean product" is sharp and efficient, perfectly Vark. The framing sentence before and the summary sentence after dilute it.
**Revision approach:** Cut "That was the standard." Cut "Everything else was waste." Leave: "Clean kill, clean product." Period. The reader hears the mantra.
**Priority:** Medium

### 21.
**Passage:** "He was a factor for one of the southern trading houses -- a middleman who connected hunters like Vark to the buyers who consumed what hunters provided."
**Problem:** Unnecessary explanation. "A middleman who connected hunters like Vark to the buyers who consumed what hunters provided" is the definition of "factor" spelled out. The word "factor" already means middleman. Even if the reader doesn't know the word, the context makes the role clear in the next paragraph.
**Why it weakens:** This is exposition disguised as narration. Vark knows what a factor is. She wouldn't define the term in her own thoughts.
**Revision approach:** Cut "a middleman who connected hunters like Vark to the buyers who consumed what hunters provided." Leave "He was a factor for one of the southern trading houses." The reader will understand from context.
**Priority:** Medium

### 22.
**Passage:** "He was not fat, exactly, but he carried the softness of a man whose labor was conducted at desks and in counting rooms."
**Problem:** Wordiness. "He was not fat, exactly, but he carried the softness of a man whose labor was conducted at desks and in counting rooms" is a long way to say he was desk-soft. The "not fat, exactly, but" construction is hedging.
**Why it weakens:** The hedge ("not fat, exactly") is a tic of diplomatic narration, but Vark isn't diplomatic. She's observational. She'd note what she sees, not qualify it.
**Revision approach:** "He carried the softness of desk-work and counting rooms." Or simpler: "He was soft the way men who work at desks are soft." Cut the negation-hedge.
**Priority:** Low

### 25.

### 26.
**Passage:** "The ritual of inspection was part of the transaction."
**Problem:** Throat-clearing / telling. The sentence names what the reader is watching ("ritual of inspection") and classifies it ("part of the transaction"). The reader already sees this from Koss's behavior.
**Why it weakens:** The physical description of Koss (lifting lids, leaning in, not getting close enough to smell) is doing the work. This sentence stops the scene to label it.

### 27.
**Passage:** "Vark doubted Koss could tell a good liver from a bad one without help, but the performance served its purpose and she allowed it because Koss paid reliably, did not renegotiate after agreeing to a price, and did not ask where the bodies came from. That last quality was worth more than the other two combined."
**Problem:** Mostly good, but "the performance served its purpose and she allowed it" is slightly wordy. Vark "allowing" the performance implies she has a choice about whether to let her buyer inspect the product, which overstates her position. The three-item list of Koss's virtues is strong. "That last quality was worth more than the other two combined" is effective.
**Why it weakens:** Minor. The word "allowed" slightly mischaracterizes the power dynamic. Sellers don't typically "allow" buyers to inspect goods -- it's standard practice.
**Revision approach:** Cut "the performance served its purpose and she allowed it." Restructure: "Vark doubted Koss could tell a good liver from a bad one without help, but he paid reliably, did not renegotiate after agreeing to a price, and did not ask where the bodies came from." Tighter.
**Priority:** Low

### 28.
**Passage:** "Last time the client was blending product from three suppliers and calling it a single source. That is a quality problem I cannot solve from my end."

### 29.
**Passage:** "Koss conceded the point with a movement of his head that fell short of a nod."
**Problem:** Wordiness. "A movement of his head that fell short of a nod" is a circumlocution for a near-nod or a half-nod.
**Why it weakens:** The precision is admirable (he doesn't quite nod), but the construction takes too many words to convey it. The reader processes "movement of his head that fell short of a nod" more slowly than the gesture it describes.
**Revision approach:** "Koss almost nodded." Or: "Koss tipped his head -- not quite a nod." Shorter, same image.
**Priority:** Low

### 30.
**Passage:** "She said it the way she said most things -- as a statement of fact that did not require agreement in order to be true."

**Why it weakens:** Vark's speech throughout the chapter already demonstrates that she states facts without seeking agreement. This sentence is redundant with the entire characterization.
**Revision approach:** Cut the sentence. The dialogue speaks for itself.

### 31.

The next two sentences explain why she watches, but the explanation -- "the discipline of being someone who was not shorted" -- is an abstraction layered on top of a behavior that was already clear. The negation-negation construction ("Not because... Because") is a rhetorical structure that draws attention to itself.
**Why it weakens:** "She always watched" tells the reader everything. The philosophical gloss about discipline dilutes the image of a woman silently counting alongside a man who is counting.
**Revision approach:** Cut from "Not because" through "was not shorted." Leave: "Vark watched him count because she always watched." Period.
**Priority:** High

### 35.
**Passage:** "Someone is paying a premium for fresh material from the interior. Not aged, not dried, not preserved. Fresh. They want it still warm when it arrives."
**Problem:** Repetition. "Fresh" appears twice -- once in "fresh material from the interior" and once as a standalone sentence. The "Not aged, not dried, not preserved" list is strong on its own, but the second "Fresh" is a dramatic restatement that tips into a speech pattern more theatrical than Koss's established voice.
**Why it weakens:** Koss has been presented as a nervous, soft middleman. The dramatic repetition ("Fresh.") and the capper ("still warm when it arrives") make him sound like he's performing a monologue. The first three sentences of the speech are more natural.
**Revision approach:** Cut the standalone "Fresh." Go from "Not aged, not dried, not preserved" directly to "They want it still warm when it arrives." The last line does the dramatic work without the theatrical pause.
**Priority:** Low

### 36.
**Passage:** "She folded it with one hand, gripping and turning the stiff leather with an ease that had nothing to do with technique. It was strength. More strength than a woman her size should carry"
**Problem:** Overwriting / telling. "An ease that had nothing to do with technique" names the source of the ease before the next sentence does. "It was strength" is the reveal, but "had nothing to do with technique" pre-reveals it. Then "More strength than a woman her size should carry" restates the same idea with explicit gendered framing that isn't Vark's register.
**Why it weakens:** Three sentences to make one point: she's unnaturally strong. The first sentence pre-explains, the second names, the third emphasizes. One would do.
**Revision approach:** "She folded it with one hand. More strength than her frame should have carried, and Koss looked away..." Cut the middle steps. Let the physical action and Koss's reaction convey the unnaturalness.
**Priority:** High

### 37.
**Passage:** "and Koss looked away from the display with the practiced discomfort of a man who knew exactly what that strength meant and preferred not to think about it"
**Problem:** Explaining after showing. Koss looking away is the show. "With the practiced discomfort of a man who knew exactly what that strength meant and preferred not to think about it" is the tell. The sentence names Koss's knowledge, his discomfort, and his preference for avoidance -- three emotional states for one gesture (looking away).
**Why it weakens:** The looking-away is eloquent on its own. The reader, who has been told about demon-consumption properties, can supply the meaning. The explanatory tail removes the reader's opportunity to make the connection.
**Revision approach:** "Koss looked away." Two words. The reader knows why.
**Priority:** High

### 38.
**Passage:** "That was fair. Most people preferred not to think about it."
**Problem:** Continuing after the point / throat-clearing. "That was fair" is Vark's editorial on Koss's reaction, but it's a weak observation -- the reader doesn't need Vark's judgment of Koss's discomfort. "Most people preferred not to think about it" generalizes the specific moment into a universal observation.
**Why it weakens:** Both sentences pull the reader out of the scene and into commentary. The next paragraph (about Vark's own consumption) is the real destination, and these two sentences are a landing strip.
**Revision approach:** Cut both sentences. Go directly from Koss looking away to Vark's internal reflection on her own consumption.
**Priority:** Medium

### 39.
**Passage:** "Vark herself thought about it only in practical terms. You ate what you killed, in measured amounts, over time. The eating changed you. The changes made you better at the killing, which improved the quality of what you ate next, which deepened the changes further. It was a closed system. Not moral, not immoral. Efficient."
**Problem:** Over-explanation of the system. This paragraph is worldbuilding delivered as Vark's interior thought, but it reads as an author's explainer. The cyclical description (eat -> change -> better at killing -> eat better -> deeper changes) is a logical chain that sounds like a textbook, not a person's thoughts. "It was a closed system. Not moral, not immoral. Efficient." is three sentences for one idea.
**Why it weakens:** Vark would not walk herself through this chain of reasoning in this moment. She's been doing this for years. A person who has fully internalized a practice doesn't re-derive the logic while folding a hide. This reads as the author using Vark to explain the world to the reader.
**Revision approach:** Compress radically. "You ate what you killed. The eating changed you. She had stopped thinking about it in moral terms years ago." Let the earlier demonstrations (the butchery, the strength) carry the rest. The reader can infer the cycle.
**Priority:** High

### 40.

### 52.
**Passage:** "These things happened periodically. A physician with a new theory. A noble with a new craving. A collector who had read too many old texts and believed that the right combination of parts, consumed in the right order, could grant something more than ordinary strength."
**Problem:** The list of types (physician, noble, collector) is good worldbuilding, but the collector's description is overlong. "Who had read too many old texts and believed that the right combination of parts, consumed in the right order, could grant something more than ordinary strength" is a sentence-within-a-sentence that over-specifies one item in a list that should be parallel and quick.
**Why it weakens:** The physician and noble get four words each. The collector gets twenty-eight. The imbalance breaks the list's rhythm.
**Revision approach:** Shorten the collector: "A collector convinced that the right combination of parts could grant more than strength." Cut "who had read too many old texts" and "consumed in the right order" -- those are details the reader doesn't need.
**Priority:** Low

### 53.
**Passage:** "Usually it came to nothing. Usually the money evaporated when the results failed to match the promise, and the hunters who had chased the contract found themselves overextended in dangerous country with nothing to show for it but risk."
**Problem:** Wordiness. The second "Usually" sentence is long and its point (hunters take risks for bad contracts) can be compressed. "Found themselves overextended in dangerous country with nothing to show for it but risk" -- "with nothing to show for it but risk" is the payoff, buried at the end of a long construction.
**Why it weakens:** The repetition of "Usually" is fine as structure, but the second sentence wanders.
**Revision approach:** "Usually it came to nothing. The money dried up, and the hunters who chased the contract came back with nothing but risk." Compressed.
**Priority:** Low

### 54.
**Passage:** "But the number Koss had named was not a collector's number. It was an investor's number."
**Problem:** None. This is strong. The collector/investor distinction is sharp and advances Vark's analysis. The parallel construction earns its symmetry because the contrast is meaningful.
**Priority:** N/A -- this works.

### 55.
**Passage:** "And investors, in Vark's experience, did not pay four times the market rate unless they had already seen returns that justified the expense."
**Problem:** Slight over-explanation. "In Vark's experience" is a qualifier that softens the statement. Vark is presented as a woman who states facts without hedging. The qualification doesn't match her voice.
**Why it weakens:** Minor. "In Vark's experience" is a throat-clearing phrase. Cut it, and the sentence is sharper.
**Revision approach:** "Investors did not pay four times the market rate unless they had already seen returns." Drop the hedge and the redundant "that justified the expense."
**Priority:** Low

### 56.
**Passage:** "She filed it away. She would ask around. She would do it carefully, because careful was the only way she had ever done anything that mattered."
**Problem:** Overwriting / explaining character. "Because careful was the only way she had ever done anything that mattered" is an aphorism that summarizes Vark's character for the reader. The entire chapter has demonstrated her carefulness -- the butchery, the cleaning, the coin-counting, the scheduling of work between midnight and dawn.
**Why it weakens:** The reader doesn't need to be told Vark is careful. The reader has watched her be careful for the entire chapter. The sentence is a bow on a package the reader already unwrapped.
**Revision approach:** Cut "because careful was the only way she had ever done anything that mattered." End on: "She would ask around. Carefully." The adverb alone is sufficient, if even that's needed.
**Priority:** Medium

### 57.
**Passage:** "The streets were quiet. The work was done. The money was good. / That was enough for tonight."
**Problem:** Mostly strong. The three short declaratives work as closing rhythm. "That was enough for tonight" is a clean ending. The only question is whether "The money was good" is necessary given the established context -- the reader already knows the money is good.
**Why it weakens:** Very minor. "The money was good" is the weakest of the three because it's the most evaluated. "The streets were quiet" is observation. "The work was done" is fact. "The money was good" is judgment, and it's a judgment the reader has already made.
**Revision approach:** Consider cutting "The money was good." The closing becomes: "The streets were quiet. The work was done. / That was enough for tonight." Tighter, more Vark.
**Priority:** Low

## Overall Diagnosis

This is a strong chapter. Vark is rendered with precision and economy, and the prose largely matches her character -- professional, observational, unsentimental. The butchery sequence is excellent: physical, detailed, and neither flinching nor lingering. The Koss transaction is well-structured and the dialogue is sharp and natural. The chapter accomplishes significant worldbuilding (the flesh economy, the supply chain, the consumption effects) without ever stopping the narrative to lecture.

The primary weakness is **explaining after showing**. The chapter repeatedly delivers a strong image or scene, then adds a sentence (sometimes two or three) that names the meaning or frames the insight. Vark's actions speak clearly; the narrative commentary that follows them is consistently weaker than what it's commenting on. The best moments are the ones that end when the physical action ends: "His eyes found the carcass on the gambrel. His eyes left the carcass." The weakest moments are when the prose circles back to interpret what just happened: "attention without ceremony, care without sentiment. A job. Her job. She was good at it."

The secondary weakness is **over-explained worldbuilding**, particularly in the consumption-effects paragraph. Vark has internalized this system over years. She would not re-derive its logic while folding a hide. The chapter works best when it conveys the world through Vark's actions and pricing knowledge, and weakest when it pauses to explain the mechanics.

A tertiary pattern is **stacked metaphors and aphorisms** -- the architecture/anatomy/hands-and-table sequence, the mountain cold as patient predator, the squeamishness epigram. Vark's voice is at its most distinctive when it's flat and concrete. The poetic gestures belong to a different narrator.

## Top 10 Problems, Ranked by Impact

1. **"Attention without ceremony... A job. Her job. She was good at it."** (issue #17) -- The chapter's most explicit show-then-tell. Four sentences naming what the entire butchery scene already demonstrated. Cut them all.

3. **Strength reveal over-explanation** (issue #36) -- Three sentences for one point (she's unnaturally strong). Pre-reveals, names, then emphasizes.

4. **Koss's looking-away explanation** (issue #37) -- Koss looking away is the scene. The emotional annotation removes the reader's work.

5. **Watching-him-count discipline speech** (issue #31) -- "She always watched" is strong. The philosophy of why she watches weakens it.

6. **Architecture/anatomy/hands-and-table stack** (issue #13) -- Three metaphors for one insight. The hands-and-table image is strong alone.

7. **"She said it the way she said most things"** (issue #30) -- The dialogue already conveys the quality. The narrative names it redundantly.

8. **Eastern-passes recap** (issue #50) -- Restates Koss's information in his own words. The reader just heard the conversation.

9. **Carefulness aphorism** (issue #56) -- Summarizes Vark's character at the end of a chapter that has demonstrated it throughout.

10. **"It was not" after the anger conditional** (issue #12) -- Explains the conditional that was already self-evident.

## Pattern Summary: Recurring Weaknesses

**1. Explaining after showing.** The chapter's most persistent habit. A strong physical scene or line of dialogue is followed by a sentence naming its meaning or classifying its quality. The butchery quality summary, the counting-discipline, Koss's flinch, Vark's dialogue style, and the closing carefulness observation all follow this pattern. The prose trusts the reader during the showing and then loses trust during the telling.

**2. Over-explained worldbuilding.** The consumption-effects paragraph and the factor definition are the clearest examples. When the chapter conveys the world through Vark's actions (pricing organs, folding hide with one hand, working between midnight and dawn), the worldbuilding is invisible and powerful. When it pauses to explain mechanisms, it reads as exposition.

**3. Stacked metaphors and aphorisms.** The architecture/anatomy/table sequence, the mountain-cold personification, the squeamishness epigram, and the carpenter simile are all moments where the prose reaches for polish that doesn't match Vark's flat, concrete voice. Her best lines are undecorated: "The market set the price and the price was the price."

**4. Continuing past the point.** Several passages make their point in one sentence and then add one to three more sentences restating or refining it. "She knew the map now" / "She could have drawn the cuts with her eyes closed." "That was the standard" / "Clean kill, clean product" / "Everything else was waste." "It was not" after "if anger were useful." The first statement is almost always the best.

## Revision Plan (Priority Order)

1. **Cut all explaining-after-showing sentences.** Every passage where a strong image or scene is followed by a sentence naming its meaning. Targets: the butchery quality summary (#17), the counting-discipline speech (#31), the dialogue-style comment (#30), Koss's flinch explanation (#37), the "That was fair" bridge (#38), the "It was not" explanation (#12), and the carefulness aphorism (#56).

2. **Compress the consumption-effects paragraph.** Reduce the cycle-explanation and power-inventory to two or three concrete sentences. Cut the carpenter simile and "professional advantages" label. Let the hide-folding scene carry the weight.

3. **Simplify the strength reveal.** One sentence of action (folding the hide with one hand), one sentence of reaction (Koss looking away). Cut the pre-explanation and the gendered framing.

4. **Collapse stacked metaphors to their strongest single image.** Architecture/anatomy/table -> keep only the hands-and-table. Mountain cold -> cut the personification, stop at "nothing like the cold in the mountains." Squeamishness -> cut the epigram, keep the knot-teaching detail.

5. **Cut the eastern-passes recap.** Start Vark's internal reflection with new analysis, not restated information.

6. **Trim passages that continue past their point.** "She knew the map now" (cut eyes-closed). "Clean kill, clean product" (cut framing). "The ritual of inspection" (cut the label). The factor definition (cut the explanation).

7. **Fix vague similes.** "Something consenting to come apart" -> commit to a concrete sound.

8. **Tighten minor wordiness.** The Koss near-nod, the "not fat exactly" hedge, the "in Vark's experience" qualifier, the collector's over-long description.
