Chapter 13: The Surrender

The marks on the wall above the platform continued — one for each waking — but the wakings themselves had lost their edges. He slept in fragments, never deeply, never for long, and the fragments ran together so that he could not always tell whether the mark he was scratching was for today’s waking or yesterday’s forgotten one or the one before that. The marks were no longer a count. They were something for the hand to do with a knife besides the thing the hand had been thinking about.

Standing from the platform now required a sequence of movements that a month ago would have been a single act. His body was consuming itself — fat first, now muscle.

The fire continued because he fed it. The feeding was the thread connecting one day to the next, thin but holding. He melted snow. He drank. He lay on the platform and stared at the ceiling.

The infection in his forearm had not improved. The skin around the bite was tight and shiny and hot, and the heat was a wrong heat, the heat of something inside the flesh that was growing while the rest of him was shrinking. He opened it with the knife. The fluid that came out smelled of corruption. He sat shaking on the platform, sweat on his face despite the cold. Afterward the swelling subsided slightly and the pain changed from a deep throb to a sharper, more focused ache.

He stopped talking to Hakiia. The talking required energy he did not have.

The rationalizations came slowly, the way weather comes.

The body is meat. The elk he had eaten on the trail was muscle and bone and organ and blood. What was in the alcove was muscle and bone and organ and blood. The materials were the same. Only the meaning was different.

He touched that thought and pulled back. He touched it again. He circled it the way a tongue circles a broken tooth — unable to press hard enough to reach the nerve.

Animals eat their dead. Wolves, ravens, insects, fish — the dead fed the living. The dead did not need their bodies. The living did.

The world did not care what he ate. The world had dropped him and his brother off a cliff and sent wolves and cold and starvation. Weakness drew predation.

He opened the alcove door.

He had not opened it since putting the body inside. The cold air that came through the doorway was sharper than the cabin cold, the preserved cold of a space that had not been warmed, and in the dim light from the hearth behind him he could see the shape on the floor — the furs, the body beneath them.

He looked at Hakiia. He had been avoiding this — the eyes especially. Hakiia’s eyes had always seen him too clearly. The furs were over the face. He did not move them.

He stood in the doorway for a long time. The cold from the alcove met the warmth from the hearth and the two currents mixed around his body.

He went back to the main room. He sat on the platform. There was nothing to eat. He thought about the shape in the alcove. The silence felt like a gaze, and the gaze felt like Hakiia, and Hakiia’s judgment had always found him insufficient.

He closed the alcove door and returned to the platform and lay down. He did not eat that day, and the not-eating was the last thing left that felt like his own.

He did not eat the next day. He searched the cabin again — every corner, every crack. He went outside, into the chest-deep snow, and dug toward the tree line and looked for anything — bark, roots, frozen berries, the corpse of a small animal that the snow had buried — and found only snow and more snow and a cold that would kill him if he stayed.

He went back inside. He closed the door. He sat.

On the third day without food, he woke and could not stand immediately. The waking was a long process. Consciousness arrived in stages. The muscles first — heavy, reluctant, operating on what was left. Then the head, aching with a fuel-starved ache. Then the stomach, which had stopped signaling hunger and started signaling something worse.

He stood. Standing was what was left.

He opened the alcove door. He knelt beside his brother’s body. He pulled back the furs from the left shoulder and the arm that had not moved since the wolf fight — the nerve-dead arm, the one that had been dead weight before Hakiia himself was dead. The shoulder was already gone. The tissue there had been dying since the cliff, the nerves severed, the flesh cooled and discolored days before the man it belonged to had stopped breathing. Cutting from the part that was already lost felt — not easier. Less impossible. The flesh underneath was cold and firm, preserved by the temperature, and the skin had the waxy quality that cold gives to dead things — not quite frozen, not quite flesh.

He knelt there for a long time. The cold from the stone floor moved through his knees. In the other room, the fire was dying because he had not fed it. He knelt with the knife in his hand.

He cut.

The flesh gave way with the sound of something yielding. The cabin was quiet. The world outside was quiet. The only thing that was not quiet was the thing inside him that was screaming — not a sound, a sensation that settled into his hands. His hands continued the work.

He cut a small piece. He took it to the hearth. He cooked it in the iron pan over the coals. Eating it raw was a line he could not cross. The line that mattered was already behind him.

The smell of the cooking was the worst part. It smelled like meat over heat. It smelled like food. His body wanted it, and the wanting was the thing he could not forgive.

He ate.

It tasted like meat. It had the texture of meat. His body received it with the blind efficiency of an animal that had been waiting for exactly this. He chewed and swallowed and was done before the mind had finished refusing.

He put the pan down. He sat beside the fire. The fire was dying, and he was alive.

He made it to the stone floor before the retching took him.

He sat against the wall afterward, shaking. Not from cold. He sat there for a long time — long enough that the fire burned lower and the shadows lengthened and the cold crept back in from the edges of the room where the warmth no longer reached.

He did not look at the alcove door, which was still open. He did not look at the pan, which was still on the hearth. He looked at his hands. They did not look different. The same hands that had wrapped the dog’s cracked pad and closed Hakiia’s eyes. He looked at them and they showed nothing.

He slept. He did not mean to sleep. The body took what it needed and pulled him under.

When he woke, he was less weak. The difference was small but real — he could stand in one movement again, his hands were steadier, his vision was clearer. The meat had worked. His brother’s flesh had fed him and made him stronger, and the strength was as real as the horror.

He wished Hakiia would stop looking at him. The furs were over the face. The alcove door was open and the dim light from the hearth fell across the floor of the alcove and the shape under the furs was still and the face was covered and the eyes were covered and still Hurkuzak felt them. Felt the gaze that had measured him his entire life now measuring this. Insufficient? Monstrous? Strong? Hakiia had always judged and the judgment had always been clear, and now there was no one left to tell him whether what he had done was the hard thing Hakiia had always wanted him to do or the unforgivable thing that proved Hakiia had been right about him.

He closed the alcove door. He went back to the fire. He sat.

The cabin was quiet. He was a man who had killed his brother and eaten his brother and was sitting beside a fire that burned on wood while the rest of him burned on something else entirely.

He was alive. That was all.

He sat with it.

He would eat again tomorrow.
