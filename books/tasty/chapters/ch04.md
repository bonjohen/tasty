Chapter 4: The Road Gives Way

They set out at first light, and the cabin was close enough now that Hurkuzak could almost believe in it. The trail had narrowed overnight, or the snow had narrowed it, and they moved in a single line — Hakiia ahead, the dogs behind him, the sled behind the dogs, and Hurkuzak at the rear with one hand on the rail and his eyes on the ground. The sky was low and gray, and the wind had died to nothing.

Hakiia was moving well. Better than the terrain justified. He picked the line quickly, precisely, without looking back. The dogs followed him without persuasion. They had learned what Hurkuzak had spent years learning, slowly and with more bruises than he liked to count — that Hakiia moved through the world as if it would accommodate him, and more often than not, it did.

The trail ran along the edge of a drop. Hurkuzak could see it through the trees on their left — a long fall through open air to a snow-covered basin below, the rock face jagged where erosion and frost had pulled it apart in layers. The drop was simply there, a fact of the terrain that required no attention as long as the ground beneath your feet remained ground.

There was no warning. No crack, no settling. The trail was simply gone. The outer edge dropped away in a mass of snow and frozen earth and loose stone — there was a sound, enormous, tearing — and Hakiia was on it, running up through the debris, his hand catching the dog’s harness, and he hung there for an instant as the ground fell away below him. Then the ground beneath the sled cracked and tilted, the earth moved under his boots. He threw himself backward and to the right, away from the edge, scrambling. His left side hit a lump of packed snow, and the impact drove the air from his lungs.

The harness where it connected to the sled had caught on an outcropping of rock, and the whole thing hung from a single strap. Hakiia, hanging from the lead dog’s harness, began to climb. The dogs, terrified, tried to get purchase, but their paws only scratched away the frozen surface soil. Then it snapped. The single piece of rawhide and rope came free. The last thing holding Hakiia above the precipice had given up. Hurkuzak watched in slow motion as Hakiia became wrapped in the harness, and end over end the sled bounced down the new cliff face, and after the third bounce Hakiia was thrown free.

Then silence. Then his own breathing. Then a dog screaming.

He rolled onto his hands and knees and crawled to the edge. The edge was raw now, the stone broken in a clean face where the trail had been, and below it was ruin. The collapse had taken a section of the cliff face twenty paces wide and dropped it thirty paces or more into the basin. Snow and rock and earth were still settling, small slides running down the debris field, and through the dust and powder Hurkuzak could see a runner, a flank, the bent ribs of the sled.

The sled was destroyed. It lay on its side at the base of the fall, one runner snapped free, the crossbars shattered, gear scattered across the snow in a radius of wreckage. Furs, tools, bundles of dried meat, the traps — everything they had carried was strewn across the broken ground.

The dogs. Two were still in harness, tangled in the traces and the wreckage, and they were not moving. A third lay on the snow apart from the others with its neck broken. The fourth — the left rear, the quiet one — was alive and screaming. It had landed on a shelf of rock below the main debris field, and its back legs were wrong, and it was trying to pull itself forward with its front legs, and the sound it made cut across the basin without pause or variation.

Hakiia.

Hurkuzak could not see him. The wreckage was a confusion of snow and stone and broken wood and dead animals and scattered gear, and his brother was somewhere inside it. He put his face closer to the edge, searching the debris field, and the wind came up briefly and cleared some of the powder, and still he could not find a shape that was Hakiia. The dog screamed. Something small shifted in the debris below and then stopped. He called his brother’s name, and the basin swallowed it.

He called again, louder. His voice did not sound like his.

Nothing. Then — from somewhere below, muffled — a sound. Not a word. A cough, or a groan, or the noise of a body deciding whether to breathe. He waited.

Then a voice, low and tight with pain but unmistakably his brother’s.

“Stop shouting. Come down.”

He pressed his hands against the broken edge. His legs were shaking. He breathed until the shaking was only in his hands, and then he swung his legs over and began to descend.

He moved on his hands and his seat, grabbing at whatever held — jutting stone, frozen roots, the edge of a buried boulder — and twice his grip failed and he slid half again his height before catching himself on something that might hold or might not. His left hand caught a sharp edge and the skin split across his palm, the blood bright and hot. His ribs took a blow against a rock shelf that left him gasping. His knee struck rock, and the pain shot through his thigh.

Somewhere above him, from the trail they had come, a howl rose — long, even, rising at the end. Then another, answering. Then a third, from a different direction, and the three voices braided together across the distance.

He kept moving. He did not look up.

He reached the bottom. He found Hakiia twenty paces from the sled, half-buried in the debris, trying to pull himself free with his arms.

His legs were wrong. Both of them. Hurkuzak could see it before he was close enough to touch him — the angles were wrong. The left was bent below the knee in a direction that made Hurkuzak’s stomach lurch. The right was straighter, but the foot hung loose, and the boot was dark with blood. There were other injuries — a gash across his forehead that had sheeted blood down the left side of his face, something wrong with his left shoulder — but it was the legs Hurkuzak could not look away from.

Hakiia looked up at him. His face was white with pain and his eyes were sharp and clear and furious.

“Get me out of this,” he said. His voice was compressed. “Then deal with the dog.”

“Your legs — ”

“I know about my legs. Get me free.”

Hurkuzak dug. He pulled stone and snow and pieces of the sled away from Hakiia’s body. He worked around the broken legs, careful not to shift them. Hakiia did not cry out. He gripped Hurkuzak’s arm once, hard enough to bruise, when a shifting rock pressed against his left shin, and his breath came in short, controlled bursts that sounded like someone counting. Then his grip loosened and his breathing steadied, and he said:

“The dog first. Then the gear. What can you see of the sled?”

“Destroyed.”

“How much of the gear is loose?”

“Most of it. It’s everywhere.”

“Gather what you can reach. Food first, then the furs, then the tools. Leave the traps.” He closed his eyes. Opened them. “Kill the dog.”

Hurkuzak looked at the left rear dog on the ledge above. It had stopped screaming and was lying on its side, panting, watching them.

“I’ll do it,” Hurkuzak said.

“I know you will. Do it now.”

He climbed the short distance to the ledge. The dog watched him come. He knelt beside it and put his hand on its head and felt the heat of its body and the trembling in its muscles and the wrongness in its spine where something had broken that would not unbreak. He drew his knife.

The dog died under his hand. Quickly.

He climbed back down. The other three dogs were dead. The traces he cut free from the wreckage and bundled. The harnesses he left.

Below him, Hakiia had pulled himself to a sitting position against a rock and was watching the cliff face above them. His hands were flat on the ground at his sides, steadying his body.

“How bad is the fall?” Hakiia asked. “Can you climb back up?”

Hurkuzak looked at the cliff. It was climbable — barely — but not while carrying anything. And not while carrying anyone.

“I can get up. I can’t carry you.”

“I didn’t ask about carrying me. I asked about you.”

“I can climb it.”

“Good. Then we go forward, not up.” Hakiia turned his head, slowly, and looked out across the basin toward where the valley continued below them. “The cabin is ahead. Below us now, but still ahead. How far?”

“I don’t know. A day, maybe more.”

“Then that is the direction we go.” He looked at his legs. “You’ll need to make something to move me. Drag, not carry. Use the sled rails if they’re intact.”

“One is broken.”

“Use the one that isn’t.”

Somewhere above them, from the trail they could no longer reach, the wolves howled again. Closer now. The sound rolled down the cliff face and spread across the basin. The dogs’ blood was in the snow. Their bodies lay in the open. The smell rose into the cold air.

Hakiia’s eyes went to the cliff edge, then the dead dogs, then Hurkuzak.

“Gather the gear,” he said. “Quickly now.”
