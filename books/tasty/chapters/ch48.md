Chapter 48: What Was Passed On

Weeks later.

Hurkuzak sat in a market town he had not intended to reach, eating food that tasted like food again — not the flat nothing of the early months after the cabin, but actual flavor, texture, the ordinary pleasure of a body receiving what it needed. The town was small, east of the hill country, surrounded by farms and orchards just beginning to fruit. He had arrived because the road led here and he had no reason to go elsewhere.

He was not hiding. He was simply present — buying bread, sitting on a stone bench near the well, watching the commerce of a place that had nothing to do with him.

His body was healed. The burns on his forearms had closed and faded to smooth, tight skin. The cut across his palm was a white line. The bolt wound in his shoulder was a dimple of scar tissue that ached in cold weather. He catalogued these in the mornings now, not from anxiety but from habit — a practice that had begun in the stronghold and that he had not yet learned to stop. Taking inventory of what he was. Checking the list for new entries.

He noticed, as he had been noticing for days, the small scar on the back of his left hand.

He did not remember receiving it. It was not from the fight with Vark or the confrontation with Tessivane or the fire or the guards. It was new and it was wrong — wrong in the way that a wound is wrong when it has been made by teeth rather than by a blade. Small, crescented, the shape of a bite that had broken the surface and taken something away.

It had happened during the destruction. He was certain of that because the scar's healing stage matched the timeline of the fire and the chaos and the carrying of the unresponsive captive through the burning corridors. Someone had bitten him. In the smoke, in the confusion, someone had put their mouth on his hand and taken a piece of him. The bite had been so small and the chaos so large that he had not registered it at the time. He had only begun to notice the scar days later, when the larger wounds had healed and the smaller one remained.

He looked at it. The shape of someone's need in the dark.

He looked away from his hand. Ate his bread. Watched the square.

He could not identify who had bitten him. He could not undo what the bite had transferred. He could not pursue it and he did not try. A man moved through the world, and the world was changed by his moving through it. The change did not always take the shape he intended.

He was still working out what it meant to be what he was. He was not the person who had left with Hakiia through the snow. He was not Hakiia. He had spent a long time looking for the name of what he was now. One morning he had stopped looking. The searching was costing him mornings.

Two presences. His whole life he had been one thing and now he was two things and the second thing was learning, slowly, what to make of the first. They were not at war. That was the discovery that had taken longest — he had been braced for war, for the domination his brother had always wielded, the elder's weight pressing down on the younger's will. It had not come. What had come instead was something harder to name: a kind of presence at his shoulder, a second weight on every decision, a voice that spoke most clearly when he was afraid and sometimes spoke when he was not, and that he was learning to hear the way a man learns to hear his own heartbeat — not always, not with effort, but when the listening was right.

He ate his bread. He let the sun warm his face. He thought about nothing in particular and was aware, faintly, that the thinking was his own.

He did not know what the bite had passed on. One voice or two. The brothers as they had been, or the composite they had become. Something recognizable or something changed beyond recognition by the passage through a new body. He had no way to know. He could only control what he did with what he carried.

He let it pass. Watched the children chasing each other through the square. Finished his bread.

---

The woman's name was Ila. She lived in a village three weeks' walk from the market town where the man with the scars had eaten bread. Her village was coastal — salt air, fishing boats, the rhythm of a community built around the tides. She mended nets. Had mended them since she was old enough to hold a needle.

She was mending nets on the shore when it happened.

The afternoon was warm and the water was calm and the boats were out. The village behind her was quiet — gulls and rope-creak and nothing else. The light was different here than inland, softer at the edges, diffused by salt and distance. She worked without thinking, the way she always worked, her fingers moving through the netting without consulting her, the cord passing through her hands by its own logic.

She reached for a new length of cord.

And stopped.

She held still. The cord hung between her fingers. The net lay across her lap. The shore stretched in both directions, empty and indifferent.

Something had shifted inside her. The way a room feels different when someone has entered it while your back was turned. The way the air changes when it is no longer your air alone.

She held still and did not move and did not breathe and waited to understand what had happened.

Nothing changed. The shore was the shore. The gulls moved across the water. The rope-creak continued. The world outside her was entirely ordinary and the world inside her was not, and the distance between those two facts was the largest distance she had ever crossed without moving.

She listened.

She was not certain she had chosen to listen. The listening was not a choice so much as a condition — a state she found herself in the way she found herself wet when the tide came in too fast. It had already happened. She was already inside it.

What she heard was not one voice.

It was two.

They were not arguing. They were not speaking — not in words, not in anything she could call language. They were present the way two people in a small room are present, the way you know without looking that someone is behind you, except that there was no room and she was behind herself and what was behind her was not one thing but two, distinct, layered, distinct, layered — one lean and impatient, pressing forward against the front of her perception; one that waited.

She had no language for the fact that they were aware of her — she could feel the awareness, the mutual attention, something that had just now realized it was being heard by someone who had not been there before and who was hearing it, in turn, for the first time.

She held still. The net was in her lap. The cord hung from her fingers. The rough presence pressed forward. The still one waited.

She pressed a hand to her chest. Her breath caught. The two things inside her turned — together, at the same moment, with the same motion — and looked.

She felt that looking. She did not know what she was to them. She did not know what they were to her. She knew only that they were real, that they were not going, and that the inside of her skull was no longer only hers.

The cord fell from her fingers. The net slipped from her lap. On the water, far out, a boat turned for home. The shore received the tide the way it always received the tide, without surprise, without acknowledgment, one more thing arriving from somewhere else with nowhere left to go.

Inside her, neither presence spoke.

They waited, together, in the new dark.
