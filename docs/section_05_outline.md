# Section 5: The March to the Cabin - Chapter Outline 

**Analysis section:** 5. The march to the cabin
**Chapters:** 2 (Ch 10-11)
**Goal:** Transform Hakiia from person to weight to burden across a long, hallucinatory march through winter. Hurkuzak carries his brother's body to the cabin and collapses. The cabin becomes the sealed chamber where mourning will turn into survival horror. This section must feel like labor - relentless, physical, and slowly corrosive to the distinction between love and burden. 

---

## Chapter 10: The Carrying

**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Word target:** 2,500-3,000

**Beats:**

* Open where Chapter 9 closed. Hurkuzak is beside Hakiia's body in the snow. Time has passed - minutes or hours, unclear to him. The cold has not stopped. The world did not pause for the killing. He must move or die beside his brother.
* The first problem is mechanical. How do you carry a dead man through deep snow when you are injured, exhausted, and the travois is half-wrecked from the wolves and the cliff? Hurkuzak has to solve this with his hands and whatever material remains. The practical difficulty matters because it forces him into a physical relationship with the body that is already beginning to strip away the sacred.
* He lifts Hakiia. The weight is wrong. Not heavier than expected - Hakiia was always bigger - but differently distributed. A living body cooperates. A dead body is only mass. Hurkuzak should feel that difference physically before he can process it emotionally.
* The march begins. It should feel interminable on the page without becoming tedious to read. Use the landscape as a clock: the quality of light, the depth of snow, the changing texture of exhaustion. Every stretch of ground costs something measurable.
* Hakiia begins to change categories. Early in the march, Hurkuzak still thinks of what he is carrying as his brother. He adjusts the body with care. He apologizes when the travois catches on a root or stone. He looks at Hakiia's face. But as the hours grind on and exhaustion deepens, the body becomes weight first and brother second. Hurkuzak stops apologizing. He repositions the body for efficiency, not tenderness. This shift should happen gradually, without commentary. The reader should see it in changed behavior, not self-analysis.
* Hallucination begins to bleed into the march. Not dramatic visions - small wrongnesses. He hears Hakiia's voice giving directions that are only memory. He sees the cabin ahead and it is not there. The snow seems to rise or the trees seem to lean. Exhaustion and grief are disassembling his perception, but gently, in ways he cannot always identify as false.
* Give Hurkuzak a moment where he talks to Hakiia. Not to the body, but to the absence. He says something aloud that he would never have said when Hakiia was alive - an accusation, a confession, a question. Whatever it is, the silence afterward should feel like the first real proof that Hakiia is gone.
* Close with Hurkuzak still moving. He has not reached the cabin. He is moving on a body's momentum, not on will. End mid-labor, not at rest, so the reader is carried straight into Chapter 11.

**Function:** This chapter establishes the physical and emotional reality of carrying the dead. Its essential work is eroding the boundary between person and object - not through a conscious choice by Hurkuzak, but through exhaustion, gravity, and time. The hallucinations plant a seed: Hakiia's voice in Hurkuzak's head, for now only memory, will later become something else.

## Chapter 11: The Cabin at the End

**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Word target:** 2,500-3,000

**Beats:**

* The march continues. Hurkuzak is beyond exhaustion now - operating in the stripped-down mechanical space where the body moves because stopping is dying. The landscape should feel hostile not through drama, but through indifference. The cold does not intensify. It simply does not stop.
* He falls. Not once - several times. Each fall costs more to recover from. Each time he must get Hakiia's body back onto the travois, his back, or whatever carrying method is still working. Each time the body is less his brother and more the problem between him and survival.
* Include a beat where Hurkuzak considers leaving Hakiia's body. Not a long moral deliberation - a flash of practical clarity. He would move faster alone. The cabin might be reachable without the weight. The thought comes, and he rejects it, but not through formal reasoning. He rejects it because he cannot yet make Hakiia into something he leaves behind.
* He smells smoke, glimpses a shape through trees, or recognizes a landmark half-buried in snow. The cabin is real. It is close. This should not feel like relief. It should feel like the end of one kind of suffering and the beginning of another.
* Arrival. The cabin is small, cold, dark, and empty. No rescue. Few supplies worth counting. No warmth until he makes it. He drags Hakiia's body inside because leaving it in the snow is still unthinkable. The body is still his brother. The body is still the burden.
* Hurkuzak tries to build a fire. He tries to assess the space. He tries to think about what comes next. But his body is shutting down. Injuries, cold, exhaustion, and grief arrive all at once now that the march is over and the body has permission to stop performing.
* He collapses. Not dramatically - he simply loses the ability to continue. He ends on the floor of the cabin beside Hakiia's body. The chapter should close with Hurkuzak losing consciousness, not falling asleep. This is not rest. It is the body taking what the mind refused it during the march.
* Final image: the two brothers in the dark cabin, one dead and one barely alive, and the silence that is not silence because the wind is still working at the walls.

**Function:** This chapter delivers Hurkuzak to the cabin and seals him inside with the body. The cabin must register as both salvation and trap from the first moment - shelter from the cold, but also the locked room where everything that follows will happen. Hurkuzak's collapse should feel total. When he wakes in Section 6, he wakes into a new story: no longer a brother carrying his dead, but a starving man alone with meat.

## Notes

* No Vark intercut. The sealed wilderness pressure must hold. Cutting to Vark here would release tension the cabin section needs.
* Bleed stage: pre-Bleed. The voice has not begun. The hallucinations in Chapter 10 are exhaustion and grief, not Hakiia's presence. They should feel like replayed memory, not a second consciousness. That distinction matters because the reader must later recognize the difference between what hallucination sounds like here and what the Bleed sounds like later.
* The category shift is the core work of this section. Hakiia moves from loved person to weight to burden across these chapters. This is not cruelty on Hurkuzak's part. It is what carrying the dead does to the living. The shift should be visible to the reader before Hurkuzak fully understands it.
* Physical specificity matters. Snow, cold, weight, the improvised carrying method, and Hurkuzak's injuries must all feel real and costly. This section earns the cabin's claustrophobia by first earning the open landscape's hostility.
* The cabin must not feel safe. It is shelter, not rescue. It changes the shape of the problem without solving it.
* Do not foreshadow the consumption. Hurkuzak drags Hakiia's body inside because he is not ready to leave it outside. The decision must read as grief, not unconscious provisioning.
* Plant the geography. The cabin's layout, contents or lack of contents, and relationship to the outside world all need to be established now because they become the full setting for the next movement of the story.
