# Section 3: The Wolves - Chapter Outline 

**Analysis section:** 3. The wolves
**Chapters:** 2 (Ch 6-7)
**Goal:** Show the last full expression of Hakiia as the brother Hurkuzak has always known - fierce, tactically brilliant, and terrifying even when broken. Then show the cost. The wolf attack turns disaster into doom. After this section, recovery is no longer a possibility for Hakiia.

---

## Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Word target:** 2,500-3,000

**Beats:**

* Pick up where Chapter 5 left off. The wolves are close, visible, and testing. The dead sled dogs have given them blood and confidence. Hurkuzak is trying to move Hakiia on the improvised travois, but the wolves are not allowing a clean retreat.
* The attack should begin not as a single dramatic charge, but as a tightening. One wolf comes closer than the others. Then two. Then a feint from the side. The pack is reading weakness methodically, looking for the gap that pays.
* Hakiia takes command of the fight from the ground. This is the critical beat of the chapter. Even with both legs broken, he is tactically sharp, terrifyingly calm, and absolutely certain about violence. He tells Hurkuzak where to stand, when to strike, and where the next approach will come from. He positions himself as both bait and trap - the visible weakness that draws the wolves into range of whatever weapon he can still use.
* Hurkuzak fights. He is bad at it - clumsy, frightened, too slow, swinging wide - but he fights. He should land at least one kill that costs him something real: a bite, a mauling, a moment where a wolf gets close enough that he feels its weight and heat before he drives it off. The violence should feel ugly and desperate, not choreographed.
* Hakiia, from the ground, kills or cripples at least one wolf with an act of violence so disproportionate to his condition that it unsettles the reader. This is the man Hurkuzak has always known - the brother who turns pain into function and function into dominance. Even now, even broken, he is the more dangerous creature in the clearing.
* The wolves break. Not all dead, but enough hurt or killed that the pack withdraws. The silence after should feel temporary, not safe.
* The cost arrives immediately after the kill. The dying wolf's body convulses — a death spasm, the jaw clamping reflexively on Hakiia's right hand, which is still pressed against the wolf's throat. The convulsion shears through fingers with the blind mechanical force of a jaw that no longer has a brain telling it to stop. The hand that held the knife is destroyed: thumb stump remains, index and middle fingers gone, ring and little fingers broken. The blood will not stop. The act of competence IS the cause of the destruction — the cost and the kill are the same moment.
* The left arm, which Hakiia forced to work during the kill despite the shoulder damage from the cliff, completes its failure. The nerve damage that was partial becomes total. The arm stops responding. Dead weight. Between the destroyed right hand and the dead left arm, Hakiia has NO functional upper limbs.
* Cauterization. The bleeding will not stop — too much open surface, too many torn vessels. Hakiia, still lucid, tells Hurkuzak to heat the knife (or a piece of metal) in the fire. Hurkuzak must burn his brother's destroyed hand shut. The sound. The smell. Hakiia's compressed reaction. This scene is intimate, horrifying, and deeply physical — a rehearsal for the mercy killing. Causing pain to prevent worse pain.
* Close on the full cost. Both brothers are bleeding. Hurkuzak is bitten, torn, and exhausted. Hakiia has spent everything he had left. He is still conscious, still lucid, but something has ended. The reader should feel it: this man cannot hold anything. Cannot grip a weapon. Cannot feed himself. Cannot, when the time comes, end his own life. The hope of surviving together dies here.

**Function:** This is the last time Hakiia is fully himself in action. The reader must see exactly what Hurkuzak has lived beside his whole life — then see the cost of that final expression destroy his capacity for any future action. The hand destruction is causally linked to the kill: the price of the last act of competence is the instrument of competence itself. That image — the terrifying wolf kill followed by the ruined hand — needs to be vivid enough to haunt the rest of the book.

## Chapter 7: The Weight of What Is Left

**POV:** Hurkuzak
**Word target:** 2,500-3,000

**Beats:**

* The aftermath. The wolves are gone for now, but not permanently. The brothers are at the bottom of a cliff in winter with dead animals, scattered wreckage, and no transport. Hurkuzak must decide what to do next, and for the first time, the decision is entirely his. Hakiia can advise. He cannot act.
* Hakiia's condition should become quietly, unmistakably terminal over the course of this chapter. Not through a single dramatic revelation, but through accumulation. His breathing changes. His focus drifts and returns. He gives an order, then forgets he gave it. He is bleeding internally, or his injuries have compounded past what any body can manage in this cold without real help. The reader should understand what Hurkuzak is not yet ready to say aloud.
* Hurkuzak tends to Hakiia again — resetting splints, cleaning wounds, assessing the new damage. The right hand: cauterized stump discolored, hot, beginning to smell wrong. Infection setting in. The left arm: lifts it, it falls. Dead weight. No sensation, no movement. Hakiia has no functional hands or arms. The physical intimacy of this care should echo Chapter 5 but feel far worse. In Chapter 5 there was still a plan. Now there is only maintenance of a body that is losing itself in pieces.
* Include a moment between them that is almost honest. Not a speech, not a confession, but a pause where the dynamic loosens. Hakiia says something that almost acknowledges what Hurkuzak has done — "You fought well." The moment should also be physical: Hakiia's ruined right hand moves toward Hurkuzak's arm. Not a grip — cannot grip. The thumb stump presses against the skin. The broken fingers do not close. The touch is light because it cannot be otherwise. The attempt IS the tenderness. This should be MORE devastating than a strong grip, not less — the reader knows what this hand was and what it can no longer do.
* Hurkuzak rigs the travois again and begins hauling Hakiia toward the cabin. The distance should feel immense. Every step is labor. The wolf-torn ground, the blood in the snow, the dead dogs behind them - all of it is already becoming landscape. The world absorbs violence quickly.
* Close with Hurkuzak pulling and Hakiia silent behind him. The silence is new. Hakiia has always filled space with commands, corrections, and criticism. His quiet is the first real evidence that something has ended. Not yet his life, but his authority over the situation. Hurkuzak is alone with the weight.

**Function:** This chapter transitions from the fight to the march. The wolves have done their work: they have pushed Hakiia past recovery and left Hurkuzak as the only functional body. The reader should feel the shift from "both might survive" to "one of them will not." The almost-tender moment must land hard enough that the mercy killing in Section 4 feels like losing something real, not resolving a problem.

## Notes

* No Vark intercut. The wilderness sections should remain an enclosed pressure chamber. Vark's next appearance works best after the cabin, when distance between the storylines creates dramatic irony rather than releasing tension.
* The wolves should feel like animals, not symbols. They are opportunists following blood and weakness. The symbolic weight - bodies are meat, weakness draws predators, love does not stop hunger - should emerge from the situation, not from the prose pointing at it.
* Hakiia's fight from the ground is the image the book will return to. It needs to be strong enough that when Hurkuzak later moves with Hakiia's speed and violence, the reader remembers this scene.
* The hand destruction must be causally linked to the kill — the dying wolf's jaw convulsion, not a separate attack. The cost and the act are the same moment. This makes the destruction feel like inevitability, not bad luck.
* The cauterization scene should follow the book's principle of restrained horror: the sound, the smell, Hakiia's compressed reaction. Do not linger on the visual of the destroyed hand itself. Establish it once, clearly, then let the reader carry the image.
* After Ch 6, Hakiia has no functional hands or arms. This physical fact is what makes the mercy killing demand in Ch 8 unanswerable: he cannot end his own life. The cultural death practice (seeded in Ch 3) gives the demand dignity and context.
* The almost-tender moment in Chapter 7 must not be oversold. If it becomes sentimental, it cheapens everything. Hakiia's love is real, but it cannot express itself cleanly. The warmth should arrive damaged, brief, and ambiguous enough that Hurkuzak can doubt it later.
* Hurkuzak's fighting in Chapter 6 should be genuinely bad. He survives through desperation, not hidden talent. The skill comes later, inherited. Right now he is only stubborn.
* The section should end with Hurkuzak hauling Hakiia in silence, not with Hakiia dying. The mercy killing belongs to Section 4. This section's job is to make that moment feel inevitable without arriving there yet.
