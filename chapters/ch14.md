# Chapter 14: What Repetition Builds

He ate again the next day. And the day after. And the day after that.

The second time was harder to excuse. The first had been desperation — a body at the end of its reserves, a decision that was not a decision but a collapse. The second time he was not collapsing. He was hungry, deeply hungry, but he was not dying. He could have waited another day, maybe two, before the body forced the issue again. He did not wait. He went to the alcove and he cut and he cooked and he ate, and the difference between the first time and the second was that the second time he could not pretend he had been forced. The second time was a choice. The third time was easier. The fourth time was almost routine.

That was the horror.

Not the act itself — the act was a sequence of physical steps that his hands had learned and could repeat without his mind's full participation. The horror was the ease. The rate at which the unthinkable became the familiar. He had spent days circling the first consumption, building scaffolding of rationalization, arguing with himself in the dark. By the fifth time, the rationalizations had compressed into a single sentence that required no scaffolding at all: the body needed food and the food was there. The sentence contained no grief and no horror and no self-examination and its absence of those things was the deepest evidence that something in him had changed in a way that would not change back.

He developed methods. The practicality of this disturbed him in the moments when he was capable of being disturbed, which were fewer each day. He learned which cuts preserved best in the alcove cold — the larger muscle groups held longest, the organs needed to be consumed within a day or two of being taken. He learned how to portion: small amounts, regularly, enough to sustain function without excess, stretching the supply the way he had stretched the grain. He learned that cooking slowly over low heat produced something his stomach accepted more easily than anything seared fast over high flame, and he learned this through trial and the body's instruction, and the learning was the learning of a person who was becoming skilled at a practice he would rather not have needed.

He learned the interior geography of his brother's body. Not willingly, not with curiosity, but with the grim necessity of a man who could not afford to waste what was given and who therefore needed to know where the valuable cuts lived and how to reach them. He learned where the muscle was dense and where it was thin and where the bone required the saw and where the knife alone was enough. He learned this the way Hakiia had learned to dress the elk — through repetition, through practice, through the specific education that touching and cutting a body provides to the hands that do the work.

He used the rendered fat from the jar to cook with. He discovered that the iron pan conducted heat more evenly than the stone hearth and that the candles, which he had hoarded, could be melted and mixed with the fat to extend it. He discovered that bone could be boiled into a broth that sustained him on days when cutting felt like too much and that the broth was warm and filling and tasted of nothing he could identify as wrong.

These were the behaviors of a survivor. They were also the behaviors of a person building a household economy from his brother's body, and the two descriptions applied to the same actions and the same person and neither was less true than the other.

Weeks passed. The marks on the wall multiplied until they covered the space above the platform and began to creep along the adjacent wall. He stopped counting them individually and started counting in groups of seven because groups of seven were weeks and weeks were a unit that made the time feel measurable even if the measurement carried no information about when the time would end.

The cabin became his world in the way that a prison becomes a world — not chosen, not comfortable, but known in every detail until the knowing itself became a kind of relationship. He knew which stone in the floor rocked when he stepped on it. He knew that the draft came from the gap between the door frame and the wall and could be reduced but not eliminated by stuffing it with cloth torn from the blankets. He knew that the fire burned best with the wood stacked in a specific pattern — small pieces at the base, larger pieces above, angled so the air could move between them — and the knowing of this had come from somewhere he did not examine because examining the source would mean acknowledging that the knowledge was not his.

He had a day when the wind stopped. It had been blowing for so long that the blowing had become the cabin's background noise, the sound underneath all other sounds, and when it stopped the silence was so complete that it felt like a pressure change, like the world had been holding its breath and had suddenly exhaled. He sat in the silence and listened to nothing and the nothing was louder than the wind had ever been, because nothing was the sound of a room with one person in it who used to be a person who lived in rooms with other people and who was now something else, and the something else did not have a word yet, and the silence was not going to provide one.

He had a night when he woke and forgot. For one breath — less than a breath, a fraction of a moment between sleep and waking — he did not know where he was or what had happened. The cabin was just a room. The cold was just cold. The body in the alcove was just the absence of warmth from the other side of the wall. For that fragment of time he was only Hurkuzak, waking in a dark place, and the dark place had not yet told him its name. Then memory returned, all of it, in a cascade that left him sitting upright on the platform with his hands gripping the edge of the wood and his heart doing something fast and wrong and his breath coming in the short, controlled bursts that he recognized from Hakiia, because Hakiia's body had breathed that way under pain and Hurkuzak's body was learning the same rhythm for the same reasons.

He had a morning when he caught himself humming. He was working at the hearth — tending the fire, preparing the day's meal, performing the domestic routine that had become the structure of his existence — and the sound came from his chest without his permission, a low drone that was not a tune but was the shape of a tune, the ghost of something he might have heard once or might be inventing or might be remembering from a source he did not want to identify. He heard himself and he stopped, and the stopping was as violent as a slap, and the silence that followed the stopping was the silence of a man who had discovered himself behaving normally in a situation that was not normal and could never be normal and the normalcy was the most frightening thing that had happened to him since the knife.

He wished Hakiia would stop looking at him. The eyes were covered. The door was closed. He had arranged the furs carefully to ensure that the face was not visible when he entered the alcove for provisions. And still the looking continued. Not from the body — from somewhere else, from the corners of the room, from the dark spaces between the stones, from the place behind his own eyes where Hakiia had always lived as critic and corrector and the standard against which all of Hurkuzak's actions were measured. The looking was not supernatural. It was guilt. It was the knowledge that the person whose body he was consuming had been his brother and had loved him badly and had died under his hand and was now sustaining his life, and that sequence of facts produced a gaze that no closed door and no covered face could block.

Sometimes the looking was the body. He would open the alcove door and the furs would seem to have shifted — not much, just enough to suggest that the face underneath was closer to visible than it had been, that the eyes beneath the fabric were oriented toward the doorway rather than the ceiling. He would fix the furs. He would close the door. He would know, with the rational part of his mind that still functioned, that dead bodies did not move and dead eyes did not track and the shifting was thermal expansion, settling, the slow mechanical behavior of mass under gravity and temperature change. He would know this. The knowing did not help.

The dark humor came on a day when the fire was good and the wind was quiet and the meal had been more substantial than usual — a cut from the thigh, cooked slowly in the pan with rendered fat, seasoned with nothing because there was nothing to season with but eaten with a thoroughness that left the pan clean and his stomach full for the first time in days.

He sat beside the fire afterward with the pan in his lap and the fire warm on his face and his belly full and the fullness was the fullness of a person who had eaten well and the eating well was a fact and the fact was grotesque and the grotesque was a fact and the facts would not stop layering themselves on top of each other until the weight of them became so absurd that the only possible response was the one that lives at the intersection of grief and horror and the specific kind of recognition that arrives when a person sees exactly what they have become and the seeing is so total that the only alternative to laughing is a kind of madness that does not recover.

Demon meat is very tasty.

The thought arrived and he heard it and the hearing of it was the most honest thing that had happened to him since the knife entered his brother's body. It was true. That was the unbearable part. The meat had tasted good. Not merely edible, not merely tolerable, not merely fuel that the body accepted and the mind endured. Good. Specific, identifiable, genuine good. The kind of good that a person notices, the kind of good that registers as flavor rather than nutrition, the kind of good that meant his body did not merely need what he was eating but wanted it, and the wanting was a betrayal so complete that the only possible container for it was humor, and the humor was black and bottomless and he was laughing alone in a cabin in the mountains with a dead brother in the next room and the taste of that brother in his mouth and the laughter was not joy and was not madness and was not anything except the sound a person makes when the truth becomes so terrible that no other sound is available.

He laughed until the laughing turned into something else and the something else was not crying because he had passed the point where crying was useful but it was not nothing either, it was a third thing, a vibration that lived in his chest and his throat and meant that he was still alive and still feeling and still capable of recognizing what he had become and the recognition was a kind of survival that had nothing to do with food.

He used every part. The thoroughness emerged not from plan but from the logic of scarcity. Waste was the only sin left to him that he could avoid. If the body was going to be consumed — and it was being consumed, day by day, piece by piece, the inventory in the alcove diminishing with the same slow certainty that the marks on the wall accumulated — then the consuming would be complete. Bone for broth. Marrow for fat. Organ for density. Sinew and tendon boiled soft for sustenance on days when the cuts were gone and the between-times needed filling. He used what Hakiia had given him the way Hakiia had used the elk — efficiently, completely, with the practical respect of a person who understood that the dead had made a gift of their body whether they had intended to or not.

The contradiction did not resolve. Honoring the body by using it completely was also the most terrible thing he could do to it. Both statements were true. He carried them the way he carried everything else — simultaneously, without resolution, without the grace of simplicity.

His body improved. Not dramatically — not in a way that announced itself with fanfare or forced immediate attention. But the improvements accumulated, the way the marks on the wall accumulated, until their sum was impossible to ignore. The wolf bite healed. Not slowly, the way a badly infected wound heals when the body barely has the resources to fight the infection, but quickly, completely, the flesh closing over itself in days rather than weeks and the skin beneath the new scar smooth and dense and unfamiliar. His ribs stopped hurting. His knee stopped grinding. His palm, which had scarred thick and tight, softened and the scar thinned and his grip strengthened until the hand worked better than it had before the cliff.

He was stronger than he should have been. Not in the general way that food restores a starving body — that would have been slow, incremental, proportional to the calories consumed. This was different. The strength arrived in discrete packets, as though each meal unlocked a specific capacity that had been dormant. His arms could lift the wood pile that had required two trips when he was starving. His legs carried him through snow that had been impassable a week ago. His hands closed around the axe handle with a grip that felt borrowed, as though the muscles had been taught something overnight that years of practice had never provided.

He was warmer than he should have been. More alert than he should have been. He noticed things at a distance that he should not have been able to see in winter light — the movement of a bird at the tree line, the track of an animal in the snow two hundred paces from the cabin door. His hearing had sharpened. The cold that had been killing him slowly since the trail gave way no longer reached as deep. He went outside in temperatures that should have driven him back to the fire within minutes and stayed for an hour and felt only the abstract sensation of cold rather than its physical cost. His body was answering to a different set of rules, and the rules were not the rules his body had followed before, and the difference was measurable and undeniable and he told himself it was the food and the rest and the shelter because telling himself anything else would mean admitting something about the food that he was not ready to admit.

The weeks continued. The marks on the wall spread. The alcove emptied as the cabin filled — filled with warmth, with strength, with the slow accumulation of physical capacity that grew each day as the body in the other room diminished. The symmetry was not lost on him. Hakiia was disappearing from the alcove. Hakiia was not disappearing from Hurkuzak. Something of his brother was being translated from one form into another, from flesh to fuel to function, and the function was building a person who moved through the cabin with a competence and a physical assurance that did not belong to the person who had entered it.

Winter went on. The days lengthened by increments too small to see but large enough to feel — a few extra minutes of gray light at each end of the day, a subtle softening of the darkness that no longer felt absolute. He did not know if this meant spring was approaching. Spring felt like a concept from another life, a life in which seasons changed and time moved forward and people left cabins and returned to a world that contained other people. That life existed somewhere. It did not exist here.

He settled into a terrible equilibrium. He had food. He had warmth. He had shelter. He had built a life from his brother's body and the life was sustaining him. The grief had not diminished. It had become structural — part of the architecture of each day, load-bearing, integrated into the routine of fire and food and water and sleep. He grieved the way the cabin held heat: imperfectly, with losses at every edge, but enough to continue, enough to survive, enough to wake each morning and scratch another mark on the wall and know that the marks measured something real, even if what they measured was a life built on the foundation of the most terrible thing he had ever done.

He was surviving. The cost of surviving was becoming who he was. The marks on the wall measured it. The empty alcove would eventually confirm it. And the terrible equilibrium held, day after day, because the only thing worse than what he was doing was the alternative, and the alternative was a death that wasted everything — the mercy killing, the march, the carrying, the suffering, and the brother who had given him everything, including the one gift that could never be acknowledged and could never be returned.
