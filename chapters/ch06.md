# Chapter 6: What Hunger Teaches

The first wolf came at dusk.

Hurkuzak had been pulling the travois for what felt like hours but may have been less. The basin had narrowed into a shallow valley between rocky slopes, the trees thin and bent with altitude, the snow deep enough that each step required him to lift his knees above the surface and press forward and sink and lift again. His legs burned. His ribs sent a flare of pain with every breath that went deeper than shallow. His cut palm had opened again under the friction of the drag poles and the blood made the wood slick and warm in a way that felt like the only warmth left in the world.

Behind him, Hakiia lay on the travois with his eyes open and his hands at his sides and his broken legs pointing at the sky that was darkening from gray toward the black that meant real cold, the kind that killed at its own pace and did not care whether you were ready.

The wolves had been following since the basin. Hurkuzak had seen them at intervals — gray shapes at the edge of sight, appearing between trees and disappearing, never close enough to count reliably but never far enough to forget. They moved in the same direction he moved. They maintained the same pace. They were not pursuing. They were accompanying, the way a current accompanies a leaf, patient and steady and moving toward the same destination for different reasons.

Then one of them was closer.

It came from the left, angling in from behind a stand of dead timber, and it stopped twenty paces away and stood there. It was large — larger than the dogs had been, with a thick winter coat and a wide head and eyes that held the flat reflective quality of something that did not need to understand what it was looking at in order to know what to do with it.

Hurkuzak stopped pulling. He turned to face the wolf and his hand went to the hatchet on his belt and the wolf watched his hand move and did not retreat.

"It's testing," Hakiia said from the travois. His voice was thin but steady. "It wants to see if you respond. Don't move toward it. Don't move away. Stand."

Hurkuzak stood. The wolf stood. The cold stood between them and did not choose a side.

After a time the wolf turned and walked back toward the trees. Not running. Walking. With the unhurried gait of something that had seen what it needed to see and was going to report.

"Two more," Hakiia said. "Behind us, right side."

Hurkuzak turned. Two wolves stood at the tree line to the right, thirty paces back. They were smaller than the first but their positioning was different — angled, spread apart, covering different lines of approach. They were not watching Hurkuzak. They were watching the travois.

"They can see I'm down," Hakiia said. "They know what that means." He pushed himself up on his elbows, and the movement cost him something visible — his face tightened and his breathing shortened and the muscles in his arms trembled with the effort of holding himself partially upright. "Pull me closer to those rocks. The ones at the base of the slope."

Hurkuzak dragged the travois to the rocks Hakiia had indicated — a low cluster of boulders at the foot of the valley wall, large enough to block approach from one direction and create a corner where the wolves could not come from behind. He set the travois down with Hakiia's back to the stone and his face toward the open ground.

"Give me the knife," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak gave him the knife. Hakiia held it the way he held all weapons — low, angled, the blade an extension of his forearm rather than something gripped in his fist. Even lying down. Even with his legs ruined and his body broken in ways that should have made the gesture absurd. The knife in Hakiia's hand did not look absurd. It looked like a threat.

"You'll take the hatchet," Hakiia said. "Stand in front of me. When they come — and they will come — they'll come from the sides first, testing. Then from the front. The first one through is not the attack. The first one through is the question. The attack comes from the one you're not looking at."

"How do you know — "

"Because that is how anything that hunts in groups kills something bigger than itself. They separate attention. They stretch the defense. Then they come through the gap." He looked at Hurkuzak with eyes that were bright with pain and clear with something else — a quality of focus that burned off everything unnecessary, leaving only the part of Hakiia that had always been the most frightening and the most certain: the part that understood violence the way Hurkuzak understood tenderness, as something that lived in his bones.

"I'll be the bait," Hakiia said. "I'm already down. They can see it. They'll come for me because I'm the easy kill. When they commit to me, you hit whatever is closest and you hit it hard enough that it does not get up."

"You can't fight from the ground."

"Watch me."

The wait was worse than the attack would be. Hurkuzak stood in front of the travois with the hatchet in his right hand and his body angled the way Hakiia had told him — weight forward, weapon high enough to swing, feet set wide because narrow feet are what falls down when something hits them. The wolves moved at the edge of sight. They circled. They came closer and pulled back and came closer again, and each approach was a little nearer than the last, and each retreat was a little shorter, and the pattern had a rhythm to it that Hurkuzak could feel the way you feel a tide: not fast, not violent, but inevitable.

Five of them visible now. The three from before and two more that had joined from the basin, drawn by the commotion or the blood or the simple gravity of opportunity. They spread themselves around the clearing in a loose formation that covered every approach, and they watched with the shared attention of animals that had hunted together long enough to communicate through position rather than sound.

The wolves came.

They came the way Hakiia said they would. One from the left, trotting forward in a half-circle that drew Hurkuzak's eyes and his body to that side, its head low and its steps deliberate, testing. Then one from the right, faster, cutting closer, angling toward the travois where Hakiia lay with the knife low and his breathing controlled. Hurkuzak turned to face the right-side wolf and the one on the left darted forward.

Hakiia's voice cut through the cold. "Left. Now."

Hurkuzak swung. The hatchet caught air. The wolf had already veered, pulling back with the liquid speed of something that had calculated the reach of the weapon and stayed one stride outside it. Hurkuzak stumbled forward and his bad knee buckled and he caught himself and the wolf on the right was closer now, much closer, and he could see its teeth and the breath coming from its mouth in short controlled bursts.

He swung again. The blade glanced off the wolf's shoulder and the impact jolted up through his arm and the wolf yelped and scrambled sideways and Hurkuzak followed it, swinging a third time, and the third swing went wide because his footing was wrong and his weight was forward and the hatchet buried itself in the snow instead of the animal.

Something hit him from the left. Weight and heat and the smell of breath and the scrape of claws through his coat and then teeth, teeth in the meat of his forearm, and the pain was immediate and enormous and deeply wrong — not the clean pain of a cut or a blow but the grinding pressure of jaws designed to hold and not let go. He screamed. He did not decide to scream. His body screamed because it had been seized by something that intended to kill it and the scream was the body's honest opinion about the situation.

He swung the hatchet with his free arm. The angle was bad and the force was worse but the blade struck bone — skull or jaw, he couldn't tell — and the wolf released and stumbled and Hurkuzak hit it again while it was still staggering and this time the hatchet sank into something that gave way and the wolf went down and did not get up.

Blood was running down his forearm and into his hand and the world was loud with his own breathing and behind him Hakiia was making a sound that was not a word but a command — a compressed bark of direction that meant move, turn, look.

A wolf had reached the travois.

It had come while Hurkuzak fought, using the distraction exactly as Hakiia had predicted, slipping past the defense to reach the body on the ground. It was on the travois, its front paws on the furs, its head low and its weight forward and it was going for Hakiia's legs because the legs were broken and the broken place was where the weakness was and wolves knew this the way they knew everything about damage — not through thought but through something older and more reliable.

Hakiia moved.

What happened next was fast enough that Hurkuzak would only reconstruct it later, piecing together what he saw and what he felt and what the evidence in the snow told him. Hakiia's upper body, which was the only part of him that still functioned as a weapon, rose from the travois in a motion that should not have been possible for a man with two shattered legs and a body running on nothing but pain and fury. His left hand caught the wolf by the scruff of the neck. His right hand drove the knife into the animal's throat and pulled it sideways and the wolf's blood came in a sheet that covered Hakiia's chest and face and arms and the wolf convulsed and Hakiia held it — held the dying animal against his own body with a strength that had no right to exist in a man as broken as he was, held it while it thrashed and kicked and the kicks struck his broken legs and the pain must have been beyond anything language could carry because the sound Hakiia made was not a sound that came from a place where words existed.

He held it until it stopped.

Then he let go and the wolf slid off the travois and lay in the blood-soaked snow and Hakiia lay on his back with the knife still in his hand and his body rigid and his breathing the kind of breathing that comes after a person has done something that cost them everything they had left to spend.

The remaining wolves watched from a distance. Two dead. One limping from the blow to its shoulder. The rest — three, maybe four — stood in a loose arc at the edge of the clearing and looked at what had just happened and recalculated.

Hurkuzak stood in the bloody snow with the hatchet shaking in his hand and his forearm bleeding and his body vibrating with something that was not courage and was not fear but was the aftermath of both arriving at the same time and canceling each other into a pure, shaking blank.

"They'll go," Hakiia said. His voice was barely a voice. It came from somewhere underneath the pain. "They lost two. They don't have the numbers to absorb that and press the attack. They'll go."

The wolves went. Not all at once. The limping one first, turning and moving back through the trees with the careful steps of something that had learned a new fact about the world and did not like it. Then the others, one by one, drifting back toward the basin where the dead dogs still lay and where the arithmetic of easy meat was better than the arithmetic of these two damaged creatures beside the rocks.

The silence after was not peace. It was the temporary absence of violence, which was a different thing entirely. The snow around them was churned and bloody, trampled into a dark confusion of boot prints and paw prints and drag marks. Two dead wolves lay in the clearing — the one Hurkuzak had killed with the hatchet and the one Hakiia had killed with the knife and his hands and whatever remained of the force that had always made him the more dangerous thing in any space he occupied. The dead wolves looked smaller than they had alive. They looked like animals. The living ones had looked like something else.

Hurkuzak looked at his brother. Hakiia lay in the wolf's blood with his eyes closed and his chest rising and falling in a pattern that was wrong — too shallow, too fast, too uneven, as though the breathing mechanism itself had been damaged by the effort and could no longer find its proper rhythm. The kill from the ground had cost him something that went beyond physical strain. Something had shifted in the way his body occupied the travois, a settling that looked less like rest and more like the beginning of a process that rest could not reverse.

But his hand still held the knife. The grip had not loosened. The fingers were locked around the handle with the same unconscious authority they had always carried, as though the knife were not a tool but a part of him, and the part of him that held weapons would be the last part to let go.

Hurkuzak knelt beside him. His forearm was bleeding freely — the bite had gone deep, through coat and clothing and into the muscle underneath, and the edges of the wound were ragged and the pain was settling from the initial scream into a lower, steadier burn that would be with him for days. He wrapped it with a strip torn from his undershirt, pulling the binding tight with his teeth, and the cloth turned red almost immediately.

"Your arm," Hakiia said without opening his eyes.

"It's wrapped."

"How deep?"

"Deep enough."

"Can you pull?"

Hurkuzak looked at his hands. The left was cut. The right was bitten. Between them they held one working grip and the memory of what two good hands used to feel like.

"I can pull," he said.

Hakiia opened his eyes. He looked at Hurkuzak. The look was not the look of correction or assessment or the perpetual measurement of inadequacy that Hurkuzak had lived under for his entire life. It was something else. Something that lived behind those other things and rarely found the surface.

Then it was gone. Hakiia closed his eyes again and his breathing continued its wrong rhythm and the blood on his face and chest began to freeze in the falling temperature and the cold continued its work on everything.

"Then pull," Hakiia said.
