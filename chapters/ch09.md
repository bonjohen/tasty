# Chapter 9: The Line He Crosses

He stopped pulling when the light failed because there was nothing left in his body that could pull in the dark. He found a hollow between rocks where the wind broke and the snow was shallow and he dragged the travois into it and built a fire that took him four attempts and most of the feeling in his fingers, and the flame when it came was small and uncertain and it was all there was.

Hakiia had not spoken since the demand. Hours of silence, broken only by the wrong breathing and the occasional small sound that was not a word but was the body's response to being moved over uneven ground with two shattered legs. Hurkuzak had pulled through that silence as though the silence were the medium they were traveling through — thicker than air, heavier than snow, a substance made of the thing that lay between them now, the demand and the refusal and the knowledge that the refusal was temporary.

He sat beside the fire and watched his brother's face. The lucidity from earlier was gone. Hakiia's eyes were closed and his features had settled into something that was no longer the composed, calculating mask Hurkuzak had known his entire life. The face was loose. The jaw hung slightly open. The lines that had always held Hakiia's expression in its permanent configuration of authority had softened, and what remained was the face of a man who was enduring something that had moved past the reach of endurance and was now simply being weathered, the way stone is weathered, not through resistance but through the slow erosion of everything that was not pain.

Hurkuzak did not sleep. He sat beside the fire and watched the embers and watched his brother's breathing and counted the pauses between breaths because counting them was a way of knowing how much time was left without having to say it. The pauses were longer. Five heartbeats between one breath and the next. Then six. Then a stretch of seven that made his own heart stumble before Hakiia's chest rose again, shallow and reluctant, as though breathing had become something the body did out of habit rather than need.

He thought about what Hakiia had asked. He thought about it the way a man thinks about a wound he has been told to look at — with the specific reluctance of someone who knows that looking will make the thing real in a way it was not before. The demand was there. It had been there since the afternoon. It occupied the space between them the way the fire occupied the space between the rocks — present, visible, throwing light and heat in all directions, impossible to sit beside without feeling.

He thought about the dog. The quiet one, the left rear, with its cracked pad and its patient eyes and the wrongness in its spine. He had put his hand on its head and drawn his knife and done what needed to be done and the dog had died quickly and the doing had been clean and afterward he had climbed down from the ledge and continued working. He had not felt the killing afterward. Not the way he would feel this.

He thought about the elk. The dark eye, the held breath, the moment where the arrow was drawn and the animal was alive and the decision not to release had felt like mercy and Hakiia had said it was weakness. The same weakness. The same hesitation. The same flinch that Hakiia had spent a lifetime trying to beat out of him because he believed the flinch was what killed people.

And now Hakiia was asking him not to flinch.

The fire burned lower. He fed it with the last of the deadwood he had gathered, and the flames brightened and then settled, and the warmth was small but it was all there was and he sat inside it and felt the hours pass.

Sometime in the deep part of the night, Hakiia woke.

Hurkuzak knew it because the breathing changed. Not improved — it could not improve — but it shifted rhythm, the way a person's breathing shifts when consciousness returns and the body remembers it has a mind to report to. Then Hakiia's eyes opened and they were clear again, as clear as they had been that afternoon, and they found Hurkuzak's face across the fire and held it.

Neither of them spoke for a long time. The fire cracked. An ember popped and a flare of light moved across Hakiia's face and the blood crust on his forehead caught the light and held it and for a moment the wound looked fresh.

"You have been sitting there all night," Hakiia said.

"Yes."

"Have you slept?"

"No."

Hakiia looked at the fire. His voice when he spoke again was different from the afternoon. The argument was gone from it. The contempt was gone, or nearly gone — there was a trace of it left, because Hakiia could no more fully shed contempt than Hurkuzak could fully shed tenderness, but it was faint, and what remained underneath it was something Hurkuzak had never heard before. Tiredness. Real tiredness. Not the tiredness of a body at the end of its resources, though that was there too. The tiredness of a man who had spent his life being the stronger one and was, for the first time, too weak to maintain the performance.

"I am not going to argue again," Hakiia said. "I said what I said. The facts have not changed. If anything they are worse. The right leg is further gone. I can feel less of it now than I could this afternoon, and that is not recovery."

"I know."

"I know you know." He paused. The pause was not rhetorical. It was the body demanding its due, the breathing that required a gap between sentences that would not have required one a week ago. "I know what I am asking you to carry. After. I know that the weight of it does not end when my weight ends. I know that you will walk away from this with something inside you that will not leave."

Hurkuzak's throat tightened. Hakiia had never said anything like this. In all the years of their relationship — the corrections, the commands, the cruelties that were also care — Hakiia had never once acknowledged that what he asked of Hurkuzak had a cost to Hurkuzak that mattered. The world, in Hakiia's model, was a series of hard things that needed doing, and the doing was the only relevant fact, and the cost of doing was irrelevant because the alternative was always worse. That model had governed everything between them. It governed now. But for the first time, Hakiia was looking at the cost and naming it.

"I would not ask if there were another way," Hakiia said. "There is not. I have looked for one. I have spent more hours looking for one than you know, because looking was easier than asking, and I have not found it."

The words were quiet. They came without pressure or manipulation or the tactical precision that Hakiia normally used when he wanted something. They came the way a stone falls: straight down, without art, because gravity is not a persuasive technique.

"You are the only one here," Hakiia said. "And what I am asking is the hardest thing I have ever asked anyone. And I am asking you because you are the one who will do it with mercy, and because I trust you to carry it afterward, and because if I am going to die I want it to be by the hand of someone who will remember that it mattered."

The fire burned. The wind moved through the trees above them and the sound it made was the sound the world had been making since before either of them existed and would continue making after both of them were gone.

"Please," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak had never heard that word from his brother. Not once. Not in anger, not in need, not in the worst moments of the last three days when his body was broken and his control was slipping and the pain was beyond anything either of them had a name for. Hakiia did not say please. Hakiia commanded, argued, demanded, and when none of those worked he fell silent and waited for reality to do the convincing for him. Please was not in his vocabulary because please acknowledged that the other person had the power to refuse and that the refusal would be respected.

He said it now. He said it without emphasis, without the intonation of begging, but with the simple weight of a man who had reached the end of every other approach and was offering the only thing he had left: the admission that he needed something he could not take.

Hurkuzak felt the refusal inside him give way. Not dramatically. Not with the crash of a wall falling or the snap of something breaking. It gave way the way ice gives way in spring — slowly, from beneath, the structure still standing for a moment after the support was gone, and then settling, and then yielding, and then the water that had been held back moving through the gap with a quiet, unstoppable certainty.

"Yes," he said.

The word was small. It was the smallest word he had ever spoken and it was the largest thing he had ever done, and the space between the size of the word and the size of what it meant was a distance he would spend the rest of his life trying to cross.

Hakiia closed his eyes. When he opened them again, there was something in them that might have been gratitude and might have been grief and was probably both. He did not say thank you. He did not need to. The word Hurkuzak had said was answer enough, and the silence that followed it was the silence of two people who had arrived at the end of an argument that neither of them had wanted to have and that both of them had known the outcome of before the first word was spoken.

Hurkuzak stood. His legs held him. His body, which had been failing for hours, found something — not strength, but the absence of any alternative to strength — and he stood beside the fire and looked down at his brother and felt the full, unprocessed weight of what he had agreed to do.

He picked up the knife. It was Hakiia's knife — the one Hakiia had used to kill the elk and the wolf, the one that had always been an extension of his brother's hand, a tool that belonged to a man whose relationship to violence was as natural and unquestioned as his relationship to breathing. The handle was cold. The blade was clean — Hurkuzak had cleaned it after the wolf fight, out of habit, because Hakiia's tools were always clean and the maintaining of them was one of the small acts of care that had always been his.

He knelt beside the travois. He put his free hand on Hakiia's chest. Under his palm he could feel the heartbeat — fast, shallow, irregular, but present. Still present. Still his brother. Still the beating of the heart that had kept time in the chest of the man who had shaped every day of Hurkuzak's life through pressure and contempt and the monstrous, inescapable reality of love.

Hakiia looked at him. The look was steady. It held no fear, because Hakiia had never feared anything that he had decided to face, and he had decided to face this, and the decision had been his, and the consent was absolute.

"Don't hesitate," he said.

Hurkuzak did not hesitate.

The knife went where mercy lived. He felt the blade enter and felt the resistance and felt the giving way and Hakiia's hand came up and found his wrist and held it — not to stop him, not to pull the blade free, but to hold, to be touching his brother at the moment of crossing — and the grip was strong, stronger than a dying man's grip should have been, and then the grip loosened, and then it was gone.

Hakiia's eyes stayed open. They held Hurkuzak's face for a moment that lasted longer than any moment Hurkuzak had ever lived through, a moment in which the distance between alive and dead was visible and crossable and being crossed, and then the crossing was complete, and the eyes were still open but they were not seeing anymore, and the hand that had gripped his wrist lay on the furs with its fingers curled slightly inward, relaxed, as though the body's last act had been to let go of something it had been holding for a very long time.

The breathing stopped. Not gradually, the way it had been failing for hours, but completely, the way a sound stops when the thing making it ceases to exist. One moment there was breath. The next there was the absence of breath. The fire still burned. The wind still moved through the trees. The world continued its work on everything it had always been working on, and the only thing that had changed was that there was one fewer person in it, and the one fewer person was Hakiia, and Hakiia was dead, and Hurkuzak had killed him.

He did not withdraw the knife immediately. He knelt beside his brother's body with his hand on the handle and his other hand still on Hakiia's chest where the heartbeat had been and he stayed there. The fire lit them both. The blood that came from the wound was dark and slow and it pooled in the furs and the pooling was something that was happening outside of him, in a world that no longer contained the version of himself that had existed before this moment.

He was a different person now. He had been one kind of being before the knife and he was another kind of being after it. The line he had crossed was not a metaphor. It was a physical fact, as real as the blade and the blood and the silence that filled the space where his brother's breathing had been. He had killed. He had killed his brother. He had killed his brother because his brother had asked him to and because the asking had been right and because the alternative was worse and because love had demanded it and love had also made it unbearable and both of those things were true and neither of them canceled the other and they never would.

He pulled the knife free. He cleaned it. He cleaned it because Hakiia's tools were always clean and the maintaining of them was one of the small acts of care that had always been his, and the habit survived the killing because habits live in the body and the body had not yet received the news that everything had changed.

He closed Hakiia's eyes. The lids came down easily, as though they had been waiting for permission. The face underneath was the face from sleep — unclenched, unheld, the face of someone who had finally held still long enough to be loved without complication. It was the face Hurkuzak had watched by firelight two nights ago and thought looked like someone who might have been easy to love if he had ever stopped moving.

He was stopped now. He would not move again.

Hurkuzak sat beside his brother's body in the cold. The fire was dying. He did not feed it. The wind moved through the trees and the snow fell in small, intermittent flurries that landed on Hakiia's face and did not melt because the face was cooling and the warmth that had melted snow was leaving it in the slow, invisible way that warmth leaves all things when the source is gone.

He did not think about the cabin. He did not think about the morning or the march or the question of what came next. He did not think about survival or food or the cold or the wolves or the distance still to cover. He sat beside the body that had been his brother and the knife that had been his brother's and the furs that were soaked with his brother's blood and he felt the weight of what he had done settle into him like something swallowed, something that would live inside him now, something that would not dissolve or diminish or be reasoned away.

The mercy had been real. Hakiia had asked. Hakiia had been lucid. Hakiia had been certain. The consent was unquestionable and the suffering was real and the alternative was a slow death that would have taken both of them.

The killing had also been real. His hand on the knife. The blade going in. The resistance and the giving way. The grip on his wrist and the loosening of the grip. The last breath and then the absence. He had done that. His hand. His choice. His brother's death lived in his muscles and his bones and behind his eyes and it would live there for as long as he lived, which might not be very long, but would feel long enough.

Both things were true. The mercy and the killing. The love and the act. They occupied the same space inside him without canceling each other, without resolving into something simpler, without allowing him the grace of believing that one was more real than the other. He had done the right thing. The right thing had destroyed something in him that would not come back.

He sat with that. He could not do anything else.

The fire died. The cold came in. The snow fell on his brother's face and on his hands and on the furs and on the blood that was freezing now, turning dark and solid, becoming part of the winter the way everything became part of the winter eventually — the elk, the dogs, the wolves, the sled, the trail, the brothers.

Hurkuzak sat beside Hakiia's body and did not move. He would move later. He would have to. The cabin was ahead and the cold would kill him too if he stayed, and somewhere inside the numb wreckage of his mind there was a mechanism that would eventually engage and tell his body to stand and lift and carry and continue, because that is what bodies do when the person inside them has stopped making decisions.

But not yet. For now he sat. For now the only thing in the world was his brother's face, unclenched and still, and the silence that had replaced the wrong breathing, and the snow that fell without opinion on the living and the dead alike, and the cold, patient and absolute, continuing its work on everything.
