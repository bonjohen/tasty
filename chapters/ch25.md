# Chapter 25: Two Sets of Eyes

He woke from a dream that had belonged to someone else.

The details were precise and wrong. He was standing in a place he had never been — a high meadow with the tree line far below and the sky wide and clear and the wind carrying the smell of ice and stone. His body was larger than his body. His hands, when he looked at them, were broader, the knuckles thicker, the grip strength evident even at rest. He was looking at the landscape with the particular attention of a man who was planning something — assessing routes, calculating distances, measuring the terrain against a purpose that lived in his mind as a fixed point, a destination he was moving toward with the certainty that defined everything he did.

The purpose was unclear. The dream did not provide it. But the feeling of purpose was overwhelming — a dense, directed intentionality that organized every perception around a single question: how do I get there from here? The question was so characteristically Hakiia that waking from it felt like waking from a visit, as though Hurkuzak had been a guest in his brother's mind and had been returned to his own with the residue of the visit still clinging to his thoughts.

He lay on the ground where he had slept — no fire, a cold camp, the spring night mild enough that the blanket was sufficient — and waited for the presence to settle. The waiting had become a practice. Each morning now there was a period of calibration, a few minutes where the boundary between his thoughts and the other thoughts reestablished itself after the looseness of sleep, and the reestablishing was imperfect, and the imperfection was growing larger with each day.

The voice had not spoken during the dream. But the voice had been present — as the atmosphere of the dream, as the quality of attention that organized the dreaming mind's perceptions, as the certainty that permeated every detail. The voice was not separate from the dream. The voice was the dream. Or the dream was the voice's way of expressing something that could not be compressed into terse corrections and tactical assessments.

He moved through the day with the new awareness that the reconciliation of the previous days had produced. The awareness was not peace. It was proximity — a closeness to the presence inside him that he had stopped fighting and had not yet accepted and was occupying the space between those two states with the uncomfortable honesty of a man who knows he is changing and cannot stop the change and is trying at least to watch it clearly while it happens.

The voice was more continuous now. Not louder — more frequent, and more various. It reacted to the world with emotions that were not Hurkuzak's emotions. A well-built stone wall produced a feeling of approval that was aesthetic, not practical — the approval of someone who valued construction for its own sake. A poorly maintained road produced irritation out of proportion to the inconvenience — the irritation of someone who took infrastructure personally, who saw neglected public works as evidence of a culture's decline. A bird of prey circling above the road produced a moment of focused admiration that Hurkuzak felt in his chest as a warmth, as recognition, as the specific pleasure that a violent person takes in watching violence done beautifully by something that does not know it is beautiful.

These were Hakiia's responses. Hurkuzak knew it now. He had stopped pretending otherwise in the privacy of his own mind, which was no longer entirely private. The voice was Hakiia. The corrections were Hakiia. The dreams were Hakiia. The physical capabilities, the combat reflexes, the enhanced senses — all Hakiia, transferred through the consumption and integrated into Hurkuzak's body at a level deeper than conscious access could reach.

He moved through the morning with this knowledge — the private, unspoken acknowledgment that the thing inside him was his brother — and the knowledge changed the texture of every interaction between his mind and the presence. The corrections were still corrections, still terse and impatient, but now they carried a context that transformed them from noise into communication. When the voice directed him to take a higher path along a ridge, it was not an anonymous instinct offering tactical advice. It was Hakiia, telling his younger brother where to walk, the way Hakiia had always told his younger brother where to walk, with the same certainty and the same contempt for the slower mind's hesitation. The dynamic was the dynamic. It had survived death, survived consumption, survived the transfer from one body to another. Hakiia corrected. Hurkuzak followed. The choreography was ancient and it was theirs.

He did not say the name aloud. Not yet. The saying aloud would be a different kind of acknowledgment — public, even if the only audience was the trees and the road and the spring air. Private acknowledgment was a thought held silently. Public acknowledgment was a name spoken into the world, and the speaking of names had power, and the power was the power of making real what had previously been allowed to remain possible.

He thought about the dream's purpose — the fixed point, the destination the dreaming mind had been moving toward. He had felt it as a direction, a pull, an orientation that organized everything around reaching a specific place for a specific reason. The reason had been unclear in the dream but the certainty of the reason had been absolute, and the absoluteness was Hakiia's. Hakiia had always known where he was going and why, and the why was always sufficient and the going was always committed and the combination of sufficient and committed was what made Hakiia impossible to argue with, because arguing with a man who knows his destination and will not stop is like arguing with water about the direction of downhill.

What had the destination been? The cabin? The hunting trip had been aimed at the cabin. Hakiia had known the route. Hakiia had led them there. The dream's perspective — standing in a high meadow, assessing routes, calculating with a purpose — was consistent with a man planning a journey he intended to make, and the journey he had made was the journey to the cabin, and the cabin had become the place where everything happened.

The thought circled something he could not yet see. He let it circle.

He passed through a camp midday — not a settlement but a temporary gathering of travelers sharing a fire. He bought bread from a woman who baked it in the coals and the bread was warm and real and tasted of grain and ash and it was the first food in weeks that tasted of anything at all. The tasting surprised him. He ate slowly, noticing the flavor, noticing that the noticing was possible again, and the possibility felt like a small recovery of something the cabin had taken.

He handled the interaction competently. The competence was not entirely his. He read the woman's body language the way Hakiia read people — as systems of intention and response, calibrated to provide information about mood, threat level, and the probability that the interaction would produce what he needed. He chose his words the way Hakiia chose words — to control the exchange, to reveal nothing, to create the impression of ordinariness that would dissolve from memory as soon as he left the fire.

He noticed himself doing this and the noticing was the strangeness: the simultaneous awareness of acting and being acted through, of choosing and being chosen for, of the two sets of priorities — his own desire for invisibility and the voice's desire for efficiency — aligning on the same behavior for different reasons.

He noticed, as he left the camp, that the woman watched him go with an expression he had seen before — the slightly unsettled look of a person who has interacted with something that was almost normal and the almost was visible and the visibility was unnameable. She did not look afraid. She looked uncertain. The uncertainty was what he produced in people now: not fear but the sense that the categories they used to organize the world did not quite contain him.

The afternoon was quiet. He walked through woodland that was coming fully into leaf, the canopy closing overhead, the light filtering green and gold. He stopped in a clearing where the light was strong enough to warm the ground and sat with his back against a tree and felt the sun on his face and the wind in the new leaves and the presence inside him that was always there and would always be there and was, he understood now, not going away.

"Hakiia," he said.

He said the name once. Quietly, without inflection, the way a person says the name of someone they are acknowledging rather than addressing. He did not expect a response. He did not receive one — not in words, not in the terse corrections that had become the voice's primary mode. But something shifted. An attention. A focusing of the presence, as though the name had been a key that opened something or a call that turned something's head. The presence did not speak. But it listened, more completely than it had listened before, and the listening was the closest thing to a reply that a dead brother could offer.

He sat with the name in the air and the presence in his mind and the spring around him and the grief that was not only guilt anymore. The grief had expanded. It contained the cabin and the killing and the eating and the memories and the reinterpretation and the love that came wrapped in damage and the damage that came wrapped in love and the terrible, complex, unresolvable wholeness of a brother who had been all of those things at once and whose presence inside him was all of those things at once and whose name, spoken into the quiet of a spring afternoon, felt like the first honest thing Hurkuzak had said since the night he said yes to the knife.

He was not at peace. He was not healed. He was not resolved. He was something harder than any of those: he was carrying his brother with open eyes, seeing the monstrous and the loving and the unbearable simultaneously, and the seeing was the closest thing to understanding he was capable of, and the understanding was not forgiveness but something older and more painful — acceptance without absolution, love without excuse, the knowledge that the person inside him was exactly what he had always been and that what he had always been was too many things to be held in a single judgment.

He thought about the dream again. The meadow, the purpose, the direction. The thought circled closer this time and almost touched the thing it was circling, and the thing it was circling was a question he had not yet asked: if Hakiia had planned the journey to the cabin with such certainty and such purpose, what had the purpose been? The trip had been presented as a hunting expedition, a hardening exercise, another chapter in the lifelong project of making Hurkuzak into something that could survive the world. But the dream's purpose had felt deeper than that. More specific. More directed. The feeling was a seed. He did not know what it would grow into. He would.

He stood. He walked east. The name stayed in the air behind him and the presence walked with him and the walking was the walking of two beings in one body, one alive and one dead and both of them real, and the spring continued its indifferent work of turning the world green and warm and alive, and the work included them, and the inclusion was neither comfort nor cruelty but simply the world doing what the world did, which was to go on.
