# Chapter 3: The Distance Between Them

They broke camp before dawn. Hakiia had the fire killed and the sled repacked while the sky was still black, moving through the work with the unhurried certainty of a man who had already planned the day in the minutes between waking and standing. Hurkuzak's hands were clumsy with cold. He fumbled with the dogs' harnesses, pulling straps tight and then tighter when Hakiia glanced over, and the dogs stood patient under his attention as though they understood he was being watched and were trying not to make it worse for him.

The terrain changed within the first hour. The forest thickened, then gave way to a long slope of exposed rock and ice where the snow had been scoured thin by wind and the ground underneath was slick and treacherous. The dogs struggled for footing. The sled fishtailed on the patches of bare stone and the runners scraped with a sound that set Hurkuzak's teeth together. Hakiia walked ahead, picking the line, and Hurkuzak followed with one hand on the sled rail and the other braced against whatever the ground offered — a rock, a frozen stump, the bark of a tree growing at the angle wind and gravity had negotiated between them.

The slope steepened. The dogs dropped their heads and hauled with the grim efficiency of animals that had learned the only way through a hill was through a hill, and Hurkuzak leaned into the sled from behind and pushed until the muscles in his legs burned and his breathing became something he could hear over the wind. Ahead of him, Hakiia's pace did not change. His brother climbed the way he did everything — as though the hill existed as a formality and his body had already settled the matter.

At the crest the wind hit them full. It came from the north with nothing between its origin and their faces but open sky, and the cold inside it was a different thing from the cold they had been carrying. This was cold that moved. It found the gaps in Hurkuzak's wrappings and pressed through them like water through cracked stone. The dogs curled their tails under and tucked their ears and kept pulling.

Hurkuzak looked at the terrain ahead and saw that the trail Hakiia was following ran along the ridgeline, exposed and bare, for what looked like half a league before descending back into tree cover. Below them, to the left, the forest continued in a sheltered draw where the wind would break against the trunks and the snow would be deeper but softer. The dogs would move more easily there. The sled would ride better. The difference would cost them perhaps an hour of extra distance but save twice that in effort.

He said nothing for a while. Then he said it.

"The trees would be easier on the dogs."

Hakiia did not stop walking. "The ridgeline is faster."

"The dogs are working harder up here. The surface is wrong for the runners."

"The dogs will manage."

"The left rear is still favoring the pad I wrapped last night. If she splits it again up here she'll bleed through the binding and we'll have to stop."

Hakiia turned his head. Not all the way. Just enough to show that the comment had registered, which was more than most of Hurkuzak's observations received. He looked at the ridgeline ahead, then at the draw below, and then forward again. He did not change course.

They took the ridge. The wind battered them for the full distance. The dogs worked twice as hard as they would have in the trees, and by the time the trail dropped back into cover the left rear was limping badly and Hurkuzak had to stop and rewrap the pad with numb fingers while the dog stood trembling and Hakiia stood watching, saying nothing, which could have meant anything but which Hurkuzak read as the particular silence his brother used when reality had confirmed something he preferred not to acknowledge.

Hurkuzak did not say anything about it either. There was no currency in being right about the dog. Hakiia's model of the world did not include spaces where Hurkuzak saw things more clearly. If the dog was hurting, it was because the dog was weak, or the terrain was unlucky, or the pad had been poorly wrapped in the first place. The possibility that the route itself had been the error — that Hurkuzak's suggestion had been correct — would not register. Not because Hakiia was stupid. Because the architecture of his attention did not contain a room where his younger brother's judgment lived.

They descended into the valley and the trees closed around them again and the wind died to something bearable. The dogs settled into their pace. The sled found its rhythm. Hurkuzak walked behind and watched his brother's back and felt the old confusion settle over him like weather — the admiration and the anger, the gratitude and the resentment, the knowledge that Hakiia had been wrong about the ridge and would never know it and would never be diminished by not knowing it.

It was past midday when Hakiia stopped again. They had been climbing steadily since the valley floor, the trail switching back and forth through heavy timber, and the altitude had thinned the air enough that Hurkuzak's lungs worked harder than his legs. Hakiia built a small fire — more for the dogs than for themselves, though he would not have said so — and they sat across from each other on fallen logs and ate dried meat and drank snowmelt that tasted of nothing.

Hakiia ate the way he did everything: efficiently, without apparent pleasure, as though food were fuel and the body a mechanism that required it at intervals. Hurkuzak ate slowly. The meat was tough and salted and his jaw ached from the cold, and there was a quality to eating beside his brother that always made him aware of the difference between them. Hakiia consumed. Hurkuzak ate. The distinction felt like it should mean something.

"Father would have taken the trees," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak looked up. Hakiia was staring into the fire. His face was the face it always was — angular, composed, harder than the wood it was lit by — but something in his voice had shifted, a loosening at the edges that appeared sometimes when he spoke about their father and vanished again before it could be mistaken for softness.

"He would have taken the trees," Hakiia said again. "And he would have been right about the dogs and wrong about the rest."

"Wrong about what?"

"About what the extra hour costs. You save the dogs, you lose the daylight, you camp in a worse position, you eat into the supplies. Father always thought the gentle choice was free. It never is." He broke a stick and fed the pieces to the fire. "He was a kind man. Kind men can afford kindness when the world is being kind back. When the world stops, they break."

Hurkuzak felt the conversation tightening. He knew this ground. Hakiia walked onto it periodically the way a man returns to a wound, probing it to see if the pain has changed.

"He didn't break," Hurkuzak said. "He died."

"He died because he broke. Because when the hard thing was in front of him he looked for the gentle way around it and the gentle way was not there and he did not have a second answer." Hakiia's voice was flat. Not cruel, exactly. Certain. The way a man is certain about weather or stone. "I loved him. You know that. But loving a man and learning from his death are not exclusive acts."

"And the lesson was what?"

Hakiia looked at him across the fire. The look had weight. Hurkuzak could feel it the way he felt the cold — not as a single sensation but as an accumulating pressure that settled into all the places where his defenses were thin.

"The lesson," Hakiia said, "is that you are exactly like him. You have his hands. His hesitation. His way of looking at a dying animal and feeling something for it before you finish the work. And if you stay this way — if nobody teaches you what the world does to people who flinch — you will die the way he died. Not old. Not ready. Just surprised."

The words landed the way Hakiia's words always landed: with the precision of something thrown by someone who understood exactly where the bruise already was. Hurkuzak sat with the heat of the fire on his face and the cold at his back and felt the old argument assemble itself inside him. The argument he never won because Hakiia did not argue the way other people argued. Hakiia stated. He corrected. He declared what the world was and then treated disagreement as evidence of the weakness he had just diagnosed.

"You think strength is one thing," Hurkuzak said. He said it quietly, not because he was afraid but because the truth of it did not need volume. "You think it looks like you. Sounds like you. Moves like you. And anything that looks different is just weakness waiting to get someone killed."

Hakiia's expression did not change.

"The dog," Hurkuzak said. "I told you about the trees and the dog. You took the ridge. The dog is bleeding through the wrap. You were wrong about the route and you'll never know it because you don't track that kind of cost. You track speed and distance and position and those are the things that matter to you and so those are the only things that are real." He paused. The fire cracked. "Father noticed things too. Maybe they weren't the things that would have saved him. But pretending they're nothing — pretending that seeing is the same as flinching — that's not strength. That's just a smaller room."

Hakiia looked at him for a long time. Long enough that Hurkuzak's heart moved from steady into something faster and less comfortable, because Hakiia's silences were where the dangerous things lived. When Hakiia spoke, when he criticized and corrected and dismissed, the dynamic was old and survivable. When he went quiet, the air changed.

Then Hakiia looked away. He looked at the fire and his mouth moved the way it moved when he was deciding whether something was worth responding to, and whatever he decided stayed inside him, and what he said instead was:

"We'll reach the cabin by tomorrow if the weather holds."

That was all. He stood and kicked snow onto the fire and began repacking the sled, and the conversation was over in the way that Hakiia's conversations were always over — not resolved, not finished, simply discontinued because he had decided the remaining words were not worth the time it would take to say them.

But something had shifted. Hurkuzak had said a true thing — a thing he had thought many times but never put into the air between them — and Hakiia had heard it and had not dismissed it and had not punished it. He had simply moved past it. Which was not the same as ignoring it. Hakiia did not move past things that did not register. He moved past things that he could not answer without admitting something he was not prepared to admit.

They walked through the afternoon. The trail narrowed and climbed and the trees grew smaller and more twisted with altitude. The snow was deep here, undisturbed, and the dogs worked through it with the patient endurance of creatures that did not expect the world to be easy and were only occasionally disappointed by that expectation. The sky remained heavy and gray and low, pressing down on the peaks like a hand holding a lid.

Near evening, Hakiia pointed. Hurkuzak followed the gesture and saw, in the far distance where the valley narrowed and the tree line bent against a long slope of exposed granite, something that was not a natural feature of the landscape. A line too straight. A shape too regular. The suggestion of walls and a roof, half-buried in snow, sheltered by the rock face behind it.

The cabin. Still distant — a day's travel, maybe more if the snow deepened — but real. A destination that until now had been only a word Hakiia used to justify another hour of walking.

"There," Hakiia said. Nothing else. He did not describe it. He did not explain what they would find there or what the plan was once they arrived. The cabin was a fact in the distance, and like all of Hakiia's facts, it existed on his terms and would be revealed according to his schedule.

They made camp that night in a hollow where the trees grew dense enough to block the worst of the wind. Hakiia built the fire. Hurkuzak tended the dogs. The routine was the same as the night before, the same as every night of the trip — Hakiia commanded the large decisions and Hurkuzak managed the small ones, and between them the camp assembled itself through a choreography so practiced it no longer required words.

He checked the left rear dog last. The pad had bled through the wrapping, as he had known it would, and the blood had frozen into the binding and the whole thing would need to be soaked and replaced. He melted snow in his cupped hands and held the warmth against the dog's paw until the ice released, then cleaned the wound and wrapped it again with fresh hide, tighter this time, with a layer of rendered fat beneath the binding to keep the moisture out. The dog watched him with the same expression as before — patience, trust, the quiet willingness of a creature that had learned to let someone else manage the pain.

He spent a long time with the dogs that evening. Longer than necessary. He adjusted harnesses, checked feet, ran his hands along their backs and flanks and legs looking for the heat that meant swelling or the flinch that meant a strain. He did this because the dogs needed it and because the work was honest and because it placed him in a part of the world where his kind of attention was not merely tolerated but required.

When he returned to the fire, Hakiia was already wrapped in his furs and looking at the sky through the gap in the canopy where the smoke rose and dissolved. His brother's face in firelight had always been the same face — hard, certain, shaped by a conviction that the world was a test and he was the only person in it who had studied the correct answers. But tonight there was something else in it. Not doubt. Hakiia did not doubt. Something closer to calculation, as though he were measuring a distance that had nothing to do with the trail or the cabin or the remaining days of travel.

Hurkuzak watched him and felt the thing he always felt. The weight of loving someone who believed that love was a training exercise. The confusion of being protected by someone whose protection felt like punishment. The impossible simultaneous truth that Hakiia was the person who had shaped him and the person who could not see what he had shaped.

He thought of their father. A kind man. Hakiia had said it without irony, which meant it was true, which meant Hakiia was capable of recognizing kindness and valuing it and still believing it was fatal. That was the distance between them. Not temperament. Not talent. Hakiia looked at their father and learned that gentleness kills. Hurkuzak looked at their father and learned that gentleness is what makes the killing matter.

They were both right. That was the worst part.

Hakiia slept first, as he always did. The transition from wakefulness to sleep was invisible, as though consciousness were a garment he could remove and set aside at will. One breath he was there. The next he was somewhere else, breathing slowly, his body relaxing into the ground with the trust of a man who had never been attacked in his sleep and could not imagine why anyone would be.

Hurkuzak lay awake and watched his brother in the firelight. Hakiia's face was different in sleep — not softer, exactly, but unclenched. The perpetual assessment was gone. The measuring. The readiness to correct. What remained was just a man's face, angular and strong and weathered by the same cold and wind that had weathered the landscape around them. He looked, in sleep, like someone who might have been easy to love if he had ever held still long enough.

Tomorrow they would reach the cabin, or the day after. The trip would become whatever Hakiia had always intended it to become, and Hurkuzak would follow because following was what he did, and Hakiia would lead because leading was the only language he spoke, and somewhere between the two of them there was a country neither of them could enter — a place where the older brother's strength and the younger brother's sight existed in the same body, where hardness and tenderness were not opposites but companions, where love did not have to be delivered as a wound in order to be real.

He watched Hakiia sleep and felt the full weight of it: the love, the fear, the envy, the resentment, and beneath all of them the thing he could not name, the thing that was not any of those feelings but was made of all of them pressed together until they became something dense and permanent and impossible to put down.

Shield and wound. Protector and tormentor. Walking ahead through the snow toward the place where everything would change, though neither of them knew it yet.

Hurkuzak closed his eyes. Sleep was a long time coming.

The fire burned low. The dogs breathed. The cold, patient and absolute, continued its work on everything.
