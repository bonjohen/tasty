# Chapter 5: What the Wolves Smell

Hurkuzak gathered what the fall had left him.

The work took time he did not have and he did it anyway because Hakiia told him to and because there was no alternative that did not end with them dead in the snow. He moved through the debris field in a widening circuit, crouching and digging and pulling things from the wreckage with his cut hand and his good hand, and every piece he found felt like a negotiation with the disaster — what it had taken, what it had allowed to survive, and what it had scattered so far that finding it cost more than losing it.

The food was mostly gone. The bundles of dried meat had burst on impact and the strips were scattered across the snow, half-buried, already stiffening in the cold. He gathered what he could see and stuffed it into his coat. One skin of water had survived intact. The others had split or lost their stoppers and the contents had frozen into the snow where they fell. He found two of the furs — heavy, blood-spotted, but whole — and dragged them back to where Hakiia sat against his rock.

The skinning tools he found in a leather roll that had landed in a crevice between two rocks. The knife was there. The small saw. The awl. The hide scraper. All of Hakiia's instruments, packed with the same precision their owner brought to everything, and the roll had held through the fall because the stitching was Hakiia's stitching and Hakiia's stitching did not fail.

He found the hatchet ten paces from the sled, half-buried in loose earth. He found the surviving sled runner — cracked but not broken — and dragged it free from the wreckage. He found a coil of rope, frayed at one end but usable. He found the bow. It had snapped at the grip.

He carried what he had back to Hakiia and laid it out in the snow and Hakiia looked at it the way a general looks at the remains of an army after a defeat — not with despair, but with the cold assessment of a man already calculating what kind of fight the survivors could still put up.

"The bow is gone," Hakiia said.

"Yes."

"The traps?"

"Buried. I couldn't reach them."

Hakiia's jaw tightened. The loss registered, was processed, was filed alongside the legs and the sled and the dogs in whatever inventory he was keeping of things the world had taken from him. "The knife. The hatchet. The runner. The rope. That will do." He shifted against the rock and his face went white and came back. "Now my legs."

Hurkuzak knelt beside him. The snow around Hakiia's lower body was dark with blood that had melted into the surface and then refrozen, creating a crust that cracked under Hurkuzak's knees like thin glass. The smell was sharp — iron and cold and something underneath that was the smell of a body in distress, a scent he would learn to recognize in the days that followed.

"The left first," Hakiia said. "It's worse."

Hurkuzak looked at the left leg. The break was below the knee, and the lower segment had rotated in a way that made the anatomy difficult to read. The boot was still on. The flesh above the boot was swollen and discolored and the shape of the leg was wrong in a way that his hands did not want to approach.

"I need to straighten it."

"I know what you need to do."

"It's going to — "

"I know what it is going to do. Do it."

Hurkuzak put both hands on Hakiia's lower leg. The flesh was hot beneath the cold skin, swollen tight, and under his fingers he could feel the edges of bone that were not where bone was supposed to be. Hakiia's breathing changed — faster, shallower, controlled — and Hurkuzak looked at his brother's face and saw something he had never seen there before. Not fear. Hakiia did not show fear. But a readiness for what was coming, a bracing, as though his body were a wall and he was pressing himself against it from the inside to keep it from falling.

Hurkuzak pulled. The leg resisted and then gave way and the bone shifted under his hands with a grinding sensation that traveled up through his arms and into his chest and Hakiia made a sound that was not a scream but was worse than a scream — a short, explosive compression of air through clenched teeth, the sound of a man swallowing something that his body desperately wanted to expel. His hands found the snow on either side of him and his fingers drove into the frozen ground and his whole body locked rigid and held.

The leg was straighter. Not right, but straighter. Hurkuzak kept his hands where they were and waited for his own breathing to steady and for the nausea to pass.

"Splint it," Hakiia said. His voice was different now. Thinner. The pain had taken something from it that authority alone could not replace. "Use the crossbar pieces. Bind it tight. Not at the break — above and below."

Hurkuzak found two pieces of the shattered sled crossbar that were the right length and relatively straight. He positioned them on either side of Hakiia's lower leg and bound them with strips of leather he cut from the harness traces. The binding had to be tight enough to hold the bone and loose enough not to cut off the blood, and he had never done this before, and Hakiia directed him through each step with the precise, patient irritability of a man who was teaching someone a skill while in the worst pain of his life.

"Tighter at the top. Looser at the ankle. You're wrapping it like a gift. Wrap it like you're trying to hold something together that wants to come apart."

Hurkuzak adjusted. Hakiia watched and said nothing, which meant adequate.

The right leg was less dramatic but no simpler. The break was at the ankle — the foot hung at a wrong angle and the boot was full of blood. Hurkuzak cut the boot away and found the ankle joint swollen to twice its size, the skin purple-black, the bones underneath shifted in a way that he could feel but could not see. He set it as well as he could, using the last of the crossbar pieces and more harness leather, and Hakiia endured it with the same compressed silence, and when it was done they sat together in the bloody snow and breathed.

"You'll need to build the travois," Hakiia said. "The runner and the rope. Two poles if you can cut them. Use the furs to pad it."

"I know."

"You don't know. You think you know. I'm going to tell you how."

Even now. Even with both legs broken and his face gray with pain and his body reduced to something that could not stand or walk or crawl without becoming something worse. Even now, Hakiia corrected. The pattern was so deep that not even this could interrupt it. Hurkuzak felt the familiar anger rise and then something else rose with it — something that was not anger but was built from the same material, rearranged. His brother could not stop teaching because teaching was the only way he knew to care, and caring was the only thing keeping him conscious, and consciousness was the only thing keeping him alive.

"Then tell me," Hurkuzak said.

Hakiia told him. Cut two poles from the stand of young trees at the edge of the basin — wrist-thick, straight, long enough that Hurkuzak could walk between them and still clear the drag behind. Lash the surviving runner across as a crosspiece, two-thirds of the way back. Use the rope in a figure pattern, not a spiral, because a spiral would work loose under load and drop the crosspiece and then the whole thing would fold. Lay the furs across the frame with the hide side down, because hide on snow slides and fur on snow grips and the difference between those two things was the difference between dragging his brother and dragging an anchor.

Hurkuzak cut the poles with the hatchet. The wood was green and tough and the hatchet was not a felling tool and each stroke sent a jarring shock up through his bruised ribs. He worked through it. The cold was deepening as the afternoon settled toward evening, and the temperature drop was not gradual — it came in steps, each one lower than the last, as though the air were descending a staircase toward a place where blood stopped moving and breath turned to ice before it left the mouth. His fingers were losing feeling. His cut palm had stopped bleeding but the wound had frozen open and the cold inside it felt like a second injury layered on top of the first.

He lashed the frame together while Hakiia watched and corrected. The rope was stiff with cold and fought the knots, and twice Hurkuzak had to undo a lashing and redo it because the angle was wrong or the tension was uneven, and each time Hakiia told him what to fix with the same patient exhaustion of a man who understood that his survival now depended on the quality of someone else's hands.

The travois took shape in the snow beside them — crude, heavy, built from wreckage and rope and the stubborn refusal of two brothers to accept what the cliff had done to their future.

While he worked, the wolves arrived.

They came from above. The cliff face was too steep for a direct descent, but the collapse had created a debris slope at the far end of the basin that angled up to the trail, and the wolves found it the way they found everything — by following the smell of blood downhill. The first one appeared at the top of the slope and stood there, watching. Gray and lean and still, with the patience of something that had never once been in a hurry because hunger always won eventually.

Then a second. Then a third.

They did not come down immediately. They watched. The dead dogs were below them, and the smell of those dogs was doing the work that the wolves' courage had not yet finished. The first wolf started down the slope in a careful, sideways descent, testing the footing, pausing every few steps to look at the two living things at the center of the basin and judge the cost of approaching them against the certainty of the meal that was already dead and waiting.

It reached the nearest dog carcass and began to feed.

The sound was the sound of tearing. Wet, muscular, deliberate. The wolf gripped and pulled and the frozen flesh resisted and then gave way and the wolf ate with the focused efficiency of an animal that understood scarcity. The other two descended. They spread out. Each found a carcass. The feeding was not frantic. It was systematic. Organized. The pack was eating methodically, conserving energy, watching the living things at the center while the dead things lasted.

"How many?" Hakiia asked.

"Three. Feeding on the dogs."

"There will be more. How far are they from us?"

"Forty paces. Maybe fifty."

"They won't stay on the dogs. Not all of them. Not once the feeding gives them confidence." Hakiia looked at the hatchet in Hurkuzak's belt and the knife beside the travois. "When they're done with the easy meat, they'll test us. They can see I'm down. They can smell the blood."

Hurkuzak looked at the wolves. They were eating with their heads low and their eyes up, watching him between bites. The nearest one had blood on its muzzle that was not its own and its gaze held the quality he recognized from every predator he had ever seen — not malice, not cruelty, just the steady appraisal of something deciding whether another thing was food.

He understood, with a clarity that felt like cold water, what the fall had done. Not just the physical damage. The fall had created a trap. The cliff behind them cut off retreat. The debris field channeled movement. The dead dogs held the wolves in the basin, and the wolves would eat the dogs, and then the dogs would be gone, and then there would be only two living bodies in the snow and one of them could not move and the other was bleeding and hurt and holding a hatchet he carried like something he was afraid of.

"We need to move," Hurkuzak said.

"Not yet. Finish the travois."

"They're getting closer."

"They're eating. As long as they're eating, they're not hunting. Finish the travois."

Hurkuzak finished the travois. His hands were shaking and his cut palm left blood on the rope and the lashing was rougher than it should have been, but the joints held when he tested them and the furs lay flat on the frame and it would slide. It would have to.

He moved Hakiia onto it. This was the worst part of the day, and the day had contained the worst things Hurkuzak had ever experienced. He had to lift his brother — grip him under the arms, brace against the dead weight, and lower him onto the frame — and Hakiia's broken legs dragged across the snow and the sound he made was the sound from earlier, the compressed explosion of swallowed agony, except this time it was longer and his hands found Hurkuzak's shoulders and gripped and his fingers dug into the muscle hard enough that Hurkuzak felt the bruises forming in real time.

Then Hakiia was on the travois. He lay on his back with his legs splinted and his face the color of the snow beneath him and his breathing was wrong — too fast, too shallow, the breathing of a body trying to manage pain that exceeded its capacity to manage pain. He closed his eyes. He opened them.

"Go," he said.

Hurkuzak picked up the drag poles. The travois was heavy — Hakiia was a large man and the furs added weight and the snow beneath the frame was soft enough to grip rather than slide. He leaned into the poles and pulled and the travois moved, slowly, grudgingly, like something that resented being asked to do what sleds were built for.

Behind them, the wolves lifted their heads. Three of them, muzzles bloody, watching the movement with the still attention of animals that had learned that things which moved away from them were things worth following.

A fourth wolf had appeared at the top of the debris slope. Then a fifth. They descended and joined the others at the carcasses, but their heads turned to follow the travois as it scraped across the snow, and Hurkuzak could feel their attention on his back like heat, like pressure, like the weight of something patient deciding how much longer it was willing to wait.

He pulled. The basin extended ahead of him, white and flat and featureless, and the valley narrowed in the distance toward the place where the cabin might be, and behind him the wolves fed and watched and grew bolder by the hour, and the cold continued its work, and the blood in the snow was a map that pointed in one direction, and the direction was them.
