# Chapter 26: The Market of Parts

The town was called Merrath and it existed for the sole purpose of turning one thing into another — raw material into goods, goods into money, money into the kinds of lives that money purchases in places where nothing grows that can be eaten without first being processed, packaged, and sold. It sat at the junction of three trade roads and the junction gave it power and the power gave it size and the size gave it the particular anonymity that Hurkuzak needed, which was the anonymity of a crowd large enough that one more face was not a face but a number.

He entered from the north road in midmorning and the market was already working. Stalls lined the central street, awnings of stretched hide and cloth shading tables of goods. The noise was the noise of commerce — haggling, hammering, the clatter of wheels and the complaints of animals and the layered conversation of people doing the thing that people in trading towns did, which was converting proximity to profit.

He moved through it the way the voice had taught him to move through populated spaces: peripheral, unhurried, eyes forward but attention wide, reading the crowd's currents for the gaps that would let him pass without generating friction. The voice offered its usual commentary — that man is armed, the alley on the left has two exits, the garrison presence is light — and Hurkuzak received it the way he received all of the voice's observations now: as information from a source he had stopped questioning and had not yet fully accepted.

He noticed the merchant's stall because the smell was wrong.

Not bad — wrong. The stall sold preserved meats in wrapped parcels and the smell that came from the parcels was the smell of salted, dried, rendered flesh, and the smell was familiar in a way that made his stomach clench, because the familiarity was the familiarity of the cabin, of the pan over the hearth, of the specific scent that demon flesh produced when it was cooked and the cooking was something he knew as intimately as his own hands.

He stopped. He looked at the parcels. They were neatly wrapped and labeled with a notation system he did not recognize — symbols and numbers, a grading language, the shorthand of a trade that had developed its own vocabulary. The merchant behind the stall was a small man with careful hands who watched Hurkuzak look and said nothing because saying nothing was part of the transaction: the customer either knew what they were looking at or they did not, and the knowing was its own qualification.

Hurkuzak did not buy anything. He walked on. But the stall stayed with him — the smell, the parcels, the notation — and as he moved through the market the stall multiplied. Not identical stalls, but adjacent ones. A physician's shop with jars on shelves, the jars containing materials he could not see clearly but could smell through the glass, and the smell was the same wrong-familiar smell, and the labels on the jars used terms that the voice recognized before Hurkuzak did: the voice supplied the meanings the way it supplied corrections — automatically, without being asked, as though the knowledge were already present and merely needed to be surfaced.

Liver extract, rendered. Marrow suspension. Sinew concentrate, dried. The terms were the terms of a trade, and the trade was the trade of bodies like his, and the bodies had been disassembled with the same kind of care he had used in the cabin — systematically, efficiently, with the specific knowledge of which parts carried which value and which preparations preserved which properties.

He stood outside the physician's shop and stared at the jars and felt the voice supply information in a continuous stream: the preparation methods, the shelf life, the interaction effects when different tissues were consumed in combination. The information arrived without source attribution, without the contextual markers that ordinary memory carries — the when and where and how of learning. It was simply there, as though a library had been opened inside his mind and the catalogue was being browsed by a reader who knew exactly what they were looking for.

The reader was Hakiia. The knowledge was Hakiia's. How his brother had come to possess such specific understanding of the flesh trade's pharmacology was a question that settled into Hurkuzak's mind and did not leave. Hakiia had been a hunter, a mountain man, a man who lived by violence and competence in the wild. He had not been a merchant or a physician. Yet the knowledge was there, and the knowledge was detailed, and the detail implied proximity to the trade that went beyond what a hunter's general awareness would provide.

He stood outside the physician's shop and felt the world rearrange itself around a new understanding. The understanding was not that demon flesh was consumed — he had known that since the silence in the trading post, since the word that was not repeated and the conversation that was steered away. The understanding was that the consumption was organized. It was not desperation or depravity or the isolated acts of people in extremis. It was an industry. It had suppliers, processors, distributors, and consumers. It had pricing and grading and quality standards. It had professional vocabulary and established trade routes and the specific kind of normalized infrastructure that meant it had been operating for long enough that the operating had become ordinary.

He walked further. The market's structure became visible once he knew what he was looking at. Not every stall dealt in demon flesh — most sold ordinary goods, the grain and cloth and tools that any trade town offered. But woven through the ordinary commerce was a second economy, visible to those who knew the signs and invisible to those who did not. The signs were the smell, first. The notation, second. The particular quality of discretion that the merchants maintained — not secrecy, but tact, the professional restraint of people who understood that their trade was legal and normalized but not conversational, the way certain aspects of medicine or certain functions of the body are legal and normalized but not discussed at dinner.

A butcher's stall occupied a position at the market's edge, slightly removed from the main traffic, shaded by a canvas awning that was cleaner than its neighbors. The cuts on display were too carefully graded for common livestock, the butcher's hands too precise, the blade work too anatomically specific. Each cut was wrapped in treated cloth and marked with the same notation system the preserved-meat merchant had used. The butcher worked with the focused efficiency of someone who understood the material at a level that ordinary meat did not require, and the understanding was written in the economy of his movements: the way he handled each piece as though it were irreplaceable, the way he cleaned his blade between cuts with a thoroughness that suggested the residue mattered, the way he weighed each portion on a scale that was finer than any scale a mutton seller would own.

A woman was purchasing from the butcher. She discussed her order the way a person discusses a prescription — specific requirements, dosage concerns, questions about freshness and origin. The butcher answered with the measured expertise of a pharmacist. This cut was from the upper limb, good for general vitality. That preparation was concentrated marrow, recommended for healing injuries that would not close on their own. The rendering was done within twelve hours of harvest, which preserved the active properties. The price reflected the quality. She paid without negotiating, which meant the price was expected, which meant the price was established, which meant this transaction was not an event but a repetition of transactions that had established the price through the same mechanism that established all prices: reliable demand.

He overheard a conversation. Two merchants, or a merchant and a buyer, standing beside a wagon and discussing shipment in the same tone that grain merchants used to discuss grain. The numbers were quantities — weights, probably, in the local system of measurement. The descriptors were tissue types: long muscle, dense bone, organ suite. One of them mentioned freshness and the other mentioned price and the price was discussed with the same matter-of-fact specificity that any commodity price is discussed by people who trade in it daily.

He heard a man at a nearby stall complaining about import tariffs on preserved organ shipments from the eastern provinces. The complaint was ordinary — the complaint of a businessman whose margins were being squeezed by regulation, delivered in the universal tone of commercial grievance that sounded the same whether the product was wool or wheat or the liver of a creature that had been alive and thinking a week ago. The tariffs were too high. The inspectors were too frequent. The freshness requirements were unreasonable given the distance the product had to travel. The man wanted what all men in trade wanted: fewer obstacles between the product and the profit.

Children ran past the wagon. A woman at the adjacent stall called to them — something about supper, something ordinary — and the ordinariness was the thing that struck Hurkuzak with the force of a physical blow. The children. The supper. The normal life being lived adjacent to the commerce of bodies. No one in this marketplace was horrified. No one was performing a secret. They were doing business, and the business was the disassembly and sale of beings like Hurkuzak, and the doing was unremarkable to them in the way that all sufficiently established practices become unremarkable: through repetition, through normalization, through the slow erosion of the original shock until what remains is simply how things work.

The voice stirred. The stirring was not the usual tactical assessment. It was something else — darker, more personal, carrying an emotional charge that felt specific. It was contempt. Not for the merchants. For the system itself. The contempt of someone who had lived as the kind of creature this system harvested and who understood, with an intimacy that the merchants could never match, what it meant to be the material rather than the merchant.

And underneath the contempt, something colder. The voice — Hakiia — recognized the vocabulary. Not all of it. But enough. The terms for preparations, the grading system, the notation on the physician's jars. Some of it was familiar to the presence inside him in a way that Hurkuzak's own experience could not account for. Hakiia had known something about this system. How much, from what angle, with what intent — those questions had no answers yet. But the familiarity was there, and the familiarity was a seed, and the seed would grow into something terrible when the time came.

Hurkuzak's reaction was layered in ways that surprised him. The revulsion was there — automatic, visceral, the instinct of a person confronted with the systematic exploitation of his own kind. But underneath the revulsion was a recognition that cut deeper. He had already done what these people paid for. He had consumed his brother completely — every tissue, every organ, every part. The effects they purchased in careful doses, he had inherited in total. The market was a mirror, and the reflection it showed was himself.

The voice reacted to this recognition with something that was almost laughter — not mirth but the bitter, compressed sound of a truth too dark for any other container. Hakiia had always understood the world in terms of what it could take from you if you were not hard enough to prevent the taking. Now Hurkuzak was standing in a marketplace that had turned the taking into an industry, and the industry had proved Hakiia right in the most terrible possible way: the world did not kill gentle creatures first. It priced them.

Hurkuzak left Merrath in the late afternoon carrying provisions and a new understanding of his own body. He was not merely hunted because he was strange. He was hunted because every tissue in his body had a market price, and the total integration — the strength, the reflexes, the healing, the voice — was exactly what the market's wealthiest buyers had been trying to achieve through crude commercial means for decades. He was not an anomaly. He was a proof of concept. He was what happened when the mechanism worked the way the ancient texts said it could work, and the working was worth more than the sum of his parts because the sum of his parts was merely meat while the working was power, and power was what the people at the top of this system had always been buying.

The seed of doubt was small but it was real. Hakiia had known about the rite. Hakiia had known about the preparations. Hakiia had led them to the cabin with a purpose that the dream in Chapter 25 had hinted at — a fixed point, a certainty, a direction that organized everything else. The question that the dream had planted was growing now, fed by the market's evidence: if the flesh economy was this organized, this established, this wealthy in its upper tiers, then the people at the top would not merely wait for the rite to occur naturally. They would commission it. They would recruit participants. They would find the right demons, the right pairing, the right circumstances.

They would find a pair of brothers traveling to a remote cabin in winter.

The thought was incomplete. He did not follow it to its conclusion. The conclusion was too terrible and the reconciliation too fragile and the love he had just reopened too new. But the thought existed now, and it would not uninvent itself, and the not-uninventing was the first tremor of the earthquake that was coming.

He walked east. The voice was quiet but the quiet was not empty. It was the quiet of someone processing information that had changed the shape of every other piece of information they held, the way one key fact, properly placed, can restructure an entire understanding.

Hakiia had known. Not everything. But something. And the something was a thread that led from the cabin to the market to whatever waited at the end of the chain, and the chain was long and the end was far and Hurkuzak was walking toward it without knowing what he would find when he arrived.
