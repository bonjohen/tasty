# Chapter 7: The Weight of What Is Left

He did not pull far.

Fifty paces from the rocks, maybe less, the travois snagged on something buried in the snow and Hurkuzak's legs folded and he went down to his knees and could not immediately stand again. The cold pressed in against his face where it met the snow, and for a moment the snow seemed warm by comparison with the air above it, and that wrongness was how he knew he was in trouble.

He got up. He freed the travois. He pulled.

The wolves did not return. Their absence was a presence of its own — a space in the landscape where attention had been, now empty but still shaped like watching. Hurkuzak moved through it with the slow, grinding effort of a body that had exceeded its reserves and was now borrowing from something deeper, something that would need to be repaid but not yet, not tonight, not until the shape of the valley changed or the trees thickened or the cabin appeared or his body decided on its own that it was finished with him.

Behind him, Hakiia was quiet.

That was the first thing Hurkuzak noticed and the thing he tried not to think about. Since the cliff, since the fall, since the wolves, Hakiia had been the voice that held the situation together. He had directed, corrected, assessed, and commanded from the ground with the same relentless authority he had exercised from the front of the line, and that authority had been the architecture of Hurkuzak's obedience, the structure that told his body where to go and what to do and in what order. Without it, the march was just a man pulling weight through snow.

He stopped after another hundred paces and turned to look at his brother.

Hakiia's eyes were open. That was good. But the quality of them had changed. They were focused on something above him — the sky, the tree canopy, the darkness that was settling over the valley like a lid — and the focus was not the sharp, tactical focus Hurkuzak had always known. It was softer. Duller. The look of a man watching something he could not quite make sense of.

"Hakiia."

The eyes moved. Slowly. They found Hurkuzak and held him and something behind them was working, trying to assemble the information — the face, the snow, the direction of travel — into something coherent.

"The cabin," Hakiia said.

"I'm heading toward it."

"Good." A pause. "Check the splints."

Hurkuzak set down the drag poles and knelt beside the travois. The splints on the left leg had shifted during the fight. The binding had loosened where the wolf's thrashing had knocked against the crossbar pieces, and the leg underneath had moved in a way that sent a deep, sick feeling through Hurkuzak's stomach. He retightened the binding. Hakiia's face did not change. The pain was still there — it had to be enormous — but it was registering differently now, as though the mechanism that translated pain into expression had been damaged or overwhelmed or simply run out of material.

The right leg was worse. The ankle splint had held, but the skin above it had darkened further, almost black, and the swelling had spread upward into the calf. The flesh was hot to the touch — a wrong heat, the heat of a body fighting something it could not reach. Hurkuzak pressed gently against the swelling and felt the skin resist and then yield, and the yielding was soft in a way that skin should not be soft, and he pulled his hand back.

"How does it look?" Hakiia asked.

Hurkuzak did not want to answer that question. He could see what was happening inside the leg even if he could not name it. The color, the heat, the softness — these were the signs of a body losing the battle with its own damage. Blood was pooling where it should not pool. Tissue was dying where it should not die. Whatever was happening below the break was happening faster than cold or binding or prayer could stop.

"The splint is holding."

"That's not what I asked."

"It's swollen. The color is bad."

Hakiia looked at the sky. He said nothing for a while. Then he said, "Check the splints," and Hurkuzak realized that his brother had already said that, less than a minute ago, and had forgotten that he had said it, and that the forgetting was new.

He cleaned what he could reach. The gash on Hakiia's forehead had stopped bleeding and frozen over in a dark crust that made the left side of his face look like it belonged to a different man. The right hand — what was left of it — was worse than when he had cauterized it. The sealed tissue had darkened further, the skin around the burns hot and swollen in the way that meant infection was already working inside the damage. The thumb stump was discolored. The two remaining fingers, broken and fixed at their wrong angles, had swelled against the cauterized edges. The hand smelled wrong — not the clean smell of a fresh wound but the thick, sweet smell of flesh beginning to lose the argument with its own decay.

The left arm had not moved since the fight. He lifted it to check the shoulder and the arm came up like something that did not belong to a living body — dead weight, the joints loose, the fingers hanging without tension. He let it go. It fell against the furs. It did not twitch. Whatever the cliff had started in the shoulder, the wolf fight had finished. The nerve that connected Hakiia's will to the arm's action was gone, and the gone was permanent, and the permanence meant that between the destroyed right hand and the dead left arm, Hakiia could not hold food, could not hold water, could not hold the knife that lay in the snow beside the travois, could not do any of the things that a man does to keep himself alive or to end himself when the keeping alive becomes worse than the ending.

He checked Hakiia's breathing. It was faster than it should have been and shallower than it should have been and every few breaths there was a pause — a gap where the rhythm skipped, as though the body had briefly considered stopping and then changed its mind. The pauses were short. They would get longer.

He tried to stop the bleeding from his own forearm. The wrap had soaked through and the wound underneath was still seeping, and the cold had slowed it but not stopped it. He tore another strip from his shirt and bound it over the first, pulling it tight enough that the pressure sent a wave of pain from wrist to elbow. His fingers were stiffening. The bite had done something to the tendons or the muscle that affected his grip, and when he flexed his hand the response was slow and incomplete, as though the messages between his mind and his fingers were traveling through something thicker than usual.

He looked at his brother again. Hakiia's eyes had closed. His face, half-crusted with dried blood, half-shadowed by the failing light, had settled into an expression Hurkuzak had never seen on it before. Not pain, though the pain was clearly there. Not the composed, measuring look that Hakiia wore the way other people wore skin. Something looser. As though the mechanism that held his face in its permanent configuration of authority had begun to slip, and what lay underneath was simply a man, tired beyond any tiredness Hurkuzak had ever witnessed, holding onto consciousness because letting go of it meant letting go of everything else.

He looked at their situation with the clear, exhausted eyes of a man who had run out of alternatives except the one in front of him. They were in a shallow valley in deep winter with no shelter, no fire, minimal food, and a body that was dying. The wolves were gone but the cold was not. The cabin was ahead, somewhere in the dark that was now falling in earnest, and the distance between here and there was made of snow and pain and every step cost more than the step before.

For the first time since the cliff, the decision was his. Not because Hakiia had yielded authority. Because authority required a mind that could track the situation, and Hakiia's mind was drifting. He was conscious. He was present. But the commands were coming slower and repeating, and the gaps between them were filling with a silence that had nothing to do with composure.

Hurkuzak built a fire. It took him a long time — his hands were bad, the wood was wet, and the cold was working against the spark with the patience of something that had been winning this particular argument since long before people existed. But the fire caught, eventually, because he would not let it not catch, and the warmth was small and insufficient and it was the only warmth there was.

He moved the travois closer to the flames. He arranged the furs over Hakiia's body and tucked them around the legs and the feet and the places where the cold entered first. The furs were stiff with blood — Hakiia's, the wolf's, his own, the distinctions lost in the freezing dark. He heated snow in his cupped hands and held the water to Hakiia's mouth. Hakiia drank some. Most of it ran down his chin and froze in the stubble there.

He sat beside his brother in the firelight and ate a strip of dried meat that tasted of nothing and chewed it until his jaw ached and swallowed it because a body needed fuel even when fueling it felt like a betrayal of the body beside it that was past such maintenance. He tried to give Hakiia a strip. Hakiia took it, held it, and did not eat it. After a while Hurkuzak took it back.

"You fought well," Hakiia said.

Hurkuzak turned. Hakiia was looking at him. The eyes were clearer now — the firelight had brought them back from wherever they had drifted, or the warmth had, or the pain that the warmth made sharper by giving the body enough energy to feel it again.

"I fought badly," Hurkuzak said. "I missed twice. I got bitten."

"You killed it."

"You killed the one that mattered."

Hakiia made a sound that might have been a laugh if his body had contained enough air for laughter. "I killed it from the ground with two broken legs and the killing took the last of what my hands had to give. That is not a model for a second engagement." He paused. His breathing hitched and steadied. "You stood. When it came, you stood. That is not nothing."

Hurkuzak did not know what to do with the words. In all the years of corrections and dismissals and the perpetual measurement of his inadequacy, Hakiia had never said anything that sounded like this. Not praise. Not quite. But an acknowledgment that something had happened and that the thing that had happened had been enough. From Hakiia, from a man whose definition of enough had always existed at a height that Hurkuzak could not reach, the acknowledgment landed with the weight of something that had never been given before and might not be given again.

Then Hakiia's right hand moved. What was left of it. The ruined thing came off the furs and moved toward Hurkuzak's arm — the unbitten one, the left — and the movement was slow, effortful, the remnant of a hand driven by a will that had outlasted the body's capacity to execute it. The thumb stump found the skin of Hurkuzak's forearm and pressed. The broken fingers did not close — could not close, would never close again. What touched Hurkuzak was not a grip but a pressure, the damaged remnant of a hand that had once held knives and wolf throats and gripped his brother's arm with casual authority, and that remnant was pressing against his skin with everything it had left, which was almost nothing, which was enough. The touch lasted for a breath, maybe two. Then the hand fell back to the furs, and the effort of the gesture was visible in the way the stump trembled as it withdrew, and the gesture was over, and it had meant more than every correction and criticism and cutting remark Hurkuzak had endured in his life.

He sat with it. He did not name it. If he named it, the naming would make it smaller. He let it stay what it was — a broken hand on his arm, too damaged to grip and too stubborn to stop reaching — and the cold pressed in and the fire burned and the night was very large around them.

In the morning — if the time before dawn could be called morning when the darkness was still total and the only light was embers — Hurkuzak repacked the travois and checked the splints and checked Hakiia's breathing, which was worse, and checked his eyes, which were slower, and picked up the drag poles and began to pull.

Hakiia did not speak. Not to direct. Not to correct. Not to tell Hurkuzak that his angle was wrong or his pace was too slow or his grip on the poles was the grip of a man who was afraid of what he was holding. The silence filled the space between them like a new element — not cold, not dark, not pain, but something made of all three, a substance that had no name but that Hurkuzak could feel against his skin as surely as the wind.

He pulled through the valley. The snow was deep and his body had found a rhythm that was not strength but the absence of any alternative to strength — a mechanical repetition of effort that asked nothing of his mind and demanded everything of the muscles in his back and legs and the ruined hands that held the poles. The dawn came late and gray, and when it arrived it illuminated a landscape that looked exactly like the landscape that had tried to kill them in the dark, except now he could see how much of it remained. The valley went on. The trees went on. The cold went on. The horizon never moved. It was always ahead, always the same distance, as though the world were a wheel turning beneath his feet and the cabin existed at its farthest point, receding at exactly the rate he approached.

He thought about nothing. Thought was a luxury the march did not allow. His world contracted to the poles in his hands, the drag of weight behind him, and the next place his boot would fall. Step and sink and lift and step and sink and lift, and each cycle cost a piece of something he had started the day with and would not have again. The cold went on. The snow went on. The cabin was somewhere ahead, and every step toward it was a step that cost something he could not get back.

Behind him, Hakiia breathed. The breathing was the sound of a man whose body had begun the work of deciding whether to continue, and the pauses between breaths were longer now, and the returns were shallower, and Hurkuzak could hear the whole process of decline in the rhythm the way you could hear a fire dying if you listened — not a dramatic change, just a gradual loosening of the hold that burning has on air.

He did not look back. Looking back meant seeing what was happening, and seeing what was happening meant acknowledging it, and acknowledging it meant admitting that the brother on the travois was no longer being carried toward rescue but toward a place where the end would be sheltered rather than exposed. Hurkuzak did not have the language for what he was carrying. Not a patient, because patients could recover. Not a burden, because burden erased the person underneath the weight. What he carried was his brother, and his brother was dying, and the dying was a process he could hear in the breathing and feel in the changing weight on the travois and see, when he did look back, in the way the furs rose and fell with less conviction each hour.

The cabin had become something else. Not a destination. A room.

Hurkuzak pulled. The poles wore grooves in his hands. The snow hissed beneath the travois. Behind him, the silence where Hakiia's voice had been was louder than any sound the winter had made.

He pulled because pulling was all that was left, and because the last thing Hakiia had given him was a hand on his arm that meant something neither of them could say, and because the snow was very deep and the cabin was very far and the world, patient and indifferent, had never once stopped to ask whether what it was doing was fair.
