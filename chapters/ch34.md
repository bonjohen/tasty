# Chapter 34: The Professional

She found his camp on the sixth morning.

Not the camp itself — the subject had broken his fire and scattered the ash and moved on before dawn, the way he always moved now, with the disciplined pre-sunrise departures that told her the transferred instincts were fully integrated into his daily function. But the camp's remains were legible to someone who had been reading this particular trail for weeks. The depression in the grass where he had slept. The slight warmth still held in the earth where the fire had been. The direction he had taken, east and slightly north, toward the trade road that ran along the river valley — the same river valley where the principal's people had established the collection point.

He was walking into it. He did not know he was walking into it. That was the architecture of the thing she had built.

Vark crouched beside the dead fire and read the camp with the thoroughness of a woman whose entire career had been built on reading what bodies left behind — the tracks, the traces, the biological evidence that every living creature deposited in its wake like a signature it could not suppress. The subject's track was strong. Heavier than it had been a month ago — the musculature was still increasing, the transfer still depositing physical mass in ways that exceeded normal growth. The stride had lengthened. The foot placement showed confidence in the terrain, the kind of confidence that experienced mountain hunters carried and that this man had not been born with and had acquired from the thing inside him. He was moving well. He was moving like the kind of specimen the market would pay a premium for, which was convenient, because the premium had already been quoted and the quote had been the highest figure Vark had ever been offered for anything, living or dead.

She packed her kit and followed. The following was not urgent. It had not been urgent for days. The urgency had been in the first weeks — locating the trail, confirming the hypothesis, establishing the tracking rhythm, closing the initial distance. Now the work was different. Now the work was geometry. She was not chasing. She was shaping. The subject moved east because east was the direction his purpose carried him, and his purpose — she did not know its specifics, only that something had changed in his pattern weeks ago, replacing the aimless avoidance with directed travel — aligned with the direction she needed him to go, which meant the most efficient approach was not interception but guidance. Let him walk where he wanted to walk. Ensure that where he wanted to walk led where she needed him to arrive.

The principal's intermediary had been a man named Saaren — mid-tier in the network, someone who knew enough to arrange logistics and not enough to be trusted with strategy. Vark had met him at a relay station on the eastern road three weeks ago, and the meeting had confirmed what the premium suggested: whatever she was delivering, the people above Saaren wanted it badly enough to mobilize resources that ordinary contracts did not justify. Saaren had provided sedation compounds — three doses, each calibrated for an augmented demon with enhanced metabolic resistance, each sealed in ceramic vials with the careful packaging of materials that cost more than most hunters earned in a year. He had provided restraint equipment: layered cord treated with a chemical that suppressed muscular contraction on contact, reducing the subject's enhanced strength to something manageable without damaging the tissues. The principal wanted the specimen intact. The specimen's commercial or experimental value depended on the integrity of the integration, and the integrity depended on the body arriving undamaged, functional, and conscious enough that the transferred presence remained active.

The principal wanted the voice alive. That was the detail that had told Vark more than anything else about what she was delivering and who she was delivering it to. An ordinary buyer wanted meat. A sophisticated buyer wanted specific tissues, properly preserved. The principal wanted a living consciousness — two of them, apparently, sharing one body — because the consciousness was the product. The flesh was merely the container.

She had spent three weeks shaping the ground. The work was methodical, patient, and largely invisible — the kind of work that required understanding how a hunted man moves through a landscape and then altering the landscape so that the movement funneled toward a single point without the man realizing the funnel existed. She had spoken to people ahead of his track — innkeepers, trappers, patrol riders on the trade roads. Small conversations, small coins, small adjustments. A road mentioned as dangerous because of bandits. A ford described as washed out. A camp known to be hostile to strangers. Each piece of information, delivered to the right person at the right time, created a tiny pressure on the subject's decision-making, and the pressures accumulated the way water accumulates behind a dam — invisible individually, decisive collectively.

She had also placed bodies. Not her own — she could not be at the collection point and on his trail simultaneously. The principal's network had provided three men, professionals in a secondary way, experienced enough to be useful and expendable enough to be positioned without concern for their comfort or survival. Vark had placed them along the convergence corridor — not as a cordon, which was too obvious for a subject with transferred tactical instincts, but as presence. A hunter's camp here. A patrol there. The smell of an occupied fire at a crossroads. Each presence closed an option the subject might otherwise have taken, and the closing was invisible because each presence appeared to be what it claimed to be: ordinary people doing ordinary things in ordinary places.

The subject's transferred instincts were good. Better than she had expected. He had avoided two of the pressure points she had created — routes she had seeded with discouraging information that he had rejected in favor of alternatives his inherited tactical sense had evaluated as superior. But the alternatives were also part of the funnel. She had built the architecture with redundancy, the way a good trap is built with the understanding that intelligent prey will find and avoid the obvious mechanisms and the real mechanism is the thing they choose instead.

She was not underestimating him. She had watched him fight — the bandit ambush, the public eruption. She had seen the speed, the efficiency, the terrifying competence that emerged when the transferred instincts overrode his own hesitation. He was dangerous in the way that augmented demons were dangerous: unpredictably, under pressure, with capabilities that exceeded his visible physiology.

His behavioral profile had changed again in recent weeks. The pattern shift she had noted before Merrath — the slowing, the deliberateness, the thinking-while-walking quality — had resolved into something different. He moved now with purpose rather than avoidance. His route selection had tightened. He no longer oscillated between road and tree line the way a frightened man oscillated between exposure and concealment. He chose routes with the decisiveness of someone traveling toward a destination rather than away from a threat. The change told her the integration was continuing, but it told her something else as well: something had happened to the subject, something that had reorganized his internal architecture and given him a direction. He was no longer merely running. He was going somewhere.

She did not need to know where. She only needed to know that where he thought he was going and where she needed him to arrive could be made to overlap, and the overlapping was the entire architecture of her plan.

But dangerous and uncatchable were different categories, and the difference was the difference between an individual's strength and a system's design, and the system's design had been built to contain exactly this level of individual strength.

The collection point was a trading compound in the river valley — a walled enclosure used by merchant caravans as a staging area, with stone buildings, a central courtyard, and a single gated entrance that could be sealed. Vark had selected it because it offered the combination she needed: a space the subject would approach as an ordinary waypoint on his eastward route, and a space that could be converted from waypoint to containment in the time it took to close a gate and deploy three sedation doses.

She thought about the taxonomy of what she was delivering as she walked. In fifteen years of hunting she had processed demons across the full spectrum of the market — baseline specimens worth their weight in common trade goods, premium specimens worth ten times that, rare physiological variants worth enough to fund a season of work from a single carcass. She had never delivered something like this. The subject was not merely rare. He was categorical — a being who existed in a classification the market had not yet created, because the market had not believed this classification was possible. Stable sibling integration, with full personality transfer, intact identity overlay, and a secondary consciousness that persisted and grew rather than degrading. Every prior case she had heard of through the trade's whisper network — and there had been cases, fragments of rumor, stories told by hunters who had encountered composites that were obviously broken, degraded, or insane — every prior case had been a failure. This was the first success. The first living proof that the transfer the upper tiers of the market had been pursuing for decades was not merely theoretical.

The value was not in his organs. It was not in his blood or his bone or his muscle. Those would be extraordinary — years of sibling consumption had produced physical properties that exceeded anything she had measured in her career. But the true value was in the mechanism. In the how. In the fact that his body contained the answer to the question the principal had been spending a fortune to answer: how does stable identity transfer work?

She would deliver the answer. She would name her price. The price would be high enough that the naming of it would change her life, and the changing would be permanent, and the permanence was the reason she had spent three weeks building this trap instead of taking the faster, riskier approach of direct engagement.

She reached the compound at midday and walked its perimeter and confirmed what she already knew from scouting it a week prior. The walls were sound — thick enough to absorb impact, high enough to prevent a climber without equipment, gated with a mechanism that could be operated by one person. The interior offered cover, sight lines, and the physical geometry she needed: a courtyard open enough that the subject would enter it without reading it as a trap, surrounded by walls and buildings that would become containment once the gate closed behind him.

She spoke with the three positioned men. Professional conversations, brief, specific. Their roles were defined. Their timing was established. The sedation compounds were distributed — one to each, with instructions on delivery method, dosage, and the absolute requirement that the subject not be killed or critically injured during the application. The principal's instructions had been clear on this point, delivered through Saaren with the particular emphasis of someone who is transmitting a message they do not fully understand but whose source they take very seriously: the subject is to arrive alive, conscious, and intact. The integration is the asset. Damage the body, damage the asset. Kill the body, destroy the asset entirely.

Vark understood. She had been delivering living specimens for years — not always, but often enough that the logistics were familiar. A living delivery was harder than a dead one. A dead body could be preserved, transported, processed at the buyer's convenience. A living body fought, fled, deteriorated, and required maintenance. But a living body with an active transferred consciousness was something she had never delivered before, and the novelty was a professional challenge she found herself appreciating the way she appreciated all professional challenges: as an opportunity to demonstrate the competence that justified her position in the hierarchy.

The afternoon passed. She ate. The meal was demon flesh — a strip from her kit, chewed thoroughly, the fuel entering her system with the efficiency of long practice. She checked her crossbow. She checked her restraints. She checked the compound's gate mechanism twice. She ran the scenario in her mind: the subject enters, drawn by the compound's apparent normalcy, looking for water or information or simply passing through. The gate closes. The men move. The sedation is applied — not all at once, but in sequence, allowing each dose to build on the previous, managing the subject's enhanced metabolic resistance through accumulation rather than a single overwhelming application. Restraints follow sedation. Transport follows restraint.

She would be on the wall when it happened. Above, watching, directing with signals the men understood. She would not engage directly unless the situation required it — the subject's combat capabilities made direct engagement a risk she did not need to take when the architecture did the work instead. She was not there to fight him. She was there to ensure that the system she had built performed as designed, and to make the adjustments that all systems require when theory meets application.

The sun lowered. The compound settled into the particular quiet of a place that is waiting for something and knows what it is waiting for and has arranged itself accordingly. Vark sat on the wall with her back to the stone and her crossbow across her knees and watched the eastern road where it emerged from the tree line half a mile out, and she waited with the patience of a woman who had built her career on patience, and the patience had always paid, and the paying was what made the patience possible, and the possibility was what made the patience professional rather than passive.

He would come tomorrow. Or the day after. The trail said tomorrow. His pace said tomorrow. The direction of his travel and the logic of the terrain said tomorrow. She had built the ground to receive him, and the ground was ready, and she was ready, and the readiness was the product of weeks of work and years of practice and the specific understanding that the most dangerous prey was prey that thought it was hunting — because prey that thinks it is hunting stops watching for the things that hunt it, and the stopping was the opening, and the opening was all she had ever needed.

She closed her eyes briefly and let the compound's quiet settle around her and felt the particular satisfaction of a professional who has done the difficult preparatory work and can now wait for the work to produce its result. The result would arrive on the road from the east, carrying inside it the most valuable thing the flesh economy had ever sought, and Vark would be ready, and the readiness would be the last professional act she needed to perform before the delivery changed her position in the hierarchy permanently.

She opened her eyes. She watched the road. The road was empty and patient and led east into the country where the subject was walking toward her without knowing it, and the walking was the last thing he would do freely for a long time, and the long time was not her concern, and the not-concerning was the thing that made her good at what she did, and the goodness was real, and the goodness was monstrous, and both were true, and neither troubled her sleep.
