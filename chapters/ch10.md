# Chapter 10: The Carrying

He did not know how long he sat. The fire died and the cold replaced it and the cold was absolute, the way facts are absolute — not an argument, not a threat, just the condition of the world with no fire in it. The snow fell on Hakiia's face and on his own hands and on the furs between them and the snow was the same snow it had always been and it did not know what had happened here and would not have changed its behavior if it did.

At some point his body began to shiver. The shivering was not a decision. It was the body announcing that it had a separate opinion about what happened next, and the body's opinion was that sitting still in this temperature with no fire would result in a second death, and the body was not interested in dying, and the body did not care that the mind inside it was somewhere else entirely.

Hurkuzak stood. The standing took a long time and required several intermediate stages — hands on the ground, knees braced, a push that failed and a second push that succeeded — and when he was upright the world tilted and steadied and tilted again and he held his arms out for balance and the balance came eventually, not through strength but through stubbornness, and he looked down at what was on the travois and the looking was the hardest thing he had done since the knife.

Hakiia was dead. The word was simple. The fact of it was not. His brother lay on the travois with his eyes closed and his face still and the blood on the furs frozen and the body was no longer the body of a man who was dying or a man who was in pain or a man who was commanding or arguing or demanding or saying please. The body was only a body. The person who had lived in it was gone, and what remained was form without function, mass without will, the physical residue of someone who had been the largest presence in Hurkuzak's life and was now the absence of that presence shaped like a man.

The first problem was the travois. The wolf fight and the long dragging had wrecked it — the lashings had loosened, one of the poles had cracked near the crosspiece, and the furs underneath were stiff with frozen blood and no longer slid cleanly. Hurkuzak knelt and retied what he could retie and reinforced the cracked pole with a strip of leather and repositioned the furs and tested the frame by lifting the drag poles and pulling. It moved. Barely. The resistance was different now and it took him several steps to understand why.

A living body on a travois cooperates in small ways the living person does not notice. Muscles adjust. Weight shifts. The body finds its center on the frame without being told to. A dead body does none of these things. A dead body is only mass, and mass obeys gravity and friction and nothing else, and the travois that had carried a wounded man with difficulty now carried a dead man with more difficulty, because the dead man did not help.

Hurkuzak pulled. The poles bit into the grooves they had already worn in his palms, and the pain was not new but the context was — yesterday these hands had pulled a living man, and now they pulled a dead one, and the poles did not know the difference and his palms did not know the difference and the only thing that knew was his mind, and his mind was the least useful part of him.

The valley continued. The snow was deep and the trees were sparse and the dawn was coming in the slow, reluctant way winter dawns arrive — not as light but as a lessening of dark, a gradual dilution of the black into gray and the gray into a flat, colorless illumination that showed the landscape without warming it. He pulled through this half-light and the travois scraped and caught and scraped again and his arms burned and his ribs burned and his bitten forearm sent a pulse of pain with every pull that was so regular it became a kind of clock, marking time in units of hurt.

He pulled for an hour. Maybe more. The travois caught on roots buried under the snow and on rocks hidden beneath the white surface and each time it caught he had to stop and pull it free and each time he pulled it free the jolt traveled through the frame and the body on the frame shifted and the shifting was the movement of something that was not choosing to move, that was only responding to force, and every shift reminded him that the cooperation was gone and what remained was physics.

He stopped and rested and checked the travois and adjusted the body because the body had shifted and one of Hakiia's arms had fallen off the side and was dragging in the snow. The arm had to be placed back because leaving it to drag felt wrong, felt like carelessness, felt like something Hakiia would have criticized. He lifted the arm — heavy, stiff, the cold had begun its work on the joints — and laid it across the chest and the hand rested there the way it had rested after the grip on his wrist had loosened, the fingers slightly curled, and the sight of those fingers was a thing he could not look at for very long.

He apologized.

The word came out of him without thought. "Sorry," he said, and the sound of his own voice in the silent landscape was startling and strange, and the silence that followed it was worse than the silence before, because the silence before had simply been empty and the silence now was the specific emptiness that follows a word spoken to someone who cannot hear.

He pulled again. The landscape became a single texture — white, cold, featureless, stretching in every direction without landmark or variation. The trees were bare poles. The sky was a ceiling. The ground was a problem that repeated itself with every step, and the repetition was the rhythm of the march and the rhythm of the march was the only thing between him and the full arrival of what he had done, and so he held onto the rhythm the way a drowning man holds onto a plank, not because it was taking him anywhere but because letting go meant sinking.

After another hour — or what felt like another hour, though time had lost its edges and the distinction between minutes and hours had dissolved into the general texture of endurance — the hallucinations began.

They were small. The first was a voice, or the memory of a voice, or the imagined continuation of a voice that had been part of his life so long that its absence was an active presence. It said: Lower your shoulder. You are pulling from the arms. Pull from the legs and the back.

He heard it. He almost turned around. Then the voice was gone and there was only the wind and the scraping of the travois and the sound of his own breathing, which was loud and labored and not unlike the wrong breathing Hakiia had made in the hours before the end.

The second hallucination was visual. He looked ahead and saw, through the trees, a shape that was the cabin — the straight lines, the right angles, the suggestion of a roof under snow. He walked toward it for ten minutes before the shape resolved into a rock face with a shelf of ice along its top edge that had caught the light in a way that mimicked walls and windows. The cabin was not there. The cabin was still ahead, in the same indeterminate distance it had always occupied, receding as he approached.

The third was a feeling. A warmth on his left side, the side closest to the travois, as though the body behind him were still radiating heat. It was not. The body was cold. The warmth was a fabrication of his exhausted mind, conjuring the sensation of his brother's proximity because his body was not yet willing to accept that the proximity had ended.

More came after that. Small wrongnesses in the landscape, in his perception, in the boundary between what was real and what his mind was building from exhaustion and grief. A tree that had not been there and then was. A sound like a voice carried on the wind that resolved, when he listened, into nothing. A moment where the ground seemed to tilt and the snow slid sideways and he braced himself against a fall that was not happening because the ground was level and his mind was not. He felt Hakiia's hand on his shoulder — the rough, corrective grip that had steered him a thousand times — and he turned and there was only the travois and the body and the cold, and the grip dissolved into the wind's pressure on his coat.

These were not visions. They were gaps. Places where his mind had worn thin from labor and loss and the unprocessed weight of what his hands had done, and through those thin places the past was leaking in, filling the present with fragments that had the shape and texture of Hakiia but none of his substance. They were memories replayed as sensation. Grief translated into perception. The mind's desperate insistence that the man who had filled every space in Hurkuzak's life could not simply be absent from it.

He stopped in the early afternoon, if it was the afternoon — the light had not changed, the gray was the same gray it had been since dawn, and the only reason he believed it was later was that his body was more exhausted than it had been, and exhaustion, like pain, was a form of time. He sat beside the travois and ate the last strip of dried meat and chewed it mechanically and swallowed it without tasting it.

He looked at Hakiia. The face had changed. Not in the way faces change through decay — the cold was preserving everything, holding the body in the state it had been in at death, frozen and still. But the relationship between Hurkuzak and the face had changed. In the hours since the mercy killing, Hurkuzak had looked at the face and seen his brother. Now, after hours of pulling, after the mechanical repetition of effort that had ground his mind down to its functional minimum, he looked at the face and saw weight. Not only weight. But weight first, and brother second, and the order of those two perceptions had reversed without his permission and the reversing felt like a betrayal and also like the truth.

He was carrying weight. He was carrying his brother. Both of these things were true, the way the mercy and the killing had been true, and the weight was winning because weight was the thing his body measured and his body was the only part of him still functioning.

He adjusted the body on the travois. He did not apologize this time. The adjustment was efficient, mechanical — a repositioning of mass for better balance, a straightening of the limbs so they did not catch on the frame. He noticed that his hands moved differently now. Earlier, he had touched Hakiia's body with the care of a man handling something sacred. Now his hands were solving a problem. The sacred thing was still there, underneath the problem, but the problem was what his hands were for and the hands had made their choice.

He pulled.

The afternoon wore into something that might have been evening. The light did not change — winter light did not change, it simply was and then it was not — but the temperature dropped in the small, measurable increments that meant the sun, wherever it hid behind the gray, was leaving.

He stopped again. He did not build a fire because building a fire required hands and focus and material and he had the first of these barely and the second not at all and the third would require searching and searching would require stopping the march and stopping the march was something he did not want to do because the march was the rhythm and the rhythm was the plank and the plank was all that was between him and the water.

He spoke to Hakiia.

Not to the body. To the space where Hakiia had been. He spoke aloud because the silence had become a thing with mass and he needed to push against it with something, and the only something he had was words.

"You were right," he said. "About the leg. About the cold. About the mathematics of it. You were right about all of it and I knew you were right and I refused because I could not bear to be the one who made it real."

The silence listened. The wind moved through the trees and the snow fell and the travois sat behind him with its burden and the burden did not respond.

"You were wrong about the rest," he said. "You were wrong about father and about me and about what softness means. You were wrong that the only honest love is hard love. You were wrong that seeing is the same as flinching." He paused. His voice was the only warm thing in the landscape and even it was cooling, thinning, becoming harder to push out of his chest. "But you were right about the leg. And that is what I will carry. Not that I did it. That you were right. That the cruel thing and the kind thing were the same thing, and I still did not want to do it, and I did it anyway, and it does not feel like either cruelty or kindness. It feels like what it was. A killing."

The silence absorbed the words the way the snow absorbed the blood — without response, without judgment, without any indication that the words had changed anything in the world they had been spoken into.

He picked up the drag poles. His hands closed around them and the wood was cold and the grooves in his palms had deepened and the blood in the grooves had dried and the dried blood held the wood the way the wood held his weight and the system of hand and pole and pain and forward motion was the only system left that worked.

He pulled. The valley went on. The trees went on. The snow went on. Behind him, the body of his brother slid through the world on a broken travois, changing categories with every mile — from person to weight to burden, from beloved to cargo, from the thing he could not leave to the thing he was not sure he could carry — and Hurkuzak pulled because pulling was the only verb his body had left, and because the cabin was ahead, and because stopping meant something he was not yet ready to become.
