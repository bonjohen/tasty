# Chapter 8: What Hakiia Asks

The march was failing and Hurkuzak knew it and the knowing changed nothing. His body moved because his body had been moving, the way a wheel turns after the hand that spun it is gone, and the travois dragged behind him through the snow with a sound that had become the only rhythm left in the world. Step and drag. Step and drag. The poles had worn grooves into his palms that went deeper than skin and the blood that came from them had frozen into the wood and the wood had become a part of his hands or his hands had become a part of the wood and the distinction no longer seemed important.

The valley had narrowed into something close to a gorge. The trees pressed in on both sides, dark and heavy with snow, and the sky above was a strip of gray that gave no warmth and no direction. The cabin was ahead. He believed this. He had to believe it because the alternative was that he was hauling his dying brother through an infinite corridor of snow toward a destination that did not exist, and that thought had a weight to it that he could not carry in addition to everything else.

Behind him, Hakiia had been silent for hours. Not the commanded silence of a man who chooses when to speak. A different silence. The silence of withdrawal, of a body and mind turning inward toward a conversation that did not include Hurkuzak or the snow or the cold or the future. When Hurkuzak had checked the travois at the last stop, Hakiia's eyes had been open but unfocused, watching the sky with the patience of someone who was waiting for something he had already identified and did not fear.

Then Hakiia spoke.

"Stop."

Hurkuzak stopped. He set down the drag poles and his shoulders screamed with the release of tension and his knees tried to fold and he locked them and stood. He turned to look at his brother.

Hakiia was looking at him. The eyes were clear. Not the drifting, dulled eyes of the last several hours but the sharp, certain eyes that Hurkuzak had known his entire life — the eyes that assessed, measured, judged, and found the world precisely as legible as Hakiia had always believed it to be. Whatever fog had settled over him during the march had burned off. He was fully present. He was fully himself.

"Sit down," Hakiia said. His voice was thin — the voice of a body that was running on reserves it did not have — but the authority in it was intact. "We need to talk."

Hurkuzak sat in the snow beside the travois. The cold came up through his clothing and into his body and he did not care because the cold had been there for days and it was no longer an event but a condition, like gravity or grief.

Hakiia looked at the sky for a moment. Then at his legs. Then at Hurkuzak. He looked at him the way he had looked at the elk's body before cutting it open — with the practical attention of a man who understood what was inside and was deciding where to make the first incision.

"My right leg is dead," he said. "The tissue is dying from the ankle upward. By tonight or tomorrow it will be the knee. After that, the infection will move into the blood, if it hasn't already. I can feel it. It is warm in ways that warm should not be."

"We can still — "

"We cannot. Listen to me." The sharpness was back. The precise, impatient sharpness of a man who had calculated the answer and was waiting for the slower mind across from him to stop offering alternatives that did not exist. "The left leg is broken in two places. Even if the right leg were whole, I would not walk for months. With both legs broken and one of them rotting, I will not walk again. I know this. You know this. We have been pretending otherwise because pretending was easier than what comes after we stop, and now we are going to stop."

The air between them felt different. Charged, the way air feels before a storm, except the storm was not in the sky. It was in the space between two brothers in the snow, one of whom was about to say something the other could not hear.

"The cabin may be a day away," Hakiia said. "It may be two. You are injured, exhausted, and moving at a pace that is slower every hour. If you reach it with me, you arrive with a dying man who needs constant care, no food worth counting, no medicine, no help coming, and winter still at the door. You know what happens then. You spend your strength keeping me alive for days, maybe a week, and then I die anyway, and then you are alone in a cabin in the mountains in winter with nothing, and you die too." He paused. "That is the math. That is the situation. And we are done pretending the math works out any other way."

"What are you asking?"

But he knew. He already knew, the way a body knows it is falling before the mind names the sensation. He knew it in the drop of his stomach and the tightening of his throat and the way his hands, ruined as they were, had curled into themselves as though trying to hold something that was already gone.

"Kill me," Hakiia said. "Here. Now. While I am still myself and the asking is mine."

The word came from Hurkuzak's body before his mind could shape it. "No."

"Listen — "

"No."

The refusal was not a word. It was a physical event. His body rejected the demand the way a body rejects poison — completely, violently, and without any consideration of whether the rejection was wise. He stood up from the snow and the standing was too fast and his bad knee buckled and he caught himself and he did not sit back down. He could not sit beside this demand. He needed to be higher than it, further from it, anywhere but next to the sound of his brother asking him to do the thing that would end everything.

"Sit down," Hakiia said.

"No."

"You are standing in the snow refusing to listen because you are afraid of what you will hear. Sit down. I am not going to stop saying this because you are standing."

Hurkuzak sat. Not because Hakiia told him to. Because his legs gave out.

Hakiia watched him settle into the snow and then he spoke again, and the speaking was deliberate and measured and it used the same voice he had used for every correction, every criticism, every hard lesson he had ever delivered — the voice that said I know the world and you do not and the knowledge I am giving you is the only thing between you and what the world will do to those who do not know it.

"I have thought about this," Hakiia said. "Not for minutes. For hours. Since the wolves, since the fire, since I felt the leg stop hurting in the way that means the nerves are dying. I have assessed my body the way I assess any situation and the assessment is final. I am dying. The question is not whether. The question is how long it takes and how much it costs. If I die slowly — in the cabin, over days, while you watch — the cost is both of us. If I die here, now, by your hand, you walk to the cabin alone and lighter and you survive the winter." He held Hurkuzak's gaze. "That is not cruelty. That is the clearest act of love I can offer you with what I have left."

"Don't call it love."

"What would you call it?"

"I don't know. I don't care. You are asking me to kill my brother and I am not going to do it."

Hakiia's mouth moved in the shape that was not a smile. "You killed the dog."

The words landed in a place that had no defense. Hurkuzak felt them arrive and felt the damage they did and could not speak for a moment because the comparison was precise and monstrous and it used the truth in the way Hakiia had always used the truth — as a blade.

"The dog was different."

"The dog was in pain. The dog could not survive. You saw the situation clearly and you acted. That was not weakness. That was the first useful thing you did after the cliff." Hakiia's voice softened, and the softening was worse than the blade because it carried inside it the thing Hurkuzak had felt at the fire — the almost-tenderness, the brief warmth that lived behind the correction. "Do the same for me. See the situation. Act."

"You are not a dog."

"No. I am your brother, who is asking you. The dog did not ask. I am asking. That should make it easier, not harder."

"It makes it impossible."

The silence after was long. The wind moved through the trees and the snow shifted on the branches above them and a small cascade of powder fell through the air and landed on Hakiia's chest and he did not brush it away. The cold did not care that they were arguing about something that would change everything. The cold had its own work and its own pace and their decision was irrelevant to it.

"You want me to die slowly," Hakiia said. "You want to drag me to the cabin and watch me rot because watching is easier than acting. Because as long as I am breathing, you can pretend that what is happening is not what is happening, and the pretending is more important to you than my pain."

"That is not what I want."

"Then what do you want? Tell me. I am lying here with both legs broken and one of them dying and I am asking you to help me and you are refusing because it makes you feel something you do not want to feel. That is not mercy. That is not love. That is a man choosing his own comfort over his brother's suffering."

The accusation hit somewhere Hurkuzak did not have armor. Because there was truth in it. Not all truth — it was not comfort he was protecting, it was something deeper and less nameable, something about who he would be on the other side of this act — but there was enough truth in Hakiia's words that they could not be dismissed, and Hakiia knew it, and Hakiia used that knowledge the way he used everything: precisely.

"Tell me the plan," Hakiia said. His voice had dropped. The anger was gone from it, or the anger had changed into something colder, something that was not anger at Hurkuzak but anger at the situation, at the legs, at the cliff that had broken them and the wolves that had worsened them and the cold that was finishing the work. "Tell me the version of this where you haul me to a cabin with no food and no help and both of us survive. I want to hear it. I want to hear the plan that makes refusing me something other than selfishness."

Hurkuzak opened his mouth and closed it. The plan did not exist. He knew it did not exist. The cabin was shelter, not rescue. There was no one coming. There was no help within reach. There was only winter and cold and two brothers, one of whom was dying and the other of whom was being asked to make the dying faster and cleaner and he could not, he could not, the word was in his body like a bone, load-bearing and immovable, he could not kill his brother.

"I don't have a plan," he said. "I don't have an answer. I have that I don't want to do this, and I know that isn't enough, and I know you're right about the leg and the cold and what happens next, and I still cannot do what you are asking me to do."

Hakiia looked at him. The look lasted a long time. In it was everything the brothers had ever been to each other — the corrections and the care, the contempt and the protection, the lifelong argument about what strength meant and who possessed it and whether the world rewarded hardness or punished softness or simply ground down everything regardless of how it was built.

"You can," Hakiia said. "You killed the wolf. You killed the dog. You have done hard things in the last three days that I did not believe you could do. Do not tell me you cannot do this. Tell me you will not. Those are different words and you know the difference."

"I will not."

"Yet."

The word hung in the cold air between them. Yet. Not a command. Not an accusation. A prediction, delivered with the quiet certainty of a man who understood that time and pain and the slow mathematics of a dying body would do the work that his arguments had not yet finished.

Hakiia closed his eyes. His breathing was the wrong breathing — the shallow, irregular rhythm that had become his body's new language, the language of a machine running on less than it needed. He did not withdraw the demand. He simply stopped pressing it, the way water stops pressing against a dam, not because the pressure has ended but because the water knows the dam will give way on its own.

The snow fell. The trees creaked. The cold continued.

Hurkuzak sat beside his brother and felt the refusal inside him and felt the erosion underneath it and knew, with the clarity of a man watching his own house burn from inside, that Hakiia was right. Not about everything. Not about the way he delivered it or the contempt he used or the comparison to the dog, which had been cruel even by his standards. But about the leg. About the cold. About the mathematics of what happened next if Hurkuzak dragged him to the cabin and watched.

He was right. And the rightness was the thing that was wearing through the refusal, not from the outside but from below, like water under ice, finding the cracks that stubbornness could not seal.

He did not say yes. He did not say anything. He picked up the drag poles and he pulled.

Behind him, Hakiia breathed the wrong breathing and said nothing, and the silence between them was no longer the silence of a man who had stopped commanding. It was the silence of a man who had said what needed to be said and was waiting, with the patience of someone who had already seen the answer, for his brother to arrive at it on his own.
