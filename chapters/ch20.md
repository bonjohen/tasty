# Chapter 20: Rarity

The scene told its own story. Vark read it the way she read all scenes — from the physical evidence outward, letting the material speak before imposing narrative.

Two men on the road, both injured, both mobile but diminished. One with a wrist injury consistent with a joint lock applied with sufficient force to strain the ligaments without completing the break — controlled, which meant the person who applied it had the strength to break and chose not to. One with cracked ribs, curled on the roadside, breathing in the careful pattern of a man who had discovered his thorax had new limits. A club on the ground, dropped. A knife with blood on it — the subject's blood, not the assailants', which meant the subject had caught the blade, which meant either desperation or a pain tolerance so far beyond normal that metal through the palm registered as an acceptable trade for control of the weapon.

She interviewed the traders. The man was eager to talk. The woman was cautious. Both described the same event with the same expression of bewildered alarm: a lone traveler, confronted by three road bandits, had disarmed and disabled two of them in a span of time neither witness could accurately quantify because the speed had exceeded their ability to track. Fast, the man said. Faster than anyone he had seen. Not faster than anyone the woman had seen — she had been in the eastern cities and had seen demonstration bouts between augmented fighters — but faster than she expected from a man who looked the way this one looked: thin-faced, poorly dressed, carrying a blanket bundle on his back like a refugee.

"His hands," the woman said. "When he moved, his hands knew what they were doing before the rest of him did. Like the hands belonged to someone else."

Vark noted this. The observation was precise and she stored it alongside the track data and the campsite analysis and the rabbit bone technique. The picture was assembling itself. Each new piece of evidence narrowed the range of possibilities and widened the value.

She examined the blood on the knife. Fresh, several hours old, already oxidizing in the spring air. She touched it. Brought her finger to her nose. The scent was wrong — not the standard iron-and-salt smell of human blood, and not the slightly denser, slightly sharper smell of demon blood as she normally encountered it. This was both. Or neither. The scent occupied a space between the two categories that she had not encountered before, and the between was itself information, because between meant mixed, and mixed meant the body that produced this blood was operating on a biology that had been modified at a level deeper than the surface enhancements she was accustomed to seeing.

She spoke to the two injured men. The one with the wrist was cooperative in the way that injured people who want sympathy are cooperative — eager to explain that they had been wronged by a creature that was not natural, that moved in ways that no ordinary man moved, and that the three of them had been simply defending the road and the creature had attacked without provocation. Vark let the narrative play itself out. She did not believe it and did not need to. The facts were in the ground, not in the telling.

The third man — the one who had run — she found at a campsite a mile west, nursing his courage with alcohol stolen from a cache he had hidden in the rocks. He was more honest, or more afraid, and his account was more useful. He had seen the traveler's face. Young, but not young in the way youth normally presented — the face was marked, worn, carrying an experience weight that did not match the age of the skin. The eyes had been wrong. Not the color or the shape but the quality — the eyes had assessed the three of them the way a wolf assesses prey, with a flat efficiency that did not contain fear or anger but only calculation, and the calculation had been completed before any of them realized they were being calculated.

Vark noted this too. The assessment was consistent. Everyone who had seen the subject described a disjunction between appearance and capability, between the external and the performance, between what the body looked like and what the body did.

She followed the trail east from the scene of the fight. The subject had taken the road for a quarter mile, moving fast — the stride length was extended, the pace aggressive, the gait of someone leaving a location rather than traveling toward one. Then the trail left the road and entered the tree line and the change was immediate: the pace slowed, the movement became deliberate and tactical, the path chose cover and concealment over speed. The transition from road to forest was the transition from panic to competence, and the competence was professional-grade.

She tracked him for two days.

The tracking was the most informative thing she had done since beginning the pursuit. The subject moved through the forest with the specific skill set she had identified at the first campsite — economical, terrain-aware, noise-disciplined — but now, under pressure, the skill set was operating at full capacity rather than the relaxed travel mode of the earlier track. He changed direction twice to test for pursuit, doubling back on his own trail in a pattern that would have confused an ordinary tracker and that cost Vark an hour the first time and twenty minutes the second. He crossed water three times — not at fords but at points where the current was strongest and the bottom was rocky, choosing difficulty over detection. He camped without fire, ate without cooking, and slept — if he slept at all — in positions that left no compression marks she could find.

He was good. Better than standard quarry. Better than most professional fugitives she had tracked. And the goodness had the same quality she had noticed in the initial track assessment: learned rather than inherent. The movements were correct but the corrections were recent, as though the body had acquired the knowledge through a mechanism other than years of practice. The hands knew what they were doing before the rest of him did. The trader's wife had seen it clearly. The capabilities were installed. They were running on hardware that had not originally possessed them.

On the second afternoon, she closed the distance.

She had been maintaining a gap of four hours — close enough to track reliably, far enough to avoid detection by senses she now knew were augmented. But the subject had made a mistake. He had stopped to drink at a stream and the stopping had taken longer than the tactical discipline should have allowed, which meant either injury, exhaustion, or a lapse in the attention that had been maintaining his operational security. She used the lapse. She moved to a position above and behind him, on a ridge that offered a clear line of sight to the stream below, and she assessed.

He was crouching at the water's edge. She could see his profile — the thin face, the strong frame, the posture that was wrong for the body type, the stillness that belonged to a man who was listening to the forest the way she listened to the forest. His left hand was in the water. His right hand — the one that had caught the knife — was wrapped in cloth and he was unwrapping it and she saw the cut and she saw what the cut was doing.

It was healing.

The edges of the wound were visibly closer together than a two-day-old palm laceration should have allowed. The tissue was pink and healthy where it should have been inflamed and seeping. The rate of closure suggested a healing factor that exceeded any baseline demon capability she had observed in the field, and she had observed many, and the observation had taught her the normal range, and this was outside it.

She loaded the crossbow.

The bolt was standard — a broadhead designed for penetration testing. Not lethal at this range unless she chose a lethal placement, and she did not choose a lethal placement because killing this subject without understanding him would be the professional equivalent of burning currency. She chose the outer thigh. Muscle, not bone. Painful, impairing, and diagnostic: the thigh wound would give her data on blood response, healing rate, and pain tolerance that no amount of tracking could provide.

She sighted. She exhaled. She released.

The bolt struck exactly where she intended. The subject reacted — and the reaction was data. He dropped from the crouch to a roll in a motion that was faster than the pain should have allowed, which meant either the pain threshold was extreme or the reflexes were operating ahead of the pain signals. He came up facing her direction — not her position, but the correct general direction, which meant he had calculated the bolt's trajectory from the angle of entry, which meant spatial processing at a level that required either training or inherited capability.

He ran. The bolt was in his thigh and he ran with it, and the running was fast, and the gait adjustment he made for the wound was minimal, and the minimality was another data point: the body was managing the injury as a variable in its performance rather than as a crisis, the way a well-tuned mechanism adjusts for load rather than failing under it.

She did not pursue immediately. She descended to the stream and examined the place where he had been crouching. Blood. She touched it, smelled it. The same wrong-right scent from the knife. She collected a small amount in a vial from her kit — standard practice for high-value specimens — and sealed it.

She examined the tracks of his departure. The stride was shorter than normal — the wound was affecting his gait — but the pace was high and the route selection was still tactical. He was heading for dense cover, for terrain that would make crossbow follow-up shots difficult, and the choosing of that terrain under duress was another expression of the borrowed expertise that defined his movement profile.

She estimated his healing rate from the blood volume at the stream and the pattern of drops on his trail. He was bleeding, but he was bleeding less than the wound should have produced. The hemorrhage was controlled — not by bandaging, because she could see from the tracks that he had not stopped to apply pressure, but by the body itself, which was closing the wound at a rate she could calculate from the diminishing drop frequency.

She ran the numbers. At the current rate, the bolt wound would be functionally closed within a day. A standard demon would require three to five days for the same injury. An unaugmented human would require weeks. This subject's healing factor was a multiple of the best she had seen in fifteen years of fieldwork.

She watched him disappear into the trees. Through her augmented vision she tracked the diminishing figure — the limp that was already evening out, the gait that was already adjusting, the body already performing the damage management that she had predicted. She noted the time. She would check the stream site again in an hour, count the blood drops, measure the trail, and have a preliminary healing curve by nightfall.

This was how she worked. Data, patience, and the discipline to let a valuable thing run when the running generated more information than the catching.

She let him go.

The decision was professional, not compassionate. A live specimen of this quality was worth more than a dead one by a factor she could not yet calculate because she did not yet know the ceiling of his capabilities. Killing him now would yield organs and tissue that would command impressive prices — the blood alone, with its mixed scent profile, would interest the kind of buyers who paid for anomaly. But alive, studied, and eventually delivered to the right buyer, he was worth more than her last ten kills combined.

She would need to be more careful. He knew he was being hunted now. The bolt had told him that. His response — the immediate tactical flight, the terrain selection, the controlled management of the wound — told her that he was capable of evading pursuit at a level that would require her full professional attention. This was not a borderland demon wandering the passes. This was something new, something that combined augmented physicality with learned fieldcraft and a healing factor that redrew the boundaries of what she understood about demon biology.

She sat beside the stream and thought about markets. The flesh economy operated on established tiers: common demon parts at the base, augmented specimens in the middle, and at the top, reserved for collectors and theorists and the kind of wealth that did not negotiate because the object of its desire had no comparable, the category of unprecedented. Unprecedented was where this subject lived. If sibling consumption produced what the profile suggested — a body carrying two demons' properties in integrated, active form — then the subject was not merely valuable. He was a proof of concept. He was evidence that a mechanism the trade had theorized about for generations actually worked, and the working was stable, and the stability was expressed in a living, functional, recoverable body.

The buyers who wanted that proof would pay whatever it cost to obtain it.

She cleaned her crossbow. She packed the blood vial. She noted the direction of his flight and the rate of his movement and the diminishing blood trail that confirmed her healing estimate. She would follow at distance. She would test again when the opportunity presented itself. She would build a complete profile — speed, strength, healing, senses, pain tolerance, tactical capability — and when the profile was complete she would contact Koss and through Koss the buyer, and the price she named would be a price that reflected rarity.

Rarity was the most valuable thing in the trade. Common demons had common prices. Augmented demons had premium prices. But a sibling-consumed demon — if that was what this was, and the evidence was converging toward that conclusion with a weight she could no longer dismiss — had no price, because the market had never established one, and the establishing of a price for something unprecedented was the kind of opportunity that came once in a professional lifetime.

Vark started walking. The forest was quiet. The subject was ahead, wounded and running, and she was behind, patient and certain, and the distance between them was a number she would manage with the same precision she applied to everything: carefully, professionally, and with the understanding that the number would eventually reach zero, and when it did, she would be ready.
