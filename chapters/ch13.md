# Chapter 13: The Surrender

More days passed. Or he believed they did. The marks on the wall above the platform continued — one for each waking — but the wakings themselves had lost their edges. He slept in fragments, never deeply, never for long, and the fragments ran together so that he could not always tell whether the mark he was scratching was for today's waking or yesterday's forgotten one or the one before that. The marks were no longer a count. They were a habit. They were the act of a hand that needed something to do with a knife besides the thing the hand had been thinking about.

He was weaker. The weakness was not the sharp exhaustion of the trail, which had been urgent and temporary and attached to physical labor. This was a different substance. It lived in his muscles, in the slow reluctance of his limbs to respond to instructions, in the way standing up from the platform now required a sequence of movements that a month ago would have been a single act. He was aware of his body consuming itself — the fat first, what little there had been, and now the muscle, the reserves that a body hoards beneath the skin and surrenders only when surrender is the only remaining source.

The fire continued because he continued to feed it, and the feeding of the fire was the thread that connected each day to the next, and the thread was thin but it held. He melted snow. He drank. He lay on the platform and stared at the ceiling and the ceiling stared back and neither of them had anything to say.

The infection in his forearm had not improved. The skin around the bite was tight and shiny and hot, and the heat was a wrong heat, the heat of something inside the flesh that was growing while the rest of him was shrinking. He lanced it with the knife — a small, brutal act of self-surgery that produced a quantity of fluid that smelled of corruption and left him shaking on the platform with sweat on his face despite the cold. Afterward the swelling subsided slightly and the pain changed from a deep throb to a sharper, more focused ache, and he did not know whether this meant improvement or simply a different stage of the same deterioration.

He stopped talking to Hakiia. Not because the impulse had faded. Because the talking required energy, and energy was the thing he had the least of, and the silence that replaced the talking was a silence he occupied the way a body occupies a bed — not by choice, but by the simple absence of the strength to be anywhere else.

The rationalizations came.

They came slowly, the way weather comes — not as a single storm but as a change in atmospheric pressure that altered the quality of everything without announcing itself. He did not sit down and construct arguments. The arguments assembled themselves in the background of his mind while the foreground managed the fire and the water and the marks on the wall.

The body is meat. All bodies are meat. The distinction between the body of an elk and the body of a man is a distinction of category, not of substance. The elk he had eaten on the trail was muscle and bone and organ and blood. What was in the alcove was muscle and bone and organ and blood. The materials were the same. Only the meaning was different.

He touched that thought and pulled back. He touched it again. He circled it the way a tongue circles a broken tooth — compulsively, painfully, unable to leave it alone and unable to press hard enough to reach the nerve.

Animals eat their dead. This was a fact he had observed many times in the wilderness, without horror, without judgment. Wolves, ravens, insects, fish — the dead fed the living and the feeding was not moral and not immoral but merely the mechanism by which energy moved from one state to another. The dead did not need their bodies. The living did.

Survival is not a moral category. The world did not care what he ate. The world had never cared. The world had dropped him and his brother off a cliff and sent wolves and cold and starvation because the world operated on the same principle as the wolves — weakness drew predation, and the dead fed the living, and the feelings of the living about the feeding were irrelevant to the mechanism.

He knew what he was doing. The rationalizations were not beliefs. They were scaffolding. They were the structures his mind was building around the thought it could not yet think directly, the thought that lived behind the alcove door and grew louder with every day that the fire consumed wood and his body consumed itself and the only food in the world was in the next room and had a name and a face and had once pressed against his wrist at the moment of death.

He opened the alcove door.

He had not opened it since putting the body inside. The cold air that came through the doorway was sharper than the cabin cold, the preserved cold of a space that had not been warmed, and in the dim light from the hearth behind him he could see the shape on the floor — the furs, the body beneath them, the outline of a man who was not a man anymore but was also not yet something else.

He looked at Hakiia. He had been avoiding this. He had been avoiding the eyes especially, because the eyes were Hakiia's and Hakiia's eyes had always seen him clearly, too clearly, with the penetrating assessment of a brother who believed he understood what Hurkuzak was and what Hurkuzak needed and what Hurkuzak would become if left to his own insufficient devices. The furs were over the face. He did not move them. He looked at the body through the covering and the covering was a kindness he had given the dead and also a kindness he had given himself, because what he was about to consider would be harder — perhaps impossible — with Hakiia's face watching.

He stood in the doorway for a long time. The cold from the alcove met the warmth from the hearth and the two currents mixed around his body and he stood in the boundary between them and felt the decision forming inside him the way a crack forms in ice — not all at once, not visibly, but in the slow accumulation of pressure against a surface that was already weakened by days of strain.

He went back to the main room. He sat on the platform. He did not eat anything because there was nothing to eat. He thought about the shape in the alcove and the shape in the alcove thought about nothing because the dead do not think, but the living project thoughts onto the dead and the projection was strong enough to feel like a gaze and the gaze felt like judgment and the judgment felt like Hakiia, which was the worst part because Hakiia's judgment had always been the thing that measured him and found him insufficient and the insufficiency he was being measured against now was the insufficiency of a man who would rather die than do what needed to be done.

He did not eat that day. He closed the alcove door and returned to the platform and lay down and the lying down felt like a choice and the choice felt like virtue and the virtue was the last luxury available to a man who had nothing left to eat except the one thing he would not eat.

He did not eat the next day. He searched the cabin again — every corner, every crack in the stone, every gap between the floor slabs where something edible might have fallen or been cached or grown. He found nothing. He went outside, into the chest-deep snow, and dug toward the tree line and looked for anything — bark, roots, frozen berries, the corpse of a small animal that the snow had buried — and found only snow and more snow and the cold that had been waiting for him to come outside and was perfectly happy to start the work of killing him in the open air.

He went back inside. He closed the door. He sat.

On the third day without food, he woke and could not stand immediately. The waking was a long process — consciousness arrived in stages, each one delivering a new inventory of failure. The muscles first, reporting that they had consumed what they could consume and were now operating on the structural material that held the body together. Then the head, which ached with the specific ache of a brain deprived of fuel. Then the stomach, which had stopped sending hunger signals and had moved to a deeper register, a whole-body demand that felt less like appetite and more like the body's formal notification that it was beginning to die.

He stood. He stood because standing was the thing between him and the alternative, and the alternative was lying on the platform until the body completed its own consumption and stopped.

He opened the alcove door. He knelt beside his brother's body. He pulled back the furs from the left shoulder and the arm that had not moved since the wolf fight — the nerve-dead arm, the one that had been dead weight before Hakiia himself was dead. He chose the shoulder because the shoulder was already gone. The tissue there had been dying since the cliff, the nerves severed, the flesh cooled and discolored days before the man it belonged to had stopped breathing. Cutting from the part that was already lost felt — not easier. Less impossible. A fraction of a degree less impossible, which was the only margin he had left. The flesh underneath was cold and firm, preserved by the temperature, and the skin had the waxy quality that cold gives to dead things — not quite frozen, not quite flesh, something between the two states that did not have a name because the naming of it was not something people normally needed.

He knelt there for a long time. The cold from the stone floor moved through his knees and into his bones and the fire in the other room was dying because he had not fed it and the dying of the fire was the dying of the last reason not to be here, in this room, on his knees, with the knife in his hand.

He did not decide. That was the truth he would carry afterward, and it was a lie, and the lie and the truth existed in the same space the way the mercy and the killing had existed in the same space, and neither one canceled the other. He did not decide because the decision had been made by the body that was starving and the winter that would not end and the cliff that had broken his brother's legs and the mercy killing that had left him alone with what remained. The decision was the sum of everything that had happened since the trail gave way, and the sum had already been calculated, and what he was doing now was not choosing but arriving — arriving at the place the sum had always pointed toward.

He cut.

The blade went into the flesh of his brother's shoulder and the flesh gave way with the sound of something consenting and the sound was quiet and the act was quiet and the world outside the cabin was quiet and the only thing that was not quiet was the thing inside Hurkuzak that was screaming, and the screaming was not a sound but a sensation, a vibration that ran through his entire body and settled into his hands and his hands continued the work because the hands had been given a task and the task was survival and survival was the cruelest verb he knew.

He cut a small piece. He took it to the hearth. He cooked it in the iron pan over the coals because eating it raw was a line he could not cross today, though he was aware that the line between raw and cooked was not the line that mattered and the line that mattered was already behind him.

The smell of the cooking was the worst part. It was a smell he knew — the smell of meat over heat, the smell of food, the smell of something the body recognized and wanted — and the wanting was the betrayal, because the body did not care what the meat was or who the meat had been or what the eating of it meant. The body smelled food and the body wanted food and the body was right to want it and the rightness was the thing he could not forgive.

He ate.

The meat was in his mouth and the taste was the taste of meat and the texture was the texture of meat and his body received it with the blind, grateful efficiency of a system that had been waiting for exactly this and did not care about the cost. He chewed and swallowed and the swallowing was the crossing, the real crossing, the line that could not be uncrossed, and he was past it before he fully understood that he was past it.

He ate the small piece and then he put the pan down and he sat beside the fire and the fire was dying and he was alive and the two facts existed together in the cabin and neither of them felt like the right fact for the room they were in.

He was sick. Not immediately — the body held onto what it had been given for as long as it could — but within the hour his stomach revolted and he was on his knees on the stone floor retching and the retching produced nothing because there was nothing to produce except what he had just swallowed, and the body fought to keep it, and the fight between the body's need and the mind's horror was a war that happened inside him without his consent, and the body won, because the body always won.

He sat against the wall afterward, shaking. Not from cold. From the aftermath of the act and the body's response and the mind's belated, useless outrage. He sat there for a long time — long enough that the fire burned lower and the shadows lengthened and the cold crept back in from the edges of the room where the warmth no longer reached.

He did not look at the alcove door, which was still open. He did not look at the pan, which was still on the hearth. He looked at his hands, which were the hands that had held the knife and cut the flesh and cooked it and fed it to him, and the hands did not look different, which was the most terrible thing about them. They were the same hands that had wrapped the dog's cracked pad and held the drag poles and closed Hakiia's eyes and they were now also the hands that had done this, and the hands contained all of these acts without showing any of them.

He slept. He did not mean to sleep, but the body had received fuel for the first time in days and the fuel unlocked a kind of exhaustion that had been waiting behind the starvation, held at bay by the body's refusal to waste energy on sleep when there was no energy to waste. Now there was energy. The body took what it needed and shut him down.

When he woke, he was less weak. The difference was small but measurable — he could stand without the intermediate stages, his hands were steadier, his vision was clearer. The body had processed what he had given it and the processing had produced results and the results were undeniable. The meat had worked. His brother's flesh had fed him and the feeding had made him stronger and the strength was real and the horror was also real and the two of them occupied the same body because they had no choice.

He wished Hakiia would stop looking at him. The furs were over the face. The alcove door was open and the dim light from the hearth fell across the floor of the alcove and the shape under the furs was still and the face was covered and the eyes were covered and still Hurkuzak felt them. Felt the gaze that had measured him his entire life now measuring this, measuring what he had done with the body it used to inhabit, and finding him — what? Insufficient? Monstrous? Practical? He did not know what Hakiia's judgment would have been and that was the worst uncertainty of all, because Hakiia had always judged and the judgment had always been clear and now the judge was the thing being judged and the categories had collapsed into each other and there was no one left to tell him whether what he had done was the hard thing Hakiia had always wanted him to do or the unforgivable thing that proved he had been right to see his brother as something less than whole.

He closed the alcove door. He went back to the fire. He sat.

The cabin was very quiet. Something had changed in the room that had nothing to do with what he had consumed and everything to do with who he now understood himself to be. He was a man who had killed his brother and eaten his brother and was sitting beside a fire that burned on wood while the rest of him burned on something else entirely, and the burning would not stop because the fuel it ran on was not a thing that could be exhausted.

He was alive. That was the fact. The cost of the fact was everything he had believed about himself, and the cost had been paid, and the payment was not refundable.

He sat with it.

He would eat again tomorrow.
