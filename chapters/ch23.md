# Chapter 23: The Familiar Tongue

He was three days east of the market town and still running from what his hands had done.

Not running physically — his pace was measured, deliberate, the tree-line travel that the voice had been demanding since the bandit fight and that he now maintained without argument because the argument had been settled by the market eruption and the settling had left no room for the position he had previously held, which was that hiding on public roads was adequate and that his body could be trusted to behave like a normal body in normal circumstances. His body could not be trusted. That was established now. The body contained a violence that activated under pressure with the efficiency of a mechanism, and the mechanism did not consult him before engaging, and the engagement was Hakiia's kind of engagement — total, precise, and accomplished with a contempt for hesitation that belonged to a man who had never once in his life been uncertain about the correct application of force.

The market was behind him but the market was not behind him. It lived in his memory the way the cabin lived — as a sealed room of experience that he carried whether he wanted to or not. The faces of the crowd. The sound the enforcer's jaw had made. The way his own hands had moved with a fluency that was not learned but inherited, and the inheritance had been public, and the public had seen what it meant.

He traveled through spring forest and the forest asked nothing of him and the nothing was a relief and the relief was temporary because the relief depended on solitude and solitude was a condition he was no longer sure he occupied. The cabin had been solitary. The road had been crowded. And inside him, regardless of what was outside, there was a presence that was neither solitary nor crowded but was the specific company of one, and the one had opinions and the opinions were getting harder to separate from his own.

The voice was different now. The corrections had not stopped — they continued in the same register, terse assessments of terrain, route, and posture — but something underneath them had changed. The texture was more specific. The impatience had acquired a familiar cadence. It no longer sounded like the generalized hostility of a survival instinct sharpened by crisis. It sounded like the specific hostility of a person Hurkuzak knew, directed at specific failures he had been corrected for his entire life.

You are walking like something that wants to be caught. Feet are too heavy. Weight is wrong. Commit to the step or do not take it.

The criticism arrived and Hurkuzak heard it and felt the old architecture of obedience activate — the instinct to adjust, to comply, to make the correction before the next criticism landed. He adjusted. His feet lightened. His weight shifted forward. The adjustment was immediate, automatic, and it was not his adjustment. It was the response of a body that had been trained since childhood to modify its behavior at the sound of that particular quality of dissatisfaction.

He caught himself mid-step. The phrase that formed in his mind — lazy ground, a contemptuous term for terrain that invited careless walking — was not a phrase he used. It was not a phrase anyone used. It was a phrase Hakiia had invented, or inherited from their father's trade language, and it had lived exclusively in Hakiia's vocabulary for Hurkuzak's entire life. He had heard it dozens of times, always delivered with the flat certainty that what the phrase named was obvious and that Hurkuzak's failure to see it was evidence of the fundamental insufficiency Hakiia had spent his life trying to correct.

The phrase had arrived in his mind already shaped. He had not retrieved it from memory. He had not recalled it through association. It had appeared the way the corrections appeared — as a finished product, delivered by a mechanism he could not see, carrying the specific weight of Hakiia's diction and Hakiia's contempt and Hakiia's particular assumption that the world was legible if you were not too soft to read it.

He stopped walking. He stood in the forest and listened to the silence that followed the phrase and the silence was not empty. It was the silence of a room with someone else in it. A specific someone. A someone whose patience was not infinite and whose opinion of Hurkuzak had always been a compound of love and disappointment and the unshakable conviction that the raw material was adequate but the training had been insufficient.

He did not name the someone. Not yet. The naming was a door he could see and could not open because opening it meant accepting a truth that restructured everything — not just the voice and the corrections but the body and the strength and the reflexes and the violence in the market and the whole of what the winter had made him. If the voice was Hakiia, then Hakiia was inside him. If Hakiia was inside him, then the consumption had transferred not merely strength but self — instinct, memory, identity. If identity had been transferred, then the thing living in Hurkuzak's body was not a side effect of nutrition. It was his brother.

He started walking again. The terrain required attention and the attention was a relief because attention directed outward was attention not directed inward, and inward was where the named and unnamed things lived together without resolution.

The forest was spring forest now — wet underfoot, the canopy showing green at the margins where buds were opening, the air carrying the smell of turned earth and snowmelt and the particular vitality that spring brought to everything it touched. He moved through it with the borrowed fluency that had become his default: reading terrain, choosing footfall, navigating by the automatic calculations that his mind performed without being asked. The calculations were good. They were always good. They saved him time, saved him effort, kept him on paths that minimized exposure and maximized options. And they were not his calculations. They were the calculations of someone who had walked mountains for a lifetime and whose expertise had been compressed into instinct and transferred into a body that had never earned it.

He reached a river crossing by midafternoon. The river was swollen with melt — fast, brown, carrying debris — and the crossing options were limited. A ford downstream, visible from the road, exposed. A narrowing upstream where the current was faster but the distance was shorter and the banks were screened by trees. A fallen tree further up that spanned the gap but sat high and was slick with spray.

The voice had an opinion. The log. Cross high, stay dry, keep moving. The ford is visible from three directions and anyone on the road sees you standing in the water.

Hurkuzak looked at the log. It was high and the surface was wet and the water beneath it was fast enough that a fall would mean being carried downstream, battered against rocks, into the open water where the road ran close.

The ford is safer for the crossing and more dangerous for the exposure. The log is dangerous for the crossing and safer for everything after. Choose.

He chose the log. He crossed it with a balance that his body provided and his mind watched with detached disbelief — feet placing themselves on the slick bark with the precise weight distribution of someone who had crossed a thousand such logs and knew exactly how the bark gripped and where the rot spots hid and how the spray pattern affected traction at the midpoint where the water hit. He reached the far side without slipping and stood on solid ground and felt the authority of the choice settle into his body the way every correction settled: as proof that following the voice was smarter than following himself.

That was the pattern Hakiia had always used. Not persuasion. Demonstration. You followed Hakiia's direction and the direction proved right and the proving accumulated until the following was automatic and the automaticity was obedience and the obedience was so deep you could not tell it apart from trust.

Hurkuzak had followed. He had followed the way he had always followed. And the following felt like homecoming, which was the most disturbing thing of all.

That evening he found a sheltered camp between two rock faces and built a fire the way the corrections had taught him — draft at the base, angled wood, the configuration that maximized heat retention. He ate from his dwindling supplies and the eating was mechanical and the food was still the wrong food, flat and textureless, his body accepting it without enthusiasm the way a machine accepts inferior fuel.

A memory arrived. Not his.

It was brief — a flash of sensory information that belonged to a different body and a different time. A ridge seen from above, the perspective of someone taller, the snow bright and the wind strong and the dogs visible far below, laboring through the draw while the watcher — not Hurkuzak, the perspective was wrong for Hurkuzak, the height was wrong, the sense of physical ease in the cold was wrong — assessed the terrain ahead and chose the route that offered the best combination of speed and visibility.

The ridge. The route Hakiia had chosen the day Hurkuzak suggested the trees. Seen from Hakiia's perspective. Seen through eyes that were assessing sightlines and wolf approaches while Hurkuzak below was worrying about the dog's pad.

The memory dissolved as quickly as it had arrived, leaving behind an impression that was more sensation than image — the specific quality of cold mountain wind on a face that was not his face, the weight of a body that was larger than his body, and a feeling that was not quite an emotion but was the residual atmosphere of the memory: certainty. The watcher on the ridge had been certain. Certain about the route, the sightlines, the risk calculation. The certainty was Hakiia's certainty, the bedrock quality that had defined his brother more than any other trait — the absolute confidence that his reading of the world was correct and that anyone who read it differently was simply wrong.

Hurkuzak had never experienced that kind of certainty from the inside. He had experienced it from the outside his entire life — as pressure, as judgment, as the relentless standard against which his own uncertainty was measured and found wanting. Now he had felt it from within, as though the memory had carried not just an image but the emotional state of the person who had lived it, and the emotional state was certainty, and the certainty felt like Hakiia, and the feeling was so specific that no amount of rational reconstruction could have produced it.

Hurkuzak sat beside the fire and felt the space the memory had occupied in his mind and the space was warm, the way a recently vacated seat is warm, and the warmth was the residue of a presence that had been there and was still there and was not leaving.

He told himself it was stress. He told himself exhaustion fabricated memories and that the specificity of the vision — the angle, the height, the assessment — was his own mind reconstructing what Hakiia might have seen based on what Hurkuzak already knew about the terrain. The reconstruction was plausible. Minds did this. Minds filled gaps with invention and called the invention memory and the calling was a normal function of consciousness under duress.

He told himself this. The telling held. But the holding required more effort than it had the day before, and the effort was a measure of how much the truth was pressing against the story he was choosing to believe, and the pressing was not going to stop.

He sat with the memory's afterimage and felt the night deepen around him and the fire settle into its work and the forest make its sounds — the creak of branches, the distant movement of water, the small lives of small creatures going about the business of being alive without the complication of carrying anyone else inside them.

He thought about the phrase. Lazy ground. A contemptuous two-word judgment that Hakiia had used a hundred times and that had arrived in Hurkuzak's mind without summoning, without context, without the associative chain that ordinary memory requires. It had simply been there, the way Hakiia's corrections had always simply been there — waiting for the moment when they would be most useful and most devastating simultaneously.

He thought about the log crossing. The voice had directed and he had obeyed and the obedience had felt natural and the natural was the trap, because obedience to Hakiia had always felt natural, had always been the path of least resistance, and the path of least resistance was the path Hakiia had designed by making every other path feel wrong.

The fire burned. The night settled around him. The voice was quiet, the way a person is quiet when they have said what needed saying and are waiting, with characteristic impatience, for the slower mind to catch up.

For the first time, the quiet did not feel like absence. It felt like company. Difficult, impatient, impossible company — the kind that corrected and judged and would not leave — but company nonetheless. And the loneliness that had been the cabin's deepest wound was, for one moment, less absolute.

He did not speak. He did not acknowledge the company. He was not ready to acknowledge it because acknowledging it meant accepting that what lived inside him was not a residue or a symptom but a presence, and a presence required a name, and the name was a door he could see and had been approaching for weeks and would eventually have to open because the door was not going to go away and the presence behind it was not going to go away and the pretending that the door was not there was costing him more each day than opening it would cost all at once.

He lay beside the fire and closed his eyes. The closing was easier than it had been in weeks. The darkness behind his eyelids was not empty, and the not-empty was terrible and comforting in equal measure, and the two responses lived side by side in the same breath the way the mercy and the killing had lived side by side, the way the love and the cruelty had always lived side by side in the man whose name he would not yet say but whose presence he could no longer deny.

He slept. And the sleeping was almost like not being alone.
